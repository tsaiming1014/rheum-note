## Introduction

The inflammation within blood vessel walls that characterizes
vasculitis frequently leads to cellular destruction, damage to the
vascular structures, compromised blood flow to organs, and organ
dysfunction. Immune complex (IC)–mediated mechanisms play
critical roles in many forms of systemic vasculitis, particularly
those that involve primarily small blood vessels. The use of horse
serum and sulfonamides as therapeutic agents for infectious diseases in the early 1900s frequently led to small vessel vasculitis on
the basis of serum sickness or hypersensitivity phenomena.
Hypersensitivity angiitis,
often confused with the pauci-immune form
of vasculitis now termed
microscopic polyangiitis
(see
Chapter 90
,
Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody–Associated Vasculitis),
was one of five disorders included in the original classification of
the vasculitides by Zeek in 1952.
This chapter focuses on forms of small vessel vasculitis that are
mediated by IC deposition. These disorders include hypersensitivity vasculitis, immunoglobulin (Ig)A vasculitis, mixed cryoglobulinemia, hypocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis (UV),
and erythema elevatum diutinum (EED). IgG4-related disease
can also be associated with an IC-mediated vasculitis of the skin
and possibly other organs, but that disease is discussed primarily in its own chapter (see
Chapter 126
, IgG4-Related Disease).
In addition, forms of vasculitis associated with connective tissue diseases (CTDs), particularly systemic lupus erythematosus
(SLE), Sjögren’s syndrome, and rheumatoid vasculitis (RV), are
discussed briefly. Anti–glomerular basement membrane disease
and the pauci-immune forms of vasculitis such as those associated
with anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCAs) are discussed elsewhere. Throughout this chapter, the terms
vasculitis
and
angiitis
are used interchangeably when referring to inflammation
involving small blood vessels (capillaries, venules, and arterioles).
All forms of IC-mediated vasculitis share certain elements of
pathogenesis and have many cutaneous findings in common. The
differential diagnoses for these conditions are also very similar, and
it is therefore justifiable to consider many aspects of these diseases together. However, the epidemiology, etiology (to the extent
that it is known), unique pathophysiologic mechanisms, distinctive clinical features, and approaches to treatment are discussed
separately for each condition. Treatments are also summarized in
Table 92.1

The formation of ICs in the Arthus reaction initiates complement activation and an influx of inflammatory cells, followed
by thrombus formation and hemorrhagic infarction in the areas
of most intense inflammation. ICs, formed by the combination of
antibody and antigen, are continuously created by the reticuloendothelial system as a means of neutralizing foreign antigens, but
these are usually cleared swiftly and efficiently. Under some circumstances, however, ICs escape clearance and become deposited
within the walls of blood vessels, joints, and other tissues, inciting
inflammation and causing disease. ICs deposited in the blood vessel
walls lead to vasculitis. Similarly, those deposited within small blood
vessels of the kidney—the glomeruli—cause glomerulonephritis.

### Immunogenicity

The fate of ICs formed by the reticuloendothelial system is governed by several major factors. These include: (1) antigen load, (2)
antibody response, (3) efficiency of the reticuloendothelial system,
(4) the flow dynamics of blood vessels, (5) previous endothelial
damage, and (6) the solubility of the ICs themselves. The ratio of
antibody to antigen determines the solubility of ICs. Large ICs,
formed when antibody and antigen are present in approximately
equal proportions, are identified and removed easily by the reticuloendothelial system. Smaller ICs, formed in conditions of antibody excess, remain in the serum and do not elicit an immune
response within tissues. In the setting of slight antigen excess,
however, ICs precipitate from the serum and become trapped
within certain vascular beds. Following the deposition of ICs in
tissue, a cascade of pathologic events ensues: complement fixation,
neutrophil recruitment, local inflammation, lysosomal release,
oxygen free radical generation, and tissue injury.

## Pathology

Full pathologic assessment of cutaneous vasculitis involves examination of a skin biopsy specimen by both light microscopy and
direct immunofluorescence (DIF). DIF is a particularly critical
procedure in the evaluation of small vessel vasculitides. DIF studies must be planned at the time the biopsy is performed because
they require a fresh skin biopsy sample. It is sometimes possible to
split a skin biopsy, sending one part for routine histopathological
staining by light microscopy and the other portion for DIF.

### Light Microscopy

Fig. 92.4A
displays the light microscopy findings of cutaneous
vasculitis. The optimal time for skin biopsy is 24 to 48 hours after
the appearance of a lesion. Biopsies should be obtained from a
nonulcerated site. For ulcerated lesions—usually more typical of
a medium vessel vasculitis—biopsies should be taken from the
ulcer’s edge. The cellular infiltrates in cutaneous vasculitis are usually composed of a combination of neutrophils and lymphocytes,
but most cases demonstrate a predominance of one cell type or
the other. Lymphocyte-rich infiltrates may be seen in specimens
taken from either new (<12 hours) or old (>48 hours) lesions,
regardless of the underlying type of vasculitis. Even in connective
tissue disorders such as Sjögren’s syndrome, the typical finding is
a leukocytoclastic vasculitis rather than a lymphocytic vasculitis.
The essential histologic feature in any form of cutaneous vasculitis is the disruption of blood vessel architecture by an inflammatory infiltrate within and around the vessel walls.
Leukocytoclasis
refers to the degranulation of neutrophils (leading to the production of nuclear “dust”; see
Fig. 92.4
). Endothelial swelling and
proliferation is also evident. Extravasation of erythrocytes may be
evident in the biopsy but are not essential to the diagnosis.

### Direct Immunofluorescence

Although the diagnosis of cutaneous vasculitis rests on routine
histology, the features revealed by hematoxylin and eosin stains
do not distinguish between pauci-immune and IC-mediated disorders. DIF studies complement the histologic information and
provide the only way of diagnosing with certainty some types of
IC-mediated vasculitis (e.g., IgA vasculitis). The findings on DIF
also often yield important clues regarding the nature of the underlying disease. The performance of
separate
biopsies for histologic
and DIF analyses is recommended if sufficient numbers of lesions
exist, as is usually the case with IC-mediated vasculitis.
To perform DIF studies, frozen sections are incubated with
fluorescein-labeled anti-human IgG, IgM, IgA, and C3. Staining
for other potential immunoreactants such as C1q and IgG subclasses (e.g., IgG4) is also available but may need to be requested

## Clinical Syndromes

### Hypersensitivity Vasculitis

The term
hypersensitivity vasculitis
(see
Chapter 88
, Classification
and Epidemiology of Systemic Vasculitis) generally refers to an
IC-mediated small vessel vasculitis of the skin that spares internal
organs and usually follows drug exposures or infections. Consensus conference meetings on the nomenclature of the vasculitides
have grappled with the most appropriate way to address the terminology for hypersensitivity vasculitis, a term first used by Zeek
in 1952. The first Chapel Hill Consensus Conference in the early
1990s recommended eliminating the term
hypersensitivity vasculitis
in favor of
cutaneous leukocytoclastic angiitis
because of the
disorder’s usual confinement to the skin and its predominant cell
type, the neutrophil.
Twenty years later, the second Chapel Hill Consensus Conference retained the term
cutaneous leukocytoclastic angiitis
under a newly created category of
single-organ vasculitis,
consistent with the idea that hypersensitivity has a predilection for
the skin. That same conference also added a new nomenclature term—
drug-induced immune complex vasculitis
—under a
new category of
vasculitis associated with probable etiology
. The
concept of hypersensitivity vasculitis, a condition characterized
pathologically by IC deposition in capillaries, postcapillary
venules, and arterioles, remains firmly embedded in the medical literature and clearly encompasses both cutaneous leukocytoclastic angiitis and drug-induced IC vasculitis. Because both
the pathophysiologies and treatments of these hypersensitivity
vasculitis subsets are similar, it makes sense to consider them
together. An illness that is similar to hypersensitivity vasculitis—serum sickness—is a systemic illness that includes not
only cutaneous involvement but also inflammation of joints,
kidneys, and other organs.
In 1990 the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) performed a study designed to identify features that distinguished
one form of vasculitis from others.
The resulting ACR classification criteria for hypersensitivity vasculitis are shown in

all patients with presumed hypersensitivity vasculitis, no inciting
agent can be identified. Under the agreements of second Chapel

such a presentation would be diagnosed most precisely as having

### IgA Vasculitis (Henoch–Schönlein Purpura)

IgA vasculitis is an IC-mediated form of small vessel vasculitis
that is strongly associated with IgA deposition within blood
vessel walls. Many IgA vasculitis cases occur after upper respiratory tract infections. Multiple bacterial, viral, and other
infectious agents have been suggested as the cause of IgA vasculitis, but the true cause remains unknown. The 1990 ACR
criteria for the classification of IgA vasculitis are shown in
Table 92.4
.
A major risk factor for Henoch–Schönlein purpura (HSP) (as
well as IgA nephropathy, which has renal findings identical to
those of HSP) is aberrant glycosylation of
O
-linked glycans in the
hinge region of a fraction of IgA1 molecules.
Rather than terminating with galactose, the aberrant galactose-deficient
O
-glycans
end with
N
-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc) or sialylated GalNAc.
The terminal GalNAc moiety on the aberrantly glycosylated IgA1
may in turn be recognized by antiglycan antibodies, leading to
the formation of circulating ICs that deposit in the skin, joints,
kidneys, and other organs.
A high serum Ga-IgA1 level is not sufficient for the development of clinical symptoms. Rather, a “second hit” in the form
of another environmental or inherited risk factor is required to
produce IgA vasculitis. This probably explains why multiple types
of infections and many different drugs (e.g., antibiotics) have been
linked etiologically with IgA vasculitis.
The hallmarks of IgA vasculitis include an upper respiratory
tract infection followed by a syndrome characterized by a purpuric rash (
Fig. 92.6

and the majority of cases affect children younger than 5 years.
Nevertheless, adults can also be affected by IgA vasculitis and
have a greater tendency toward a prolonged disease course (with
recurrent bouts of purpura) than do children.

### Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis

Cryoglobulins are immunoglobulins characterized by a tendency
to precipitate from serum under conditions of cold.
Such proteins, detectable to various degrees in a wide array of inflammatory conditions, do not always cause disease. That is to say that
a patient’s serum may contain immunoglobulins that behave in
the laboratory as a cryoglobulin—precipitating from serum once
placed in a very cold refrigerator—but such immunoglobulins
may not lead to the clinical syndrome of cryoglobulinemia in the
patient possessing them. In some patients, however, cryoglobulins bind to circulating antigen (e.g., portions of the hepatitis
C virion), are deposited in the walls of small and medium-sized
blood vessels, and activate complement, leading to cryoglobulinemic vasculitis.
In contrast to most other forms of IC-mediated vasculitis,
cryoglobulinemia tends to involve both small- and medium-sized
blood vessels. Thus the syndrome of cryoglobulinemic vasculitis
can be associated with the development of large cutaneous ulcers,
digital ischemia, and livedo racemosa—findings characteristic of
disturbances in medium-sized vessels—as well as palpable purpura
and other hallmarks of small-vessel vasculitis. The first Chapel Hill
Consensus Conference provided a consensus definition for mixed
cryoglobulinemia (
Table 92.5
).
Three major types of cryoglobulinemia are recognized, defined
by the specific kinds of immunoglobulins with which they are
associated (
Table 92.6
). Type I, characterized by a monoclonal
gammopathy (generally IgG or IgM), differs substantially from
types II and III in its clinical presentation and disease associations.

infection and the cryoproteins consist of monoclonal IgM and
polyclonal IgG. Cases of type II cryoglobulinemia not associated
with hepatitis C are sometimes termed
essential mixed cryoglobulinemia
because their cause is not known. Type III cryoglobulinemia, typically associated with polyclonal IgG and polyclonal IgM,
is associated with many forms of chronic inflammation including
infection and autoimmune disease.
Type II and III cryoglobulinemias often present with a triad
of signs and symptoms: purpura, arthralgias, and myalgias. The
purpura may be extensive and confluent (
Fig. 92.7
), sometimes
involving the trunk, upper extremities, and even the face; in most
cases, however, the rash is confined to the lower extremities. Other
organ systems commonly involved in mixed cryoglobulinemia are
the kidneys and peripheral nerves. Mixed cryoglobulinemia may
cause membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis that resembles
lupus nephritis histopathologically. It may also cause a vasculitic
neuropathy, usually with sensory symptoms predominating over
motor symptoms. Finally, in rare cases, cryoglobulinemia is associated with alveolar hemorrhage.
Skin biopsy is the most straightforward method of confirming
the diagnosis. Light microscopy of purpuric lesions demonstrates
leukocytoclastic vasculitis. In addition, DIF studies reveal various
types of immunoglobulin and complement deposition, depending
on the type. In type II cryoglobulinemia, for example, DIF reveals
IgG and IgM deposition, as well as complement components.
Serologic testing may also yield clues to the presence of mixed
cryoglobulinemia. To assay for serum cryoglobulins, the blood
is collected in a prewarmed apparatus, allowed to clot at 37° C
before processing, and then refrigerated at 4° C for several days.
The percentage of the serum occupied by the cryoprecipitate is
referred to as the
cryocrit.
The difficulties involved in performing
cryoglobulin assays often lead to false-negative results. As noted,
not all detectable cryoglobulins are associated with disease.

The monoclonal component of type II cryoglobulins almost

citrullinated protein antibody negative. As markers of clinical
disease activity, C4 levels, rheumatoid factor titers, and cryocrits
all fare poorly, often remaining abnormal in the face of clinically
improved disease.
Treatment of cryoglobulinemia has seen substantial progress in
recent years, both because of therapeutic advances in the treatment of hepatitis C and because of the efficacy of B cell depletion
in treating many cases of truly essential mixed cryoglobulinemia.
For hepatitis C–associated mixed cryoglobulinemic vasculitis,
dual treatment strategies that combine anti-viral therapies and B

cell depletion approaches with rituximab are synergistic in treating
this condition and often lead to long-term treatment responses.
The rationale behind rituximab use in cryoglobulinemia is
that peripheral B lymphocyte depletion will lead to a reduction
in plasma cells that produce cryoglobulins. Studies of these treatment modalities have suggested that disease relapses are associated with the absence of virologic control and peripheral B
cell recovery, implying the need to combine the two treatment
strategies. The optimal timing of anti-viral and B cell depletion
strategies is not clear. Nevertheless, one reasonable approach is
to initiate anti-viral strategies first and then to employ rituximab
within several weeks.
Patients who present with overwhelming illness, such as the
unusual patient with alveolar hemorrhage or with symptoms
of hyperviscosity, are candidates for plasma exchange, with the
goal of removing the pathogenic ICs as quickly as possible.
One approach is to perform plasma exchange every other day
with concomitant glucocorticoid therapy for a total of seven
exchanges or until there is sufficient clinical improvement, followed by B cell depletion after plasma exchange is complete
so that the B cell depletion agent is not removed by plasma
exchange.
The prognosis of patients with cryoglobulinemia generally
depends on the underlying cause. The outcome of type I cryoglobulinemia relates closely to the success in treating the cause.
Type II or III cryoglobulinemia secondary to hepatitis C can be
treated effectively if the viral infection is responsive to therapy. If
patients do not tolerate anti-viral therapy well or if the treatment
is ineffective, they may require low to moderate doses of prednisone to control the disease.
Patients with mixed cryoglobulinemia whose disease is not
associated with either hepatitis C or a lymphoproliferative malignancy often respond well to rituximab plus glucocorticoids, with
tapering of the glucocorticoids over several weeks. Such patients
often require regular treatment with rituximab (e.g., every 6
months) to prevent disease return.

### IgG4-Related Vasculitis

IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD; see
Chapter 126
) has long been
known to be associated with a large-vessel vasculitis that is a
cause of aortitis.
More recently, it has been recognized that
IgG4-RD in fact may be classified as a variable-vessel vasculitis.
This is because it is capable of affecting blood vessels of
any size, from the aorta through medium-sized arteries (e.g.,
the coronary vessels) to the small blood vessels of the skin. One
of the histologic hallmarks of the disease—obliterative phlebitis—vividly illustrates the vascular tropism of this condition.
ICs clearly participate in the small-vessel manifestations of the
disease that present with cutaneous findings and have also been
identified in the kidney and pancreas. Their specific contributions to obliterative phlebitis and to larger vessel involvement, if
any, remain to be defined.
Cutaneous vasculitis in IgG4-RD is typically observed in
the setting of extremely high serum IgG4 concentrations, elevations of other IgG subclasses (e.g., IgG1), C3 and C4 hypocomplementemia, and multiorgan IgG4-RD. The cutaneous
findings in IgG4-related vasculitis, generally palpable purpura
or confluent purpura, are indistinguishable from other types of
cutaneous vasculitis but are typical of IC-mediated cutaneous
vasculitides in being widespread in their distribution across the
skin (
Fig. 92.8
).

### Hypocomplementemic Urticarial Vasculitis

Three different syndromes of UV are recognized: normocomplementemic UV, hypocomplementemic UV (HUV), and the
hypocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis syndrome (HUVS).
Normocomplementemic UV is typically a self-limited subset of
hypersensitivity vasculitis. In chronic cases, normocomplementemic UV must be distinguished carefully from neutrophilic urticaria, a persistent form of urticaria not associated with vasculitis.
In contrast, HUV is more likely to be a chronic disorder that has
certain overlapping features with SLE: low serum complements,
autoantibodies, and an interface dermatitis characterized by
immunoreactant deposition (complement and immunoglobulins)
at the dermal–epidermal junction in a pattern essentially identical to the lupus band test. Finally, HUVS is a severe form of
the disease associated with extracutaneous disease and an array of
organ system findings atypical of SLE.
For example, HUVS may
be associated with uveitis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
(COPD), and angioedema.
Unlike common urticaria, UV lesions are frequently associated with moderate pain, burning, and tenderness in addition
to pruritus. Whereas common urticaria typically resolve completely within 24 to 48 hours, UV lesions may take days to resolve

completely, often leaving residual hyperpigmentation; they may
worsen without therapy. Patients with HUV or HUVS may pre
sent with purpuric lesions that are more dominant than urticarial
lesions.
The skin lesions in UV tend to be centripetal, favoring the
trunk and proximal extremities more than dependent regions.
The lesions are painful and associated with a burning sensation rather than the pruritus of common urticaria. Biopsy of
an urticarial wheal in UV demonstrates evidence of leukocytoclastic vasculitis, including injury to the endothelial cells of
the postcapillary venules, erythrocyte extravasation, leukocytoclasis, fibrin deposition, and a perivascular neutrophilic (or,
less commonly, lymphocytic) infiltrate. DIF demonstrates IC
deposition around blood vessels in the superficial dermis and
a striking deposition of immunoglobulins and complement
along the dermal–epidermal junction. In the proper setting,
these findings (interface dermatitis and immunoreactant deposition within blood vessels) are diagnostic of HUV. HUVS, in
contrast, is a clinical diagnosis based on the presence of UV
and the occurrence of typical features in extracutaneous organ
systems.
Some cases of HUV respond to therapies commonly used
for the treatment of SLE, including low-dose prednisone,
hydroxychloroquine, dapsone, or other immunomodulatory
agents. Serious cases of HUVS, particularly those presenting with glomerulonephritis or other forms of serious organ
involvement, may require high doses of glucocorticoids or
biologic agents such as tumor necrosis factor inhibitors or B
cell depletion. Both COPD and cardiac valve abnormalities are
associated with HUVS and may require specific treatment as
well.
The prognosis of UV is often linked to the disorder with which
it is associated. SLE, COPD, angioedema, and valvular abnormalities are all known to occur in association with this disorder and
may strongly influence both quality and quantity of life. Some
cases of HUV and HUVS resolve completely following courses of
immunosuppression.

### Erythema Elevatum Diutinum

EED is a rare, distinctive form of leukocytoclastic vasculitis
limited to the skin.
The disorder is distinctive because of the
unusual distribution of skin lesions (found symmetrically over the
extensor surfaces of joints) and the prompt response to sulfone
medications. The cutaneous findings are typical of any small vessel
vasculitis, with a predominance of papules, plaques, and nodules.
Early lesions are often pink or yellowish and then become red or
purple (
Fig. 92.9
). The natural history of untreated lesions is to
persist for years, becoming doughy or hard with time. The lesions
have a predilection for the skin overlying the small joints of the
hands and the knees; they can also affect the buttocks. The trunk
is generally spared.
The principal histopathologic findings of EED are leukocytoclastic vasculitis with fibrinoid necrosis. Although an IC basis is
suspected in this disorder, DIF studies are not distinctive. EED
has been associated with various CTDs, rheumatoid arthritis,
other forms of vasculitis (such as GPA), HIV infections, and
paraproteinemias (particularly IgA). EED typically responds
promptly to dapsone or sulfapyridine, but chronic therapy
may be required because skin lesions recur after cessation of
treatment.

### Autoimmune Disease–Associated Vasculitis

Vasculitis rarely occurs in autoimmune diseases or CTDs
without overt manifestations of the underlying disorder. The
forms of CTD typically complicated by vasculitis include those
related to SLE: lupus itself, mixed CTD, Sjögren’s syndrome,
and overlap CTD. Although vasculitis clearly occurs in some
CTD settings, it is commonly overdiagnosed in attempts to
explain perplexing disease features in patients with known
rheumatic illnesses. For example, neuropsychiatric SLE is generally not caused by a true vasculitis but rather by other mechanisms that remain poorly defined. Whenever possible, the
clinical hypothesis of vasculitis should be confirmed by biopsy
before a label of “vasculitis” is given to poorly understood disease manifestations.
Cutaneous vasculitis in CTDs is associated almost invariably with hypocomplementemia and high titers of anti-nuclear
antibodies (ANAs). DIF examination of skin lesions shows
granular IgG and C3 deposition in and around dermal vessels, with or without IgM, reflecting the contribution of ICs to
disease pathogenesis. The phenomenon of the “in vivo ANA” is
also observed in keratinocytes and dermal cells in DIF studies
(
Fig. 92.10
).
Vasculitis in patients with SLE-related disorders is more likely
than other forms of vasculitis to be associated with a lymphocytic
predominance. One variant of CTD-associated cutaneous vasculitis, the so-called
benign hypergammaglobulinemia of Waldenström,
is usually a true lymphocytic vasculitis. Patients with this disorder invariably have anti-Ro antibodies, and many have subclinical
Sjögren’s syndrome. Lymphocytic vasculitis typically demonstrates
less disruption of blood vessel architecture than does leukocytoclastic vasculitis, perhaps because lymphocytes contain fewer of
the destructive enzymes found within neutrophil granules. Fibrinoid necrosis, for example, is rare in lymphocytic vasculitis. True
lymphocytic vasculitis is nearly always confined to the small blood
vessels of the superficial papillary dermis. Even in Sjögren’s syndrome, however, the histopathology encountered in most cases is
that of leukocytoclastic vasculitis.

### Rheumatoid Vasculitis

RV must be distinguished from the isolated, periungual digital vasculitis that, in the absence of severe involvement, does not require
intensive, vasculitis-specific therapy. Isolated digital vasculitis in
patients with rheumatoid arthritis, characterized by splinter-like
lesions in the periungual region (Bywaters lesions), is not necessarily associated with a poorer prognosis than rheumatoid arthritis without digital vasculitic lesions and does not require specific
therapy for vasculitis. In contrast, RV is a potentially devastating complication that may involve both medium and small blood
vessels and requires the most aggressive therapeutic interventions.
Many clinical manifestations of RV are indistinguishable from
polyarteritis nodosa, although microaneurysms are less common
in RV. RV classically occurs in patients with nodular, rheumatoid
factor–positive, joint-destructive disease who have few clinical
indications of active synovitis at the time vasculitis begins. RV
occasionally complicates early disease.
The most common presentation of RV includes purpuric
lesions with or without evidence of concomitant medium vessel
vasculitis. DIF examination of the skin lesions shows granular
IgM and C3 deposition in vessels, consistent with an IC-mediated
pathophysiology in which rheumatoid factor, complement, and
cryoglobulins may all participate.
Deep cutaneous ulcers near the malleoli are a hallmark of RV
and require scrupulous local care, as well as judicious immunosuppression. Mononeuritis multiplex often complicates RV. Highdose glucocorticoids plus rituximab is an appropriate first-choice
regimen for many patients with RV, with the addition of cyclophosphamide if there is evidence of advancing disease. Tumor
necrosis factor inhibitors may also be reasonable options for
patients with milder disease.
