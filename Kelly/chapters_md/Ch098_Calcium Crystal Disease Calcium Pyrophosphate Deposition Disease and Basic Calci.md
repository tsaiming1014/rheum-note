## Introduction

Both calcium pyrophosphate (CPP) and basic calcium phosphate
(BCP) crystals are highly inflammatory in some settings and can
coexist in individual joints. They share some common mineralization pathways. No disease-remitting agents for either calcium
pyrophosphate deposition (CPPD) disease or BCP-associated
arthritis currently exist. Attention to the important clinical syndromes associated with calcium-containing crystals in rheumatology has lagged behind many less common rheumatic diseases. In
this chapter, we discuss the clinical and epidemiologic features of
CPPD and BCP-associated disease, explore pathogenic pathways,
and summarize current diagnostic strategies and management
paradigms.

## Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition Disease

CPPD is a relatively new disease, first described in the early
1960s. It is defined by the presence of articular CPP crystals and
includes a spectrum of both acute and chronic presentations of
arthritis collectively known as CPPD. It remains considerably
underdiagnosed and is quite common in elderly patients. The
diagnosis is primarily based on CPP crystal identification using
polarized light microscopy and characteristic radiographic findings. Treatment modalities remain largely symptomatic and are
supported by few high-quality studies.

### Clinical Features

KEY POINTS

> CPPD is associated with a widely variable group of clinical features.

> The most commonly recognized form of symptomatic CPPD is an acute

> monoarticular gout-like arthritis known as acute CPP crystal arthritis.

> CPPD can also present in a chronic osteoarthritis-like pattern and as a

> chronic polyarticular inflammatory arthritis resembling rheumatoid

> arthritis.

> Asymptomatic CPPD is frequently recognized as radiographic

> chondrocalcinosis.

> Other, less common presentations include crowned dens syndrome,

> tophaceous CPPD, and a Charcot-like arthropathy.

Acute Calcium Pyrophosphate Crystal Arthritis
Acute CPP crystal arthritis, formerly known as pseudogout, is
by far the most commonly recognized clinical presentation of

CPPD disease. This type of arthritis produces acute onset of
severe pain, swelling, erythema, and warmth of the involved
joint. It typically causes a monoarticular or oligoarticular
arthritis. The knee is most commonly involved, followed by
the wrist.
The ankles, shoulders, and elbows are also common
sites of acute CPP crystal arthritis. Small joints in the feet and
hands may also be involved and, in these cases, inflammation
often spreads beyond the joint. Fever is common, and accompanying signs of inflammation, including circulating leukocytosis, and high inflammatory markers are typical. These features
render acute CPP crystal arthritis indistinguishable from gout
and septic arthritis in the absence of synovial analysis. Acute
CPP crystal arthritis episodes are self-limited but typically
last longer than gout attacks, with symptoms present for up
to several weeks in the absence of treatment.
The natural history of acute CPP crystal arthritis is not fully understood. A
recent study showed that in 70 patients with acute CPP crystal
arthritis, 25% had a second flare with a rate of 11.4 per 100
person-years.
Crowned dens syndrome
is a variant of acute CPP crystal arthritis involving the cervical spine. It typically presents with acute or
subacute onset of severe neck pain and limited range of motion.
This is often accompanied by fever, and sometimes by delirium.
Chondrocalcinosis seen on computed tomography (CT) scanning
in the soft tissues around the dens helps confirm the diagnosis.
Occasionally, aspiration is done, but this is a tricky area to access.
As in all forms of acute CPP crystal arthritis, inflammatory markers are elevated and peripheral white blood cell counts may be
high. Crowned dens syndrome is often mistaken for meningitis.
Less commonly, the differential diagnosis includes giant cell arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica, tumor, epidural abscess, or fever
of unknown origin. Like all acute CPP crystal arthritis, crowned
dens syndrome is self-limited, but attack length can vary from
days to months.

Chronic Inflammatory Calcium Pyrophosphate Crystal
Arthritis
Chronic inflammatory CPPD is challenging to diagnosis and
often falls under the umbrella terms of seronegative inflammatory arthritis or seronegative rheumatoid arthritis (RA).
It is characterized by chronic inflammation in multiple joints. The joint
involvement is less symmetric than RA.
The joint distribution is
similar to that of acute CPP crystal arthritis and typically involves
the 2nd and 3rd metacarpophalangeal joints, wrists, elbows,
shoulders, knees, and ankles. Accompanying symptoms and signs
may include prolonged morning stiffness, fatigue, synovitis on
physical examination, and high inflammatory markers. About
10% of patients will have low-titer positive rheumatoid factors
(RFs), likely related to the increased prevalence of RF positivity
with age.

Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition With Osteoarthritis
CPP crystals are present in 20% to 30% of patients diagnosed
with knee osteoarthritis (OA). This rate is very consistent across
different studies.
CPPD limited to the knee accompanied by a
history of prior meniscal surgery or injury may represent a unique
limited form of CPPD.
If CPPD involves multiple joints, the
pattern of joint involvement differs from typical OA. CPPD with
OA involves joints such as the shoulder, wrist, metacarpophalangeal joints, and ankles. Affected individuals may or may not have
attacks of acute CPP crystal arthritis. Interestingly, several studies
have demonstrated higher pain scores in patients with calciumcontaining crystals in knee joints compared with those without
evidence of crystals.
Asymptomatic Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition
Chondrocalcinosis is often seen as an incidental finding on conventional radiographs in the absence of articular symptoms.
This
may be an early phase of CPPD disease, which precedes symptom
onset. As such, it is analogous to asymptomatic hyperuricemia,
which increases the risk for symptomatic disease but in and of
itself does not warrant therapy.
Less Common Clinical Manifestations of Calcium
Pyrophosphate Deposition
Tophaceous CPPD
. Tophaceous CPP crystal deposits are
unusual and described almost exclusively in case reports.
They
are often tumor-like in that they invade bone and very frequently
are diagnosed by biopsy. They are most frequently described in the
periarticular areas around large joints such as hips and shoulders
and can also occur in the temporomandibular joint.
Spinal Involvement in CPPD
. Radiographic chondrocalcinosis is a common finding in the spine of older patients,
yet the
incidence of symptoms attributable to spinal CPPD is unknown.
Radiographic CPPD in the spine typically involves annulus fibrosus calcifications, subchondral erosions, and vacuum phenomena
of the sacroiliac joints. Crowned dens syndrome is the most commonly recognized form of spinal CPPD. However, other clinical
presentations abound. In the largest study of spinal CPPD, 37
of 152 hospitalized patients with spinal CPPD had symptoms of
inflammation.
Peripheral joint involvement was common and
the most frequent site of involvement was the cervical spine. The
ligamentum flavum is a common site of crystal deposition. Presentations other than crowned dens syndrome varied from discitis to sacroiliitis to mass effects from tophaceous CPP crystal
deposits in or near the spine. Facet joints can be involved. Severe
degenerative changes were also noted.
Charcot-like Arthropathy
. This is a very rare type of CPPD,
characterized by a severe destructive arthritis similar to that seen
with Charcot arthropathy, but in the absence of significant neuropathology.
It has been most frequently described in the foot
and ankle.

Metabolic Causes
CPPD. Indeed, the prevalence of chondrocalcinosis doubles with
each decade over the age of 60 and is rare in younger people.
Joint injury as a risk factor for CPPD has been best studied in the
knee
but has also been observed in discs after spinal surgeries.
The onset of CPPD in patients under the age of 60 without injury
warrants investigation for familial or metabolic causes.
Genetic Causes
. Familial forms of CPPD were described in
the early CPPD literature.
Most kindreds show an autosomal
dominant pattern of inheritance. Initial analysis of multiple kindreds showed two clusters of mutations in affected families.
The
cluster of mutations on chromosome 5P in the region known as
CCAL2 were noted to be in a gene coding for a protein called
progressive ankylosis protein homolog (ANKH) in 2002.
The
multipass transmembrane ANKH protein increases local levels of
pyrophosphate in cartilage by regulating extra-cellular adenosine
triphosphate (ATP) levels.
Presumed gain of function mutations
cause CPPD. The second cluster of mutations on 8q was termed

CCAL1.
In 2014 a family with premature OA and CPPD was
shown to have a mutation in the osteoprotegerin gene.
Subsequently, other CPPD kindreds were described with this identical
mutation.
This mutation has recently been shown to produce a
loss of function in this critical mineralization-regulating protein
best known for its role in bone metabolism.
The mechanism
through which it contributes to CPPD remains to be determined.

Hyperparathyroidism and hereditary hemochromatosis have consistently shown associations with chondrocalcinosis.
Hypophosphatasia, characterized by deficiency of alkaline phosphatase—the enzyme responsible for breakdown of inorganic
pyrophosphate—has been linked to CPPD disease in case reports
and has not been carefully investigated in large-scale studies, perhaps because of the low prevalence of this metabolic disorder.
Interestingly, arthritis rarely improves after appropriate management of the underlying cause in cases of metabolic diseases.
Many of the publications on CPPD disease before the 21st
century were based on case reports and small clinic-based cohorts.
Larger-scale epidemiologic studies in the 21st century evaluating associations between CPPD disease and other factors typically identified patients using diagnosis codes. Diagnosis codes
for chondrocalcinosis and calcium metabolism disorders demonstrated high positive predictive value (PPV) of 91% for symptomatic CPPD disease in a nationwide Veterans Administration data
set using other types of inflammatory arthritis as the comparator
group but had a less robust PPV of 68% in an academic medical
center.
Accurate identification of acute CPP crystal arthritis in
electronic health record data was possible using machine learning
approaches that integrate data from narrative notes and radiology reports with structured data, including synovial fluid crystal
analysis laboratory results and diagnosis codes, achieving a PPV
of 81%.
Application of these methods to population-based data
in the United States is challenging given the lack of a nationwide
electronic health record system, though may be promising in contexts in which electronic health records are available for an entire
population.

### Long-Term Outcomes in Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition Disease

Approximately 25% of individuals with an initial episode of
acute CPP crystal arthritis have recurrent episodes, as reported
in two small cohort studies.
Patients with a history of cancer
or chemotherapy may be at greater risk for recurrent episodes of
acute CPP crystal arthritis.
Studies investigating the association between radiographic chondrocalcinosis and progression of
OA have produced conflicting results, as summarized in
Table
98.3
. Two large cohort studies identified no association, one study
identified an inverse association, and yet another study reported
a positive association between chondrocalcinosis and progressive
cartilage loss.
Differences in study population demographics and modes of cartilage assessment might account for discrepant findings.
Extra-articular outcomes in CPPD disease remain understudied. Cardiovascular disease is a leading cause of morbidity and
mortality in gout, RA, psoriatic arthritis, and systemic lupus erythematosus, but few studies have focused on cardiovascular disease
in CPPD disease.
Two recent cohort studies reported a significantly increased risk of nonfatal cardiovascular events (myocardial
infarction, acute coronary syndrome, coronary revascularization,
or stroke) in patients with CPPD disease versus matched comparators.
Risk of cardiovascular events was 25% greater in U.S.
Veterans with CPPD disease versus matched controls.
Individuals having had an episode of acute CPP crystal arthritis had an
even higher two-fold increased risk for cardiovascular events in an
academic medical center cohort study.

### Diagnosis

KEY POINTS

> Compensated polarized light microscopy is an imperfect gold standard but

> remains a critical test for confirming a diagnosis of CPPD disease.

> Ryan and McCarty’s proposed diagnostic criteria were developed in an

> era preceding availability of advanced imaging modalities that have

> since demonstrated high accuracy for identifying CPP deposits.

> Ultrasound demonstrates greater sensitivity and only slightly lower

> specificity for CPP deposits compared with conventional radiography.

Diagnostic uncertainty and delay in diagnosing CPPD disease
impact the patient experience of disease.
Individuals with
chronic CPP crystal inflammatory arthritis may be initially
misdiagnosed with seronegative RA due to the clinical similarity and lack of recognition of chronic CPP crystal inflammatory arthritis as a disease entity, leading to unsuccessful courses
of RA treatments.
Methods to improve diagnostic accuracy for CPPD disease, as well as campaigns to increase provider awareness, have potential to facilitate earlier diagnosis,
especially for patients with chronic CPP crystal inflammatory
arthritis.
Ryan and McCarty’s proposed diagnostic criteria for CPPD
disease characterize three degrees of certainty based on the amount
of evidence: definite, probable, and possible.
In their schematic,
a definite diagnosis of CPPD requires both synovial fluid crystal
analysis demonstrating CPP crystals and chondrocalcinosis on CR
in a symptomatic joint.
A probable diagnosis of CPPD disease
requires just one or the other of these, and possible CPPD disease
is characterized by OA in unusual joints or a number of other
features.
Synovial fluid analysis remains a critical diagnostic tool
for CPPD. As shown in
Fig. 98.1
, CPP crystals are positively
birefringent and rhomboid or rod shaped. They can be seen
inside cells and are also significant when found extra-cellularly.

Crystal Formation
Many factors are involved in CPP crystal formation. This process most often takes place in cartilage matrix and is rare in other
tissues. An overabundance of pyrophosphate (PPi) in the extracellular matrix of cartilage is an essential requirement for crystal
formation. Thus PPi metabolism has been the focus of much of
the work on CPP pathogenesis.
PPi is generated constitutively
by chondrocytes and regulated by factors such as transforming

growth factorβ
and interleukin (IL)-1
β
. ATP transporters, notably the ANKH protein, also regulate chondrocyte PPi levels.
Extra-cellular matrix also plays a role in crystal formation.
More
recently, bone abnormalities have been implicated in CPP crystal
formation.
Inflammation
CPP crystals induce inflammation through pathways similar to
those of monosodium urate crystals, which involve the NLRP3
inflammasome,
and neutrophil extra-cellular traps.
Through
activation of nuclear factorκ
B and mitogen-activated protein
kinases, CPP crystals promote the release of active IL-1
β
from
inflammatory cells. While factors triggering an inflammatory
response from crystals (an acute attack) have not been fully elucidated, it has been proposed that joint injury may dislodge crystals
from their protected environment in cartilage. Similar to what
occurs in gout, events that promote crystal dissolution may also
initiate inflammation as small crystals are more inflammatory
than large ones.

Direct Effects on Cartilage and Synovium
CPP crystals exert direct effects on chondrocytes and synovial
cells. They induce synovial fibroblast replication in synovial cells
and protease production by chondrocytes.

### Treatment

KEY POINTS

> Targeted therapies to prevent or remove CPP crystals do not exist.

> EULAR CPPD disease management recommendations include

> nonpharmacologic measures and medical therapy (intra-articular

> glucocorticoid injection, oral glucocorticoids, colchicine, nonsteroidal

> anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), methotrexate, and/or

> hydroxychloroquine), depending on the CPPD disease manifestation.

> Only a few small randomized trials of CPPD treatments have been

> published to date (methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine, magnesium,

> anakinra). A recently completed large randomized trial comparing

> colchicine versus prednisone suggests similar efficacy for acute CPP

> crystal arthritis.

> IL-1 inhibition and IL-6 receptor inhibition are promising treatments for

> acute CPP crystal arthritis and chronic CPP inflammatory arthritis.

Management of CPPD disease is guided by the presenting
manifestation(s). The EULAR 2011 CPPD management recommendations include pharmacologic and nonpharmacologic
treatment for acute CPP crystal arthritis and chronic CPP crystal inflammatory arthritis; no specific therapies are recommended
for OA with CPPD aside from typical OA treatments.
Medical therapies to prevent CPP crystal formation or to remove CPP
crystals from the joint do not currently exist.
Acute CPP crystal arthritis is usually treated with NSAIDs,
colchicine, and/or glucocorticoids, and treatment selection is
partly guided by comorbidities and avoidance of medication
interactions.
Rest and ice may help with symptoms but are
unlikely to be adequate alone. Joint aspiration aids in diagnosis
and ruling out gout or septic arthritis and can also provide therapeutic benefit. Intra-articular glucocorticoid injection is a good
option for treating monoarthritis once septic arthritis has been
ruled out. A short course of oral glucocorticoids (several days to
several weeks) is often used, especially for patients with acute CPP
crystal arthritis in multiple joints. A short course (e.g., 3 to 9 days)
of the IL-1 inhibitor anakinra showed benefit for treating acute
CPP crystal arthritis in a number of case reports.
A randomized controlled trial comparing a 3-day regimen of anakinra versus
prednisone was terminated early due to recruitment challenges;
among the 15 participants, changes in pain scores and C-reactive
protein (CRP) showed a trend toward favoring anakinra without
reaching statistical significance.
A recent large randomized controlled trial of treatment for
acute CPP crystal arthritis merits specific mention. One group
enrolled hospitalized adults age 65 and older with an estimated
glomerular filtration rate greater than 30 mL/min/1.73 m
and
acute CPP crystal arthritis duration of 36 hours or less.
One
hundred and twelve participants were randomized a 2-day regimen of oral prednisone 30 mg daily (days 1 and 2) or oral colchicine (1.0 mg followed by 0.5 mg 1 hour later on day 1, plus 1 mg
once on day 2). All participants received acetaminophen 1000 mg
three times daily and tramadol 50 mg three times daily on day 1.
Providers were permitted to prescribe prednisone, colchicine,
or other treatments as needed on day 3 onward. The betweengroup difference in change in pain on the visual analog scale at 24
hours was −1.2 mm, which was not statistically significant. Notably, some patients continued to receive prednisone, colchicine,
or other treatments after day 2 for pain relief. Diarrhea was the
main adverse event in the colchicine group (26%), and hypertension was the most common adverse event in the prednisone group
(13%). These results support the equivalence of a short course
of colchicine or prednisone for patients with acute CPP crystal
arthritis, in the context of other pain relief medications.
Chronic CPP crystal inflammatory arthritis can be challenging to treat. Methotrexate has been evaluated in several studies,
including a randomized controlled trial among 26 participants
that did not detect a difference in outcomes between methotrexate and placebo; however, it should be noted that the maximum
methotrexate dose was only 15 mg per week and the study duration was only 3 months, which likely limited the ability to truly
assess the potential impact of methotrexate on CPPD disease.
Hydroxychloroquine 400 mg per day had a beneficial effect on
swollen and tender joint counts and pain in patients with CPPD
disease (phenotypes were not well described) versus placebo in a
randomized trial among 36 participants.
A small randomized
controlled trial in 38 participants with “chronic pyrophosphate
arthropathy” compared 6 months of oral magnesium 30 milliequivalents per day versus placebo and reported a nonsignificant
trend toward improved joint stiffness with magnesium regardless
of initial serum magnesium level.
Oral colchicine demonstrated
a reduced number of flares in a small trial of 10 patients with
chronic CPP crystal arthritis, though the majority of patients were
also using NSAIDs, which may have contributed to the observed
reduction in flares.
Synovial destruction via intra-articular irradiation produced reductions in pain, joint stiffness, and effusion
among 15 patients with chronic CPP crystal arthritis of the knee,
though this mechanism has not been well explored in recent
decades.
IL-1 and IL-6 receptor inhibitors are promising for the treatment of chronic CPP inflammatory arthritis. A systematic literature review included pooled data from seven patients with
chronic CPP crystal arthritis treated with anakinra for 139 days
on average; tender and swollen counts and CRP levels decreased
post-anakinra compared with pre-anakinra.
A recent pilot study
evaluating the use of tocilizumab in 11 patients with CPPD disease

(7 with chronic CPP crystal inflammatory arthritis and 4 with
recurrent acute CPP crystal arthritis) reported improved patient
global assessment at 3 months and ability to taper prednisone.
Classification criteria for CPPD disease, recently published
and endorsed jointly by the American College of Rheumatology
and EULAR, will facilitate identification of patients for enrollment in clinical trials to identify effective treatments for CPPD
disease.
The OMERACT CPPD Working Group is establishing a set of core outcome domains to be measured in future
clinical trials, which will facilitate comparisons across studies.

## Basic Calcium Phosphate–Associated Arthritis

BCP crystals refer to submicron-sized calcium phosphate crystals including carbonate-substituted hydroxyapatite, octacalcium, tricalcium phosphates, and magnesium whitlockite.
In
the musculoskeletal system, BCP crystals are most commonly
associated with two clinical syndromes; calcific periarthritis and
BCP-associated arthritis. The detection of BCP crystals in the
synovial fluid is fraught with challenges due to the submicroscopic size of BCP, the complex nature of the matrix in which
they are found, and the fact that other crystals can coexist.
Hence, diagnosis is based on radiographic detection of calcifications characteristic of BCP crystals or detection of BCP crystals
confirmed by transmission electron microscopy or specialized
crystal analytic approaches.

### Clinical Manifestations

KEY POINTS

> BCP crystals are associated with multiple musculoskeletal syndromes.

> Periarticular BCP crystal deposition can produce acute calcific

> periarthritis.

> Intra-articular BCP crystals are associated with the rare destructive

> non-inflammatory arthritis known as Milwaukee shoulder syndrome.

> Intra-articular BCP crystals are also common in synovial fluid and

> tissues from osteoarthritic joints.

BCP deposition is often asymptomatic but can be associated with
a wide spectrum of clinical manifestations. Although BCP crystals
most frequently deposit in joint tissue, these deposits can also be
found in the arteries, skin, breasts, and other tissues. In the musculoskeletal system, crystals are localized to cartilage, synovium,
bursae, intervertebral discs, and tendon.
Periarticular BCP
crystal deposition can lead to calcific tendinitis, periarthritis, bursitis, and bone erosions.
Intra-articular BCP crystal deposition
is associated with a variety of degenerative manifestations, most
notably Milwaukee shoulder syndrome (MSS), a form of apatiteassociated destructive arthropathy. It can also cause acute synovitis (albeit to a much lesser extent than CPP) and severe OA.
Several secondary forms of BCP crystal-associated arthropathy
have also been described (e.g., tumoral calcinosis, and calciphylaxis, which is a severe complication of renal failure characterized
by nodular subcutaneous calcifications).
Currently, there are no
validated classification criteria for BCP arthritis. In CPPD arthritis, a data- and expert-driven process is currently underway and
is near completion to develop CPPD classification criteria. It is
likely there will be similar work on establishing BCP classification
in the future. Such criteria would facilitate clinical research and
help address the major shortfalls in recognizing, understanding,
and specifically treating the disorder (
Table 98.4
).
Periarticular Basic Calcium Phosphate
Acute calcific periarthritis (ACP) is a self-limiting, monoarticular, periarticular process of dystrophic mineral deposition
and adjacent inflammation. It is thought to be caused by the
rupture of pre-existing calcific deposits and their transport into
an adjacent soft tissue space, which in turn drives an acute
inflammatory reaction. Patients present with a sudden onset of
pain, localized swelling, erythema, tenderness, and restricted
range of motion. Symptoms typically reduce in severity within
4 to 7 days and generally self-resolve in 3 to 4 weeks.
As the
name suggests, mineral deposition is most prevalent in a periarticular location; however, deposits within a bursa, at tendon
insertions, or fascial attachments distant to the joint may also
occur.
Calcific periarthritis has a predilection for the shoulder but it can be found in any joint area of the body. Shoulder
involvement is typically a painful disorder characterized by the
presence of calcifications in either the midsubstance or insertion of the rotator cuff tendons and in the synovial tissues,
including the subacromial bursa. The supraspinatus tendon
(78%) is the most frequently involved, followed by the infraspinatus (16%), the subscapularis (6%), and the long biceps
tendons.
The calcific material is composed of clusters of
calcium hydroxyapatite in crystalline or amorphous form.
Clinical features include pain related to repetitive activities,
tenderness, local edema, and varying degrees of movement
impairment. Even without treatment, most patients report
symptom resolution in 3 to 4 weeks.
Outside the shoulder
joint, the periarticular structures are more superficial, and
clinical signs such as erythema, swelling, and local tenderness
will therefore be more prominent in more superficial joints and
may mimic an infective cause.
Relapse of ACP is uncommon
but a significant number of patients remain symptomatic, with
no radiographic evidence of improvement.
In these cases, the
natural cycle of calcific deposition within tendons followed by
resorption is somehow halted. Persistent calcification can lead
to local complications of joint tenderness, tendon tears, adhesive capsulitis, and chronic pain.

### Epidemiology

KEY POINT

> Much about the prevalence of BCP-associated clinical syndromes

> remains to be understood.

The epidemiology of BCP crystal deposition is poorly understood,
and its true prevalence remains unknown.
Prevalence of the various forms of articular BCP crystal deposition disease has been predominantly based on characteristic plain radiographic features of
the disease in limited numbers of joints. Other studies have based
prevalence on the results of synovial fluid analyses, but definitive
studies based on pathologic findings on examination of articular
cartilages, or imaging superior to plain radiography, are lacking.
As such, the true prevalence of pathologic articular BCP crystal
deposition is not known.
BCP crystal deposition was first described by Robert Adams,
and later by Duplay, in the shoulder, which remains the most
affected site.
The best epidemiologic data on calcific periarthritis comes from a U.S. study of more than 12,000 shoulder
radiographs undertaken as part of an insurance medical assessment. Calcific tendonitis occurred in 3% of this adult population.
Calcifications were more common in women and, unlike
other crystal deposition (urate, CPPD), the prevalence was highest in those younger than age 40.
Calcific periarthritis occurring
at sites other than the shoulder is uncommon but not rare. Cases
have been reported in the hand, around the elbow, and in the
knee, hip, ankle, and foot.
Although a periarticular location
is most common, deposits may be extra-articular and found in

bursae, tendon insertions of large muscles, or fascial attachments.
Most calcifications, however, are asymptomatic, with just more
than one-third of patients with shoulder involvement being symptomatic
and one-third reporting antecedent trauma.
Calcific
shoulder periarthritis is three-fold more common in people with
type 2 diabetes mellitus than in age- and sex-matched controls.
It also has associations with hyperlipidemia, minor trauma, and
hypomagnesemia.
Diabetes of protracted duration treated
with insulin for a long time was associated with a larger percentage
of shoulder calcifications.
Periarticular calcification is also seen
in scleroderma patients and may result in acute calcific periarthritis. In contrast to idiopathic acute calcific periarthritis, resorption of periarticular calcifications after resolution of acute attacks
occurs both less frequently and incompletely in scleroderma-associated ACP.
Intra-articular BCP crystal deposition is associated with
increasing age and OA. The presence of BCP crystals in the synovial fluid of osteoarthritic knees correlates strongly with the severity of radiographic OA, compared with synovial fluids derived
from OA patients without BCP crystals.
In contrast to calcific
periarthritis, apatite associated destructive arthropathy is more
common in elderly women.
It targets the shoulders, knees, hips,
elbows, and ankles. Synovial fluid is often viscous, with low cell
counts.
In a comprehensive review using data from more than
72 patients with MSS, the mean (range) age of patients was 72
(50–90) years, and there was a 4:1 female preponderance.
The
dominant shoulder is more commonly affected. Trauma, overuse,
neurologic involvement, dialysis, and CPPD were the identifiable
risk factors, however, no risk factors were apparent in a large proportion of patients.

### Diagnostic Tools

KEY POINTS

> The diagnosis of BCP-related syndromes typically relies on clinical

> symptoms and signs.

> Periarthritis is characterized by visible calcified soft tissue deposits on

> radiographs.

> Because BCP crystals are not consistently birefringent and typically too

> small to see on regular light microscopy, stains or advanced analytic

> techniques are necessary to confirm their presence.

The diagnosis of BCP arthritis is based on the presence of characteristic clinical features of joint swelling and pain, in association
with synovial fluid analysis that excludes inflammation, infection, and other crystal arthropathies and radiographic imaging
showing substantial joint injury. No simple test for BCP crystals
is currently available and their detection is difficult due to their
small size (nm) and the complexity of the synovial fluid samples
in which they are found. Individual crystals are typically less than
1 mm (20–100 nm) and aggregate in the synovial fluid to form
amorphous-looking clumps.
Light microscopy, which is readily
available, has a limit of detection (>1
μ
m) insufficient for detecting most BCP crystals except when they are present in clumps.
Microscopy with specific staining such as alizarin red staining of
synovial fluid can identify the presence of calcium-containing
crystals without distinguishing BCP from CPP crystals (
Fig.
98.9
). Although CPP crystals stain weaker than BCP crystals, this
technique often leads to a false-positive interpretation of results.
The preparation and dyeing of samples are laborious processes. As
a result of this, more specific methods have been used, including
14 C-ethane-1-hydroxy-1,1-diphosphonate binding assay, which
is sensitive to within 2
μ
g of standard hydroxyapatite crystals/
mL,
and tetracycline staining, which binds to BCP crystals and
appears fluorescent under ultraviolet light.
X-ray diffraction is used for the examination of BCP crystals
and other crystalline materials. It involves a beam of x-rays passed
through the specimen and scattered. The scattering of x-rays is
observed with a detector that can determine the structural characteristics of the material. Raman spectroscopy is another technique that involves focusing a laser beam onto a sample through
a confocal microscope. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy
involves making pellets of 1 to 2 mg of crushed samples mixed
with potassium bromide, which are then pressed, and infrared
absorption spectra by transmission are collected. Atomic force
microscopy can measure microparticles in the order of nanometers and can measure force and topographic images. Transmission
electron microscopy uses an accelerated beam of electrons, which
passes through a very thin specimen to observe features such as
the structure and morphology of electron-dense clumps of needlelike crystals that may be necessary to confirm BCP crystal deposition. Access to this imaging apparatus is limited and widespread
availability in clinical practice is limited. Many centers rely on the
clinical features of presentation and radiologic imaging for diagnosis. Thus a simple, reliable, and inexpensive method for BCP
crystal identification is still needed.
X-ray is useful for identifying calcific tendonitis and MSS.
Calcific tendonitis appears on x-ray with homogenous hyperdense
calcifications with variable morphology but is typically globular
with smooth or ill-defined margins.
Milwaukee shoulder has a
dramatic appearance on x-ray with bony erosions and destruction
of the humeral head, joint space narrowing, large soft tissue swelling, and extensive amorphous calcification. Early radiographic
changes consist of a high-riding humeral head due to rotator cuff
tear, mild subchondral bone sclerosis, and narrowing of the glenohumeral joint space, with little or no osteophytosis. These changes
may stabilize or show minimal cartilage erosions for several years,
followed by sudden and dramatic deterioration.
The bones on
both sides of the joint are severely damaged, with extension into
the undersurface of the acromion, the coracoid process, and the
distal clavicle. Pseudoarthrosis between the humeral head, coracoid, and acromion is common.
High-resolution ultrasound imaging can detect aggregated
BCP-related calcifications outside of joint cartilage including in the
rotator cuff of the shoulder. Ultrasound is expedient, inexpensive,
does not use irradiation, and can also assess for joint inflammation
and alternative diagnoses and guide therapeutic joint aspiration.
In BCP deposition, ultrasound has been used in most studies in
calcific shoulder syndromes. BCP deposits near the insertion of
the supraspinatus tendon can be readily assessed sonographically.
They frequently affect the critical zone, which is a poorly vascularized area of the supraspinatus tendon 0.5 to 1 cm proximal to its
insertion.
Subacromial-subdeltoid bursa widening and positive
power Doppler signal over calcifications are strongly associated
with increased clinical activity and pain.
However, ultrasound
cannot differentiate between calcification of different etiology.
Ultrasound has a role in guiding therapy in BCP crystal–associated arthropathies. A study reported that ultrasound-guided percutaneous needle aspiration and lavage in calcific tendinitis of the
shoulder provides significant improvement in shoulder motion,
pain, and disability.

the shoulder requiring surgery, pre-operative marking of calcific

### Pathogenesis

Both the avascular connective tissue of articular cartilage and the
fibrocartilaginous meniscus are particularly susceptible to calcification. Crystals of BCP including partially carbonate-substituted
hydroxyapatite (HA) may be deposited pathologically in articular
cartilage, particularly in OA. Importantly, physiologic non-inflammatory deposition of HA is essential because HA is the principal
mineral phase laid down during growth in cartilage and bone.
Inflammatory conditions may also result from deposition of HA, as
well as the closely related BCP crystals, octacalcium phosphate, and
tricalcium phosphate or “whitlockite” in periarticular structures such
as the rotator cuff (calcific tendinitis) and subacromial bursa of the
shoulder.
The precise mechanisms through which BCP crystal-induced
inflammation occurs are still being investigated. In mice, intraarticular injection of BCP crystals results in synovial inflammation,
chondrocyte apoptosis and proteoglycan loss, and upregulation
of inflammatory cytokines and cartilage-degrading enzymes.
OA-associated BCP crystals are considered damage-associated
molecular patterns (DAMPs) and can exert an effect on articular cells including fibroblasts, macrophages, and chondrocytes.
In vitro, BCP crystals promote mitogenesis in osteoarthritic synovial fibroblasts and upregulate matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)
expression in both porcine articular chondrocytes
and human
fibroblasts.
These crystals have been shown to promote prostaglandin E
, IL-1
β
, and cyclooxygenase production by human
fibroblasts,
TNF and IL-1 production by macrophages,
and IL-6 production by chondrocytes.
The direct binding of BCP crystals to the cell membrane of
macrophages activates the membrane-proximal tyrosine-protein
kinase SYK and downstream phosphoinositide 3-kinase, resulting
in the upregulation of proinflammatory cytokines, MMPs, and
DAMPs such as protein S100A8.
Furthermore, the release
of S100A8 can function in a feedback manner and bind to Tolllike receptor 4 and/or advanced glycosylation end product–specific receptor expressed on the cell surface to directly upregulate
inflammatory mediators.
Although not a DAMP, IL-6 is a potential contributor to BCP
crystal-induced inflammation and is an essential mediator of
hypoxia-inducible factor 2
α
(HIF
α
)–induced cartilage destruction in mice.
The catabolic effects of HIF2
α

been attributed to IL-6-induced MMP3 and MMP13 expression by chondrocytes.
Furthermore, the addition of recombinant IL-6 to murine chondrocyte cultures resulted in increased
calcium-containing crystal formation and the expression of genes
encoding proteins involved in the calcification process including
ANKH, the sodium/phosphate cotransporter pituitary-specific
positive transcription factor and the calcium channel annexin
A5.

regulator of biomineralization and a potent inhibitor of vascular calcification and restrains BCP crystal deposition.
