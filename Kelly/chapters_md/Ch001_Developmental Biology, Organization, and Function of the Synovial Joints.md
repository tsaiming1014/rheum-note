# Developmental Biology, Organization, and Function of the Synovial Joints

CARLA R. SCANZELLO AND MAURIZIO PACIFICI

KEY POINTS

> Synovial joints in the limbs develop from a specialized cohort of

> mesenchymal progenitor cells collectively called the interzone.

> Interzone cells diversify over developmental time and produce the

> major tissue components of the joints, including articular cartilage,

> intrajoint ligaments, and synovium.

> Neonatal articular cartilage is primitive and undergoes a complex

> maturation process postnatally to produce the definitive, zonal,

> matrix-rich, mechanically competent tissue needed for lifelong joint

> function and endurance.

> In the adult, the synovium provides important functions to maintain

> the health of the joint and articular cartilage. These include the

> production of synovial fluid components that contribute to the unique

> low-friction environment and maintain the integrity of the articular

> cartilage, the trafficking of nutrients and metabolites to and from the

> joint space, and the prevention of pathologic inflammation to limit

> joint pathology.

> In many rheumatic and musculoskeletal diseases, synovial pathology

> develops, which can compromise normal synovial functions and

> contribute directly to progressive damage of other articular tissues.

> The subchondral bone continuously remodels throughout adult life in

> response to changing loads to allow for optimal load distribution

> in the joint. This remodeling is driven by the coordinate action of

> bone-resorbing osteoclasts and bone-forming osteoblasts and

> orchestrated by the activity of osteocytes.

## Classification of Joints

The joints are essential for skeletal motion, function, and quality of life. They display a broad spectrum of shapes, sizes, tissue
composition, and overall organization, with each combination
uniquely fitted to anatomic location and to the specific type and
range of motion and mechanical function needed for the performance of adjacent skeletal elements.
In consideration of such
structural and functional diversity, several classification systems
have been developed over the years to describe, distinguish, and
characterize the joints. Most often, joints are classified based on
their main histologic component, namely fibrous, cartilaginous,
or synovial, as well as degree and type of movement, with these
two main classification systems correlating with and instructing
each other (
Table 1.1
).
Fibrous joints are characterized by a dense connecting tissue
bridging and joining the adjacent bone structures. These joints
permit minimal if any movement, have no joint cavity, and are also
known as synarthroses (
Table 1.1
). One typical example of fibrous
joints are the sutures of the growing skull that are minimally flexible at birth, permit and sustain the enlargement of cranial vault
to accommodate the growing brain, and eventually ossify by the
end of skull growth. Another example is the gomphoses in which
the periodontal fibrous tissue connects the tooth to its underlying
bone socket. Syndesmoses are constituted by ligament-like connective tissue that bridges two neighboring bones such as the ulna
and radius, creating a slightly moveable joint structure in between.
Cartilaginous joints bridge bones through hyaline cartilage
or fibrocartilage, eliciting some degree of flexibility and motion,
known also as amphiarthroses (
Table 1.1
). Examples of these
joints include the joint between the first rib and sternum and the
epiphyseal growth plate and are referred to as primary cartilaginous joints or synchondroses. Additional examples are the pubic
symphysis and intervertebral disks that have a fibrocartilaginous
character and are also called secondary cartilaginous joints.
Synovial joints are characterized by a synovial cavity delimited
by synovial lining and filled with a lubricating synovial fluid composed of water, plasma filtrate, and joint lubricants including hyaluronic acid, the glycoprotein lubricin, and phospholipids (
Fig.
1.1
).
The joints are fully enclosed and separated from the body
environment by a fibrous tissue-rich capsule. The opposing articulating ends of the flanking bones are covered by articular cartilage
that is a resilient, zonal, and long-lasting tissue and provides critical mechanical and biologic function. These joints are characterized by nearly frictionless motion and a broad spectrum of range
and degree of motion, represent the major functional joints in the
skeleton, and are also known as diarthroses (
Table 1.1
). Synovial
joints are classified by the number of axes of motion they allow,
specifically uniaxial (i.e., humeroulnar), biaxial (wrist), and multiaxial (shoulder). They are also distinguished based on the type and
architecture of the anatomic connection they mediate, including

## Developmental Biology of Limb Diarthrodial Joints and Skeletal Elements

### Onset of Limb Skeletogenesis

The developing limb in mammalian and avian embryos has
for over a century represented a common and experimentally
approachable system to probe and decipher the basic cellular and
morphogenetic mechanisms by which onset and progression of
skeletal development are brought about.
Original chick-quail tissue transplantation studies and more recent genetic cell lineage
tracing mouse studies have established that the limb skeleton and
neighboring connective tissues including the tissues of the synovial joint derive from early progenitor cells initially located in the
lateral mesoderm,
migrating into the incipient limb bud along
with pioneer nerves and blood vessels.
The limb skeletal muscles
instead derive from progenitors originating in the somites.
As
the limb bud elongates along its main proximal-distal axis, the
mesodermal cells begin to aggregate with each other by production
of adhesion proteins including N-Cadherin and N-Cam (neural
cell adhesion molecule), giving rise to dense, blood vessel–free,
mesenchymal condensations that broadly pre-figure the future
limb skeleton (
Fig. 1.2A
).
Hox
genes have been demonstrated
to be essential in providing the mesenchymal condensations with
skeletal element identity instructions, enabling them to eventually
generate stylopod skeletal elements (humerus or femur), zeugopod elements (radius/ulna or tibia/fibula), and autopod elements
(carpals/tarsals and digits).
Hox10
,
Hox11
, and
Hox13
paralogous genes are respectively expressed in the stylopod, zeugopod,
or autopod regions of the early limbs, in turn dictating the future
development of region-specific skeletal elements. For example,
transgenic ablation of all
Hox11
genes (
Hoxa11, Hoxc11
and
Hoxd11

tibia/fibula in the zeugopod, without affecting the development of
elements in the stylopod and autopod regions.

of

acquiring the ability to transition from a mesenchymal character
to a differentiated cartilage phenotype (

Recent cell lineage tracing studies have provided further insights
into the biology and roles of the interzone.
The studies made
use of
Gfd5-CreER

R26tdTomato

Gdf5+

The
data also revealed that additional cells are then recruited into the
interzone from its immediate vicinity,
expanding the interzone
laterally and in turn accommodating the growing epiphyseal ends.
Such successive cohorts of interzone cells were found to make differential contributions to joint development based on their history and positional cues, with the initial central cohorts mainly
contributing to articular cartilage and ligaments and later cohorts
contributing more to synovial lining and capsule. These important
data and insights bear directly into a critical, lingering question in
this field and specifically whether the
Gdf5+
interzone cells constitute a uniform progenitor cell population able to diversify and
produce different tissues over time, whether they are composed
from the start of different subsets of progenitors already committed to distinct developmental programs, and what in either scenario regulates their contributions to the development of different
joint tissues. At present, this very challenging question is being
approached by single-cell RNAseq, a current and very powerful
analytical tool by which progenitor cell populations can be interrogated for phenotypic composition, developmental trajectories,
and fate mechanisms.
In a first-of-a-kind scRNAseq study in joint development, one
group analyzed
Gdf5+
cells isolated from the presumptive knee
region of embryonic day 12.5 (E12.5), E13.5, E14.5, and E15.5
mouse embryo limbs, with E12.5 corresponding to onset of interzone formation and E15.5 corresponding to onset of joint cavitation (see below).
The resulting single-cell transcriptional profiles
generated three large cell clusters, one of which mostly represented
the E12.5 cells (termed SC1) and the other two represented E13.5
to E15.5 cells (SC2 and SC3). Gene and developmental trajectory
analyses indicated that the SC1
Gdf5+
cells were already subdivided into fibrogenic versus interzone/chondrogenic lineages at
E12.5, and this trend was amplified over the next 3 days with
developmental diversification and commitment toward other
joint tissue phenotypes, including synovial lining and ligaments.
A second scRNAseq study focused on
Lgr5+
interzone progenitors and found that they constituted a centrally located subset within the
Gdf5+
interzone-wide population in E13.5 mouse
embryo knees, were distinguishable based on markers such as
Cilp
and
Col22a1
, and mainly contributed to the formation of articular cartilage and cruciate ligaments over time.
Taken together,
interzone cells diversify early and contribute differentially to the
genesis of joint tissues.

### Cavitation

The synovial cavity and its fluid are essential for frictionless motion
and function of the limb joints (
Fig. 1.1
) and thus there has been
continuous interest in understanding how the developing joints
undergo cavitation (
Fig. 1.3D
). Because the cavity physically separates the opposing epiphyseal ends of the adjacent long bone anlagen, it was originally proposed that interzone cells located along
the prospective joint cavity demarcation line would undergo cell
death, thus breaking the tissue continuity and facilitating separation.
These data were not confirmed,
and it is agreed now
that cell death is not involved in cavitation.
Because the opposing ends do need to separate and the cavity needs to enlarge and
create a physical distance between the opposing ends (
Fig. 1.3D
),
the process would likely be active and complex.
Tellingly, production of hyaluronic acid (HA) and its cell surface receptor Cd44 was found to accompany cavitation.
HA
has anti-adhesive properties and can attract and imbibe water and
thus has swelling capacity.
These mechanisms are thought to play
an important role in loosening interzone cell–cell contacts along
the prospective cavitation line while at the same time expanding
the water volume of the incipient cavity. This is in line with the
finding that conditional ablation of HA synthase
Has2
, the main
enzyme responsible for HA production, delays cavitation.
Soon
after, the cavitating interzone cells initiate the production of the
additional synovial fluid component lubricin, which is encoded
by
Prg4
and has important anti-adhesive and cytokine-like activities.
It appears then that cavitation is a stepwise process in
which the initial separation of cells along the prescribed cavity
line is followed by distancing of the opposing epiphyseal ends by
water/HA/lubricin buildup and by establishment of an incipient
synovial fluid eventually including also phospholipids (
Fig. 1.3E
).
Studies have shown that muscle-driven skeletal movement is also
important for cavitation, and mouse embryos receiving neuromuscular paralyzing drugs or lacking muscles exhibit a significant
delay in cavitation.
Skeletal flexion and elongation may work
in concert with skeletal motion to promote cavitation.
These
developmental studies on cavitation relate well to the clinical fact
that joints in adults are negatively affected by physical inactivity

### Articular Cartilage Genesis, Morphogenesis, and Endurance

As indicated above, Gdf5+ progenitors are responsible for the generation of articular cartilage. However, the tissue emerging from
this important embryonic process is a far cry from the functional
tissue present in an adult joint.
In mouse limbs, neonatal knee
articular cartilage is thin, highly cellular, and extra-cellular matrix
poor (
Fig. 1.4A
).
The tissue rapidly grows in thickness over the
following 2 to 3 weeks and accumulates a matrix rich in typical hyaline cartilage components including collagens II and VI,
aggrecan, and HA. The chondrocytes slightly enlarge in average
cell volume and remain dispersed throughout much of the tissue as also seen in human juvenile knee cartilage (
Fig. 1.4B
).
A mature tissue eventually forms around 8 weeks of age in mice
(corresponding to about 18 to 20 years in humans) and exhibits a
stereotypic functional organization consisting of (
Fig. 1.4C
): (1)
a top superficial zone abutting the synovial space that is made of
flat and elongated cells producing synovial fluid lubricants; (2)
a thick transition/deep zone that contains large chondrocytes in
columns orthogonal to the tissue surface (
Figs. 1.4C and 1.5
) and
embedded in an abundant and anisotropic matrix with collagen II
fibrils and aggrecan-HA-link protein complexes providing tensile
strength and elasticity, respectively; and (3) a bottom mineralized
zone with large chondrocytes that is closely linked to subchondral
bone, providing physical continuity and stabilization. The tidemark separates the unmineralized zones from the mineralized zone
(
Figs. 1.4C and 1.5B
,
arrowheads
), subdividing articular cartilage
into its upper mechanically resilient portion from its stiffer bottom
zone anchoring it to bone. The above intricate zonal organization
of mature articular cartilage is widely thought to be essential for
the tissue to withstand routine compression and shear mechanical cycles from daily activities while also providing a frictionless
lubricating function.
These essential organizational features are
regrettably deranged after acute joint injuries, natural aging, or
chronic pathologies such as osteoarthritis (OA).

### Growth Plates and Ossification Centers

The growth plates are the key engines of skeletal development,
elongation, and morphogenesis during prenatal and early postnatal life and are composed of a top resting zone followed by

maturation zones of proliferating prehypertrophic and hypertrophic chondrocytes arranged in columns (
Fig. 1.5C
).
The hypertrophic chondrocytes at the base of the growth plate mineralize
their matrix and permit the transition from hypertrophic cartilage
to new bone deposition in the process of endochondral ossification by which the bulk of the skeleton is built (except for the calvaria, mandible, and portions of the clavicle). Previous and recent
studies have indicated that some of the mineralized hypertrophic
chondrocytes persist and transdifferentiate into bone cells,
with the rest being eliminated by cell death.
Skeletal elements
such as tibia or femur contain one growth plate in each of their
metaphyseal regions, with each growth plate flanked by a primary
ossification center in the diaphysis and a secondary ossification
center in the epiphysis (
Fig. 1.1
). The growth plates undergo
involution and close at the end of puberty when skeletal growth
is complete. But when do growth plates first appear during fetal
development and how do they come about?
While the joints are forming from interzone progenitors in the
early embryo limbs, the underlying immature, small, and largely
homogenous chondrocytes constituting the entire long bone anlagen initiate a process of maturation and phenotypic diversification. The first sign of this process is appreciable in the emerging
diaphyseal region of the anlagen in which the local chondrocytes
change in shape and begin to express the signaling protein Indian
hedgehog (Ihh).
Ihh induces expression of parathyroid hormone–related protein (PthrP) in most epiphyseal cells located
in proximity to the developing joints. This establishes a critical
and fundamental Ihh-PthrP signaling loop that proceeds to regulate chondrocyte proliferation, maturation rates, and phenotypic
expression, thus creating a primitive growth plate extending from
epiphysis to diaphysis.
The diaphyseal chondrocytes are in turn
the first to progress to hypertrophy and transition to endochondral
ossification, processes that are promoted by multiple mechanisms
including such transcription factors as Runx2, Mef2c, FoxA2, and
FoxA3
and signaling pathways elicited by Wnt/
β
-catenin and
epidermal growth factor receptor.
Blood vessels and marrow progenitors from surrounding tissues invade the diaphyseal
hypertrophic cartilage through the action by matrix metalloproteases and angiogenic cues such as vascular endothelium growth
factors,
creating the primary ossification center and eliciting
the first deposition of endochondral bone (
Fig. 1.2D
). At these
early stages of skeletogenesis, Ihh has an additional function and
induces formation of a so-called bone collar, surrounding the
diaphyseal region and deriving from the direct differentiation
of osteoprogenitors into bone by intramembranous ossification.
Overall growth plate activity and rates of skeletal
element elongation are fine-tuned by negative regulators.
These
include (1) histone deacetylase 4 (Hdac4), which is expressed in
prehypertrophic chondrocytes and represses Runx2 and Mef2
activity,
and (2) fibroblast growth factor receptor 3 (Fgfr3),
which is expressed in upper growth plate zones, acts through the
Stat1 and Mapk signaling pathways, and represses chondrocyte
proliferation.
Indeed, gain-of-function
FGFR3
mutations in
patients dramatically reduce chondrocyte proliferation and cause
achondroplasia, the most severe form of human dwarfism.
The epiphyseal region of the long bone anlagen remains cartilaginous and rich in proliferative chondrocytes throughout
embryogenesis and early postnatal life. In mice, it is around 1
week of age that a metaphyseal border begins to be established
by the creation of a line of cells expressing
PthrP
, as a striking
study has revealed recently.
In this way, the epiphyseal region
further develops into a secondary ossification center (
Figs. 1.1
and 1.3E
) and the underlying and newly established metaphyseal
region comes to contain a more definitive growth plate flanked
by diaphyseal bone and the secondary ossification center itself
(eventually developing into endochondral bone). The
PthrP
-
expressing cells have now been demonstrated to exhibit traits and
behave like stem/progenitor cells,
and their functioning is also
regulated by mechanisms including FoxA2 and Wnt inhibitory
cues.
These stemlike cells are found to divide symmetrically
to maintain themselves or asymmetrically, with one cell remaining
stem in character while the other commits to chondrocyte differentiation and produces all chondrocytes in each growth plate
column.
This process ensures the maintenance and functioning
of the growth plate and continuously replenishes it with new cells
that replace those undergoing hypertrophy and replacement by
endochondral bone, allowing for growth of the long bones. The
growth plates undergo involution during the end of puberty and
disappear, a process regulated by complex interplays and involving
estrogens and other hormones.

## Organization and Physiology of the Mature Joint

In the adult, the structural, cellular, and biochemical components
of diarthrodial joints are organized into an organ structure that
is uniquely equipped to support movement and bear mechanical
loads throughout our lifetime. Mature diarthrodial joints are complex and dynamic structures, influenced by their unique anatomic
environments and mechanical demands. The shoulder joint, which
demands an enormous range of motion, is stabilized primarily by
muscles, whereas the hip, which requires motion and antigravity
stability, has an intrinsically stable ball-and-socket configuration.
The components of the “typical” synovial joint are the synovium,
muscles, tendons, ligaments, bursae, articular cartilage, and subchondral bone. Additionally, fibrocartilaginous structures such as
the menisci of the knee and the labrae of the hip and shoulder
contribute to joint function, stability, and health in important
ways. The anatomy and physiology of muscles are described in
detail in
Chapter 5
. Here we discuss the other joint tissues and
structures and how they contribute to both joint homeostasis and
disease, starting with the synovium.

### Synovium

The synovium is the lining of the joint cavity and is a thin connective tissue that covers the fibrous joint capsule and extends to the
bone–cartilage interface without encroaching on articular cartilage
(
Chapter 2

lining region (synovial intima) and the sublining (subintima) (

the cartilage surfaces. The synovial lining is loosely attached to
the subintima, which contains the blood vessels, lymphatics, and
nerves and represents the principal neurovascular supply for the
joint. Each compartment has distinct functions and collectively
is often referred to as the synovial
membrane

sublining compartments are not separated by a distinct basement
membrane. Instead, they are separated by more loosely organized
matrix, although this matrix contains many of the molecular components normally found in basement membranes (i.e., perlecan,

are compromised. For example, in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) the
synovial lining of diarthrodial joints is the site of the initial inflammatory process
(see

Synovial Innervation
Dissection studies have shown that each joint has a dual nerve supply consisting of specific articular nerves that penetrate the capsule
as independent branches of adjacent peripheral nerves and articular branches arising from related muscle nerves.
The definition
of joint position and the detection of joint motion are monitored
separately and by a combination of multiple inputs from different
receptors in varied systems. Nerve endings in muscle and skin and
in the joint capsule mediate sensation of joint position and movement. Normal joints have afferent (sensory) and efferent (motor)
innervations consisting of both unmyelinated and sensory thick
myelinated A fibers in ligaments, fibrous capsule, menisci, and
adjacent periosteum, where they are thought to function primarily as sensors for pressure and movements. Sensory A and C fibers
terminate as free nerve endings in the fibrous capsule, synovium,
adipose tissue, ligaments, menisci, and the adjacent periosteum,
where they are thought to act as nociceptors and contribute to the
regulation of synovial microvascular function.
In normal synovium, a dense network of fine unmyelinated
nerve fibers follows the courses of blood vessels and extends into
the synovial lining layers. These nerve fibers are largely unmyelinated and are slow-conducting fibers; they may transmit diffuse, burning, or aching pain sensation. Sympathetic nerve fibers
surround blood vessels, particularly in the deeper regions of
synovium, and contain and release classic neurotransmitters, such
as norepinephrine, and neuropeptides that are markers of sensory nerves including substance P, calcitonin gene-related peptide
(CGRP), neuropeptide Y, and vasoactive intestinal peptide.
Substance P and CGRP, in particular, have been implicated in
modulating inflammation and the pain pathway in OA.
In
addition, substance P is released from peripheral nerve terminals
into the joint, and specific, G protein–coupled receptors for substance P are localized to microvascular endothelium in normal
synovium. Abnormalities in neuropeptide release in arthritis may
contribute to changes in vascular permeability and the failure of
synovial inflammation to resolve.
The expression of substance
P and CGRP are upregulated by nerve growth factor (NGF),
which belongs to a family of neurotrophins that regulate neuronal
growth during embryonic development. In addition to promoting nerve growth and mediating pain perception, NGF can act
together with VEGF to promote blood vessel formation. Angiogenesis and nerve growth thus are linked by common pathways
involving NGF, VEGF, and neuropeptides such as CGRP, neuropeptide Y, and semaphorins.
Afferent nerve fibers from the joint play an important role in the
reflex inhibition of muscle contraction. Trophic factors generated

by motor neurons, such as the neuropeptide CGRP, are important
in maintaining muscle bulk and a functional neuromuscular junction. Decreases in motor neuron trophic support during articular
inflammation probably contribute to muscle wasting in arthritic
conditions. Inflammation and excessive local neuropeptide release
may result in the loss of nerve fibers, and synovial tissue proliferation without concomitant growth of new nerve fibers may lead to
an apparent partial denervation of synovium in arthritis.
However, there is also evidence from humans and pre-clinical arthritis
models of sprouting of sensory nerves in synovium and ingrowth
of neurovascular channels at the osteochondral junction.
Overall, aberrant innervation of synovium and other joint structures during arthritis development may contribute to changes in
synovial joint homeostasis, motor neuron trophic support, and
pain.
Mechanisms of joint pain have been reviewed in detail.
In a noninflamed joint, most sensory nerve fibers do not respond
to movement within the normal range; these fibers are referred
to as
silent nociceptors.
In an inflamed joint, however, these nerve
fibers become sensitized by mediators such as bradykinin, neurokinin 1, NGF, and prostaglandins, and the resulting peripheral
sensitization leads to pain during normal joint movement, a characteristic symptom of arthritis.
Pain sensation is upregulated or downregulated further in the central nervous system, at
the level of the spinal cord and in the brain, by central sensitization and “gating” of nociceptive input.
A poor correlation often
exists between the severity of apparent joint disease damage and
perceived pain in people with chronic arthritis, and sensitization
either at the peripheral or central levels may contribute to this
discrepancy.

### Synovial Fluid

The volume and composition of synovial fluid are maintained by
the synovium and its vasculature. A normal joint contains a small
quantity of fluid (
≤
2.5 mL in the knee), sufficient to coat the
synovial and cartilage surfaces. Tendon sheath fluid and synovial
fluid are biochemically similar. Both are essential for the nutrition
and lubrication of adjacent avascular structures, including tendons and articular cartilage, and for limiting adhesion formation
and maintaining friction-free movement. Characterization and
measurement of synovial fluid constituents have proven useful for
the identification of locally generated regulatory factors, markers
of cartilage turnover, and the metabolic status of the joint, as well
as for the assessment of the effects of therapy on cartilage homeostasis. However, interpretation of such data requires an understanding of the generation and clearance of synovial fluid and its
various components.
Generation and Clearance of Synovial Fluid
Synovial fluid concentrations of a protein represent the net contributions of synovial blood flow, plasma concentration, microvascular permeability, lymphatic removal, and its production and
consumption within the joint space. Synovial fluid is a mixture of
a protein-rich ultrafiltrate of plasma and carbohydrates (i.e., hyaluronan) and glycoproteins (i.e., lubricin) synthesized by synoviocytes.
Generation of this ultrafiltrate depends on the differences
between intracapillary and intraarticular hydrostatic pressures and
between osmotic pressures of capillary plasma and synovial tissue
fluid. Fenestrations (i.e., small pores covered by a thin membrane)
in the synovial capillaries and the macromolecular sieve of collagen and HA in the synovial matrix facilitate rapid exchange of
small molecules, such as glucose and lactate, assisted in the case
of glucose by an active transport system. Proteins are present in
synovial fluid at concentrations inversely proportional to molecular size, with synovial fluid albumin concentration being about
45% of that in plasma. Concentrations of electrolytes and small
molecules are equivalent to those in plasma.
Synovial fluid is
cleared through a combination of diffusion into the vasculature
and lymphatic assisted by joint movement. In contrast to ultrafiltration, lymphatic clearance of solutes is independent of molecule
size. Low-molecular-weight metabolites and regulatory peptides
may diffuse along concentration gradients into plasma. The kinetics of delivery and removal of a protein must be determined (e.g.,
using albumin as a reference solute) to assess the significance of its
concentration in the joint.
HA synthesized by type B synovial lining cells appears in high
concentrations in synovial fluid (around 3 g/L), compared with
plasma (30
μ
g/L).
Lubricin, a glycoprotein that assists articular
lubrication, is another constituent of synovial fluid that is generated by the lining cells.
It is now believed that hyaluronan functions in fluid-film lubrication, whereas lubricin is the boundary
lubricant in synovial fluid. Because the volume of synovial fluid is
determined by the amount of hyaluronan, water retention seems
to be the major function of this large molecule. Lubricin also prevents pathologic deposition of protein onto the surface of articular
cartilage.
Despite the absence of a basement membrane, synovial fluid
does not mix freely with extra-cellular synovial tissue fluid. Hyaluronan may trap molecules within the synovial cavity by acting as
a filtration screen on the surface of the synovial lining, resisting
the movement of synovial fluid out from the joint space. Synovial fluid proteins have a rapid turnover time (around 1 hour in
normal knees), and equilibrium is not usually reached among all
parts of the joint. However, the turnover time for hyaluronan in
the normal joint (13 hours) is an order of magnitude slower than
that of small solutes and proteins, so association with hyaluronan
may result in trapping of solutes within synovial fluid. Tissue fluid
around fenestrated endothelium reflects plasma ultrafiltrate most
closely, with a low content of hyaluronate compared with synovial fluid. Locally generated or released peptides within the tissue, such as endothelin and substance P, may attain much higher
perivascular concentrations than those measured in synovial fluid.
In normal joints, intraarticular pressures are slightly subatmospheric at rest (0 to −5 mm Hg). During exercise, hydrostatic pressure in the normal joint may decrease further. Repeated abnormal
mechanical stresses can interrupt synovial perfusion during joint
movement, particularly in the presence of a synovial effusion.
Resting intraarticular pressures in rheumatoid joints are around
20 mm Hg, whereas during isometric exercise, they may increase
to greater than 100 mm Hg, well above capillary perfusion pressure and, at times, above arterial pressure.

Lubrication and Nutrition of the Articular Cartilage
Synovial fluid serves as a lubricant for articular cartilage and a
source of nutrition for the chondrocytes. Lubrication is essential for protecting cartilage and other joint structures from friction and shear stresses associated with movement under loading.
There are two basic categories of joint lubrication. In fluid-film
lubrication, cartilage surfaces are separated by an incompressible
fluid film, and hyaluronan primarily provides this function. In
boundary lubrication, specialized molecules attached to the cartilage surface permit surface-to-surface contact while decreasing the
coefficient of friction. During loading, a noncompressible fluid
film is trapped between opposing cartilage surfaces and prevents
the surfaces from touching. Irregularities in the cartilage surface
and its deformation during compression may augment this trapping of fluid. This stable film is approximately 0.1-
μ
m thick in
the normal human hip joint, but it can be much thinner in the
presence of inflammatory synovial fluid or with increased cartilage
porosity.
Lubricin (also called superficial zone protein or proteoglycan
4 [PRG4]) participates in boundary lubrication in the human
joint.
Lubricin is a glycoprotein synthesized primarily by type B
synovial cells and superficial zone chondrocytes.
It has a molecular weight of 225,000, a length of 200 nm, and a diameter of 1 to
2 nm. Dipalmitoyl phosphatidylcholine, which constitutes 45%
of the lipid in normal synovial fluid, acts together with lubricin as
a boundary lubricant. In boundary lubrication, lubricin functions
as a phospholipid carrier via a mechanism that is common to all
tissues and protects the cartilage by reducing pathologic deposition of proteins at the cartilage surface. Lubricin is enriched on the
surface of articular cartilage and, although mechanisms that maintain its surface localization for boundary lubrication are unknown,
recent work suggests that both noncovalent and covalent bonding with cartilage oligomeric matrix protein may be important.
People with loss-of-function mutations in the lubricin gene
(PRG4) have camptodactyly arthropathy–coxa vara–pericarditis
syndrome, which is associated with the development of severe premature OA.
Of interest, long-term overexpression of lubricin in
animal models of OA protects against both age-related and posttraumatic OA through inhibition of transcriptional programs that
promote cartilage catabolism and chondrocyte hypertrophy.
As observed by Hunter in 1743,
normal adult articular cartilage contains no blood vessels, which is essential to maintain
its mechanical properties. If vascularized, blood flow in cartilage
would be repeatedly occluded during weight bearing and exercise, with reactive oxygen species generated during reperfusion,
resulting in repeated damage to cartilage matrix and chondrocytes. Chondrocytes synthesize specific inhibitors of angiogenesis
that maintain articular cartilage as an avascular tissue.
As a
result, chondrocytes normally live in a hypoxic and acidotic environment, with extra-cellular fluid pH values around 7.1 to 7.2,
and use anaerobic glycolysis for energy production.
High lactate levels in normal synovial fluid, compared with paired plasma
measurements, partially reflect this anaerobic metabolism. The
two sources of nutrients for articular cartilage are the synovial
fluid and subchondral blood vessels.
The synovial fluid and, indirectly, the synovial lining through
which synovial fluid is generated, are the major sources of nutrients for articular cartilage. Nutrients may enter cartilage from
synovial fluid either by diffusion or mass transport during compression–relaxation cycles.
Molecules as large as hemoglobin
(65 kDa) can diffuse through normal articular cartilage, and the
solutes needed for cellular metabolism are much smaller. Diffusion of uncharged small solutes, such as glucose, is not impaired
in matrices containing large amounts of glycosaminoglycans, and
diffusivity of small molecules through hyaluronate is enhanced.
Intermittent compression may serve as a pump mechanism for
solute exchange in cartilage. The concept has arisen from observations that joint immobilization or dislocation leads to degenerative change, while exercise increases solute penetration into
cartilage in experimental systems. During weight bearing, fluid
escapes from the load-bearing region by flowing to other cartilage
sites. When the load is removed, cartilage reexpands and draws
back fluid, exchanging nutrients with waste materials.
In a growing child, the deeper layers of cartilage are vascularized, such that blood vessels penetrate between columns of chondrocytes in the growth plate. It is likely that nutrients diffuse from
these tiny end capillaries through the matrix to chondrocytes. Diffusion from subchondral blood vessels is not considered a major
route for the nutrition of normal adult articular cartilage because
of the barrier provided by its densely calcified lower layer. Nonetheless, partial defects may normally exist in this barrier,
and
neovascularization of the deeper layers of articular cartilage in
arthritis may contribute both to cartilage nutrition and to entry of
inflammatory cells and cytokines.

### Mature Articular Cartilage

Articular cartilage is a specialized connective tissue that covers
the weight-bearing surfaces of diarthrodial joints. The principal
functions of cartilage layers covering bone ends are to permit lowfriction, high-velocity movement between bones, to absorb the
transmitted forces associated with locomotion, and to contribute
to joint stability. Lubrication by synovial fluid components provides frictionless movement of the articulating cartilage surfaces.
Chondrocytes are the single cellular component of adult hyaline
articular cartilage and are responsible for synthesizing and maintaining the highly specialized cartilage matrix macromolecules.
The cartilage extra-cellular matrix is composed of an extensive
network of collagen fibrils, which confers tensile strength, and an
interlocking mesh of proteoglycans, which provides compressive
stiffness through the ability to absorb and extrude water. Numerous other noncollagenous proteins also contribute to the unique
properties of cartilage (
Table 1.2
). Histologically, the tissue appears
homogeneous and clearly distinguished from the calcified cartilage
and underlying subchondral bone (
Fig. 1.5
). However, significant
topographic and regional differences exist in the molecular organization and composition of the articular cartilage, as described
earlier in this chapter and in detail in
Chapter 3
. The resilience of
articular cartilage is evident in its ability to absorb and transmit
loads throughout our lifespan and maintain the articular matrix
under these loads. However, several stimuli (including mechanical
and inflammatory injury) can damage the articular matrix and
disrupt the ability of the chondrocytes to repair the damage, propagating damage in the setting of various types of arthritis.

zone of calcified cartilage. This complex biocomposite of bone

loading is evidence of the unique and intimate interaction of
these tissues, but controversy remains with regard to the relationship between them in the pathogenesis of OA. Radin and
Rose

leading to deterioration in the properties of the articular cartilage and susceptibility to mechanical disruption. In addition,
subchondral bone injury can cause cartilage degeneration.
Alternatively, changes in subchondral bone stiffness may be a
result of cartilage deterioration.

of calcified cartilage where there is evidence of vascular invasion, advancement of the calcified cartilage, and duplication of

thickness.

the subchondral bone and calcified cartilage into the deep zones

mediators between these tissues, providing an additional mechanism by which the subchondral bone and articular cartilage can
communicate. These structural alterations in the articular cartilage and periarticular bone may also lead to modification of the
contours of the adjacent articulating surfaces, further contributing to the adverse biomechanical environment.

### Tendons

Tendons are functional and anatomic bridges between muscle
and bone.
Tendons focus the force of a large mass of muscle
into a localized area on bone and, by splitting to form numerous
insertions, may distribute the force of a single muscle to different
bones. Tendons are formed of longitudinally arranged collagen
fibrils embedded in an organized, hydrated proteoglycan matrix
with blood vessels, lymphatics, and fibroblasts.
Cross-links
between adjacent collagen chains or molecules contribute to the
tensile strength of the tendon. Tendon collagen fibrillogenesis is
initiated during early development by a highly ordered process of
alignment involving the actin cytoskeleton and cadherin-11.
The main cell type comprising tendon, and responsible for laying
down and maintaining this fibrillar matrix, is the tendon fibroblast, or tenocyte, of mesenchymal origin. Recent studies point
to a resident tendon macrophage population that may cooperate
with tenocytes to support homeostatic matrix turnover required
during adult life.
Many tendons, particularly those with a large
range of motion, run through vascularized, discontinuous sheaths
of collagen lined with a layer of mesenchymal cells resembling
synovium (the tenosynovium). Gliding of tendons through their
sheaths is enhanced by HA and lubricin produced by the lining
cells. Tendon movement is essential for the embryogenesis and
maintenance of tendons and their sheaths.
Degenerative
changes appear in tendons with age and injury, and fibrous adhesions form between tendons and sheaths when inflammation or
surgical incision is followed by long periods of immobilization.
At the myotendinous junction, recesses between muscle cell
processes are filled with collagen fibrils, which blend into the tendon. At its other end, collagen fibers of the tendon typically blend
into fibrocartilage, mineralize, and merge into bone through a
fibrocartilaginous transition zone termed the
enthesis
, or insertion site.
In health, this graded, transitional structure allows
for load transfer and minimization of stress at these attachments
during movement. However, these attachment sites are susceptible
to injury and degeneration and inflammatory disease.
Failure
of the muscle-tendon apparatus is rare; when it does occur, it is
the result of enormous, quickly generated forces across a joint and
usually occurs at the enthesis where the tendon inserts into bone.
Factors that may predispose to tendon failure are aging processes
including loss of extra-cellular water and the increase in intermolecular cross-links of collagen; tendon ischemia; iatrogenic factors,
including injection of glucocorticoids; and deposition of calcium
hydroxyapatite crystals within the collagen bundles.
Alterations in collagen fibril composition and structure are associated
with tendon degeneration during aging and may predispose to
enthesopathy and OA. In addition, enthesial resident innate
lymphoid cells, T lymphocytes, and the inflammatory cytokines
IL-12/23, IL-17A, and TNF may drive enthesial inflammation
in spondyloarthritis.
The clinical success of therapies directed
against these cytokines to treat axial spondyloarthritis are discussed in more detail in
Chapters 63 and 76
.
Tendon fibroblasts synthesize and secrete collagens, proteoglycans, and other matrix components, such as fibronectin and
tenascin C, and MMPs and their inhibitors, which can contribute
to the breakdown and repair of tendon components.
Collagen
fibrils in tendon are composed primarily of type I collagen with
some type III collagen, but there are regional differences in the
distribution of other matrix components. The compressed region
contains the small proteoglycans—biglycan, decorin, fibromodulin, and lumican—and the large proteoglycan versican. The major
components in the tensile region of the tendon are decorin, microfibrillar type VI collagen, fibromodulin, and the proline and arginine-rich end leucine-rich repeat protein. The presence of COMP,
aggrecan, biglycan, and collagen types II, IX, and XI is indicative
of fibrocartilage. The collagen fiber orientation at the tendon-tobone enthesis is important for maintaining microarchitecture by
reducing the stress concentrations and shielding the outward splay
of the insertion from the highest stresses.
Understanding the structure and development of the enthesis has implications for tendon repair because motion between a
tendon graft and bone tunnel (i.e., in anterior cruciate ligament
repair) may impair early graft incorporation and lead to tunnel
widening secondary to bone resorption.
Entheses develop
during the late fetal period but continue to mature during the
early postnatal period. Clonal expansion of GDF5 progenitors
promote the linear growth of the enthesis.
Ihh signaling is
important in formation of the fibrocartilaginous zone, as Hedgehog responsive cells within this region mature from unmineralized to mineralized fibrochondrocytes.
The Ihh signaling
pathway is reactivated during tendon-to-bone healing,
but this
appears to be insufficient on its own to recapitulate the structure
and strength of an enthesis attachment in pre-clinical models of
tendon repair.
One potential reason may be the loss of Ihhresponsive Gli1+ progenitor cells in the adult.
However, how
Ihh signaling intersects with other mediators and developmental pathways (such as those involving Sox9, scleraxis, Mohawk,
members of the TGFβ
/BMP superfamily, and Wnt/
β
-catenin) in
enthesis development needs further clarification to inform strategies for tendon repair in the future.

### Ligaments

Ligaments, which provide a stabilizing bridge between bones,
permit a limited range of movement.
Ligaments often are recognized only as hypertrophied components of the fibrous joint
capsule and are structurally similar to tendons.
Although the
fibers are oriented parallel to the longitudinal axis of both tissues, the collagen fibrils in ligaments are nonparallel and arranged
in fibers that are oriented roughly along the long axis in a wavy,
undulating pattern, or “crimp,” which can straighten in response
to load. Some ligaments have a higher ratio of elastin to collagen
(1:4) than do tendons (1:50), which permits a greater degree of
stretch. Ligaments also have larger amounts of reducible crosslinks, more type III collagen, slightly less total collagen, and more
glycosaminoglycans compared with tendons. The cells in ligaments seem to be more metabolically active than the tenocytes in
tendons and have more plump cellular nuclei and higher DNA
content.
During postnatal growth, the development of ligament attachment zones involves changes in the ratios and distribution of types
I, III, and V collagen and in the synthesis of type II collagen and
proteoglycans by fibrochondrocytes that develop from ligament
cells at the attachment zone. Attachment zones are believed to
permit gradual transmission of the tensile force between ligament
and bone.
Ligaments play a major role in the passive stabilization of
joints, aided by the joint capsule and, when present, menisci. In
the knee, the collateral and cruciate ligaments provide stability
when there is little or no load on the joint. As compressive load
increases, there is an increasing contribution to stability from
the joint surfaces themselves and the surrounding musculature.
Injured ligaments generally heal well, and structural integrity is

restored by contracture of the healing ligament so it can act again
as a stabilizer of the joint.

### Bursae

The many bursae in the human body facilitate gliding of one tissue over another, much as a tendon sheath facilitates movement
of its tendon. Bursae are closed sacs usually located where muscles
or tendons move over bony joints or prominences, lined sparsely
with mesenchymal cells that are similar to synovial cells, but generally less well vascularized than synovium. Most bursae differentiate concurrently with synovial joints during embryogenesis.
Throughout life, trauma or inflammation may lead to clinical
bursitis, the development of new bursae, hypertrophy of previously existing ones, or communication between deep bursae and
joints. In patients with RA, communications may exist between
the subacromial bursae and the glenohumeral joint, between the
gastrocnemius or semimembranosus bursae and the knee joint,
and between the iliopsoas bursa and the hip joint. It is unusual,
however, for subcutaneous bursae, such as the prepatellar bursa
or olecranon bursa, to develop communication with the underlying joint.

### Menisci

The meniscus, a fibrocartilaginous, wedge-shaped structure, is best
developed in the knee but menisci are also found in the acromioclavicular and sternoclavicular joints, the ulnocarpal joint, and the
temporomandibular joint. Historically, menisci were thought to
have little function and a quiescent metabolism with no capability of repair, although early observations indicated that surgical
removal of menisci from the knee could lead to premature OA.
The meniscus is now considered to be an integral component of
the knee joint that has important functions in joint stability, load
distribution across the articular surface, shock absorption, and
lubrication.
The microanatomy of the knee meniscus is complex and age
dependent.
The characteristic shapes of the lateral and the
medial menisci are achieved early in prenatal development. At that
time, the menisci are cellular and highly vascularized; with maturation, vascularity decreases progressively from the central margin
to the peripheral margin. After skeletal maturity, the peripheral
10 to 30% of the meniscus remains highly vascularized by a circumferential capillary plexus and is well innervated. Tears in this
vascularized peripheral zone may undergo repair and remodeling.
The central portion of the mature meniscus is an avascular fibrocartilage, however, without nerves or lymphatics, consisting of
cells surrounded by an abundant extra-cellular matrix of collagens,
chondroitin sulfate, dermatan sulfate, and hyaluronic acid. Tears
in this central zone heal poorly, if at all.
Collagen constitutes 60 to 70% of the dry weight of the meniscus and is mostly type I collagen, with lesser amounts of types III,
V, and VI. A small quantity of cartilage-specific type II collagen
is localized to the inner, avascular portion of the meniscus. Collagen fibers in the periphery are mostly circumferentially oriented,
with radial fibers extending toward the central portion. Elastin
content is around 0.6%, and proteoglycan content is around 2 to
3% dry weight. Aggrecan and decorin are the major proteoglycans
in the adult meniscus. Decorin is the predominant proteoglycan
synthesized in the meniscus from young people, whereas the relative proportion of aggrecan synthesis increases with age. Although
the capacity of the meniscus to synthesize sulfated proteoglycans
decreases after the teenage years, the age-related increases in
expression of decorin and aggrecan messenger RNA suggest that
the resident cells are able to respond quickly to alterations in the
biomechanical environment.
The meniscus was defined originally as a fibrocartilaginous tissue, based on the rounded or oval shape of most of the cells and
the fibrous microscopic appearance of the extra-cellular matrix.
Based on molecular and spatial criteria, three distinct populations
of cells are recognized in the meniscus of the knee joint
:
1.	 The fibrochondrocyte is the most abundant cell in the
middle and inner meniscus, synthesizing primarily type I
collagen and relatively small amounts of type II and III collagens. It is round or oval in shape and has a pericellular
filamentous matrix containing type VI collagen.
2.	 The fibroblast-like cells lack a pericellular matrix and are
located in the outer portion of the meniscus. They are distinguished by long, thin, branching cytoplasmic projections
that stain for vimentin. They make contact with other cells
in different regions via connexin 43–containing gap junctions. The presence of two centrosomes, one associated
with a primary cilium, suggests a sensory rather than motile
function that could enable the cells to respond to circumferential tensile loads rather than compressive loads.
3.	 The superficial zone cells have a characteristic fusiform shape
with no cytoplasmic projections. The occasional staining
of these cells in the uninjured meniscus with
α
-actin and
their migration into surrounding wound sites suggest that
they are specialized progenitor cells that may participate in
a remodeling response in the meniscus and surrounding tissues.
Cell lineage tracing and gene profiling studies in mouse
embryos have provided insight into the complexity of the meniscus and how it is formed.
Researchers have considerable
interest in using this information to develop new strategies for
meniscal repair and regeneration.

## Conclusion

Normal human synovial joints are complex structures that comprise interacting connective tissue elements and permit constrained
and low-friction movement of adjacent bones. The development
of synovial joints in the embryo is a highly ordered process involving complex cell–cell and cell–matrix interactions that lead to the
formation of the cartilage anlage, interzone, and joint cavitation.
Understanding of the cellular interactions and molecular factors
involved in cartilage morphogenesis and limb development has
provided clues to understanding the functions of the synovium,
articular cartilage, and associated structures in the mature joint.
The synovial joint is uniquely adapted to respond to environmental and mechanical demands. The synovial lining is composed
of two to three cell layers, with no basement membrane separating the lining cells from the underlying connective tissue, which
is innervated and vascularized. The synovium regulates molecular
trafficking between the joint space and the vascular system, and
it produces synovial fluid, which provides nutrition and lubrication to the avascular articular cartilage. Normal articular cartilage contains a single cell type, the articular chondrocyte, which
is responsible for maintaining the integrity of the extra-cellular
cartilage matrix. This matrix consists of a complex network of collagens, proteoglycans, and other noncollagenous proteins, which
provide tensile strength and compressive resistance. Proper distribution and relative composition of these proteins are required

for the maintenance and function of the articular cartilage during
joint loading and motion. The subchondral bone and the overlying articular and calcified cartilage form a unique biocomposite
that provides an optimal system for distributing loads during joint
motion. The other tissue of the joint (tendons, ligaments, bursae)
function to maintain stability and assist in joint motion. Maintenance of the unique composition and organization of each joint
tissue is crucial for normal joint function, which is compromised
in response to inflammation, biomechanical injury, and aging.
Knowledge of the normal structure–function relationships within
joint tissues is essential for understanding the pathogenesis and
consequences of joint diseases.
