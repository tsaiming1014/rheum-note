## Introduction

The term
mononuclear phagocytes
is used to describe monocytes,
macrophages, and dendritic cells (DCs). While a useful all-encompassing term, it does not allow important differences between and
within these cell populations to be discussed. Macrophages were
first described by Metchnikoff in the early 1900s,
and DCs were
first described by Steinman in 1973.
As the professional phagocytes
of the body, macrophages are typically divided into subsets based on
their origins being either tissue resident and largely derived during embryogenesis or recruited macrophages and derived in adulthood from bone marrow (BM) monocytes. However, the functional
relevance of this division is unclear. Moreover, multiple subsets of
recruited macrophages, likely with distinct functions, have been
described particularly in the context of rheumatoid arthritis (RA),
demonstrating the need for a more precise nomenclature. DCs are
further divided into plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) and conventional
DCs (cDCs), although whether the former should be considered
cDCs are the professional antiDCs is currently a topic of debate.

antigens in the periphery and then migrating to lymph nodes where
they can induce appropriate responses from naive T cells. They
can be further split into two subpopulations, namely cDC1s and
cDC2s, which are discriminated on the basis of their expression of
the receptors XCR1 and SIRP

The presence of an additional subset of DCs
in inflammatory settings has also recently been proposed; however,
the precise definition of these cells across tissues and inflammatory
settings remains unclear.
In this chapter, we aim to discuss what is
currently known regarding the biology of these different cell populations, including their definitions and functional heterogeneity in
relation to the pathogenesis of rheumatic and musculoskeletal diseases using RA as an exemplar given that the evidence base is far
advanced in this disease beyond others in the rheumatic and musculoskeletal disease (RMD) spectrum. Moreover, we will discuss the
remaining open questions regarding these cells.

## Macrophage Origin: Nature Versus Nurture

Macrophage origin has been the subject of significant scrutiny in
recent years. Originally proposed by Van Furth et al. to originate
from monocyte progenitors in the BM,
in recent years considerable research efforts have demonstrated that most tissue-resident
macrophage (TRM) populations are generated during embryogenesis and subsequently self-maintain throughout life. The precise nature of the embryonic macrophage progenitors remains
debated.
Placental erythro-myeloid progenitors (EMPs) have
been proposed to be the first source of macrophages, giving rise
to placental macrophages, termed
Hofbauer cells
.
Upon development of the yolk-sac, EMPs within the yolk-sac give rise to yolksac macrophages (
Fig. 10.1
), which can then contribute to many
TRM populations.
This EMP to yolk-sac macrophage transition
does not involve a monocyte intermediate but instead proceeds
through the development of a pre-macrophage intermediate.
In addition to becoming yolk-sac macrophages, these EMPs can
also colonize the fetal liver and subsequently generate TRMs
across the body (
Fig. 10.1
).
Whether these EMPs in the fetal
liver develop into macrophages through a monocytic intermediate is still debated. Finally, it has also been suggested TRMs may
develop from hematopoietic stem cell (HSC)–derived fetal liver
monocytes that colonize tissue before birth (
Fig. 10.1
).
With these distinct progenitors available, what determines the
precise contribution of the different progenitors to each TRM
population? This has been proposed to be regulated by two main
factors: (1) which progenitors are present at the moment the tissue becomes available for colonization and (2) the accessibility of
the tissue.
If a tissue develops after the colonization of the fetal
liver with progenitors, these will likely be the predominant origin of the macrophages generated in that tissue, for example, as
observed in the lung.
However, if the tissue is generated early
in development, then the macrophages are likely to comprise a
mixture of yolk-sac and fetal liver origin. Here tissue accessibility
will be the main determinant of the contribution of each progenitor type. For example, the blood-brain barrier begins to seal
at embryonic day 13.5, before the EMPs colonize the fetal liver
and develop into fetal liver monocytes, preventing these cells from
gaining access to the brain and becoming microglia, the TRMs of
the brain, meaning microglia are thus generated exclusively from
yolk-sac macrophages.

Fig. 10.1

Each tissue-specific

accessory functions

immunity.

and synapse pruning by microglia in the brain.
Highlighting the critical role of the local microenvironment in this process, culturing peritoneal macrophages or microglia ex vivo results
in a rapid loss of their tissue-specific identity.
This obviously also
has significant implications both regarding the study of these cells
in vitro and in terms of inflammation or tissue injury when the
local environment is altered.

### Macrophage Plasticity

Macrophages have long been considered to be one of the most
plastic cells of the immune system.
This means that they were
hypothesized to be able to completely alter their transcriptional
and epigenetic profiles and hence functions depending on the
external signals they receive. This hypothesis was based on two
main findings. First, in vitro research demonstrated that treatment
of BM-derived macrophages with different stimuli could elicit distinct responses. This led to the often-used terminology “M1 and
M2 macrophages” for pro-inflammatory (classically activated)
and resolving/repair (alternatively activated) macrophages, respectively.
Moreover, bulk RNA sequencing of macrophages isolated
from inflamed and healthy tissues revealed significant differences,
further suggesting that these cells would be very plastic.
However,
more recently, this concept of macrophage plasticity, particularly
the plasticity of TRMs, has begun to be questioned.
This change
in opinion stems from a few distinct experimental findings.
With the identification of the role of the local microenvironment in TRM imprinting, it became clear that the in vitro polarization studies and the M1/M2 classification represent a substantial
oversimplification of macrophage activation in vivo, where these
cells would likely never receive one signal at a time.
The precise
limitations of this in vitro model and the reasons why we have to
move away from this nomenclature when studying macrophages
in  vivo are discussed further in

### Studying Mononuclear Phagocyte Heterogeneity at Single-Cell Resolution

Now that we know there are likely many subsets of macrophages
present, especially in the inflamed, infected, or injured tissue, how
can we better understand them? Named Method of the Year in
2013 by
Nature Methods
,
single-cell technologies, including
scRNA-seq,
cellular indexing of transcriptomes and epitopes
by sequencing (CITE-seq),
cytometry by time of flight mass
spectrometry (CyTOF),
single-cell assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq),
and the more
recent spatial transcriptomics and spatial multiplex proteomics
technologies
are allowing the distinct subsets to be distinguished
and insights into the functional heterogeneity of these cells to be
obtained (
Fig. 10.3
). There are many distinct scRNA-seq methods
including plate-based and droplet-based options (reviewed in refs
59, 60). These methods allow for the unbiased profiling of individual cells at the level of the transcriptome, enabling the discovery
of new cell subsets or activation states. This profiling is at the level
of the transcriptome, but, of course, mRNA and protein expression do not always align. Therefore any potential subsets and functions identified still must be validated at the protein level. This
can be done using conventional flow cytometry (now capable of
measuring
∼
30 parameters simultaneously) or using spectral flow
cytometry or CyTOF methods, which enable more parameters to
be profiled simultaneously. The downside of CyTOF approaches
is that the cells are destroyed in the process, preventing any downstream sorting of the cells and functional studies. This is not the
case with either spectral or conventional flow cytometry, making
these the preferred methods to also allow biologic assessment of
the subsets identified after fluorescence-activated cell sorting.
Regardless of which method is chosen, however, the first step
is to predict from the transcriptomic data which mRNAs encode
surface proteins that can be used to identify the cells and which
antibodies against these epitopes are most likely to work (
Fig. 10.3
).
As one can imagine, this can be an expensive and time-consuming
endeavor. This is where performing CITE-seq instead of traditional
scRNA-seq can be advantageous.
This technology, also called
REAP-seq
, allows the simultaneous profiling of transcriptomes and
epitopes using barcoded antibodies. These are the same antibodies
that would be used for flow cytometry or CyTOF, except, instead of
being labeled with fluorescent tags (flow cytometry) or heavy metals
(CyTOF), they are labeled with barcodes. This prevents any issues
with compensation, meaning multiple antibodies (>200) can be
combined in one experiment, providing an unprecedented insight
into the best antibodies to distinguish between subsets identified
using the transcriptome data or a combination of the protein and
transcript data when using algorithms such as TotalVI
to analyze
the data simultaneously. A recent study employing CITE-seq in the
murine and human liver has, in one step, been able to identify the
distinct subsets of cells present and provide gating strategies to isolate each of them. Moreover, these strategies were subsequently validated with conventional flow cytometry, demonstrating the power
of this technology.

As for scRNA-seq approaches, being able to assess the chromatin state of cells at single-cell resolution using scATAC-seq is also
enabling a clearer picture of cellular heterogeneity in any given
tissue. This also allows an assessment of how these changes are
controlled through prediction of the transcription factors regulating the gene expression profiles.
Moreover, multiome assays
have recently been developed allowing the simultaneous profiling
of the transcriptome (scRNA-seq) and chromatin state (scATACseq). Note that, in these assays, RNA is isolated only from nuclei,
which may have an effect on the transcripts isolated, as recently
described for the liver.
However, working with nuclei simplifies
the process due to the ability to work with frozen tissue. It is also
worth noting that many new kits are currently in development
that aim to allow scRNA-seq to be performed on fixed tissues,
which will allow the libraries of legacy clinical material to finally
be profiled.
These approaches all make use of single-cell or single-nuclei
suspensions. This means that the tissue must be disrupted prior
to the analysis. As such, this prevents any assessment of spatial
patterns the identified cell types may follow, which may also limit
our ability to accurately define cell types/states. To overcome this
issue, recently spatial transcriptomics methods have been applied.
These technologies allow the transcriptome to be assessed in situ
on tissue sections, meaning tissue architecture and hence spatial
arrangement can be assessed (
Fig. 10.3
). There are many options
ranging from unbiased whole transcriptome profiling (not yet
at single-cell resolution) to single-cell approaches where only a
selected number of transcripts (currently up to approximately
300) are detected.
It is, however, important to mention that
currently the bottleneck in these technologies (aside from price)
is in data analysis, primarily cell segmentation. mRNA detection
is punctate, so the question becomes how to decide to which
cell the mRNA expression belongs. Approaches currently taken
include assigning the mRNA to the nearest nuclei or generating
in silico cells based on a given radius around the nuclei. While
such approaches work for homogeneous round cells like T cells,
this is not easy to do with macrophages, which are often elongated
instead of round and extend many processes in different directions. One way to overcome this is to use surface markers for the
cells to be able to segment the cells correctly. The combination of
mRNA and protein measurements, however, remains tricky with
the methods used to preserve mRNA often destroying epitopes,
while methods used to preserve epitopes do not preserve mRNA.
Therefore this represents a key area of study in the field.
To avoid this issue, such spatial profiling can also be done at the
proteomic level, again highlighting the benefits of CITE-seq as if
the specific proteins of interest are already known, which can dramatically speed up the process. There are multiple methods to do
this, including traditional confocal microscopy approaches, which
remain limited in terms of the number of antibodies that can be
imaged simultaneously. Alternatively, approaches using barcoded
antibodies or cyclical staining and bleaching or cleavage techniques are now becoming popular. The latter approach involves
repeated rounds of staining and imaging, whereby a group of antibodies are added and imaged then the fluorescence is bleached
or the fluorescent tag is cleaved and another round of antibodies
are applied and the process continued. Of course, the limitation
with these proteomic methods is the availability of antibodies to
distinguish between the cell types of interest, which can be tricky
if one is considering subtle differences between different activation
states of the same macrophage population. However, despite these
current limitations, these technologies are rapidly developing and
crucially allow spatially relevant cell–cell interactions to be profiled for the first time, hence providing further insight into functional relevance of distinct cell types in specific settings.

## Mononuclear Phagocytes in Rheumatoid Arthritis

For decades, synovial tissue macrophages (STMs) have been recognized as key contributors to RA pathogenesis, and multiple
clinical observations suggest a positive correlation between the
abundance of STMs and the magnitude of synovial damage.
In
line with that, STMs are believed to be the main producer of TNF
and interleukin 6 (IL-6), two key pro-inflammatory cytokines in
RA
supporting the putative pathogenic role of STMs. However,
the specific roles of tissue-resident and recruited STMs in RA,
as well as the different phenotypes that these macrophages might
possess, have only recently begun to be unraveled.

### Synovial Macrophages in the Human

The recent development of minimally invasive biopsy techniques
coupled with the advances in single cell technologies has allowed
a significant collection of human synovial tissue, both healthy and
inflamed, to be profiled. This has revolutionized our understanding of the functional heterogeneity within mononuclear phagocytes in both the healthy and inflamed joint.
In general, the
healthy human synovium comprises a population of tissue-resident
STMs characterized by expression of the pan-macrophage markers
CD64, CD11b, and CD68 together with the specific expression
of the proto-oncogene tyrosine-protein kinase MER (MerTK)
and the C-type lectin mannose receptor (CD206).
scRNA-seq
has revealed that these human tissue-resident STMs can be further subdivided into at least two distinct populations (
Fig. 10.4
).
First, a population of MerTK
CD206

TREM2 and folate receptor 2 (FOLR2) form a protective lining layer in the uppermost part of the synovium. These are likely
to be the human counterpart of the murine CX3CR1
TREM2

Fig. 10.4
). Second, a population
of MerTK
CD206
macrophages express the Lymphatic vessel
endothelial hyaluronan receptor 1 (LYVE-1) and FOLR2, which
mainly reside in the synovial sublining layer and hence likely represents the human equivalent of the mouse RELM
α
-resident
STMs (
Fig. 10.4
).

Recently the presence of tissue-resident LYVE-1
macrophages
has been reported in many tissues. These cells possess crucial roles
in maintaining blood vessel integrity under steady-state conditions.
Although the specific role of MerTK
CD206
LYVE-1
STMs remains to be investigated, the expression of LYVE-1 coupled with their specific location in the sublining synovial layer,
which is highly vascularized, also suggests that these tissue-resident STMs may play important roles in preserving perivascular
homeostasis.
While the embryonic origin of mouse tissue-resident STMs has
been demonstrated, the origin of human tissue-resident STMs is
unclear. Moreover, it remains to be seen if the human STMs are
also maintained through the input of local precursors as observed
in the mouse.
A cluster of human tissue-resident STMs expressing high levels of
CSF1R
and DNA-binding protein inhibitor ID-2
(ID2)
, which is a transcription factor that has been implicated in
the process of cellular self-renewal, has been recently identified
and suggested to be the human counterpart of the embryonically
derived mouse STM precursors.
However, these findings have
not yet been confirmed experimentally.
In the healthy human joint, both tissue-resident STM
populations possess important regulatory functions and hence
are proposed to contribute to synovial tissue homeostasis. As
observed in the mouse, human MerTK
CD206
TREM2
STMs express high levels of tight junction proteins, enabling
the generation of a protective barrier against pathogens and
debris.
In addition, analysis of the transcriptomic profiles
of these TRMs predict that they might play crucial roles in
maintaining tissue homeostasis through the removal of dead
cells and debris and in preventing inflammation through
their expression of defensins and complement proteins.
The
sublining layer STMs are also predicted to have a regulatory
function. Specifically, the transcriptome of these macrophages
suggests they play roles in the phagocytosis of erythrocytes and
iron recirculation, in the regulation of collagen deposition by
synovial fibroblasts, and in controlling blood vessel permeability.
Importantly, however, most of these functions
remain only as predictions based on the transcriptomic data
and further experiments are required to validate them.
Mirroring the observations made in experimental animal
models of RA,
human arthritis is also accompanied by
changes in the behavior of tissue-resident MerTK
CD206
macrophages.
Interestingly, unlike in mice, this is proposed to be
linked with significant changes in their transcriptomic profiles.
Specifically, human studies in early arthritis suggest a switch of
MerTK
CD206
TREM2
macrophages toward a TREM2
phenotype.
Moreover, a subset of LYVE-1
macrophages expressing ICAM1 has also been reported
(
Fig. 10.4
). Whether these
represent novel subsets of STMs or an altered profile of the existing macrophage subpopulations remains to be validated experimentally. MerTK
CD206
TREM2
STMs also downregulate
important immune regulatory pathways while upregulating
their expression of pro-inflammatory mediators such as alarmins and osteopontin (encoded by
Spp1
).
This altered phenotype, together with the disruption of the lining layer formed by
the MerTK
CD206
TREM2
TRMs, might represent the very
first step in the initiation of joint inflammation. While a role for
these tissue-resident STMs during RA onset has been proposed,
additional studies are now required to fully elucidate how these
macrophages behave during the progression of the disease and if
this altered gene expression profile is maintained throughout the
duration of the disease or in resolution.
Alongside these differences in the phenotype and profiles of
MerTK
CD206
STM populations, the inflamed synovium is
also characterized by the presence of a significant population of
MerTK
CD206
macrophages.
These are the predominant
macrophage populations in RA in active disease (
Fig. 10.4
).
Drawing parallels with the mouse, it is assumed that these macrophages would likely originate from recruited BM-derived monocytes. These MerTK

into different clusters based on their differential gene expression

Specifically, four clusters of MerTK
CD206
STMs
have been identified in human synovium in active RA, all of which

(encoding
for S100 calcium-binding protein A12) cluster, an SPP1

(perhaps representing the equivalent of murine BM-derived STMs

Fig. 10.4

also causing some confusion in the field, further complicating this
assessment.

pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF and IL-6 and hence, given the
clinical therapeutic validation of their pathogenetic importance,
are proposed to be the main drivers of RA synovitis. In addition to
the broad production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, each cluster
of infiltrating macrophages (or macrophages and DCs) expresses
a unique transcriptomic signature, which might reflect different
and specific functions in the pathogenesis of RA.
While additional research is required to decipher differences in function
between these mononuclear phagocyte subsets, preliminary results
obtained in vitro suggest that recruited MerTK
CD206
STMs,
but not tissue-resident MerTK
CD206
STMs, can trigger synovial fibroblast activation, which may drive the progression of RA.
Aside from their largely unknown functions, another important
question remaining is where these distinct subsets are located. Perhaps this information may also shed light on their specific roles
in the pathogenesis of RA. Spatial transcriptomic approaches, as
discussed, will provide additional information in this regard.

### Human Synovial Macrophages During Rheumatoid Arthritis Remission and Relapse

Although a definitive cure for RA does not exist, many treatments
can drive RA remission, which is characterized by the resolution
of synovial inflammation.
Unfortunately, this remission is just
temporary, and one-third of patients affected by RA will display
alternating periods of remission and relapse. While the underlying mechanisms of this relapsing–remitting phenomenon remain
largely unknown, recent studies have started to correlate the presence of different STM populations with the likelihood of remission or relapse, suggesting that STMs may hold a key in this
process. Specifically, recent evidence suggests that tissue-resident
MerTK
CD206
STMs may be critical for RA remission.
In contrast to active RA, where the synovium of patients is
filled with pro-inflammatory-recruited MerTK
CD206
STMs,
the remission phase is characterized by a contraction of the
MerTK
CD206
STM pool and restoration of the homeostatic
and anti-inflammatory pool of both MerTK
CD206
STMs
encompassing both the TREM2
and LYVE-1
subpopulations.
Interestingly, these macrophages are not identical to those present

in the healthy joint as they upregulate a number of genes alongside
their surface expression of CD163.
This of course raises the question as to whether these cells are the original tissue-resident STMs
that expand in remission or if they represent monocyte-derived
STMs (InfResMacs) that are recruited prior to or upon remission.
Moreover, while in the past it has been difficult to predict which
patients will flare when in remission, it has recently been suggested
that the prevalence of MerTK
CD206
-resident STMs may be a
good predictor of this, with patients maintaining remission having
higher numbers of these macrophages than those who relapsed.
While the exact mechanism of this phenomenon remains unclear,
the regulatory nature of tissue-resident STMs, together with their
ability to produce anti-inflammatory mediators, might drive the
resolution of inflammation and continued RA remission. In addition to MerTK
CD206
STMs, MerTK
CD206
STMs are also
suggested to play an important role in RA relapse. Specifically,
the persistence of the MerTK
CD206
CD48
S100A12
STM
cluster, which is particularly enriched in the expression of proinflammatory alarmins such as S100A8, S100A10, and S100A12,
has been suggested to predict RA flare.

## Human Macrophage Heterogeneity: Where Do We Go From Here?

For decades, synovial macrophages have been broadly considered
to be a homogeneous and pro-inflammatory population implicated in the progression of RA. Recent studies, however, have
brought this concept into question, demonstrating the existence
of different subsets of synovial macrophages with different functions and origins. We now appreciate that tissue-resident STMs,
far from being pathogenic, likely fulfill crucial roles in homeostasis
and RA remission, whereas recruited STMs may represent a more
pathogenic and heterogeneous population.
In the future, this
important finding might allow more precise therapeutic strategies
to be designed that specifically target each of these STM populations individually. For instance, given the proposed regulatory
nature of tissue-resident STMs, new therapeutic strategies might
aim to boost their differentiation and survival. On the contrary,
better inhibition of the STMs predominating in active RA may
be beneficial, as these STMs appear to be pro-inflammatory.
Such an approach would provide much more resolution than the
current approaches, which aim to generally inhibit macrophage
functions or development. Moreover, such approaches may even
be relevant in other inflammatory diseases as some signatures of
recruited STMs may be conserved with recruited macrophages in
other inflamed tissues (
Box 10.2
). It is clear that we still need a
better understanding of the precise roles played by the distinct
subsets that have now been identified in order to further guide
the development of specific therapies to counterbalance synovial
inflammation.
In conclusion, the role of the myeloid lineages in the pathogenesis of immune-mediated inflammatory diseases (IMID) is
paramount. The next frontier is to extend the detailed studies performed in RA to other IMIDs with a view to understanding common activities across the range of subsets thus far identified and
to confirm that they are indeed consistent lineages. In this respect
the advances in RA could presage a much broader revolution in
the treatment of RMDs.
