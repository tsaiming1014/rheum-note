## Introduction

Amyloidosis
is the term used to describe a heterogenous group
of disorders characterized by protein misfolding and the extracellular deposition of previously soluble proteins as insoluble
fibrils.
In amyloid these fibrils associate with other components
and accumulate, causing progressive disruption of tissue architecture and organ function. In all cases, the amyloid deposits
have a remarkably uniform morphology of rigid elongated and
nonbranching fibrils assembled on crossβ
scaffolds, reinforced
by steric zipper motifs that form a stableβ
pleated-sheet configuration that can be visualized by near-atomic resolution
studies.
The diagnostic gold standard is Congo red staining
of the tissues, with the classic finding of apple-green birefringence under cross-polarized light (see
Fig. 121.1

dermatan sulfate groups, serum amyloid P (SAP) component,
apolipoprotein E and A4, laminin, and collagen IV. The formation of amyloid under favorable conditions in vitro appears to

## AA Amyloidosis

AA amyloidosis occurs as a complication of chronic inflammation, and most patients present with proteinuric renal disease. In
AA amyloidosis the amyloid precursor protein is the SAA protein, which is a normal serum component. SAA, like C-reactive
protein (CRP), is both evolutionarily highly conserved and the
most dynamic of the hepatic acute-phase response proteins, suggesting that it plays vital roles in inflammation and innate immunity.

### β 2 Microglobulin Amyloidosis

β
2 microglobulin amyloidosis is a condition that occurs in patients
with long-standing ESRF. Renal insufficiency results in high circulating plasma levels of
β
2 microglobulin, which are not sufficiently
cleared by dialysis. Prolonged elevation of
β
2 microglobulin predisposes to deposition of
β
2 microglobulin amyloid deposits. After
3 years of commencing hemodialysis, it is estimated that 20% to
30% of patients will have developed
β
2 microglobulin amyloid
deposits.
The condition is diminishing in frequency,
but the
dominant osteoarticular deposition of the
β
2 microglobulin fibrils
mean that this condition is important for a rheumatologist to be
aware of. The condition manifests with CTS, pain and stiffness of
the large joints, bone cysts, and pathologic fractures. Extra-articular
amyloid deposition can occur, largely within the spleen. There is no
treatment except restoration of normal renal tubular function by
renal transplantation, but improved dialysis techniques have significantly reduced the incidence of this type of amyloidosis.

### Wild-Type Transthyretin Amyloidosis

Wild-type transthyretin amyloidosis (ATTR), formerly termed
“senile systemic amyloidosis,” is an increasingly diagnosed cause
of heart failure with preserved ejection fraction predominately in
elderly men.
Deposition of transthyretin can occur in the walls
of the heart, smooth and striated muscle, fat tissue, renal papillae,
and alveolar walls, but the spleen and renal glomeruli are rarely
affected.
The clinical manifestations of ATTR are predominately
due to the cardiac deposition, but 48% of patients at our center
have a history of carpal tunnel decompression,
demonstrating
that a significant proportion of ATTR patients also have CTS
and spinal stenosis
and so may present to the rheumatologist.
Conversely, evidence of amyloidosis was found in the tenosynovium of 29% of men age 50 and older and women 60 years and
older who underwent surgical carpal tunnel release, with 80% of
cases deriving from wild-type TTR, highlighting the opportunity
for rheumatologists to detect TTR amyloid early.
As novel treatments become available, this may become an important opportunity for early intervention.

### Hereditary Amyloidosis

Hereditary systemic amyloidosis, in some types also known as
familial amyloid polyneuropathy, is an autosomal dominant inherited condition characterized by features including progressive
peripheral and autonomic neuropathy in addition to involvement
of other visceral organs, which can include the heart and kidneys.
Mutations in at least nine genes can cause the condition,
but penetrance is variable and so it is often difficult to reliably predict the
clinical phenotype based on the underlying mutation. The types of
hereditary amyloidosis of interest to a rheumatologist include those
that cause proteinuria (and so are a differential to AA amyloidosis),
which includes fibrinogen
α
chain, AApoA1, AApoAIV, and gelsolin.
Gelsolin amyloidosis is also of interest to the rheumatologist
given the skin and connective tissue manifestations of this condition that could be mistaken for other rheumatological disorders.
Gelsolin is a cytoplasmic protein that binds actin. The gelsolin
variant is characterized by asparagine substituted for aspartic acid.
Patients present with corneal lattice dystrophy, cranial neuropathy,
and distal peripheral neuropathy.
Finally, other rare hereditary diseases that can cause amyloidosis that may present with a myopathy include mutations in
dysferlin and anoctamin 5, which are both recessively inherited
and result in limb girdle muscular dystrophy type 2B and 2L
respectively.

## Diagnostic Tests for AA Amyloidosis

The diagnosis of AA amyloidosis, as with all types of amyloidosis, relies on histologic proof of the presence of amyloid deposits.

Once histology and immunohistochemistry have confirmed a
diagnosis of AA amyloidosis, the next steps are to establish the
extent of organ involvement.

### Imaging, Including SAP Scintigraphy

SAP scintigraphy is a noninvasive method used to establish the
presence, distribution, and extent of amyloid deposits within the
visceral organs.
SAP is a nonfibrillary component of all amyloid deposits, and so the scan is useful for all types. Radio-labeled
SAP (123I-SAP) is intravenously injected and reversibly binds to
amyloid deposits in proportion to the quantity present. The dose
of radioactivity is small (80–90 MBq for a 6-hour scan, 120–190
MBq for a 24-hour scan), allowing serial scans to be performed
that can monitor the progression or regression of amyloid deposits
over time.
The scan is useful in detecting deposits within the liver, spleen,
adrenal glands, bones, and kidneys (see
Fig. 121.4
) (except when
patients reach ESRF when the lack of blood flow reduces the
uptake of tracer). The resolution of SAP scintigraphy is insufficient to reliably identify deposits within the GI tract. Skin, nerves,
and the heart and lungs are also not reliably evaluated due to the
blood pool.
Cardiac amyloidosis is instead evaluated by a combination
of electrocardiogram (ECG), imaging, and cardiac biomarkers.
The ECG may be normal, but in advanced cardiac involvement
small voltages are seen as well as pathologic Q waves (pseudoinfarct pattern) (see
Fig. 121.5
). A mean left-ventricular wall thickness of >12 mm on two-dimensional Doppler echocardiogram,
in the absence of hypertension or other causes of left-ventricular
hypertrophy, suggests cardiac involvement.

## Treatment of AA Amyloidosis

### Principles of Treatment

The principle of treatment of all types of amyloidosis aims to
halt, or slow, the production of new amyloid deposits to less than
the rate of clearance of amyloid deposits while supporting organ
function. In AL amyloidosis, this involves chemotherapy targeted
at the underlying plasma cell clone, whereas in AA amyloidosis,
treatment is targeted at the underlying inflammatory condition.
In the treatment of AA amyloidosis, disease activity and
response to treatment is assessed by serial SAA measurements
(where available), or CRP concentrations, given the strong correlation between both readings in most cases
; it is advised that
simultaneous measurements of SAA and CRP are taken to establish if CRP can be reliably used as a surrogate marker for disease
activity.
The aim is biochemical normalization of SAA/CRP
levels.

### General Supportive Management Including Renal Transplantation

The kidney is the major affected organ in AA amyloidosis, and so
general measures should be directed at preserving renal function;
this includes the avoidance of nephrotoxic drugs and hypo- or
hypertension (see
Table 121.4
). Patients are often nephrotic and,
provided their blood pressure is maintained, a negative sodium
balance should be aimed for; directing patients to restrict their
fluid intake to 1.5 liters and limiting their sodium intake to 3 g
per day. Loop diuretics may be required in high doses. Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors can help reduce proteinuria and
have been found to reduce the risk of progression to ESRD.
Anti-coagulation in nephrotic syndrome should be considered
in heavy proteinuria given the risk of thrombosis. Approximately 40% of patients with AA amyloidosis will require renal
replacement therapy (RRT), with a median time to dialysis of 78
months.
Survival on dialysis is variable and may be related to
the underlying disease and control of SAA levels. Peritoneal dialysis has a higher risk of infection and hypoalbuminemia, which is
known to be predictive of mortality.

### Renal Transplantation

Renal transplantation in AA amyloidosis offers improved quality of life and should be considered for patients whose underlying disease is controlled, reflected by sustained control of their
SAA levels. Excellent 5- and 10-year graft survival rates have been
reported at 74% and 68%, respectively, which is comparable to
control patients.
Patient survival is, however, reduced in AA
amyloidosis patients compared with controls at 5 years (82.5% vs.
94.2%) and 10 years (61.7% vs 83.4%), respectively.
Although
amyloid deposition in the graft is recognized,
with a recurrence
rate as high as 14%,
most patients have a functioning graft until
death, suggesting that factors other than graft failure or recurrence
of renal amyloidosis are contributing to the increased mortality;
such factors include sepsis and acute cardiovascular events, and
so an in-depth cardiac assessment is advisable as part of the renal
transplant workup process.

### Novel Treatments

Significant advances have been made in understanding the pathogenesis of amyloid deposition, which has facilitated the development of novel treatments that focus on the production of amyloid
or clearance of amyloid deposits from the tissues. Eprodisate
(1,3-propanedisulfonate), a drug developed to bind to the glycosaminoglycan binding sites on SAA and inhibit the formation of
the glycosaminoglycan-amyloid fibril aggregate, offered potential
as a targeted therapy in AA amyloidosis but unfortunately failed
to reduce progression to ESRF or death compared with current
active therapy in a phase 3 trial.
An alternative approach has been to deplete the plasma of SAP
with small palindromic drugs such as R-1-[6-[R-2-carboxy-pyrrolidin-1-yl]-6-oxo-hexanoyl] pyrrolidine-2-carboxylic acid,
an
effect that can be augmented by the use of anti-SAP antibodies to
accelerate the clearance of amyloid deposits.
In the future, silencing RNA and anti-sense oligonucleotides
may be useful. There is proof of principal data from trials in data
from ATTR,
and clearly there is a possibility of using similar approaches to knock down hepatic production of SAA. This
might be particularly beneficial in patients with AA amyloidosis
of unknown cause, in those whose underlying inflammation has
proved refractory to treatment, or those who cannot tolerate longterm immunosuppression due to infections.

## Outcomes

The overall outcome for patients with AA amyloidosis has
improved largely due to better access to systemic anti-inflammatories and earlier detection. In 2007 a review of 373 cases of AA
amyloidosis reported a median survival of 133 months from diagnosis (
∼
11 years).
Amyloid regression is seen in patients with
well-controlled inflammatory levels, with evidence of amyloid
regression on SAP scintigraphy with a corresponding improvement in both the degree of proteinuria and renal function. The
recovery of organ function is associated with excellent long-term
survival outcomes. Untreated AA amyloidosis is, however, still
associated with significant mortality. Patients with persistently
elevated SAA levels are at greater risk, with a reported
∼
18-fold
increased risk of death.

## Conclusion

The amyloidoses that are of interest to rheumatologists are the
AA type, which is a feared complication of diseases managed by
the specialty, including chronic inflammatory arthritides, vasculitis, and the rare systemic autoinflammatory diseases. In addition,
other types of amyloidosis can present to rheumatologists due to
musculoskeletal complications, CTS, and spinal stenosis, which
may lead to a diagnosis of amyloidosis and may require management by other specialties, such as hematology.
AA amyloidosis can complicate any chronic inflammatory condition. Patients present with proteinuria and a diagnosis relies on
histologic confirmation of amyloid deposits within the affected
tissue, usually with a renal biopsy. Imaging is useful to confirm
the presence, distribution, and extent of amyloid deposits. Treatment is targeted at tight control of the underlying inflammatory
disorder with systemic anti-inflammatory agents to reduce circulating SAA and CRP levels and reduce the risk of further amyloid deposition. Avoidance of further nephrotoxic insults during
systemic treatment is crucial to preserve renal function and delay
progression to ESRF. ESRF develops in up to 40% of patients
with AA amyloidosis and hemodialysis may be a preferable mode
of RRT given the lower risk of infection. Renal transplantation is
an option but carries a
∼
14% risk of amyloid recurrence, although
graft survival rates are good. Future therapies rely on reducing
hepatic production of fibril precursors, targeting SAP, accelerating clearance, and preventing further amyloid fibril production,
which hold promise for the future.
