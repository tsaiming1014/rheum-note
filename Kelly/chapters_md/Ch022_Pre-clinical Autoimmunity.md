## Introduction

In many fields of medicine, the concept of a prolonged, clinically
silent phase is well known and often a target of disease modification or prevention. Atherosclerotic changes in the coronary
or cerebral circulations are typically present for decades before a
clinical event such as a myocardial infarction or cerebrovascular
accident. Cellular dysplasia is present years before the transformation to invasive cervical or colon carcinoma. Screening strategies
such as blood pressure monitoring, measurement of serum cholesterol, and colonoscopy are widely accepted strategies to minimize
morbidity and mortality.
Over the past several decades, similar constructs have been
developed for systemic autoimmune and rheumatic diseases. The
risk factors for conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) have been identified, and specific alterations in the immune systems of people at risk have been
documented by researchers in the field. This has led to the concept
that early and widespread screening of populations can identify
those persons most at risk of developing systemic autoimmune
disease and, potentially, intervention to either extend the clinically
silent stage or prevent autoimmune disease entirely. At this time,
such strategies are not available for most autoimmune diseases—
the exception being type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM). This chapter will review the concept of “pre-classification” autoimmune and
rheumatic disease and discuss the strategies for intervention.
A general schema for the progression of individuals through
the stages of autoimmunity is shown in
Fig. 22.1
. The majority
of the population has low risk for the development of autoimmune disease. Various genetic, demographic, and exposure factors
discussed below place a fraction of the population at risk for the
development of autoimmunity. A portion of the at-risk population
then develops detectable features of autoimmunity (stage 1). The
most obvious features of autoimmunity are autoantibodies, but
increasing evidence exists for activation of pro-inflammatory gene
expression of multiple chemokines and cytokines. This stage of
autoimmunity can be relatively plastic, with the possibility of progression to one or more defined autoimmune conditions (stages 2
and 3) or to an undifferentiated state of few clinical features that
are insufficient for classification of any one disease. Alternatively,
the individual may stay in a state with no clinical features—e.g.,
the healthy patient with a positive anti-nuclear antibody (ANA)
for life. The key gaps in our understanding reflect uncertainty in
the triggers that move individuals from a pre-classification state to
the accumulation of clinical features, whether movement to one
disease or another is based on patient-specific exposures or chance
and, importantly, whether and how progression is prevented or
reversed.

## Defining Pre-classification Autoimmunity: More Than Just Semantics

One issue facing investigators, medical providers, and patients is
the lack of a precise definition of
pre-classification
or
pre-clinical
autoimmunity
. For some, the use of terms such as
incomplete lupus
spark controversy as they may label people who do not have progressive disease needing treatment.
Different terms have been
used to apply to similar clinical situations (e.g.,
incomplete lupus
,
pre-clinical lupus
, and
undifferentiated connective tissue disease
[UCTD]) by different investigators making comparison among
studies difficult.
Alternatively, these early disease states may be
thought of as the early stages of their respective diseases, or
preclassification
. This is most clear when the biomarkers or clinical
features have high specificity and progression to clinically evident
disease is clear. For example, children who are first-degree relatives (FDRs) of patients with T1DM and who have two or three
diabetes-associated antibodies have an 84% chance of developing the disease.
Thus they are considered to have early T1DM,
even though their insulin secretion is normal. Patients with anticitrullinated protein antibodies (ACPAs) and synovitis on MRI
have an approximately 39% to 65% chance of developing RA over
the next 3 years.
They can be thought of as having the earliest
stages of RA. This distinction—defining the early stages of disease to include the asymptomatic phase—is important in order
to obtain regulatory approval for interventions and to convince
these individuals to take immunomodulatory therapies.

definitions of the early stages of each autoimmune disease are

## Population Epidemiology of Autoimmunity

There is an indistinct line differentiating the majority of healthy
persons with laboratory evidence of autoimmunity (e.g., ANA or
ACPA) and those who are in the earliest phases of systemic disease. Placing patients in stages is both a theoretical and practical
construct to define those who need monitoring and consideration
for interventions. To enter into the earliest stage, a person will
have a biomarker or set of biomarkers with high sensitivity and
specificity for the particular autoimmune condition. It is tempting to use autoantibodies as screening biomarkers due to their ease
of measurement, but their lack of specificity makes them poor
choices by themselves. For example, anti-nuclear antibodies have
been reported in up to 27% of the general population depending
on the titer and are thus inappropriate as standalone screening
tests.
Rheumatoid factor (RF) is found in approximately 3% to
10% of the population, rising with age,
and ACPA is found in
under 4%.
Thyroid antibodies, including anti-thyroglobulin
and anti-thyroid peroxidase, are found in the United States population at a rate of 7.7% and 11.7%, respectively, also rising with
age.
Further complicating the use of disease-associated antibodies as specific biomarkers is the fact that FDRs of patients with
SLE and RA have antibodies of the opposite condition, as well
as thyroid and antiphospholipid antibodies.
Further, one-third
of patients with T1DM have thyroid antibodies.
Thus most
autoantibodies are general markers of autoreactivity, with only the
potential for immune dysregulation, but not useful by themselves
for the staging.
Often referred to as
benign
autoimmunity, the implications of
patients remaining in the asymptomatic autoimmunity stage are
unknown. ANAs have been studied in population-based cohorts
and after a 9-year median follow-up were associated with all-cause
mortality (hazard ratio [HR] per 1 SD of log [ANA]: 1.27; 95%
confidence interval [CI], 1.10 to 1.46;
P
= .0008), cardiovascular death (HR, 1.42; 95% CI, 1.13 to 1.77;
P
= .002), and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD; HR, 1.17; 95% CI,
1.01 to 1.35;
P
= .04) after adjusting for age, sex, race, and CVD
risk factors.

## Type 1 Diabetes as a Paradigm for Pre-classification Autoimmunity

T1DM results in loss of insulin secretion due to immune-mediated destruction of pancreatic beta cells.
Genetic risk for T1DM
includes human leukocyte antigen (HLA) DR and DQ alleles and
genes related to metabolism and immune regulation.
Environmental risk factors for developing T1DM include physical inactivity, stress, low vitamin D, obesity, and infections.
Several
autoantibodies have been described in the early stages, including
those targeting insulin, insulinoma-associated antigen-2, glutamic
acid decarboxylase, zinc-transporter 8, and islet cell antibodies.
Other biomarkers in early stage include the proinsulin to C-peptide ratio and pancreatic volume.
The pre-classification period
in T1DM has been rigorously studied in prospective, longitudinal studies and has been standardized into several stages
(
Fig.
22.2A
). Based on the presence of two or more highly specific antibodies, stage 1 characterizes 5% of the general population, largely
FDRs of T1DM patients.
The 10-year risk of progression to
stage 3 is 70%. Stage 2 includes patients with both antibodies and
early evidence of dysglycemia.
Their lifetime risk of advancing to
stage 3, which is incident diabetes with typical clinical features, is
nearly 100%.
Given the opportunity to identify those at higher
risk, there have been several investigations of therapies targeting multiple mechanisms in early stage disease, aiming to delay
or prevent the development of T1DM.
A recent study using
a specific anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody, teplizumab, delayed
the progression of symptomatic T1DM in FDRs of those with
T1DM (
Fig. 22.2B
).
In November 2022 the United States
Food and Drug Administration approved teplizumab for adults
and children in stage 2 of T1DM, that is, individuals with at least
two islet autoantibodies and an abnormal oral glucose tolerance
test, without fasting hyperglycemia. T1DM is an organ-specific
disease, and the long-term consequence of this therapy is not yet
known. However, this paradigm suggests that by precisely defining

## Pre-classification Systemic Autoimmune Disease

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

The nomenclature for the time period in RA before classification
has included
pre-RA
,
pre-clinical RA
,
at risk
, and
early RA
, each of
which emphasizes the expected development of RA.
However,
these terms are limited in that not all patients who have risk factors go on to develop clinically apparent or classifiable disease.
Similar to T1DM, efforts to standardize the nomenclature have
described specific phases that patients may progress through based
on genetic/environmental risk factors, systemic autoimmunity,
symptoms without clinical arthritis, and, finally, RA.
Pre-classification cohorts have helped bring about a better understanding of RA disease progression. These include the
Rochester Epidemiology Project in Minnesota, Epidemiological
Investigation of RA in Sweden, the Nurses’ Health Studies in the
United States, Studies of the Etiology of RA (SERA) in the United
States, and early arthritis clinics located in Europe. They have been
influential in defining phases of disease and linking the epidemiologic data with observed immunobiology.
In the right genetic framework, risk factors impart an additive
risk for developing RA and encompass the microbiome at mucosal
surfaces; infectious agents; and environmental, occupational, and
lifestyle factors.
Periodontitis is a risk factor for developing
RA.
Porphyromonas gingivalis
causes periodontitis and produces
peptidyl arginine deiminase (PAD) enzymes leading to citrullination of bacterial and host proteins.
Antibodies to this bacteria are found not only in 11% to 23% of patients with RA but
also in FDRs with RA-associated antibodies (4.89 ± 1 vs. 4.59
± 0.88;
P
= .01) compared with antibody-negative FDRs.
The gut microbiome is also under investigation.
Prevotella copri
enrichment has been shown in patients with positive autoantibodies compared with antibody-negative FDRs
and in new-onset
untreated RA.
To date, only animal data suggest that interventions such as treating gingivitis or altering gut microbiota can disrupt disease progression in the pre-classification period.
Smokers have higher levels of citrullinated peptides compared
with those who do not smoke (57% vs 7%;
P
< .05) and increased
expression of PAD2 enzymes (smokers: median, 86%; range, 65%
to 94% vs nonsmokers: 63%; range, 40% to 89%;
P
< .001) in
the alveolar compartment.
RA-related autoantibodies are detectable in the sputum of 39% at-risk patients.
In several cohorts of
patients with positive ACPA and no inflammatory arthritis, more
than three-quarters had airway disease or interstitial lung disease
and approximately 10% developed inflammatory arthritis in the
2-year follow-up period.
Knowing which antibodies and their isotypes prior to diagnosis has spearheaded research into the pathogenesis and prediction
models of RA (see
Antibodies in RA
section). Studies have shown
that once initial tolerance is broken by the creation of autoantibodies targeting post-translationally modified proteins, months
to years may go by before signs of damage start occurring in the
joint.
A serologic analysis of stored serum samples from United
States military personnel who developed RA was performed to look
for antibodies to specific citrullinated peptide antigens commonly
seen in RA synovium.
All 81 subjects developed autoantibodies
prior to onset of RA, and over 25% were noted to have antibodies
to citrullinated histone, vimentin, enolase, and fibrinogen peptide
antigen as early as 10 years prior to the diagnosis.
Several international cohorts—Swedish,
Dutch,
and American
—have
analyzed stored serum obtained prior to diagnosis. They found
immunoglobulin (Ig)A-RF or IgM-RF and IgG-ACPA ranging
from 2.5 to 17 years prior to diagnosis, followed by IgG-RF and
IgA-ACPA closer to diagnosis.
Risk factors for developing
antibodies and length of time present prior to diagnosis include
smoking and age.
More recently, antibodies to PAD-4,
an enzyme responsible for citrullination of arginine, have been
shown in 18% of patients to precede the diagnosis of RA by 4.6
years.
Beyond traditional ACPA antibodies, there are additional
post-translational modifications leading to a new class of autoantibodies termed anti-modified protein antibodies or AMPA.
Thus
far, these antibodies tend to be present in ACPA-positive subjects,
and there is cross-reactivity with ACPA.
Examples include antimalondialdehyde-acetaldehyde,
anti-acetylated antibodies,
and anti-carbamylated protein antibodies.
Cytokine upregulation and inflammatory cell changes accompany autoantibody formation. As the number of ACPA subtypes
expand, 10% to 50% of subjects subsequently develop elevated
cytokine levels 2 to 3 years prior to the RA diagnosis.
These
inflammatory cytokines include TNF, interleukin (IL)-6, IL-2,
IL-4, IL-15, IL-12p70, IL-1
β
, IL-10, IL-17, interferon (IFN)-
γ
,
granulocyte-macrophage (GM) colony-stimulating factor (CSF),
G-CSF, monocyte chemoattractant protein (MCP)1, MIP1
α
,
and high sensitivity C-reaction protein.
Following antibody
development, CD4
T cells affect the progression to inflammatory
arthritis.
A study of CD4
T cells in 158 subjects found in 37%
of those who progressed to RA had lower numbers of naive and
regulatory CD4 T cells and higher inflammation-related cells.
ACPA-positive patients having at least two of these parameters had
an odds ratio (OR) of 4.95 to progress,
whereas ACPA negative
subjects had an OR of 2.46.
In order to define stages of RA to target with prevention strategies, it is necessary to further characterize patients at the highest risk for progression to disease.
The SERA prospective
cohort followed seropositive FDRs for a median of 4 years and
found approximately 15% of subjects progressed to RA, with
ACPA being the strongest risk factor (HR, 3.95;
P
< .01).
Preclassification retrospective and prospective cohorts have shown
that ACPA, particularly at higher titers, increases the risk of RA in
3 to 5 years.
A study assessing asymptomatic individuals used
a model including family history of RA or SLE in FDRs; serology status; epidemiologic factors including age, smoking, alcohol,
BMI, parity, and education; and genetic risk score using 39 RA
risk alleles. To predict incident RA for seropositive patients with
positive family history, the area under the curve (AUC) was 0.82
(95% CI, 0.74 to 0.90).
Antibody-negative RA models performed slightly less well at AUC 0.70 (95% CI, 0.65 to 0.75).
Another study found that a biomarker profile using the autoantibodies targeting citrullinated enolase, fibrinogen, and vimentin, as
well as cytokines IL-12(p70), IL-1
β
, IL-5, IL-7, LIF, and TNFβ
predicted imminent RA in 2 years with an AUC of 0.79.
The use of imaging to evaluate the joints before clinical synovitis can be detected is another tool to quantify risk of developing
RA. However, caution must be employed in interpretation of findings as some features consistent with RA can be found in healthy
individuals or at least in individuals in whom no evidence of active
autoimmune disease is present. A contrast-enhanced MRI study of

symptom-free individuals aged 19 to 89 years found mostly lowgrade erosions, bone marrow edema, and tenosynovitis in regions
typical of inflammatory arthritis, such as metacarpophalangeal
and metatarsophalangeal joints, wrists, and ulna in a majority
(72%) of individuals.
Ultrasound evaluation of the digit flexors
and extensor carpi ulnaris tendons in healthy subjects aged 18 to
80 years noted that 15% of patients had features of tenosynovial
inflammation including power Doppler signals, tendon hypertrophy, or effusions over the hand.
The term clinically suspect arthralgia (CSA) has been employed
to identify patients at risk for developing RA who have joint discomfort but do not have synovitis. Several studies using ultrasound
or MRI found that tenosynovitis was the strongest predictor of
who would progress to inflammatory arthritis.
One study
assessing CSA patients with MRI noted 15% progressed, and
MCP-extensor tenosynovitis was predictive with a HR 4.38 (2.07
to 9.25).
While autoantibody status, particularly ACPA, can be
additive, not all patients with imaging signals progress to inflammatory arthritis.
In a multicohort of ACPA-positive patients
with arthralgia, just over 30% had subclinical synovitis by MRI
at baseline, and yet 35% to 61% of those patients with subclinical synovitis did not develop inflammatory arthritis after a 3-year
follow-up.
Future models that measure autoantibodies, inflammatory and regulatory cytokines, and cellular defects and imaging will help us get closer to staging patients to aid in prevention
trials and prognosis. As in T1DM, serial measurements will be
necessary.
RA prevention trials have aimed to capture patients in the
different stages of the pre-classification period, including those
patients with or without CSA, positive biomarkers, and suggestive imaging findings with mixed results in outcomes.

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Experienced rheumatologists are not likely to miss a case of active
SLE. However, patients are often referred for evaluation from
primary care or specialty clinics, and unfortunately, organ damage may occur while a patient is waiting for rheumatologic evaluation.
Having better screening tools or a compilation of tests
that provide better sensitivity and specificity is essential to prevent
morbidity and mortality.
Similar to the stages in T1DM, SLE has been divided into
phases: (1) genetic risk, (2) asymptomatic autoimmunity, and
(3) early symptoms of SLE.
This model is simplistic, for two
reasons. The phase of asymptomatic autoimmunity is typically
identified by the presence of one or more autoantibodies, usually including an ANA. Such individuals may go on to develop
SLE, but this phase may also precede other conditions such as
Sjögren’s disease, systemic sclerosis, or inflammatory myositis.
Second, SLE is a heterogeneous disease, with various individuals exhibiting mainly hematologic, cutaneous, musculoskeletal,
or renal manifestations. The risk factors, including genetics,
environmental exposures, and immune dysregulation, as well
as the rates of progression for each of these manifestations may
be different.
The clearest demonstration of asymptomatic autoimmunity
was the initial study of stored serum samples from the United
States Department of Defense (DoD) biorepository. In the original study, sequential stored serum samples from 130 individuals
who had subsequently developed SLE and matched controls were
analyzed for ANA and a panel of other autoantibodies frequently
found in SLE patients.
The majority of SLE patients (88%)
had autoantibodies a mean of 3.4 years before diagnosis. Some
patients had autoantibodies detected over 9 years before diagnosis.
There was a temporal pattern with ANA, anti-Ro, anti-La, and
antiphospholipid antibodies appearing a mean of 3.4 years before
diagnosis, followed by anti-Smith and anti-RNP a mean of 1.2
years before diagnosis (
P
= .005).
Anti-DNA was noted 2.7 years
prior to diagnosis.
Subsequent analyses of the DoD cohort have quantified cytokine, chemokines, and other soluble mediators of inflammation,
as well as symptoms onset, in the subjects who developed SLE
compared with controls.
In the 2 years prior to the onset of SLE,
44% of patients developed elevated IFNα
activity, and 75% had
increased type II interferon at the time of disease classification.
IFNα
and upregulation of BLyS was preceded by autoantibody
development and type II IFN activity. The median onset of clinical symptoms was 1.5 years before the diagnosis.
The cytokine changes in patients prior to diagnosis differ from
those who do not go on to develop disease.
Cytokine up-regulation includes interplay with type I interferon, enhanced T and B
cell proliferation and longevity, increased type II interferon expression, and upregulation of BLyS.
Interestingly, type II interferon
upregulation, specifically IFNγ
, CXCL10, and MCP-3 levels,
precedes the rise of IFNα
and autoantibodies.
Other mediators described include IFNγ
, IL-12p70, monokine induced by
gamma (MIG), IL-4, IL-5, and IL-6.
Assays measuring complement activation on cell surfaces (cell-bound C4d) predict progression to classifiable SLE, although the sensitivity of this assay is
such that it, too, is insufficient as a screening tool.
Investigators studying the pre-classification phase of SLE have
coined the term
incomplete lupus erythematosus
(ILE) to describe
a subset of patients who do not meet SLE classification criteria or
the clinical diagnosis of SLE.
The term was coined
to mean
people who meet two or three ACR criteria for the classification of
SLE. Since then, many other studies have used the term (a 2020
review
discusses these studies) to mean someone with an ANA
and one to two SLE criteria. Not unexpectedly, this has defined
a very heterogeneous cohort of patients. Prospective studies have
noted that up to 57% of ILE patients meet SLE classification criteria at a median of 5.3 years of follow-up.
Predictors of the ILE to
SLE progression vary among studies as well. They include female
sex, high titers of ANA, overall autoantibody burden, oral ulcers,
anti-DNA, active urinary sediment, photosensitivity, anti-DNA,
and hypocomplementemia.
Patients whose only disease manifestation is cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CLE) also meet these
definitions of ILE. Of 77 CLE patients, 13 developed criteria for
SLE over a mean of 8 years, with 38% developing moderate to
severe systemic disease.
Several techniques have been used to characterize the immune
response in people with ILE versus SLE. Using techniques to
quantify IgM and IgG antibodies to over 100 known autoantigens, investigators demonstrated that ILE patients had a higher
ratio of IgM to IgG for most antigens, and the burden of IgG
antibodies rose as the disease progressed from healthy to SLE.
IgG clusters included RNA/DNA-related antigens, histones, and
collagens. ILE patients were also found to exhibit an increased
type 1 interferon signature compared with controls and lower
IFI27, anti-C1q, IP10, MCP-1, MIPI
α
, TNF, and IL-9 compared
with SLE.
Levels of BLyS, are intermediate in ILE patients
compared with healthy controls and SLE patients.
Thus ILE is
an immunologically progressive condition with features that can
both be used to predict progression, as well as potential targets for
therapy.
FDRs of SLE patients also represent an at-risk population due
to shared genetic and environmental exposures. Some of these
FDRs also meet the definition of ILE. In a study of 409 unaffected
FDRs of lupus patients, 45 developed criteria for SLE over a mean
follow-up time of 6.4 years.
Features that characterized those
who developed lupus included elevated ANA levels; higher burden of autoantibodies; worse scores on patient reported outcomes;
increased plasma levels of BLyS, stem cell factor, and interferonregulated chemokines; and reduced levels of the regulation mediators transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
and IL-10.
Anti-nuclear antibodies are often the first test chosen to determine whether a patient has SLE or other autoimmune conditions.
As noted above, the high sensitivity of the ANA makes this reasonable, while the low specificity precludes its use as a screening test.
Additional testing may identify the few ANA-positive individuals
who go on to develop SLE. A study of 84 asymptomatic ANApositive individuals included 14 who progressed with features of
systemic autoimmune disease over a 2-year period.
Only IgG
anti-Ro52 antibody was associated with progression to symptoms
with a positive predictive value of 46% and a negative predictive
value of 89%.
In the early stage pre-classification time frame, asymptomatic
ANA-positive individuals have demonstrated alterations in both
cellular and humoral immunity.
ANA-positive individuals
have increased T follicular helper cells and Th2 cells and decreased
NK and NKT cells.
Immune regulation is altered in asymptomatic ANA-positive patients with increased T regulatory cell
populations and higher levels of plasma TGFβ
.
Thus a strategy

of performing a secondary screen of ANA-positive individuals for
features of increased risk of progression to disease is necessary,
although cost and access to specialized testing may limit feasibility.
A case-control study of innate and adaptive immune pathway
soluble mediators compared 170 controls to 246 patients who
developed classifiable SLE and found that autoantibody development occurred after immune dysregulation.
The authors used
random forest models to predict at different time points those
going on to develop classifiable SLE. At 0.9 to 3.5 years prior to
classification, cases with a positive ANA could be distinguished
from controls with 92% (±0.52%) accuracy by elevated levels of
MIG, IFNγ
–induced chemokine, and IL-12. Across the entire
pre-classification period, ANA positivity, IL-5, IL-6, and MIG
predicted SLE with 92% (±1.78%) accuracy.
A recently published paper evaluating the Nurses’ Health Study used a complex
risk model to predict SLE in a predominately White population,
with an AUC of 0.76 (95% CI, 0.72 to 0.81).
This model
was based on a weighted genetic risk score composed of 86 single nucleotide polymorphisms and 10 HLA alleles; family history of RA or SLE; and lifestyle and environmental risk factors,
including age, race/ethnicity, smoking, alcohol use, body mass
index, sleep, socioeconomic status, U.S. region, menarche age,
oral contraceptive use, and menopausal status/postmenopausal
hormone use.
Assuming that a suitable combination of patient features and
biomarkers can be developed to identify persons at high risk of
developing SLE, the next appropriate question is whether the disease onset can be slowed or prevented. Lupus prevention trials
have lagged compared with RA. A retrospective study of 130 individuals in the DoD cohort documented that those who were given
hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) or prednisone before classification
had a slower time to progression from their first clinical ACR criteria to the classification of SLE (HCQ median time 1.08 vs 0.29
years;
P
= .018).
Further, patients who were treated were less
likely to develop leukopenia and proteinuria before classification.
Patients who were not treated developed more autoantibodies
and more specificities of autoantibodies. Based on this research, a
placebo-controlled, randomized trial, the Study of Anti-Malarials
in Incomplete Lupus Erythematosus (SMILE), was undertaken
testing the effect of HCQ in ILE.
Trial results are anticipated
in 2024. Another large, randomized, double-blind, placebocontrolled trial investigating the impact of vitamin D and fish oil
found that vitamin D supplementation reduced the incidence of
developing autoimmune disease, although there were not enough
data to assess for SLE specifically.
For patients at risk for SLE,
current recommendations are to modify at-risk behaviors, including stopping smoking, exercising regularly, monitoring environmental exposures, maintaining a healthy weight, and practicing
good sleep hygiene.
Staging pre-classification SLE will be more challenging.
Within asymptomatic autoimmunity, patients may not progress
or may move on to different diseases in the autoimmune spectrum. Studying asymptomatic autoimmunity may also prove to be
resource intensive; however, ILE presents a distinct opportunity
for engagement.

### Systemic Sclerosis

Pre-classification systemic sclerosis (SSc) or scleroderma also has
several names, including
UCTD-risk-SSc, early SSc,
and
prescleroderma
.
These terms encompass a patient exhibiting various combinations of features, such as Raynaud’s phenomenon,
characteristic nailfold capillary abnormalities, and autoantibodies.
These serum biomarkers include ANA, anti-centromere,
anti-topoisomerase I, anti-RNA polymerase III, anti-Th/To, and
anti-Pm-Scl.
The strongest risk factor for developing systemic sclerosis is
having a family member affected.
Genetic risk factors for developing SSc overlap with other autoimmune conditions, or “shared
autoimmunity,” and include interferon and T cell signaling genes,
including IRF5, STAT4, and CD247.
Additional factors such
as DNA methylation, histone modifications, and microRNA contribute to risk.
In the earliest phase of scleroderma, there is an increase in type
1 interferon, which with further study may help identify those who
progress over time.
Several small studies have assessed patients
in early stages of pre-classification scleroderma who, according
to criteria established in 2001,
do not have fibrotic features
of scleroderma and are at higher risk of progression,
These
patients had higher levels of CCL2, sICAM-1, CXCL8, IL-13,
very high IL-33, TNFR2, CHI3L1, CXCL10, and CXCL11, with
the latter two being predictive of progression.
Peripheral
blood cell gene expression found increased interferon signaling
and decreased transcription of markers for erythropoiesis, cytotoxic/NK cell, and lymphoid lineage expression, with persistent
down-regulation of NK cell expression in those who progressed.
Early disease clinics and studying relatives will be critical for risk
prediction in this rare disorder.

### Sjögren’s Disease

In Sjögren’s disease, autoantibodies have been found in serum
samples in patients up to 20 years (median, 4.3 to 5.1 years) prior
to diagnosis.
These autoantibodies include ANA, RF, anti-Ro
60/SSA, anti-Ro 52/SSA, and anti-La/SS-B.
Patients less than
40 years of age had a greater prevalence of autoantibodies, higher
titers, and more antibody specificities.
Salivary proteomic biomarkers were investigated in anti-Ro/SSA asymptomatic patients
and revealed that 57 of 166 proteins were differentially expressed
compared with healthy controls. Inflammatory innate and
humoral immunity-related and typical acinar proteins (MUC5B,
PIP, CST4, and lipocalin 1) were identified.
Ongoing research
into salivary biomarkers may help aid in differentiating early
Sjögren’s disease from healthy.

### Axial Spondyloarthritis

Early disease recognition of axial spondyloarthritis (formerly ankylosing spondylitis) (axSpA) has been challenging given the lack of
autoantibodies and inflammatory biomarkers.
HLA-B27 is a
strong genetic risk factor, as are IL23R and ERAP1
; however,
broad population screening is not feasible.
Early research in the
bone morphogenic protein signaling pathway and wnt/
β
-catenin
is ongoing.
Imaging has been a useful tool; however, many of
the findings suggesting inflammatory arthritis can also be seen in
the healthy population, patients with osteoarthritis or malignancy,
and in asymptomatic individuals with inflammatory bowel disease
(IBD).
Further, entities such as nonradiographic axial spondyloarthritis are not felt to be early axSpA, given the lack of progression in many cases.
As in other conditions, FDRs of axSpA patients have been
studied.
Greater than 50% of FDRs had back pain, and
roughly 22% had inflammatory back pain at baseline.
Further,
16% to 20% of patients had MRI evidence of inflammation in the

sacroiliac joints,
and 22% of subjects had IBD-associated
antibodies.
In IBD, risk factors for Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis (UC) often have diverging impacts for developing disease.
Factors such as smoking, statin use, and appendectomy history
increase risk for one but not the other.
Dysbiosis in luminal microbiota increases the risk for IBD, as do enteric infections
and dietary factors such as fiber, life stressors, and antibiotic
usage.
Patient serum from the DoD was analyzed 5 years
prior to diagnosis.
Panels of candidate biomarkers, including
C-reactive protein (CRP), serum amyloid P, complement factors,
proteinase-3, several interleukins, and the antimicrobial marker
antiSaccharomyces
cerevisiae antibodies (ASCA)-IgA, were
found to aid in the prediction of Crohn’s disease.
For UC,
the most specific marker was pANCA.
Given the spectrum
of overlap in axSpA and IBD and strong genetic risk, long-term
follow-up of FDRs will be needed to develop and refine prediction algorithms.

### Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating, inflammatory disorder of the central nervous system with a female predominance.
Genetic risk factors are similar to other autoimmune diseases,
with a strong risk factor from HLA-DRB1 alleles, as well as gene
expression for cytokine and cytokine receptors.
Environmental triggers include cigarette smoking, vitamin D deficiency,
obesity, Epstein–Barr virus infection, and low sun exposure.
To date, no autoantibodies have been discovered to be predictive; however, the neuro-imaging biomarker radiologically isolated syndrome (RIS) and the serum protein neurofilament light
chain (sNfl) are used to characterize pre-classification MS.
In
RIS, imaging of the CNS finds MS-like lesions, but no symptoms
are present.
Long-term follow-up suggests that 50% of RIS
patients will progress to MS within a 10-year span.
sNfl can be
considered a biomarker of neuraxonal degeneration, which can
be seen a median of 6 years prior to the diagnosis.
Following the diabetes early-stage paradigm, the phase 4 CELLO study
investigating ocrelizumab, an anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody, is
enrolling.
Dimethyl fumarate reduces the risk of a first clinical
event (unadjusted HR, 0.18; 95% CI, .05 to .63;
P
= .007) in
patients with RIS.
MS, like T1DM, has described the high-risk
population and found benefit in treating patients in early-stage
disease.

## Conclusion

In this chapter, we have reviewed the research in pre-classification
autoimmune and rheumatic disease and described how these diseases may fit into stages. Autoimmunity, defined primarily by the
detection of autoantibodies, is common; however, plasticity of the
stages and patient calculations of risks and benefits affect research
and costs. T1DM and multiple sclerosis can be used as a paradigm
with defined disease stages that are susceptible to modification
and interventions. Combinations of blood biomarkers, imaging,
genetic risk models, and patient exposures will be necessary to
build impactful prediction tools to aid research and pre-classification clinical trials. The areas of opportunity for advancement in
research and therapy are broad.
