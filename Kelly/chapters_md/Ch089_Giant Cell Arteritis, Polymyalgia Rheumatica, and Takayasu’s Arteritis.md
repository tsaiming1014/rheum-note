## Introduction

Giant cell arteritis (GCA) and polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) are
discussed together because they affect similar epidemiologic subsets of patients and often occur together in the same individual.
Although GCA is a disease of older people and Takayasu’s arteritis
(TAK) is a disease of younger people, their shared predilection for
causing vasculitis of large arteries and their nearly identical histopathologic changes prompt their inclusion in the same chapter.

### Definitions

Giant Cell Arteritis
GCA is the most common form of systemic vasculitis in adults.
The disease affects primarily the thoracic aorta and its major
branches, especially the extracranial segments of the carotid artery
in patients older than 50 years. The most feared complication of
GCA is irreversible loss of vision. Because the cause of GCA is
unknown, various names—including
temporal arteritis
,
cranial
arteritis
, and
granulomatous arteritis
—have been used to highlight
different salient features.
All the names for this disease have both
merits and shortcomings. The designations
temporal arteritis
or
cranial arteritis
, for example, convey how frequently the temporal
arteries or other cranial arteries are involved but fail to capture
GCA’s more widespread nature. Although the terms
granulomatous arteritis
and
GCA
recognize an important pathologic finding,
this focus is undeserved because giant cells are absent in approximately half of the cases and may be present in other forms of
vasculitis. With no perfect name available, this chapter bows to
convention and refers to this disease as
giant cell arteritis
.

aching and morning stiffness lasting half an hour or longer in the
shoulder, hip girdle, neck, or a combination; (2) duration of these
symptoms for 1 month or longer; (3) patient age older than 50

less than 0.1 per 100,000 to 77 per 100,000 individuals age 50

Geography and race/ethnicity are
also important risk factors. The highest incidence is found in Scandinavians and in Americans of Scandinavian descent. The lowest
incidence of GCA is reported in Japanese, northern Indians, and
African Americans. In western Europe, GCA is more common in

The diagnosis of GCA

greater physician awareness.
Some studies have reported seasonal

The prevalence of GCA in Olmsted County, Minnesota,
home to many Scandinavian immigrants, is 200 per 100,000 population age 50 years or older. Autopsy studies suggest that GCA may
be more common than is clinically apparent. Östberg

Genetic susceptibility to GCA was initially suggested by reports
of it in families

association with genes in the class II human leukocyte antigen
(HLA) region.
Sixty percent of GCA patients have HLADRB1*04 haplotype variants, which have a common sequence
motif in the second hypervariable region of the B1 molecule.
This motif differs from the one associated with RA.
The low
prevalence of these alleles in African Americans might explain why
GCA is uncommon in this population. To date, it is the form of
systemic vasculitis most closely associated with class II HLA genes.
Class I HLA genes, particularly HLA-B*15, might also augment
susceptibility.
Susceptibility to GCA and PMR has also been
associated with polymorphisms of genes for TNF, intercellular
adhesion molecules, and interleukin (IL)-18.
The existence of environmental risk factors is suggested by the
geographic clustering of GCA cases. Smoking increases the risk
for GCA six-fold in women.
Of the multiple infectious diseases potentially linked to GCA, varicella zoster has received the
most attention.
One study found varicella zoster viral antigens in the inflamed temporal arteries of 74% of patients with
GCA compared with 8% of controls.
Another study, however,
identified varicella zoster viral antigen in only 3 of 25 affected
temporal arteries.
In addition, higher incidences of GCA have
not been found in countries with increased incidence of herpes
zoster.
Retrospective studies described an increased risk of GCA
after live varicella zoster vaccination (HR 2.70).
A similar effect
of the recombinant zoster vaccine on GCA incidence has not been
described. Other studies have implicated
Mycoplasma pneumoniae
,
parvovirus B19, and parainfluenza virus type I.
The results
of examining temporal artery biopsy specimens with polymerase

negative or inconsistent.
The reported association between GCA
and

One study was unable to identify bacteria in temporal artery biopsies.
Sex and health status also influence the
development of GCA. Women are affected approximately twice
as often as men.
Having diabetes reduces the risk of GCA by
50% in women.

Dendritic cells express Toll-like receptors (TLRs)
that normally serve as sentinels, monitoring for any immunologic
breach of the adventitia.
In GCA, activation of TLRs on dendritic cells appears to be the initial triggering event.
Of the
many different types of TLRs, TLR-2 and TLR-4 might be the
most important in GCA. Although the exact trigger of the adaptive immune response in GCA is not known, experiments in the
GCA-SCID mouse model have shown that blood-borne lipopolysaccharide serves as an effective TLR ligand in temporal arteries.
Other constituents of microorganisms or some self-antigens (e.g.,
oxidized lipids) might also be TLR ligands that activate the arterial dendritic cells. Dendritic cells in GCA patients are deficient in
expressing programmed death ligand-1; deficiency of this immunohibitory checkpoint contributes to T cell activation in the vessel
wall.
Once its TLRs have been engaged, dendritic cells differentiate
from the resting to the active state and release cytokines such as
IL-6 and IL-18, which recruit, activate, and retain CD4
T cells
in the blood vessel. The crucial role of dendritic cells in activating
T cells and maintaining vasculitis has been demonstrated in the
GCA-SCID mouse model. Depleting the dendritic cells markedly reduces the T cell infiltrate and suppresses the vasculitis. As
noted, two separate populations of CD4
T cells become activated
and expand: IFNγ
producing Th1 cells and IL-17 producing
Th17 cells. IFNγ
causes macrophages to migrate, differentiate,
and form granulomas.
Production of matrix metalloproteinases and lipid peroxidation agents by macrophages in the media
results in the destruction of elastic laminae. The vessel attempts to
counter the tissue destruction by elaborating a variety of growth
factors, including PDGF, vascular endothelial growth factor, and
TGFβ
, which prompt smooth muscle cells in the media to revert
from a contractile phenotype to a secretory one and migrate to the
intima. Proliferation of the intimal smooth muscle cells results in
occlusion of the lumen.
PMR appears to result from a similar but less intense adaptive immune response in blood vessels (as evidenced by the in
situ production of many inflammatory cytokines). According to
this model, both PMR and GCA begin with the activation of
dendritic cells at the adventitia-media border.
However, the
distinguishing feature of PMR is the absence of T cells producing IFNγ
. Without IFNγ
to stimulate the recruitment and differentiation of macrophages, the level of arterial inflammation in
PMR remains subclinical. Thus the development of GCA appears
to require both vascular dendritic cell activation and a diseaseinducing repertoire of T cells.
The constitutional symptoms
of PMR and GCA are attributed to the high levels of inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1 and IL-6) found in the sera. Whether
these serum cytokine elevations result from blood vessel inflammation alone or from some other source of inflammation is not
yet clear. The attractiveness of this model is increased by its ability
to explain why subsets of clinical features occur together.

### Clinical Features

The mean age at onset of GCA and PMR is approximately 79
years, with a range of approximately 50 to 90 years of age.
Younger patients with PMR have been described infrequently,
though other diagnoses should be considered with younger age of
onset. Women are affected approximately twice as often as men.
Although the onset of the disease is usually insidious, typically
evolving during weeks or months, in one-third of cases, the disease begins so abruptly that some patients recall the very day they
became ill.

Classic Manifestations.
The most common manifestations of
GCA are constitutional symptoms, headache, visual symptoms,
jaw claudication, and PMR
(
Table 89.3
). Almost all patients
experience one or more constitutional symptoms, including
fatigue, weight loss, malaise, and fever.
Besides constitutional symptoms, headache is the most common symptom in GCA, being present in nearly three-quarters
of patients.
The pain is typically described as boring in quality, of moderate severity, and most commonly appreciated in the
temporal area. However, the description of the headache varies
enormously. It can be mild to so severe that the patient seeks
immediate relief by going to the emergency department. The
pain may localize to any part of the skull, including the occiput
(because of involvement of the occipital artery).
The most
consistent characteristic is that the patient experiences the headache as something new and unusual. In untreated patients, the
headache may subside over weeks, even though the disease activity
continues. Often, the headache of GCA is not associated with any
findings on physical examination. Abnormalities of the temporal artery—including enlargement, nodular swelling, tenderness,
or loss of pulse—manifest in only approximately half of patients
(
Fig. 89.4
). Some patients note tenderness of the scalp, which can
be aggravated by brushing or combing the hair.
Visual symptoms are common in GCA, especially loss of vision
and diplopia. Vision loss can be unilateral or (less commonly) bilateral, transient or permanent, and partial or complete.
Vision
loss that lasts more than a few hours usually does not reverse.

In a series of 245 patients from the
modern era, 34 (14%) had some permanent loss of vision.
In
32 of these patients, the deficit developed before glucocorticoid
therapy was begun; in the other 2, vision loss occurred after therapy was started. Vision loss progressed in 3 of the 32 after therapy
was initiated, and it improved in 5 patients. At 5 years’ followup, among the patients who had visual deficits caused by GCA at
the time glucocorticoids were started, the risk of additional loss
of vision was 13% during the follow-up period. If no loss had
occurred at the beginning of glucocorticoid therapy, there was
only a 1% risk of new loss of vision during the subsequent 5 years.

Diplopia usually results from
ocular motor nerve palsies caused by ischemia and usually resolves
after therapy is started. Oculomotor nerve involvement in GCA
usually spares the pupil.
Rarely, arterial lesions cause infarction
of the occipital cortex and vision loss.

Intermittent claudication may occur in the muscles of mastication (jaw claudication), the extremities, and, occasionally, the
muscles of the tongue or those involved in swallowing.
In the
jaw muscles, the discomfort is noted, especially when chewing
meat, and may involve the muscles on one side of the mandible
more than those on the other. Detection of jaw claudication may
be increased by “the chewing gum test” in which jaw discomfort
is provoked by the patient chewing gum at a rate of one chew
per second for 2 to 3 minutes.
Marked vascular narrowing from
GCA may lead to gangrene of the scalp or tongue.
Atypical Manifestations.
Approximately 40% of patients are
seen with disease manifestations that are considered atypical
(
Table 89.4
). In these patients, headache, jaw claudication, visual
symptoms, and PMR do not occur or are less prominent.
Fever occurs in as many as 40% of patients with GCA, but
it is usually low grade and overshadowed by other classic symptoms. However, 15% of GCA patients may present with fever of
unknown origin (FUO) in which the temperature spikes are high,
dominating the clinical picture.
Although GCA causes only
2% of all cases of FUO, it is responsible for 16% of all such fevers
in individuals older than 65 years.
Approximately two-thirds of
patients experience shaking, chills, and drenching sweats, features
often attributed to infection or malignancy. The median temperature is 102.4° F (39.1° C), and the maximum is 103.6° F (39.8°
C). The white blood cell count in GCA-induced FUO is usually
normal or nearly so (at least before the initiation of prednisone).
Neurologic problems occur in approximately 30% of
patients.

Among the vasculitides, GCA has a nearly unique propensity for

Mononeuropathies affecting the hands and feet, so typical

Audiovestibular dysfunction can occur in the setting of GCA

Prominent respiratory tract symptoms occur in approximately

Otolaryngeal

pain, glossitis, and ulceration or infarction of the tongue.
Clinical evidence of large-artery involvement occurs in 10% to

Prominent large-vessel vasculitis associated with GCA
(LVV-GCA) tends to occur in younger patients and is less frequently associated with ischemic manifestations.

potential pathophysiologic differences in these subgroups. Positron emission tomography (PET) studies with fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) revealed that subclinical involvement of large arteries

uptake in large arteries, with subclavian involvement in 7% and
aortic involvement in 54%.

especially in the carotid, subclavian, axillary, and brachial arteries.

Clinical Subsets.
Studies suggest that GCA is not just one
disease but, rather, a number of clinical subsets that are the
result of the differential expression of inflammatory cytokines.
Ischemic events including blindness, stroke, and
large-artery disease occur more commonly in patients who
express high levels of IFNγ
and low levels of IL-6.
In contrast, patients who produce high amounts of IL-6 are more
likely to have strong inflammatory features (such as fever and
constitutional symptoms) and are less likely to develop vision
loss or other ischemic events.
Polymyalgia Rheumatica
As in GCA, PMR patients are characteristically in good health
before the onset of disease.
Systemic manifestations such as malaise, low-grade fever, and weight loss are present in more than half
of the patients and may be the initial symptoms of disease. High,
spiking fevers are uncommon in PMR in the absence of GCA.
Arthralgias and myalgias may develop abruptly or evolve insidiously during weeks or months.
Malaise, fatigue, and depression,
along with aching and stiffness, may be present for months before
the diagnosis is made. In most patients, the shoulder girdle is the
first to become symptomatic; in the remainder, the hip or neck is
involved at the onset. The discomfort may begin in one shoulder
or hip but usually becomes bilateral within weeks. Symptoms center on the proximal limb, axial musculature, and tendinous attachments. Morning stiffness resembling that of RA and “gelling” after
inactivity are usually prominent. If the symptoms are severe, aching is more persistent. Although movement of the joints accentuates the pain, it is often felt in the proximal extremities rather
than in the joints.
Pain at night is common, and movement
during sleep may awaken the patient. Muscle strength is generally
unimpaired, although pain with movement makes the interpretation of strength-testing maneuvers difficult. Pain with movement
also makes it difficult for patients to get out of bed or the bathtub.
In the later stages of the syndrome, muscle atrophy may develop,
and contracture of the shoulder capsule may result in limitation of
passive and active motion.
As noted, the presence of bursal inflammation and synovitis
in PMR has been described by many authors and is undoubtedly
the cause of many of the findings in this condition.
A careful
examination may reveal transient synovitis of the knees, wrists,
and sternoclavicular joints. The shoulders and hips are covered by
heavy muscles, and minimal effusions of slight synovitis are not
palpable on physical examination. Synovitis has been documented
by biopsies, synovial analysis, joint scintiscans, ultrasonography,
and MRI.
Involvement of distal joints and prominent synovitis, rather than periarticular inflammation, should raise suspicion
for other diseases such as elderly-onset RA.

Patients who have signs of subclavian and axillary disease manifested by arm claudication, unequal arm blood pressures, and

involvement.
MRI and CT are the best-established methods for
detecting aortic involvement by GCA.
Other imaging techniques have been proposed to assist in the
diagnosis of GCA. Color duplex ultrasonography showed abnormalities of the temporal artery in 28 of 30 patients with GCA
(sensitivity of 93%).

There is growing use of ultrasound, particularly in Europe, to aid
in diagnosis and management of GCA.
Novel ultrasound scoring systems based on the halo sign may also provide prognostic
information, including identification of those with higher risk of
ocular ischemia.
Ultrasound may also help aid in assessment
of exacerbations, with total number of halo segments and maximum temporal artery intima-media thickness correlating with
inflammatory markers and Birmingham Vasculitis Activity score,
though addition of imaging did not outperform standard clinical
measures.
However, the diagnostic value of ultrasonography is
limited by the absence of expertise with this technique at most
medical centers and conflicting estimates of its sensitivity and
specificity.
One meta-analysis of 2036 patients in 23 studies
estimated that the sensitivity and specificity of the halo sign were
69% and 82%, respectively. Another study found that ultrasonography did not improve the diagnostic accuracy of a carefully
performed physical examination.
High-resolution MRI can demonstrate contrast enhancement and mural thickening of superficial cranial arteries in GCA.
Small studies have found a sensitivity of 91% and a specificity
of 73%, compared with temporal artery biopsy.
Best results
have been achieved with 3-T MRI scanners, and 7-T MRI scanners may be even more sensitive.
PET has shown promise
in detecting occult involvement of the aorta and great vessels by
GCA; however, estimates of sensitivity have varied from 65% to
100% and specificity from 77% to 99%.
PET scanning cannot image small arteries that are near highly metabolic tissues such
as the brain, and thus cannot be used to study superficial extracranial arteries. Serial PET scans show active FDG uptake in times of
clinical remission, though those with higher activity as described
by the PET vascular activity score (PETVAS) were more likely to
have exacerbations.
However, other groups have questioned the
utility of the PETVAS for prognostic purposes.
There is suggestion that development of late vascular damage may be predicted
by PET.

GCA despite negative biopsies and imaging. In this case the physician can treat the patient for GCA while watching vigilantly for

Temporal artery biopsy is the “gold standard” for diagnosing
GCA.
Because GCA does not involve the artery in a continuous fashion, temporal artery biopsy should be directed to the

biopsy and can be used to optimize the sample.
Removing a

patients who have palpable abnormalities of the vessel.
Otherwise, the surgeon should try to excise a 4 to 6 cm sample, and the
pathologist should examine multiple sections.
In skilled hands,
temporal artery biopsy is virtually free of morbidity or mortality.
Scalp necrosis can rarely complicate active GCA but has not developed as a consequence of temporal artery biopsy.
Temporal artery biopsies performed at institutions experienced
in treating GCA are sensitive and have a high negative predictive value. At the Mayo Clinic, the sensitivity of temporal artery
biopsy is approximately 90% to 95%, meaning that only 5%
to 10% of patients with negative biopsies will subsequently be
proven (by additional biopsy, angiography, or autopsy) to have
GCA and require corticosteroid therapy.

### Differential Diagnosis

Evidence that PMR and GCA are related and should be considered different manifestations of a common disease process is abundant.
The associations with age, race/ethnicity, geographic

same inflammatory cytokines. Between 30% and 50% of patients

appear to cause vision loss and responds to low doses of glucocorticoids.

### Treatment and Outcome

Most authorities recommend starting glucocorticoid therapy as
soon as the diagnosis of GCA is strongly suspected. The main
goal of treatment is to prevent loss of vision. Because vision loss
is almost always permanent, it seems prudent to initiate corticosteroid therapy as early as possible, even before the biopsy is
performed. Fortunately, the diagnostic yield of temporal artery
biopsy is not altered by corticosteroid therapy for at least 2 weeks,
and perhaps longer.
Glucocorticoid Therapy
Until recently, corticosteroids were considered the initial treatment of choice for GCA. Questions about the previously unchallenged role of corticosteroid therapy emerged in 2017 when
tocilizumab (an inhibitor of IL-6) was approved by the U.S. Food
and Drug Administration for treating GCA. Because of important
unanswered questions about the long-term efficacy of tocilizumab
(see later), corticosteroids currently retain their title as drug of first
choice for the initial treatment of GCA (see
Fig. 89.9
for treatment algorithm).
An initial dose of prednisone 40 to 60 mg/day or equivalent
is adequate in nearly all cases.
Dividing the dose for the first 1
to 2 weeks may accelerate the rate of improvement. If the patient
does not respond promptly, the dose should be increased, no
higher than 1 mg/kg/day. One double-blind, placebo-controlled,
randomized trial involving 27 patients with GCA suggested that
initiating treatment with intravenous (IV) methylprednisolone
(15 mg/kg of ideal weight per day) for 3 days allowed more rapid
tapering of oral corticosteroids and increased the likelihood of
achieving a sustained remission.
The small size of that study
and the possible overreliance on laboratory tests to define relapse
raise questions about the generalizability of these results. Indeed,
a randomized, multicenter, controlled trial of IV pulses of a single
dose of 240 mg of methylprednisolone in the initial treatment
of 164 GCA patients showed no benefit.
High-dose, IV-pulse
methylprednisolone (1000 mg/day) for 3 days has also been tried
in patients with recent loss of vision. Unfortunately, the loss
remains permanent in the vast majority of patients. The occlusive
nature of the vasculitis argues against any role of acute thrombolytic therapy in the treatment of blindness.

activity during the first year.

at which the disease was controlled.

follow-up temporal artery biopsy, four of nine patients biopsied
12 months after beginning corticosteroid therapy had persistent
arterial inflammation.
A minority of patients run a self-limited
course of only several months.
Glucocorticoids can eventually
be reduced and discontinued in some patients. Many patients
require low doses of prednisone for several years or more, often to
control musculoskeletal symptoms.
Tocilizumab
The search for a corticosteroid alternative finally achieved success in 2017 when tocilizumab, a humanized monoclonal antiIL-6 receptor antibody, was approved for GCA in the United
States. The door to treating GCA patients with tocilizumab had
been opened by the discovery that IL-6 was highly expressed in
affected arteries and in the circulation of patients with GCA.
Encouraging results in 24 patients with GCA described in case
reports and small series prompted a phase 3 trial conducted in
251 patients with GCA randomly assigned in double-blind fashion to receive tocilizumab weekly or every other week, along with
prednisone (initially 20 to 60 mg daily) tapered over a period of
either 26 weeks or 52 weeks.
Sustained remission of GCA, the
primary outcome measure, was achieved in 56% of those receiving
weekly tocilizumab and in 53% of those on biweekly tocilizumab,
compared with 14% and 18% of the two placebo groups.
Patients treated with tocilizumab over 52 weeks registered higher
quality of life scores and received nearly half the cumulative dose
of prednisone as those in the placebo groups. Adverse events
occurred at similar frequencies in the tocilizumab- and placebotreated patients; serious adverse advents were more frequent in the
placebo groups.
Long-term follow-up, extended to 3 years, have also shown
good efficacy and safety with tocilizumab in patients with new
and relapsing GCA.
Based on these features, the 2021 ACR/
Vasculitis Foundation guidelines conditionally recommend addition of tocilizumab over glucocorticoid monotherapy.
EULAR
recommendations have suggested use as an adjunct for refractory
or relapsing disease or due to glucocorticoid-associated adverse
effects.
Our practice has favored early addition of tocilizumab
in favor of allowing a more rapid and successful glucocorticoid
taper.
There is a black box warning associated with serious infection
risk with tocilizumab. A history of diverticulitis is a relative contraindication as this can be associated with risk of bowel perforation in patients with inflammatory arthritis.
Neutropenia,
hepatitis, and hyperlipidemia are known complications of tocilizumab and should by monitored at baseline, 4 to 8 weeks after
starting therapy, and then every 3 months afterward. Tuberculosis
and hepatitis screening are necessary before starting therapy.
Optimal glucocorticoid tapering should limit cumulative glucocorticoid exposure while not increasing risk of ischemic complications. Glucocorticoid tapering in the tocilizumab studies,
targeting discontinuation at 26 weeks and 52 weeks, is more rapid
than traditionally used with glucocorticoid monotherapy. One
regimen aims to decrease prednisone to 10 mg/day by week 11 and
then another by week 13 with a plan to discontinue prednisone
by 26 and 52 weeks, respectively.
The treatment of each patient
must be individualized; patients who were unable to adhere to the
fixed glucocorticoid taper were switched to the open-label escape
therapy during this study.
In a proof-of-concept study, 18 patients with newly diagnosed
GCA were treated with IV methylprednisolone (500 mg daily
for 3 days) in addition to IV tocilizumab (8 mg/kg) followed by
weekly subcutaneous tocilizumab with no additional glucocorticoids.
Remission at 24 weeks was high (14 of 18 patients)
with low risk of relapse at 52 weeks (13 of 18). However, three
patients had either persistent cranial or PMR symptoms and two
discontinued tocilizumab due to adverse events. While this small
study opens the possibility of accelerated glucocorticoid tapering,
it is important to note that one patient developed new anterior
ischemic optic neuropathy. Further study is needed to optimize
glucocorticoid tapering.
While tocilizumab has advanced the therapy of GCA, its
efficacy has important limitations.
The possibility that IL-6
inhibitors may suppress the systemic symptoms and signs without
eliminating the vasculitis was suggested by an autopsy in the one
patient treated with tocilizumab who had appeared to respond
and died of other causes, which showed persistent active arteritis.
Other case reports have described active aortitis and dissection in patients on tocilizumab monotherapy.
One hypothesis is
that inflammatory markers are uninterpretable with tocilizumab,
with one study showing that 92% of exacerbations had normal

inflammatory markers.
Given these concerns, it is prudent to
continue monitoring for progression of aortitis while on therapy.
Other or Investigational
The nearly universal experience of serious side effects associated
with daily corticosteroids has prompted the search for alternative
steroid-sparing treatments, without much success until tocilizumab. The combination of weekly low-dose oral methotrexate
and prednisone was steroid sparing in one placebo-controlled,
double-blind treatment trial but not in another and was not associated with fewer adverse effects.
Statins do not appear to modify the course of GCA or the need for corticosteroids.
Delineation of the cells and cytokines involved in the pathogenesis of GCA has invited trials of biologic agents. For example,
the putative role of TNF in propagating a granulomatous vasculitic process suggested that TNF inhibitors might be effective in
treating GCA. Unfortunately, neither infliximab nor etanercept is
effective in GCA.
Other biologic agents might also be effective. Mavrilimumab, a granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor
receptor antagonist, was recently studied in a phase 2 trial in
addition to prednisone and showed high rates of sustained
remission at 26 weeks (82% vs. 50%,
p
= 0.0038).
Patients
in this study who had exacerbations while on mavrilimumab
had elevated inflammatory markers, an important clinical distinction from tocilizumab therapy, though further studies are
needed to determine the efficacy and safety of this medication.
Abatacept, which inhibits T cell activation by blocking the
engagement of CD28, when added to prednisone reduced the rate
of relapse in one small, double-blind, controlled trial.
Abatacept was less likely to induce partial or complete remission when
compared head-to-head with tocilizumab, and patients were more
likely to remain on higher doses of prednisone at 12 months.
In an open-label pilot study, the use of baricitinib, a Janus
kinase (JAK) inhibitor, reduced the risk of GCA relapse (7% at
52 weeks) with most patients (13 of 15) able to discontinue glucocorticoids at the end of the study.
Case reports have also suggested that other JAK inhibitors (e.g., upadacitinib) may also be
efficacious.
A few case reports and small open-label studies have described
apparent responses in GCA patients treated with the antiCD20 monoclonal antibody rituximab to deplete B cells, with
ustekinumab to block IL-12 and IL-23, or with anakinra to
inhibit IL-1 receptors.
Among nonbiologic agents, azathioprine
was glucocorticoid sparing in one small trial. A retrospective study
suggested that dapsone could also be steroid sparing.
Small,
uncontrolled studies have supported the belief that cyclophosphamide may be effective in GCA patients who cannot tolerate
prednisone reduction.
No prospective, randomized, double-blind trials have tested
the potential adjunctive role of aspirin or anti-coagulants in the
treatment of GCA.
However, aspirin is theoretically appealing
because, in experimental models of GCA, it inhibits IFNγ
production more effectively than prednisone.
In addition, three
retrospective studies found that patients with GCA taking lowdose aspirin or anti-coagulant therapy had a three- to five-fold
lower risk of an ischemic event such as vision loss.
Together,
these studies suggest that it is reasonable to administer low-dose
aspirin to patients with GCA who do not have an excessive risk
of gastrointestinal bleeding. Therefore the conditional recommendation for use of aspirin for critical or flow-limiting vertebral or
carotid artery disease is reasonable and appropriate.
Arm claudication from GCA affecting the subclavian and
axillary arteries usually improves or resolves with corticosteroid
therapy. Rare patients with GCA who have severe upper-extremity
claudication unresponsive to corticosteroid therapy may benefit
from balloon angioplasty. In one series of 10 patients, all improved
initially after angioplasty, but symptomatic restenosis developed
in 50% during 2 months.
In all cases, recurrent stenosis developed in vascular lesions that were greater than 3 cm long.
Thoracic aortic aneurysm is greatly increased in patients
with GCA. Although it can be present at the outset, aneurysms
are usually noted late in the disease course, an average of 7
years after onset. Some authorities have recommended annual
chest radiographs or echocardiograms to detect thoracic aortic
aneurysms.
Treatment for Polymyalgia Rheumatica
Patients with PMR without symptoms or signs or biopsy evidence of GCA are usually treated initially with prednisone 10
to 20 mg/day or equivalent.
A systematic review of 30
studies recommended starting treatment with prednisone at
15 mg/day; lower initial doses were less effective and higher
doses were more toxic.
Salicylates and NSAIDs have been
used but are less appealing; salicylates and NSAIDs adequately
control symptoms in only a minority of patients with milder
symptoms and add to overall adverse drug reactions when they
are used with glucocorticoids.
Prednisone therapy usually
results in rapid (often overnight) and dramatic improvement
of the musculoskeletal aching and stiffness and a more gradual
return of the ESR and CRP levels to normal. A minority of
patients with isolated PMR fail to respond to prednisone 20
mg/day after 1 week and may require as much as 30 mg/day as
initial treatment. Studies suggest that these resistant cases are
more likely to have ESRs greater than 50 mm/hr and high levels of IL-6. If a patient does not respond to prednisone 30 mg/
day for 1 week, this should prompt a search for an alternative
diagnosis (
Fig. 89.10
). Lower doses of prednisone might not
suppress an underlying arteritis if it is present. Thus the patient
must be observed carefully even though the aching improves.
In patients with PMR, the dose should be reduced gradually as
soon as symptoms permit. Pre-treatment ESR, CRP, and IL-6
concentrations and initial responses to therapy appear to be
helpful in dividing patients into subsets with different treatment requirements.
If the laboratory test results become
normal while the patient is receiving a smaller dose, the likelihood of an underlying active vasculitis seems to be much less,
and the risk of vascular complications is smaller. However,
this is not true in all instances because active arteritis has been
observed even though the ESR improved.
Once the symptoms, signs, and laboratory abnormalities of
PMR have resolved (usually after 2 to 3 weeks of therapy), the
daily dose of prednisone can be slowly tapered. Some experts recommend tapering prednisone by 2.5 mg every week until 10 mg/
day is reached, at which point the decrements should be reduced
by 1 mg each month.
Flares are common, necessitating a dose
increase to achieve remission before attempting a slower taper. The
minority of patients with PMR succeed in tapering off prednisone in less than 1 year. Many require at least 2 years of low-dose
prednisone.
Some, but not all, studies suggest that oral methotrexate (10
mg once a week for 48 weeks) can reduce the long-term need
for corticosteroids in patients with PMR.

The disease is named for the Japanese ophthalmologist who, in 1908, described a young woman with peculiar retinal arteriovenous anastomoses caused by retinal ischemia

### Epidemiology

Although TAK has been described worldwide, it occurs most
commonly in Japan, China, India, and Southeast Asia. Whereas
the incidence of TAK in Japan is nearly 150 per million per year,
it is only 8 to 25 per million in western Europe and North America.
The prevalence has increased in other parts of the world,
likely due to higher rates of immigration.
TAK affects women
eight times more frequently than men. The median patient age
at onset is 25 years; however, approximately 25% of cases begin
before age 20, and 10% to 20% of patients are seen after age 40
years.
Immunogenetic studies in Japanese patients suggest an
association with several HLAs, especially HLA-Bw52, Dw12,
DR2, and DQw1.
Different HLA associations have been found
in Koreans and Indians. No HLA association has been found in
North American patients. Large genetic studies have identified
several non-HLA loci that are associated with genetic susceptibility based on genome-wide associated level of significance (e.g.,
SVEP1

In Mexican patients,
TAK has been associated with previous exposure to
Mycobacterium tuberculosis

It is possible that the humoral immune system may play some
role in the pathogenesis of TAK. Most patients with TAK possess anti-endothelial cell antibodies that can damage vessels by
inducing endothelial inflammatory cytokine production, adhesion molecules, and apoptosis.
The geographic clustering of cases has suggested that genetics
and environmental factors participate in the pathogenesis of TAK.
However, immunogenetic studies have not identified any other
universally shared genetic risk factors. The young age of onset and
the female predominance in TAK have invited speculation about
the influence of female hormones in promoting an autoimmune
disease process. In some countries, the apparent association of

### Clinical Features

Although the presenting manifestations of TAK are protean,
the vast majority of patients are seen with symptoms and signs
of vascular insufficiency (from stenosis, occlusion, or aneurysm),
systemic inflammation, or both
(
Table 89.9
). In a North
American series of 60 patients followed up at the National Institutes of Health, the most common presenting vascular symptoms
were claudication (35%), reduced or absent pulse (25%), carotid
bruit (20%), hypertension (20%), carotidynia (20%), lightheadedness (20%), and asymmetric arm blood pressures (15%).
Stroke, aortic regurgitation, and visual abnormalities were present
at onset in less than 10% of patients. The extreme manifestations
of retinal ischemia noted in Takayasu’s original patient are rarely
seen now.
Permanent loss of vision, the major concern in GCA,
rarely develops in TAK.
Claudication affects the arms at least twice as frequently as the
legs. Arm claudication first reveals itself as arm pain or fatigue
experienced with use (e.g., while holding a hair dryer). Overall, bruit is the most common sign, eventually found in 80% of
patients. Although bruit over the carotid artery is most frequent,
it can also be found in the supraclavicular, infraclavicular, axillary,
flank, chest, abdominal, and femoral areas. One-third of patients
have multiple bruits.
Unequal arm blood pressures eventually
develop in half of all patients. As noted above for GCA, manual
blood pressure ascertainment is preferred, particularly as electronic recordings have been shown to underestimate systolic blood
pressure and overestimate diastolic blood pressure compared with
intraarterial recordings.
In the presence of bilateral subclavian
stenoses, the only reliable measurement of systemic blood pressure
may come from the lower extremities. Headache, which is common in TAK, does not correlate with carotid or vertebral disease,
which develops in nearly 40% of patients.
Constitutional, musculoskeletal, and other symptoms of systemic inflammation are also common presenting complaints.
About one in five TAK patients presents with fever and malaise,
which can be accompanied by night sweats and weight loss. A few
patients who have minimal or no signs of vascular insufficiency
may appear to have FUO for weeks or months before the diagnosis of TAK becomes evident. A minority of patients present with
myalgia or arthralgia (see
Table 89.9
). Some patients have striking
midthoracic back pain, perhaps as a result of aortic inflammation
irritating nociceptive nerve fibers.
Cardiac involvement occurs eventually in nearly one-third of
patients (see
Table 89.9
).
Aortic regurgitation develops in 20%
to 30% of patients as a result of aortic root dilation, often within
the first few years of diagnosis in patients with slow response to
immunosuppression.
Aortic regurgitation is important because
it frequently progresses and may lead to left-ventricular dilation
with secondary mitral regurgitation and congestive heart failure.
Aortic valve replacement is often required eventually. Angina can
develop as a result of coronary artery disease. TAK of the coronary
arteries most often produces ostial lesions but can also produce
either diffuse vasculitis of the coronary arteries or aneurysms.
Longitudinal evaluation of patients with TAK shows higher risk
of ischemic heart disease, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease
(adjusted hazard ratio 1.9) compared with matched healthy controls.
Myocarditis also occurs in TAK and causes potentially
reversible congestive heart failure. Pericarditis is rare. TAK is,
along with Behçet’s disease, one of the few forms of vasculitis that
can affect the large pulmonary arteries. Although TAK of the pulmonary arteries is rare (appearing in less than 3% of patients),
affected patients can be seen with cough, chest wall pain, dyspnea,
or hemoptysis.
Unlike polyarteritis or GPA, TAK rarely causes peripheral neuropathies. Cutaneous manifestations develop in less than 10% of
patients with TAK.
Erythema nodosum is most common, but
purpura, livedo reticularis, and ulceration may rarely occur. As in
GCA, a minority of patients with TAK who have active disease
have a persistent, dry cough.
Pregnant patients with TAK are at higher risk of gestational
hypertension, pre-eclampsia, and eclampsia.
Low disease activity, tight blood pressure management, correction of renal artery
stenosis, and anti-platelets are important to improve outcomes.
Pregnancy appears to be relatively well tolerated in the presence
of good medical care and in the absence of abdominal aortic
involvement.

Imaging
Vascular abnormalities in TAK can be imaged by conventional
angiography, MRI, MRA, CT angiography, or ultrasonography
(
Figs. 89.11 to 89.13
). Each imaging technique has advantages and disadvantages (
Table 89.10
). The earliest
detectable abnormality in TAK is thickening of the vessel wall
from inflammation. MRI, ultrasonography, and, to a lesser degree,
CT can detect this early vessel wall thickening. The 2018 EULAR
recommendations to prioritize MRI to evaluate for early mural
inflammation and luminal changes provide a useful algorithm for
evaluating patients.
Conventional angiography is invasive and
provides the least sensitive method for visualizing wall thickness.
While considered the “gold standard” for delineating stenoses,
occlusions, and aneurysms, conventional angiography is rarely
used in clinic practice for TAK. However, only conventional angiography allows the direct measurement of central arterial blood
pressure, which may be otherwise unobtainable in patients with
stenotic lesions affecting all four extremities. Although MRA does
not provide the same level of detail as conventional angiography,
it comes close. Because MRA is not invasive and does not involve
ionizing radiation, it has become the preferred imaging method
for assessing the extent of vascular involvement and damage, both
initially and during follow-up. The role of MRI in assessing disease activity is not clear because vessel wall edema and contrast
enhancement have not correlated well with clinical measures of
disease activity.
Although PET scanning would be expected
to be more sensitive than angiography in the early detection of
vascular inflammation that precedes the development of stenoses, its sensitivity may be no greater than MRI. PET scanning has
no established role in following disease activity in TAK. While
PETVAS may have a role in GCA, longitudinal studies show that
PETVAS does not reliably change with treatment in patients with
TAK.
This is not entirely explained by disease duration or therapy and is thought to reflect differences in vascular pathophysiology between these conditions. Indeed, one study of 28 TAK
patients showed that PET scans did not correlate with clinical,
biologic, or MRI assessment of disease activity.
The most common sites of lesions in TAK are the aorta (65%)
and the left subclavian arteries (93%)
(
Table 89.11
). The left
subclavian artery is affected slightly more frequently than the
right. Carotid, renal, and vertebral arteries are also commonly
affected.
Lesions may be stenotic (93%), occluded (57%),
dilated (16%), or aneurysmal (7%). Stenotic lesions are approximately four times more common than aneurysmal lesions.
Stenotic segments often extend a few centimeters and may be
followed by areas of dilation (see
Fig. 89.11
). The majority of
patients (53%) have vascular lesions above and below the diaphragm. However, the frequency distribution of aortic lesions
varies considerably from country to country, with a suggestion
that abdominal aorta and renal involvement is more prevalent in
patients of African descent.

treat TAK, they rarely cure it: relapses are likely when the treatment is stopped.
Tocilizumab, which blocks the IL-6 receptor,
has been effective in individual patients.
However, in
a small cohort of patients (
n
= 18), tocilizumab has no effect on
time-to-relapse compared with placebo.
There are also reports
of disease progression while on tocilizumab, masked by normal
inflammatory markers.
TAK occasionally overlaps with inflammatory bowel disease, and
when one patient with ulcerative colitis was treated with tofacitinib,
there was evidence of vasculitis improvement by PET/CT.
This
led to studies comparing tofacitinib with methotrexate that showed
that complete remission at 6 and 12 months was higher in the
tofacitinib arm, and this was associated with lower relapse rates, longer relapse-free survival, and lower glucocorticoid doses at 3, 6, and
12 months.
A similar study compared tofacitinib to leflunomide
and also showed that the former was associated with high prevalence
of remission and decreased cumulative steroid exposure.
Case reports and small series suggest that other corticosteroid-sparing drugs may include azathioprine (2 mg/kg per
day), mycophenolate mofetil (2000 mg/day), cyclosporine,
tacrolimus, leflunomide, and cyclophosphamide (2 mg/kg per
day).
The toxicity of cyclophosphamide in young
women is so high that it is rarely used in TAK.
Although abatacept (CTLA-4Ig) might be effective in treating GCA, one randomized, double-blind, controlled trial suggested this agent is
ineffective in TAK.
In a subset of patients, TAK will be identified years after the
initial inflammatory phase. These patients may have pulse discrepancies or recall a history of FUO but will not have current
fever, constitutional symptoms, anemia, or elevated inflammatory
markers at time of evaluation. We recommend pursuing vascular
imaging to confirm the diagnosis and to assess for active vascular
disease. If quiescent at time of evaluation, these patients generally do not require corticosteroid or immunosuppressive therapy;
however, longitudinal monitoring for recurrence and subclinical
vascular progression is strongly recommended.
Efforts to prevent osteoporosis should be initiated early (as
described in
Chapter 107
). Modifiable risk factors for atherosclerosis—especially hypertension, smoking, inactivity, diabetes, and
hyperlipidemia—should be treated maximally.
Surgical Therapy
TAK is the form of vasculitis most frequently requiring revascularization procedures.
A review of the experience with
vascular interventions in TAK supports several general recommendations. Medical therapy rarely reduces or reverses stenotic
lesions; however, it is critical to note that the mere presence of
stenosis does not necessitate intervention. The gut, for example, has such rich collaterals that even critical stenoses of the
celiac, superior, or inferior mesenteric arteries usually produce
no symptoms and require no surgical intervention. Moreover,
many patients with arm claudication will develop collateral circulation and improve substantially over time with medical therapy alone. For upper-extremity vascular insufficiency, patiently
waiting for a response to medical therapy usually pays higher
dividends than undertaking rapid surgical intervention. Second,
whenever possible, surgical intervention should be deferred until
TAK is in remission; procedures done during active disease often
produce disappointing results.
Third, bypass surgery yields
better results than angioplasty.
With bypass graft procedures,
autologous vessels give better results than synthetic grafts (restenosis rates of 9% vs. 36%). Patients who undergo aortic surgery
are liable to develop anastomotic aneurysms; such aneurysms
developed in nearly 14% of patients followed for 20 years.
Although angioplasty gives good short-term results, long-term
results are often disappointing except for very short stenotic
segments.
The experience with conventional stents has been
mostly disappointing.

### Outcome

Twenty percent of TAK patients have a self-limited disease. The rest
have a relapsing-remitting or progressive course requiring chronic
corticosteroid and/or immunosuppressive therapy. Nearly twothirds of patients experience new angiographic lesions. In one study
from the National Institutes of Health, 74% of patients experienced
some form of morbidity and 47% were permanently disabled.
In
a Japanese study of 120 patients, survival at 15 years was 83%.
A study of 126 TAK patients in the United States reported 10-year
survival of 97% and 15-year survival of 85%.
Patients with TAK
do have a higher mortality than the general population; the study
estimated the standardized mortality ratio in TAK to be 3.0 (95%
CI, 1.0–8.0).
Age of onset older than 35 years; development of
major complications (i.e., retinopathy, hypertension, aortic regurgitation, and aneurysm); or a progressive course was associated with
decreased survival.
Congestive heart failure and renal failure are
the most common causes of death.
