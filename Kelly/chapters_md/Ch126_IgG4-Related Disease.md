KEY POINTS

> Immunoglobulin G4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is an immune-mediated

> condition capable of affecting any organ of the body. The most

> commonly involved tissues are the major salivary glands, lacrimal

> gland, pancreas, orbits, biliary tree, and retroperitoneum.

> Most patients have elevated serum IgG4 concentrations, often to

> extremely high levels, but there is a wide range of values across

> patients. When elevated at baseline, the serum IgG4 concentration is

> a useful biomarker of disease activity.

> The hallmark pathology features of IgG4-RD are a lymphoplasmacytic

> infiltrate with a high percentage of plasma cells staining for IgG4,

> storiform fibrosis, obliterative phlebitis, and mild to moderate tissue

> eosinophilia. The foundation of the diagnosis rests upon solid

> clinicopathologic correlation.

> ultimately require long-term maintenance therapy or intermittent

> retreatment. Most patients’ disease cannot be controlled sufficiently

> over the long term with tolerable doses of glucocorticoids.

> B cell depletion strategies appear to be effective, perhaps in part

> to T cells. Other therapeutic strategies under investigation include B

> cell modulation efforts and depletion of cells of both the B and T cell

## Introduction

Immunoglobulin G4-related disease (IgG4-RD) is a multiorgan
immune-mediated condition that mimics many malignant, infectious, and inflammatory disorders.
The diagnosis links numerous conditions once regarded as isolated single-organ diseases
that existed outside of any known underlying systemic condition
(
Table 126.1
). IgG4-RD has many similarities to sarcoidosis and
certain forms of systemic vasculitis, being a remarkably protean
condition in which the histopathologic findings are consistent
across a wide range of organ systems. Classification criteria for
IgG4-RD have been developed by the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) and the European Alliance of Associations for
Rheumatology (EULAR).
The growing awareness of IgG4-RD is critical, because this
condition generally responds well to therapy, and the approaches
to the treatment of IgG4-RD differ significantly from those of
its mimics. IgG4-RD has a tendency to form mass lesions and
is therefore often confused with cancer. Moreover, autoimmune
disorders such as Sjögren’s syndrome, granulomatosis with polyangiitis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and idiopathic membranous
nephropathy can be difficult to distinguish from IgG4-RD in
many circumstances. Understanding the immune dysregulation
associated with IgG4-RD has provided important insights into
the processes of fibrosis, B and T lymphocyte cross-talk, and
approaches to antigen identification.

## Epidemiology

Mapping the epidemiology of IgG4-RD has been hampered by
insufficient awareness of this relatively newly recognized disease.
IgG4-RD first crept into the medical literature as a unique entity
only in 2003,
and a reasonable picture of the disease’s epidemiology is only now beginning to emerge. Epidemiologic studies
are hampered by disease underrecognition, frequent difficulties in
confirming the diagnosis even if it is suspected, and the absence
of a unique International Classification of Diagnoses (ICD) code.
Much of the early understanding of the epidemiology of IgG4-
RD was based on cohort studies from tertiary care centers. These
reported consistently that the disease most often affects individuals in the fifth through the seventh decades of life but noted that
cases are observed across the full spectrum of age, from toddlers to
nonagenarians. Case series also suggested that IgG4-RD was characterized by a male predominance but that the sex distribution
varies substantially, affecting different organs.
The male predilection for most organ systems and for the disease overall contrasts
with the classic autoimmune diseases. Nevertheless, women can
clearly manifest the full panoply of organ system presentations.
Investigation of a large insurance claims database recently
yielded important observations about the epidemiology of IgG4-
RD in the United States.
Two-thirds of the patients were white,
consistent with the clinical observation that the disease occurs
indiscriminately across the range of human races and ethnicities. The mean age was 56.5 years, and surprisingly, 58% were
female. During the period of observation covered by the study
(2015 to 2019), the incidence of IgG4-RD increased from 0.78
to 1.39 per 100,000 person-years, with an average annual incidence rate of IgG4-RD during that period of 1.20 per 100,000
person-years. This estimate is likely conservative, however, because
the incidence of IgG4-RD appears to be growing substantially—
nearly doubling over the 4-year period of the study. The increasing
incidence is likely attributable to broader awareness in the medical community. The point prevalence on January 1, 2019, was
5.3/100,000 persons. The data from this study overall suggest that
the incidence and prevalence of IgG4-RD are similar to those of
anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody-associated vasculitis (AAV)
and systemic sclerosis.

IgG4-RD is associated with a significant increase in mortality

2.51 (95% confidence interval [CI], 1.76 to 3.56), a figure that

A substantial portion of this increased mortality risk in IgG4-RD appears
to be related to the fact that glucocorticoids remain the first line
of treatment.

Detailed genetic studies with sufficient power have not

## Classification Criteria

The ACR and EULAR classification criteria for IgG4-RD, published in 2020, have a sensitivity of 86% and a specificity of 99%.
There are three steps to classification. First, a potential IgG4-RD
case must fulfill initial entry criteria, requiring the disease involvement in at least one of eleven typical organs. These organs include
the pancreas, bile ducts, orbits, lacrimal glands, major salivary
glands, lungs, kidneys, aorta, retroperitoneum, pachymeninges,
and thyroid gland (Riedel’s thyroiditis).
Second, the potential case is evaluated for the presence of exclusion criteria (
Table 126.2
). The exclusion criteria consist of a total
of 32 clinical, serologic, radiologic, and pathologic items that, if
present, eliminate the patient from IgG4-RD classification.

## Pathology Features

### IgG4-RD as a Variable-Vessel Vasculitis

Substantial evidence now exists that IgG4-RD should be regarded
as a primary form of vasculitis. IgG4-RD has a remarkable capability to affect blood vessels of nearly any type. In fact, IgG4-RD is
among the most versatile forms of vasculitis known. IgG4-RD is a
variable-vessel vasculitis that can affect blood vessels as large as the
aorta and as small as post-capillary venules, if not the capillaries
themselves.
The most commonly described pathology manifestation of the vascular disease in IgG4-RD is obliterative phlebitis,
a microscopic finding that is a widely recognized pathology hallmark of this condition (discussed further below). Furthermore,
the well-known clinical features of small-vessel vasculitis, which
typically present as cutaneous leukocytoclastic vasculitis, are also
reported.
IgG4-RD can also affect the aorta in the manner of other largevessel vasculitides such as giant cell arteritis and Takayasu’s arteritis.
In addition, IgG4-RD frequently leads to a “periaortitis,”
particularly in the abdominal aorta, which often accompanies retroperitoneal fibrosis.
IgG4-RD is capable of affecting medium-sized blood vessels, as
well, particularly the coronary arteries.
Coronary artery involvement in IgG4-RD can lead to wall thickening, periarterial soft
tissue encasement, luminal stenosis, arterial wall calcification,
and coronary aneurysms or ectasia. Coronary artery aneurysms,
including focal fusiform aneurysms and diffuse vascular ectasia,
occur commonly (
Fig. 126.1
).
No other known form of vasculitis affects the coronary arteries of adults in this manner. The
closest disease analogy is to IgG4-RD in this respect is Kawasaki’s
disease, which occurs essentially exclusively in children under the
age of 5.

### Morphologic Change of Affected Organs

Transformations in the gross pathology of affected organs occur in
IgG4-RD. The pancreas and kidneys become diffusely enlarged.
In contrast, ductal organs (e.g., bile duct, bronchus) assume the
appearance of a pipe stem, with diffuse wall thickening.

## Serum IgG4 Concentrations

Serum IgG4 measurements play an important role in the diagnosis and management of IgG4-RD, but the relevance of IgG4 levels
must be considered from several angles.

### Dramatic IgG4 Elevations

A serum IgG4 level is often the first diagnostic test ordered
when IgG4-RD is suspected. Large elevations of serum IgG4
concentrations (e.g., on the order of six to eight times the
upper limit of normal for a given assay) strongly suggest the
diagnosis. Nevertheless, a very high serum IgG4 concentration is not diagnostic of IgG4-RD. We queried the data
warehouses at two large academic medical centers—Stanford
University and Mass General Brigham—to examine the positive predictive value (PPV) of a serum IgG4 concentration
≥
5 times the upper limit of normal.
A diagnosis of IgG4-RD
was determined using the ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria
for IgG4-RD.
The study reported a PPV of 73% for IgG4-
RD among patients with elevations of serum IgG4 at least five
times the upper limit of normal. Although these data support
the significant weight placed on very elevated serum IgG4 in
the classification criteria,
it is notable that 27% of patients
with such high IgG4 concentrations had either an alternative
diagnosis to IgG4-RD or no identifiable cause of the serum
IgG4 elevation. This underscores the importance of the classification criteria’s entry and exclusion criteria when evaluating
a patient with a very high IgG4 concentration for IgG4-RD.
Patients with multiorgan disease can have dramatic elevations
in serum IgG4 concentrations, occasionally exceeding 4 or 5 g/dL.
In such cases, the prozone effect may lead to spuriously low IgG4
measurements in nephelometry assays, requiring additional dilutions of a patient’s sample.
Patients with IgG4-RD also often
have elevations of other IgG subclasses (particularly IgG1, IgG2,

and IgG3).
The elevations of serum IgG4 concentrations, however, are usually disproportionate to those of the other subclasses.

### IgG4: The Tissue/Serum Paradox

Despite the frequently striking serum IgG4 concentrations
observed in patients with this disease, an important paradox exists.
Among patients with active disease whose cases are identified on
the basis of histopathology rather than by elevations in serum
IgG4 concentrations, just over half of the patients have elevated
serum IgG4 concentrations before treatment.
In contrast, when
cases were sought by querying a hospital laboratory database
about the number of patients with abnormally high serum IgG4
measurements, the sensitivity of an elevated serum IgG4 concentration was 90%.
Two major points should be drawn from these studies. First,
a large proportion of patients with biopsy-proven, clinically validated IgG4-RD have normal serum IgG4 concentrations, even
before beginning treatment. Reliance upon serum IgG4 concentration elevations for diagnosis, therefore, leads to dangerous
underdiagnosis. Second, among patients with elevated serum
IgG4 levels, the range of values is extremely broad. The factors
contributing to such variability in the IgG4 response remain
poorly defined, but patients with the involvement of multiple
organs tend to have the highest blood concentrations of IgG4.

### IgG4 as a Biomarker of Disease Activity

The relationship between serum IgG4 concentrations and disease
activity in many patients—assuming that the serum IgG4 value
is elevated at baseline—is strong enough in many patients to be a
valuable disease biomarker. The relationship between serum IgG4
and disease activity is not, however, perfect. In a large retrospective study of type 1 autoimmune pancreatitis from 17 centers in
Japan,
serum IgG4 levels declined in all patients after treatment
with glucocorticoids but remained above the upper limit of normal in 63% of the patients despite good clinical responses. Of the
patients who relapsed, 30% had normal serum IgG4 levels at the
time of relapse. Thus serum IgG4 concentrations should not be
relied upon exclusively as an indicator of disease activity. Nevertheless, if a patient has a large serum IgG4 elevation at baseline,
serial measurement is likely to be extremely useful as a biomarker
of disease activity and a predictor of disease flare, particularly if
patients are not continued on a remission maintenance regimen
of glucocorticoids.

### Serum IgG4 in Other Conditions

Mild IgG4 elevations are common across a variety of conditions
that can mimic IgG4-RD. Vasculitides such as granulomatosis
with polyangiitis and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, bronchiectasis, biliary diseases such as primary sclerosing
cholangitis, pancreatic cancer, and multiple others can have confoundingly high serum IgG4 concentrations, although they rarely,
if ever, reach the highest values achieved by some patients with
IgG4-RD. This imposes additional limitations on the diagnostic
interpretation of an elevated serum IgG4; namely, false-positive
elevations of serum IgG4 are common.
In summary, substantial elevations in serum IgG4 levels are
useful in suggesting the diagnosis of IgG4-RD but are not diagnostic in and of themselves. A large minority of patients with histopathologically confirmed disease—on the order of 40%—have
normal serum IgG4 concentrations. The finding of normal serum
IgG4 concentrations is particularly high within the subset of
patients with primarily fibrotic disease.
Patients with RPF, Riedel’s thyroiditis, sclerosing mesenteritis, and fibrosing mesenteritis
are emblematic of this subset.

## Pathophysiology

Most features of the current understanding of the pathophysiology of IgG4-RD remain highly theoretical, but the field is
changing quickly. The theory that IgG4 itself is a crucial player
in disease etiology, which held sway for many years after the first
recognition of the elevated serum IgG4 concentrations in “sclerosing pancreatitis,” has been debunked.

### Is the IgG4 Molecule Central to Disease?

Many patients with IgG4-RD have striking elevations of serum
IgG4 concentrations, generally well out of proportion to those
observed in other IgG subclasses. In addition, except for advanced
cases characterized primarily by fibrotic lesions, IgG4
plasma cells
are nearly always present in abundance at sites of disease. The total
number of IgG4
plasma cells/hpf and the ratio of IgG4
to total
IgG
plasma cells in tissue provide information that often bear
heavily on the diagnosis. Substantial elevations of plasmablasts
and other B cell subsets also occur in IgG4-RD.
Consequently,
the IgG4 antibody and humoral immunity have been emphasized
most prominently in discussions of pathophysiology thus far.
Despite the tight link of “IgG4” to the name of this disease,
IgG4 itself is unlikely to drive the pathogenesis of this condition.
The subclasses of IgG, defined by the sequences of their heavy
chain constant domains, differ from each other in important ways.
IgG4, normally the least abundant of all of the IgG subclasses,
accounts for approximately 4% of the total immunoglobulins
among healthy individuals. The IgG4 molecule has unique chemical properties that render it unlikely to play a central inflammatory
role. IgG4 demonstrates weak binding to C1q and to Fcγ
receptors because of a critical few amino acid differences in its CH2
domain.
As a result, the ability of IgG4 to activate the classic
complement pathway and participate in antibody-dependent cellmediated cytotoxicity is attenuated substantially in comparison
with the capabilities of IgG1.
Another unique feature of IgG4 is its ability to form “half
antibodies” through the process of Fab arm exchange. Fab arm
exchanges result in the formation of IgG molecules with two different binding specificities.
Amino acid variation at the hinge
region of IgG4 permits the reduction of the disulfide bonds that
join the two halves of an IgG4 molecule. Recombination of the
dissociated arms leads to the random formation of “asymmetric
antibodies” composed of half-antibody fragments directed against
different antigens.
The consequence is a reduced ability to
cross-link antigens and form immune complexes.
In some circumstances, it is conceivable that the ability of IgG4
to activate complement is greater than expected, and the molecule
is capable of participating in immune complex–associated tissue
injury. IgG4 deposition has been found, in fact, within the kidneys of patients with IgG4-related tubulointerstitial nephritis.

particularly strong when serum IgG4 concentrations are dramatically elevated. Such immune complex–mediated injury, however,

### B Cell Lineage

Cells of that B lineage clearly play important roles in IgG4-RD.
This is evident by, among other observations, the swift clinical
responses of patients to B cell depletion.
In addition, active,
untreated IgG4-RD is characterized by high blood concentrations
of plasmablasts and other types of B cell subsets.
Somatic hypermutation is evident in the circulating plasmablasts in IgG4-RD,
consistent with robust interactions of these cells and their precursors with T cells. B lymphocytes play multiple roles in IgG4-RD,
including cytokine production and direct contributions to the
processes of fibrosis in this disease.
Most importantly, perhaps, B cell subsets play important roles in the presentation of
antigen(s) to T cells. Antigen presentation by B cells to specific T
cells, particularly a CD4
cytotoxic T cell, represents one of the
key cellular interactions central in this disease.

### CD4 Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes

The presence of oligoclonal expansions of CD4
cytotoxic T
lymphocytes in both the peripheral blood and fibrotic lesions in
IgG4-RD imply a central pathogenetic role for these cells.
These
cells upregulate granzymes, granulysin, perforin, and SLAM family proteins. They also elaborate interleukin-1, interferonγ
, and
TGFβ
, all of which may be important mediators of the fibrosis
observed in this disease. Both plasmablasts and CD4
cytotoxic
T lymphocyte cells (CTLs) in the circulation decline following
induction therapy with rituximab,
confirming the important
linkage of B cells with CD4
CTLs in IgG4-RD. B cell depletion
therefore has effects beyond B cells in tissue, reducing the number
of infiltrating CD4
CTLs, as well as the numbers of activated
myofibroblasts.

### Identification of Potential Autoantigens

A plethora of autoantigens and autoantibodies are associated with
IgG4-RD. Such putative autoantigens include carbonic anhydrase, plasminogen-binding protein, lactoferrin, pancreatic secretory trypsin inhibitor, amylase
α
-2A, trypsinogen, annexin-A11,
prohibitin, and laminin-511.
Most such findings, however,
have suffered from lack of external validation.
Galectin-3 was identified recently as a major potential autoantigen in IgG4-RD.
The approach involved the use of single-cell
sequencing and cloning of paired heavy and light immunoglobulin
chains.
Anti–galectin-3 autoantibodies were found in approximately 30% of a cohort of 121 IgG4-RD patients with multiple
organ involvement. The authors then demonstrated that the anti–
galectin-3 autoantibodies were restricted to the IgG4 and IgE isotypes, consistent with the idea that plasmablasts from IgG4-RD
patients can secrete either IgG4 or IgE. Moreover, the presence
of these autoantibodies correlated strongly with elevated plasma
levels of galectin-3, suggesting a breach of tolerance driven by the
accumulation of this self-protein.

### Thoracic Aorta, Branches of the Aorta, and Coronary Lesions

IgG4-related aortitis, which often is suspected on the basis of an
incidental radiologic finding, is also sometimes an unexpected
finding at surgery. IgG4-related aortitis can lead to aneurysms
or dissections in the thoracic aorta.
In contrast to giant cell
and Takayasu’s arteritis, which have a predilection for the primary
aortic branches—particularly the subclavian arteries—IgG4-RD
tends to spare these vessels, at least in terms of clinical manifestations. Small case series substantiate the concept that mediumsized blood vessels can also be affected by IgG4-RD, but definitive
histopathologic investigations of primary aortic branch vessels
have not been undertaken. As discussed earlier, however, coronary
artery lesions in IgG4-RD, sometimes associated with aneurysm
formation, are well-documented.

### Chronic Periaortitis and Retroperitoneal Fibrosis

Idiopathic RPF is now considered to belong to a larger disease
spectrum now known as chronic periaortitis. The three major
components of chronic periaortitis are IgG4-related RPF (

IgG4-RD is responsible for up to twothirds of cases of idiopathic RPF.
Some cases of idiopathic

have similar presentations and can be distinguished from IgG4-
RD through careful histopathologic and immunostaining studies.
Common presentations of IgG4-related RPF and chronic periaortitis are poorly localized pain in the back, flanks, lower abdomen, or thighs; lower extremity edema; and hydronephrosis from
ureteral involvement. These presentations are generally subtle
at first, subacute in onset, and nonspecific, leading to diagnostic
delay. The disease targets three sites: (1) periaortic/arterial regions,
involving connective tissue around the abdominal aorta or its first
branches; (2) periureteral areas, leading to ureteral obstruction and
hydronephrosis; and (3) a plaque-like mass that broadly involves the
retroperitoneum. The classic radiologic presentation of IgG4-RD
is a periaortic lesion beginning in the infrarenal region, extending
inferiorly to involve the iliac arteries. The ureters course close to the
lower aorta and iliac arteries on their way to the bladder and often
become entrapped by the periaortic inflammation.

tissue may be more helpful diagnostically than the overall number of IgG4
plasma cells/hpf in patients with advanced fibrotic
disease.

### Lung

The greatest diversity of clinical and radiologic presentations
occurs in the lung. Thickening of the bronchovascular bundle,
best appreciated on CT examination, underscores the tendency of
IgG4-RD to track along bronchi and blood vessels, which course
together. Other radiologic features of IgG4-RD include pulmonary nodules, ground-glass opacities, pleural thickening, and interstitial lung disease. The interstitial lung disease mimics nonspecific
interstitial pneumonitis and other forms of interstitial fibrosis

### Kidney

Tubulointerstitial nephritis (TIN) is the most characteristic form
of IgG4-related kidney disease. IgG4-related TIN recapitulates
the histopathology found in other organs.
IgG4-related TIN
is often distinguished from other organ manifestations of IgG4-
RD by profound hypocomplementemia. The basis of this hypocomplementemia remains poorly understood, but one potential
explanation involves the formation of immune complexes containing IgG1 or IgG3, as well as complement components. IgG1
and IgG3 generally bind complement more effectively than IgG4
does; however, it is possible that under some pathologic conditions, IgG4 is also able to bind complement. Immune complexes
involving a variety of IgG subclasses (including IgG4) and different complement components have been identified by electron
microscopy. The locations of these immune complexes within
the renal tubules is particularly characteristic of IgG4-RD, but
not diagnostic (they can also be present, for example, in lupus
nephritis).
CT scanning in IgG4-related TIN can reveal substantial renal
enlargement and hypodense lesions within the renal parenchyma.
Advanced renal dysfunction and even end-stage renal disease can
result. Proteinuria can develop, but the levels are usually subnephrotic. Kidneys affected by IgG4-RD can undergo atrophy, even
in the setting of good clinical responses to therapy.
Membranous glomerulonephropathy also occurs in IgG4-RD,
but the pathophysiology of this entity appears to have a pathophysiology different from that of IgG4-related TIN.
The antiphospholipase A
(PLA

idiopathic membranous glomerulonephropathy is not associated
with IgG4-related membranous glomerulonephropathy.
It is

IgG4-related membranous
glomerulonephropathy is less common than TIN, but sometimes
the lesions occur together.

### Pancreas

The pancreas was the first organ recognized to have an association
with elevated serum IgG4 concentrations.
Two AIP subtypes
exist, only one of which (type 1) is associated with IgG4-RD. Type 1
AIP demonstrates the classic histopathologic findings of lymphoplasmacytic sclerosing pancreatitis. The histologic features of type
2 AIP, which is less common than type 1, include neutrophilic
infiltration into the pancreatic duct epithelium.
The abdominal pain associated with IgG4-related AIP spans
a broad range from a subtle, relatively asymptomatic presentation reminiscent of indigestion to substantial abdominal pain that
mimics pancreatitis of other etiologies. In general, however, the
abdominal pain of IgG4-related AIP is less severe than that associated, for example, with alcohol-induced pancreatitis. Perhaps
because of the relatively mild abdominal symptoms accompanying
IgG4-related AIP, a common clinical presentation is obstructive
jaundice, which leads to concern for pancreatic adenocarcinoma.
Unfortunately, many IgG4-RD patients have undergone modified
Whipple procedures to resect what is presumed to be adenocarcinoma of the pancreas before the correct diagnosis is recognized.
Damage resulting from IgG4-related AIP is substantial. Secondary diabetes mellitus occurs in approximately half of all cases,
and IgG4-RD clearly causes a subset of diabetes mellitus cases
in individuals who have no overt risk factors for diabetes. Even
more common than endocrine failure of the pancreas, however, is
exocrine failure. Pancreatic atrophy and diminished ability of the
organ to produce digestive enzymes lead to malabsorption, malnutrition, and often dramatic weight loss (e.g., up to 50 pounds).
The degree of exocrine pancreatic dysfunction can be quantified
through measurements of elastase in stool samples. Oral replacement of pancreatic enzymes with meals can reverse the weight
loss caused by pancreatic damage. Pancreatic stones occur with
increased frequency among patients with type 1 AIP.
The nearly diagnostic CT features of AIP include diffuse pancreatic enlargement with delayed enhancement and a capsule-like
low-density rim (

for AIP. Endoscopic ultrasonography-guided fine-needle aspiration is a useful diagnostic approach to excluding pancreatic cancer. Empiric glucocorticoid trials of 2 to 4 weeks’ duration are also
considered in some patients with pancreatic masses in whom AIP
is considered because of the excellent response to glucocorticoids
shown by most IgG4-RD patients.

### IgG4-Related Sclerosing Cholangitis and Cholecystitis

IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis frequently occurs with type 1
AIP. The histology of IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis includes
obliterative phlebitis and transmural fibrosis with dense infiltration of IgG4
plasma cells and T cells. IgG4-related sclerosing
cholangitis must be differentiated from both primary sclerosing
cholangitis and hilar cholangiocarcinoma. Neither serum IgG4
concentrations nor cholangiographic or cholangioscopic findings
differentiate these diseases from each other with clarity. Thus an
endoscopic transpapillary biopsy is usually required. Although
cholangiocarcinoma can be excluded by endoscopic biopsy, the
superficial nature of endoscopic biopsies limits their utility for
diagnosing IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis. In such cases,
serum IgG4 concentration elevations (if particularly high) and
searches for extrahepatic biliary disease can often lead to the correct diagnosis.
IgG4-related cholecystitis sometimes occurs with IgG4-related
sclerosing cholangitis. Thickening of the gallbladder wall is
detected on imaging, but it is usually asymptomatic.

### Other Manifestations

Sclerosing lesions of both the mesentery and mediastinum are
described. Sclerosing mesenteritis often appears to originate at
the mesenteric root. The ensuing process often merges imperceptibly with RPF and can evolve in a slow but devastating manner, encasing vital organs and blood vessels and obviating any
attempt at surgical resection. Fibrosing mediastinitis can lead to
the compression of vital mediastinal structures from proliferation
of invasive fibrous tissue. The relationship between these fibrosing mediastinitis cases and antecedent histoplasmosis infections,
if any, remains unclear.
Several clinical presentations of IgG4-related skin disease have
occurred. The most common cutaneous manifestation is the presence of erythematous papules. These lesions typically affect the
region of the head and neck but have also been described on the
trunk and extremities. Among individuals with darkly pigmented
skin, hyperpigmented lesions have been observed.
Biopsy-proven IgG4-related prostatic disease leading to prostatic enlargement has been reported.
The diagnosis of IgG4-
related prostate disease is often made presumptively when the
initiation of treatment for IgG4-RD in other organs mediates
abrupt symptomatic relief of “benign prostatic hypertrophy.”

avoided with close follow-up. Laboratory surveillance in the setting of watchful waiting should include not only IgG subclasses

eosinophil count or percentage, serum chemistries, IgE, and C3
and C4. Intermittent imaging may also be important for some
disease manifestations, e.g., RPF.
Remission Induction
Glucocorticoids remain the cornerstone of treatment for most
patients with IgG4-RD, and patients typically respond well to this
intervention. The failure of a patient to respond to an appropriate
course of glucocorticoid treatment urges reconsideration of the
diagnosis. IgG4-RD usually demonstrates signs of improvement
within days to several weeks of beginning treatment, depending
on the type of organs involved. This is particularly true of the
proliferative subset of the disease.
The goal of induction therapy often is to discontinue glucocorticoids within 2.5 to 4 months, but treatment strategies
vary across countries.
The tendency at some centers, particularly those in Asia, is to maintain patients on daily prednisone doses of 5 to 10 mg for several years once remission has
been achieved. Shorter glucocorticoid courses are preferred at
other centers, leading to complete cessation and the use of a
“steroid-sparing” therapy (e.g., mycophenolate mofetil) or a
biologic agent (B cell depletion) if prednisone monotherapy
fails. Anecdotal evidence supports the efficacy of conventional
disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) in certain
settings, but in general B cell depletion is viewed as being more
effective.
Glucocorticoid Treatment
Patients with multiorgan disease and high serum IgG4 concentrations respond well to a starting dose of prednisone of 0.6 mg/
kg/day (30 to 40 mg/day), and this is often recommended for
IgG4-RD. The starting dose of prednisone, usually maintained
for 2 to 4 weeks, can be tapered thereafter through a variety of
regimens designed to discontinue prednisone within 10 to 16
weeks, guided by the rapidity of clinical improvement on physical examination, improvement in laboratory biomarkers, and
imaging changes.
Unfortunately, the impact of glucocorticoids in IgG4-RD is
generally only temporary. Patients with multiorgan disease and
high serum IgG4 concentrations at baseline are unlikely to achieve
sustained remissions with limited courses of glucocorticoids. Once
the failure of prednisone to induce long-term remission is evident,
these patients require either lengthy glucocorticoid courses, placing them at risk for treatment-induced toxicity, or the early use of
a biologic agent (preferably B cell depletion) or another treatment
approach. The reality that IgG4-RD frequently leads to substantial pancreatic damage, placing patients at risk for glucose intolerance, combined with demographic profile of the typical patient
with IgG4-RD—individuals who are middle-aged to elderly and
often have comorbidities such as diabetes, obesity, osteoporosis,
and hypertension—is a major contraindication to prolonged glucocorticoid therapy.

Biologic Agents
Treatment strategies targeting cells of both the B and T lymphocyte lineages are logical approaches to the treatment of IgG4-RD.
Given the multiple arms of the immune system involved in the
disease, however, they are almost certainly not the only ones worthy of consideration.
Abatacept (anti–CTLA4-Ig), which interferes with T cell
co-stimulation, showed modest evidence of efficacy in an openlabel trial of 10 patients.
This medication appears to be most
effective in patients with mild disease but is probably not optimal
in the absence of glucocorticoids, particularly for patients with
multiorgan disease.
Most work to date has been done in B cell depletion.
B
cell depletion likely operates by interrupting antigen presentation
to CD4
CTLs, among other mechanisms. The concentration of
CD4
CTLs in both peripheral blood and disease-affected tissue
declines following targeted B cell depletion.
B cell depletion
also reduces the number of infiltrating activated myofibroblasts,
thereby decreasing tissue fibrosis.
IgG4-RD patients may be
prone to tissue injury resulting from immune complex deposition. In theory, this may be ameliorated potentially by reducing
immunoglobulin concentrations.
B cell depletion must be readministered at varying frequency
in order to maintain efficacy. Regular retreatment with B cell–
depleting agents such as anti-CD20 (rituximab) and anti-CD19
(inebilizumab) is likely not required to maintain disease remission, albeit retreatment at some interval—probably unique to
each patient—is necessary for most. Signs of efficacy, often swift
and substantial, were observed in 29 of 30 patients treated with
a remission induction regimen of rituximab (two 1-gram doses
administered 2 weeks apart).
Of these patients, 77% achieved
the primary outcome of disease remission off of prednisone at 6
months, but disease relapses had occurred in many individuals by
1 year. Longitudinal measurement of serum IgG4 concentrations
is a useful gauge of when readministration of B cell depletion may
be indicated.
Investigational Treatment Approaches
A phase II trial of obexelimab, a monoclonal antibody directed
against both CD19 and Fcγ
RIIb, demonstrated positive treatment responses in 12 of 15 patients.
Disease control was established without glucocorticoid use in most subjects. A phase III
trial of obexelimab is now under way.
Inebilizumab, a monoclonal antibody directed against CD19
that has obtained regulatory approval in neuromyelitis optica,
offers another potential B cell depletion strategy that is now at
an advanced stage of testing in a worldwide, multicenter clinical
trial.
Finally, SLAM-F7 is a highly appealing drug target because
it is present on both CD4
CTLs and B cells. A proof-of-concept
trial with the anti-SLAM7 monoclonal antibody elotuzumab,
used to treat relapsed multiple myeloma, has begun.
