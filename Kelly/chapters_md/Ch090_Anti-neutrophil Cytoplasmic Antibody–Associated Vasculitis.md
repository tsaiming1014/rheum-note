# Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody–Associated Vasculitis

SHARON A. CHUNG AND PAUL A. MONACH

KEY POINTS

> The anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–associated

> vasculitides are granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), microscopic

> polyangiitis (MPA), and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis

> (EGPA). They affect small- and medium-sized vessels and share

> clinical, pathologic, and diagnostic features.

> ANCA testing, especially by enzyme-linked immunoassay, is useful to

> diagnose ANCA-associated vasculitides in the appropriate clinical

> context, but its utility in assessing disease activity is unclear.

> GPA is characterized histologically by necrotizing granulomatous

> inflammation in addition to vasculitis. Common clinical

> manifestations include destructive sinonasal lesions, pulmonary

> nodules, and pauci-immune glomerulonephritis (GN). GPA is

> most commonly associated with cytoplasmic-staining ANCA and

> antibodies to proteinase 3.

> MPA is characterized histologically by vasculitis without granulomatous

> inflammation. Common clinical manifestations include rapidly

> progressive pauci-immune GN (which can be the only organ system

> affected clinically) and alveolar hemorrhage. MPA is most commonly

> associated with perinuclear staining ANCA and antibodies to

> myeloperoxidase.

> EGPA is characterized histologically by eosinophilic tissue infiltration

> in addition to vasculitis. Common clinical manifestations include

> asthma and peripheral eosinophilia. Only 40% of patients produce

> detectable ANCA.

> For patients with organ- or life-threatening disease, combination

> therapy (with either glucocorticoids and/or avacopan and either

> rituximab or cyclophosphamide) is needed to induce remission.

> Remission can often be maintained with less toxic medications such

> as methotrexate, azathioprine, and lower doses of rituximab. With

> current treatment regimens, disease remission is achieved in 75%

> to 93% of patients.

## Introduction

The anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–associated
vasculitides (AAV) target small- to medium-sized vessels in multiple organ systems, with sinonasal, pulmonary, and renal involvement being particularly common. Specific diseases included in this
category are granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), microscopic
polyangiitis (MPA; including disease limited to the kidney), and
eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA; also known
as Churg-Strauss syndrome). These diseases are categorized
together, given their association with antibodies directed against
antigens in the cytoplasm of neutrophils—proteinase 3 (PR3) and
myeloperoxidase (MPO)—and many overlapping clinical manifestations, diagnostic testing, and treatment strategies.

## Classification Criteria and Nomenclature

To differentiate the various vasculitic syndromes, classification
criteria and disease definitions have been proposed by multiple
groups. The developers stress that classification criteria are not
intended to be used as diagnostic criteria in a general population
and that definitions serve as a consensus group of conditions for
which classification (and ideally diagnostic) criteria could be developed. Revised classification criteria for GPA, MPA, and EGPA,
developed in a collaboration between the American College of
Rheumatology (ACR) and the European Alliance of Associations
for Rheumatology (EAAR; formerly EULAR) were published in
2022 and are expected to be the standard for enrolling patients in
research studies.
Previously, the most widely used criteria were
ACR classification criteria endorsed in 1990, before there was
widespread ANCA testing or a consensus definition of MPA.

To better define the nomenclature of the systemic vasculitides, the Chapel Hill Consensus Conference (CHCC) was first
convened in 1994 to reach consensus on the names for the most
common forms of systemic vasculitis. Vasculitides were categorized into large-, medium-, and small-vessel groups. This conference formally defined MPA for the first time and differentiated
it from classic polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) by defining MPA as a
pauci-immune necrotizing vasculitis affecting small vessels, with
or without medium-vessel involvement. PAN was defined as an
arteritis of medium-sized and small vessels without involvement
of the arterioles, venules, or capillaries (including glomerular capillaries).
The CHCC nomenclature system was revised in 2012
and recommended a shift from using eponyms to noneponymous
replacements. Other changes include the addition of variable vessel and single-organ vasculitis, as well as vasculitis associated with
systemic disease and vasculitis associated with probable etiology
(e.g., vasculitis associated with an infection, drug, or malignancy).

have studied populations of European descent. These studies suggest an overall incidence of approximately 10 to 30 individuals

AAVs appear to be more common in populations of European

GPA and MPA are thought to be rare in people of

Additional study

The numbers of patients diagnosed with AAV have increased
since the 1990s. Between 1994 and 2006 in Germany, the prevalence of GPA increased from 58 to 98 cases per million; MPA,

from 9 to 28 cases per million; and EGPA, from 7 to 24 cases
per million.
In the United Kingdom (UK), the prevalence of
GPA increased from 28.8 per million in 1990 to 64.8 per million in 2005.
This increase likely reflects increased awareness and
diagnosis after the development of the ACR classification criteria,
CHCC definitions, and ANCA testing. In this setting, it is difficult to determine whether the incidence of a disease is changing.
In addition, improved survival increases point prevalence.
In European populations, GPA is the most prevalent AAV. The
incidence of GPA ranges from 4.9 to 10.5 cases per million per
year. The reported prevalences have ranged from 24 to 157 per
million, with the highest reported in Sweden and the UK.
Variation in incidence and prevalence between countries has suggested that disease risk declines in a north–south gradient in the
Northern Hemisphere.
The incidence of MPA among European populations ranges
from 0.5 to 11.6 cases per million per year. Unlike GPA, the highest incidence of MPA seems to occur in southern Europe, specifically in Greece and Spain. MPA is also more common in certain
non-European populations, with an incidence of 14.8 cases per
million per year in Japan and 24.0 cases in Kuwait, the highest
reported incidence thus far.
The prevalence of MPA ranges
from 0 to 66 cases per million among European countries.

UK are cytoplasmic-staining (c-ANCA) or PR3-ANCA positive.
As suggested from the serologic differences, MPA is more common in Japan (occurring in 83% of patients with AAV), whereas
GPA is more common in the UK (66%). Compared with patients
in the UK who have GPA, renal involvement is less common in
Japan.
In China, population-based estimates of disease prevalence and incidence have not been defined, although a survey of

AAV.

EGPA is the least prevalent of the AAV. Among European
populations, the incidence of EGPA ranges from 0.5 to 6.8 cases
per million individuals.

The overall prevalence

from 2 to 38 cases per million.
A sex predominance has not
been clearly demonstrated. In Japan, the prevalence of EGPA has
been estimated to be 17.8 cases per million, with women more
frequently affected than men.

### Triggering Factors

Environmental exposures, infections, and drugs have been
hypothesized to be triggering factors for the development of AAV.
Among environmental and occupational exposures, the most consistent associations have been observed with crystalline silica, with
most studies showing an odds ratio (OR) of 2.5 to 5. Associations
with other exposures such as heavy metals, farming, organic solvents, and pesticides have been studied, but results have not been
consistent.
The influence of infection on GPA pathogenesis has
been suggested by a cyclical pattern of occurrence observed in
some studies.
However, the only clear association is seen with
Staphylococcus aureus
, where nasal carriage of
S. aureus
has been
associated with an increased risk of relapse in GPA.
The role of
S. aureus
or other infectious agents in disease initiation has not
been established. Initial studies are being conducted to investigate the role of the nasal microbiome on disease development and
activity.
Lastly, the most commonly reported drugs causing AAV
include propothiouracil, hydralazine, minocycline, and levamisole-adulterated cocaine.
Use of leukotriene antagonists and
omalizumab has been linked to the development of EGPA. These
agents may unmask underlying EGPA by allowing corticosteroid
tapering or are prescribed to patients with undiagnosed and worsening EGPA, but a direct causal link cannot be excluded.

## Genetics

GPA, MPA, and EGPA are complex genetic diseases, with multiple genetic variants with modest effects influencing disease risk.
Thus, large cohorts of patients and controls are needed to identify
genetic risk factors. GPA is the most thoroughly studied of these
three diseases.
Familial studies of GPA have suggested a genetic influence
on disease susceptibility. A nationwide registry-based study in
Sweden showed that the relative risk of GPA in a first-degree
relative of an individual with GPA was estimated to be 1.56
(95% confidence interval [CI], 0.35–6.9). This familial risk
is lower than in other autoimmune diseases such as systemic
lupus erythematosus (SLE) and multiple sclerosis (MS) but is
of similar magnitude as the familial risk in rheumatoid arthritis (RA).
First-degree relatives of patients with GPA have an
increased incidence of seropositive RA (hazard ratio [HR],
1.54; 95% CI, 1.09–2.19) and other autoimmune diseases,
including MS (HR 1.92; 95% CI, 1.16–3.16).
Children
of individuals with GPA have an increased risk of RA (standardized incidence ratio, 1.34).
In an independent cohort, a
genetic risk score based on RA susceptibility genes was associated with GPA risk.
These findings suggest that GPA and RA

## Clinical Manifestations

Although AAV can affect almost all organ systems, the frequency of organ involvement varies greatly. Data on organ system involvement come from a small number of cohorts of 30 to
400 patients.
Some reports indicate that new organ system
involvement occurs frequently over time, particularly in GPA.
The diversity of presentations within a given organ system is
apparent from case series and reviews focused on that organ system and reports of particularly unusual cases.
Not all clinical features of AAV are directly attributable
to local vascular damage. This phenomenon is obvious with
regard to constitutional symptoms but, in addition, the risk
of venous thromboembolic events is elevated in the absence of
venous inflammation,
and the pathophysiology of arthritis
is unknown. The extent to which myalgia and gastrointestinal
(GI) symptoms indicate vasculitis in these organ systems is not
known.
As a rough approximation, the features of MPA can all be seen
in GPA or EGPA, particularly in ANCA-positive patients. GPA,
by definition, features perivascular necrotizing granulomatous
inflammation, and EGPA features eosinophil-rich granulomatous
inflammation and/or parenchymal infiltration by eosinophils.
Unfortunately, many of the largest series and clinical trials do not
allow comparison of organ system involvement in MPA, GPA,
and EGPA, due to pooling some or all of the entities as ANCAassociated vasculitis.

## Diagnostic Testing

The roles of laboratory tests, other diagnostic tests, and biopsy in

### Maintenance of Remission for Granulomatosis With Polyangiitis and Microscopic Polyangiitis

Given the toxicities associated with prolonged CYC use, a strategy
was developed to “induce” remission using CYC and glucocorticoids and then “maintain” remission using a less toxic regimen.
Medications studied as remission maintenance agents have
included azathioprine, MTX, MMF, leflunomide, trimethoprim/
sulfamethoxazole, etanercept, and rituximab.
Azathioprine was first demonstrated to be an effective remission maintenance agent in the Randomized Trial of Maintenance
Therapy for Vasculitis Associated with Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Autoantibodies (CYCAZAREM) trial, which treated 155
patients newly diagnosed with GPA or MPA with oral CYC for
at least 3 months and then randomized patients to continue CYC
or switch to azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day). The rate of relapse was
similar in both groups (15.5% in the azathioprine arm vs. 13.7%
in the CYC arm;
p
= 0.65), as was the rate of adverse events. This
study was the first randomized trial to indicate that the duration
of CYC therapy could be reduced without significant adverse
effects.
MTX was equally efficacious for remission maintenance compared with azathioprine in the WEGENT trial. In this study, 129
patients with GPA or MPA who achieved remission with CYC
therapy were randomized to receive either azathioprine (2 mg/kg/
day) or MTX (up to 25 mg/week) for 12 months. The two medications had similar rates of remission maintenance and adverse
events at 28 months of follow-up. Of note, 73% of patients
relapsed after discontinuing the study medication at 12 months.
Long-term follow-up of those in this trial (median duration 11.8
years) showed no difference in overall 10-year survival rates,
adverse events, or relapses. PR3-ANCA positivity was significantly
prognostic of long-term relapses.
MMF was investigated as a remission maintenance agent in the
International Mycophenolate Mofetil Protocol to Reduce Outbreaks of Vasculitides (IMPROVE) trial. After remission induction with CYC, 156 patients were randomized to receive either
azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day) or MMF (2000 mg/day) and were
followed for a median of 39 months. Relapses were more common
in the mycophenolate arm (HR for relapse in the mycophenolate
arm, 1.65; 95% CI, 1.06–2.70;
p
= 0.03). Secondary outcomes
such as renal function, proteinuria, and severe adverse event rates
did not differ between groups.
Thus MMF is believed to be less
effective than azathioprine in maintaining remission in AAV.
In a trial of 54 patients randomized to receive either leflunomide (30 mg/day) or MTX (starting at 7.5 mg/week and reaching
20 mg/week after 8 weeks), those in the leflunomide arm had a
decreased rate of relapse at 6 months (6/26 in the leflunomide
vs. 13/28 in the MTX arm). A higher rate of major relapses in
the MTX arm led to an early termination of the study. However,
adverse event rates were higher in the leflunomide arm.
Thus
additional studies are needed to determine the role of leflunomide
for remission maintenance therapy and ideally would include a
dose for which more data on safety are available (20 mg).
After reports of a beneficial effect of trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) for the treatment of GPA, a doubleblinded clinical trial randomized 81 patients with GPA who were
in remission to receive either TMP-SMX (800 mg of sulfamethoxazole and 160 mg of trimethoprim) or placebo for 24 months.
A higher proportion of patients receiving TMP-SMX remained
in remission compared with placebo (82% vs. 60%). A subgroup
analysis showed that patients with nasal or upper-airway lesions
benefited the most from TMP-SMX use.
Thus although TMPSMX is not commonly used for remission maintenance in patients
with AAV, it may be beneficial for patients with nasal or upperairway manifestations.
TNF inhibitors have had significant success as disease-modifying therapy for other autoimmune illnesses such as RA and IBD.
Early case series suggested infliximab may be efficacious in controlling disease activity in GPA and MPA.
The most rigorously
studied TNF inhibitor in GPA and MPA is etanercept for remission maintenance. In the Wegener’s Granulomatosis Etanercept
Trial (WGET), 174 patients received standard remission induction therapy (CYC or MTX) and were randomized to receive concomitant etanercept or placebo, which was continued during the
remission maintenance phase. There was no statistical difference in
the rate of sustained remission between the etanercept and placebo
arms (69.7% vs. 75.3%;
p
= 0.39) or in the time to remission.
However, the incidence of solid cancers was significantly higher in
the etanercept arm (six cancers vs. none;
p
= 0.01), suggesting that
following CYC therapy with anti-TNF therapy can substantially
increase one’s risk of cancer.
Based on these results, TNF inhibitors are generally not used to treat AAV.
Rituximab has also been investigated as a remission maintenance agent.
In the Maintenance of Remission using Rituximab in Systemic ANCA-associated Vasculitis (MAINRITSAN)
trial, 115 patients with either GPA, MPA, or renal-limited vasculitis, either new onset or relapsing, were randomized to receive
either azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day × 12 months, followed by
1.5 mg/kg/day × 6 months, then 1 mg/kg/day × 4 months) or
rituximab (500 mg IV × 2 doses at study entry and then every 6
months × 18 months), after having remission induced with CYC.
At 28 months (the primary endpoint), the rate of severe relapses
was significantly lower in the rituximab arm (5% in the rituximab
arm vs. 29% in the azathioprine arm).
The frequency of severe
adverse events, including severe infections and malignancy, was
similar in both arms. A difference in relapse rate has persisted at
60 months of follow-up off treatment since month 24 (for severe
relapses, 28.1% in the rituximab arm and 50.6% in the azathioprine arm; HR for relapse in the azathioprine arm, 2.51; 95% CI,
1.34–4.69;
p
= 0.003), but the rates of relapse clearly accelerated
in both groups after maintenance treatment was discontinued.
Very similar results were seen in the RITAZAREM trial, which also
compared rituximab to azathioprine for remission maintenance,
but with differences from MAINRITSAN: The study was limited
to patients with relapsing disease, remission induction had been
with rituximab, and the remission-maintenance regimen for rituximab was more aggressive (1000 mg every 4 months).
During

the treatment period of 24 months, relapses occurred in 13 of the
85 patients in the rituximab group and 32 of the 85 patients in
the azathioprine group (HR, 0.35; 96% CI, 0.18–0.66); 6 and 17
of these relapses, respectively, were severe. Serious adverse events
occurred in 22% (rituximab) and 36% (azathioprine) of patients
during the treatment period. Fewer relapses were seen in the rituximab group (HR, 0.41; 95% CI, 0.27–0.61). Including a followup period off treatment (to 36 months since enrollment), fewer
relapses were still seen in the rituximab group (HR, 0.41; 95%
CI, 0.27–0.61).
In a trial that compared two different rituximab regimens
for remission maintenance (MAINRITSAN-2), 162 individuals
with newly diagnosed or relapsing GPA or MPA who achieved
complete remission after induction therapy were randomized to
receive IV rituximab 500 mg according to a fixed schedule (every
6 months) or in a tailored fashion based on laboratory parameters (when CD19+ B cells became detectable or ANCA titers
doubled) until month 18. At month 28 (primary endpoint),
9.9% of the participants in the fixed-schedule group compared
with 17.7% of individuals in the tailored-infusion group had
suffered a relapse (
p
= 0.22). Those in the tailored-infusion arm
received fewer infusions compared with the fixed-schedule arm
(median three vs. five, respectively). This study suggests that
CD19+ B cell counts and ANCA titers may be used to guide
rituximab dosing for remission maintenance.
The use of lowdose glucocorticoids (prednisone or prednisolone, 5–10 mg/day)
as a standard component of maintenance therapy is a source of
considerable controversy and varying practice. No randomized
trial investigating this question has been completed. The studies
previously described that compared rituximab to azathioprine
for remission maintenance included prednis(ol)one, usually at 5
mg, in all patients in either group during the treatment period.
Comparing relapse rates between trials is problematic because
of different treatment regimens, different participant compositions, and different definitions of remission and relapse. With
these caveats, a meta-analysis showed that trials that routinely
continued glucocorticoids for 12 to 18 months reported markedly lower relapse rates than those in which they were discontinued earlier, usually by month 6.
Although not all patients with AAV are destined to relapse,
ongoing maintenance therapy has been shown to reduce relapse
risk, relative to discontinuation, for both rituximab and the combination of azathioprine and low-dose glucocorticoids.

### Adjunctive and Alternative Agents

The role of plasma exchange (PLEX) in the treatment of AAV was
investigated in two randomized trials. In the Methylprednisolone
or Plasma Exchange for Severe Renal Vasculitis (MEPEX) trial,
137 patients with newly diagnosed GPA or MPA who had renal
involvement confirmed by biopsy and serum creatinine greater
than 5.8 mg/dL (500
μ
mol/L) were randomized to receive either
seven PLEXs or 3000 mg of IV methylprednisolone, in addition
to CYC and prednisone. Compared with IV methylprednisone,
PLEX was associated with a 24% decrease in risk to progression to
end-stage renal disease at 12 months. No difference in survival was
observed.
PLEX was subsequently examined in the PEXIVAS
study, where 704 individuals with GPA or MPA with severe disease
(eGFR <50 mL/min and/or lung hemorrhage) were randomized
to receive PLEX (seven exchanges within 14 days) or no PLEX.
The rate of death from any cause or end-stage renal disease was
similar between the PLEX (28.4%) and no-PLEX (31.0%) groups
(HR 0.86; 95% CI, 0.65–1.13;
p
= 0.27). Whether some subgroup of patients still benefit from PLEX cannot be ruled out,
and analyses of numerous subgroups did not identify a disease
phenotype on which to focus further study. Meta-analysis of seven
randomized trials continues to indicate reduced risk of end-stage
kidney disease (ESKD) with PLEX,
but since that outcome was
not met in the single largest trial, use of PLEX is likely to drop and
focus on patients at highest risk for ESKD.
Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) has been considered for
patients who are unable to receive conventional immunosuppressive therapy. Case series using IVIG for remission induction have
had conflicting results. In a randomized, placebo-controlled trial
of 17 patients who received a single course of IVIG (2000 mg/
kg), 14 patients had significantly reduced disease activity, but this
effect did not persist beyond 3 months.
Larger controlled
clinical trials are needed to fully understand the efficacy of this
agent, but in light of the dubious feasibility of conducting such a
trial, IVIG is included as a possible option for patients refractory
to proven approaches in ACR treatment guidelines.
Other alternative therapies for AAV investigated in smaller
studies include anti-thymocyte globulin, which causes T cell depletion
; deoxyspergualin, which inhibits T cell maturation
; and
alemtuzumab (Campath, anti-CD52), which depletes both T cells
and B cells.

### Treatment of Eosinophilic Granulomatosis With Polyangiitis

The optimal treatment of EGPA is less clearly defined compared
with GPA and MPA. In part because of the differences in clinical
manifestations, patients with EGPA are frequently excluded from
the randomized clinical treatment trials of GPA and MPA. Even
so, treatments found to be efficacious for GPA and MPA are used
to treat EGPA, and the same general strategy of inducing remission with a more toxic medication and maintaining remission
with a less toxic one is also applied.
The vasculitis and asthma manifestations of EGPA are usually managed separately. Asthma treatment generally follows
recommended asthma treatment guidelines for patients without
EGPA. However, many patients’ asthma symptoms are corticosteroid dependent, and additional immunosuppression can be
used for steroid-sparing effects. Initial management of systemic
or vasculitic manifestations of EGPA relies on corticosteroids.
Patients with EGPA who do not have poor prognostic factors
as defined by the original Five Factor Score (serum creatinine
>1.58 mg/dL, proteinuria >1 g/day, severe GI involvement, cardiomyopathy, or CNS involvement) can be treated with corticosteroids alone, but 35% of patients relapse when the drugs
are tapered.
Other immunosuppressive agents are indicated in
addition to corticosteroids for patients with more severe disease.
As with GPA and MPA before definitive study of rituximab,
CYC is generally used to treat organ- or life-threatening manifestations.
The remission maintenance agents discussed in the
previous section have not been widely studied in EGPA. One
randomized, open-label study showed that azathioprine was efficacious for remission maintenance compared with CYC,
but
a more recent randomized trial showed no reduction in relapse
rate.
Approximately 80% of all patients require long-term use
of glucocorticoids to manage manifestations such as asthma and
sinonasal disease.
Current understanding of the efficacy of rituximab in EGPA
is derived from case reports and case series. Most patients with

EGPA were treated with rituximab because of disease refractory
to usual treatments (e.g., CYC) or medication intolerance. In
the two largest case series to date, 69 and 41 patients with EGPA
were treated with rituximab. In the first study, improvement
was noted in 77% of patients at 6 months and in 83% at 12
months. Median prednisolone doses decreased modestly from
12.5 to 7.5 mg at 12 months, and rates of flares of asthma and
sinonasal disease remained common.
In the second study, 15
patients had been refractory to conventional therapy, 21 were
relapsing, and 5 had new-onset disease. At 12 months, clinical improvement was seen in 88% of patients and prednisone
was reduced in all patients. ANCA-positive patients were more
likely to achieve or maintain remission in both studies.
In an
additional series, nine patients were all either in complete or partial remission within 3 months after rituximab treatment, with
no flares observed in three patients retreated with rituximab for
remission maintenance.
Rates of remission and relapse were
similar in 14 patients given rituximab for remission induction
as in 14 patients given CYC.
A randomized trial is needed to
determine the efficacy of rituximab in EGPA.
Options for treating patients with EGPA changed dramatically after publication of a randomized, double-blind, placebocontrolled trial of mepolizumab,
a monoclonal antibody
directed against interleukin-5, a cytokine critical for activation of
eosinophils. Among 136 participants, 28% of patients receiving
mepolizumab achieved protocol-defined remission for at least 24
weeks during the 52-week trial, compared with 3% in the placebo
group. The relapse rate was reduced by 50%, as was the average
daily dose of prednisone. This trial (MIRRA) built on previous
experience with mepolizumab in HES
and in two uncontrolled
pilot studies of mepolizumab in EGPA.
The primary and
several secondary outcomes in MIRRA were based on ability to
reduce prednisone to 4 mg or need to increase the dose, for any
reason. Therefore additional studies are needed to determine how
effective mepolizumab and similar drugs are for asthma, parenchymal eosinophilic inflammation, and vasculitic manifestations.
For now, mepolizumab should be regarded as a steroid-sparing
drug used for remission maintenance and to control non-organthreatening flares, but not as therapy for active organ-threatening
disease.

## Outcomes

Although the AAV are still sometimes fatal because of the severity of disease at onset or treatment resistance, most patients can
be successfully treated, which has two important sequelae. First,
the substantially increased risk of mortality in the first 6 months
after diagnosis (approximately 10%) is mostly due to infection as a
consequence of treatment, rather than vasculitis itself.
Second,
the moderately increased risk of long-term mortality is multifactorial, including infection, cardiovascular disease, and malignancy.
In addition, permanent organ damage affects overall prognosis.
Renal dysfunction is the only factor other than advancing age
that is strongly associated with long-term mortality in MPA and
GPA.
Analogously, cardiomyopathy is by far the strongest risk
factor for mortality in EGPA.
Although mortality has been greatly reduced, morbidity
remains high because of permanent damage (particularly renal
insufficiency, sinonasal symptoms, hearing loss, neuropathy, and
dyspnea) and consequences of treatment (diabetes, hypertension, osteoporosis, weakness, and certain malignancies).
Many
patients report fatigue and reduced energy—symptoms that
cannot be assessed objectively—as the most important negative
consequences of having vasculitis.
Previous studies have indicated an increased incidence of
cancer in GPA. However, more recent studies have shown lower
risks of bladder cancer, leukemia, and lymphoma than previously,
reflecting that current approaches are likely associated with little
increased risk.
There remains an increased risk of nonmelanoma skin cancer,
and it is difficult to assess relative risks
of uncommon cancers in rare diseases. Additional studies have
shown an increased risk of cardiovascular disease.
Relapse affects overall patient outcomes. The risk of relapse
in GPA is about 60%,
and that of MPA is about
30%.
Whether this difference is due to the disease
phenotype or ANCA specificity is uncertain.
Not surprisingly, having relapsed in the past also increases the risk of subsequent relapses.
Localized/limited GPA may have a higher rate
of relapse than systemic/severe GPA,
and 30% to 55% of such
patients may be ANCA-negative.
Persistent and rising c-ANCA/PR3-ANCA titers were associated with increased risk of flare in some but not all studies. In a
meta-analysis, the likelihood ratio was approximately 2, that is,
below the level considered to be “diagnostic” for making treatment decisions with confidence.
Relapse of vasculitis or parenchymal eosinophilic disease
beyond sinonasal disease and asthma is about as common in
EGPA as in MPA and is somewhat more common in ANCApositive than in ANCA-negative patients.
Relapses of sinonasal disease and asthma are common and often the main
reason that more than 80% of patients with EGPA continue
to take prednisone and often additional immune-suppressive
drugs.

## Pathogenesis

MPA, GPA, and EGPA all feature leukocytoclastic vasculitis:
fibrinoid necrosis of arterioles, capillaries, and venules by neutrophils, which infiltrate and die in the surrounding tissue and
leave characteristic fragments of their nuclei visible under the
microscope. Additional involvement of small arteries is sometimes seen, and that of small veins is rare.
Although some
deposition of immunoglobulins and complement can be
seen (these diseases are “pauci-immune” rather than nonimmune),
immune complex deposition is thought to play
little if any role in pathogenesis. Because ANCAs are highly associated with pauci-immune necrotizing vasculitis, much of the
study of pathogenesis has focused on the generation and mode
of action of ANCAs.
Although the bulk of MPO and PR3 is intra-cellular in neutrophil granules, these proteins may be translocated to the cell surface either constitutively or by inflammatory stimuli
and serve
as targets for ANCAs to activate neutrophils.
In addition,
MPO and PR3 may serve as targets extra-cellularly after degranulation, or particularly after release associated with neutrophil
extra-cellular traps.
The best evidence that ANCAs are directly pathogenic comes
from a mouse model in which anti-MPO antibodies (produced
by immunizing
Mpo
−/− mice with mouse MPO) produce pauciimmune GN when transferred into normal mice.
Neutrophils, and activation of the C5 component of complement with
the C5a receptor, are required to produce GN.
However,
other features than GN are not seen, and GN itself is relatively
mild.
More severe GN and additional features of systemic

vasculitis are produced by transferring spleen cells into SCID
mice,
but whether all of this disease is attributable to antiMPO antibodies or even to MPO-specific T cell immunity is
unclear.
Efforts to produce a similarly simple model using PR3 have
not been successful, and efforts to demonstrate pathogenicity
of anti-PR3 autoimmunity require considerable experimental
manipulation and produce limited disease. Whether this difficulty
is due to the different structure and biology of PR3 in humans
and rodents—or is evidence that anti-PR3 antibodies have limited
pathogenicity in isolation—is not clear. Production of vasculitis
using human anti-PR3 antibodies and mice with “humanized”
immune systems supports the human-versus-mouse explanation
and may provide the best evidence in favor of anti-PR3 antibodies
causing vasculitis.
Mouse models of HES have been developed,
but not the combination of eosinophilic and necrotizing
vasculitis that would be a more specific model for EGPA.
T cell immunity is implicated in any process that results in
high-affinity antibodies, but, in addition, CD4
T cells are found
in the necrotizing granulomatous lesions in GPA and are likely
involved in macrophage activation and neutrophil recruitment
locally.
Multiple changes in the percentages of T cell subsets—
Th1, Th2, Th17, and regulatory T cells—have been described in
patients with different forms of AAV, in different tissue sites, and
in different stages of disease activity.
The number of circulating proteins related to immunity,
inflammation, vascular injury, and tissue repair is so large in
highly active GPA and MPA that it is difficult to obtain insight
into pathophysiology from biomarker studies.
In EGPA,
circulating products of eosinophils are elevated in untreated disease.
As expected, markers of Th2 responses are elevated,
but Th17 cells have been implicated as well.
Known genetic risk factors and environmental exposures
explain only a small portion of the risk for GPA, MPA, or EGPA.
Although it is possible that rare or unique (including somatic)
genetic mutations or rare environmental exposures may contribute further to risk, it is also worth emphasizing that in many autoimmune diseases, random chance is likely to play an important
role in determining whether tolerance to a particular autoantigen
is broken.

## Conclusion

In the past 30 years, significant progress has been made in diagnosing and treating ANCA-associated vasculitis. Although this
progress has dramatically reduced the mortality of AAV, patients
continue to accumulate organ damage because of diagnostic delay
and the tendency of these diseases to relapse. Thus many challenges remain. Clinicians and investigators alike would benefit
from an improved understanding of the role of ANCAs in disease
pathogenesis, identification of clinically actionable biomarkers of
disease activity, and the development of more targeted therapies
to reduce treatment toxicity. With this additional knowledge, the
prognosis for patients with AAV may continue to improve.
