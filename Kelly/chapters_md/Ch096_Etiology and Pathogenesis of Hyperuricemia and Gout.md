## Introduction

The ancient disease gout has a complex pathogenesis, and its
modern relevance is underscored by the fact that gout is now the
most common inflammatory arthritis in the United States.
Gout is a disease of both metabolism and inflammation, and its
pathogenesis requires the intersection of two distinct processes:
(1) the intrinsic accumulation of urate, at levels sufficient to
drive the precipitation of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals,
and (2) an inflammatory response to the crystals so formed.
How these processes occur, and under what circumstances they
cross from adaptive to pathologic responses, are the subject of
this chapter.

## Evolutionary Considerations

KEY POINT

> High baseline levels of serum urate in humans may be the result of

> evolutionary pressures in an era when hyperuricemia provided

> survival benefits.

The metabolic production of uric acid is ubiquitous among mammals and many other forms of animal life, and it is important to
recognize that urate generation is not a priori pathologic. Indeed,
the production of uric acid may serve a variety of beneficial roles,
an area of interest to molecular immunologists and molecular
anthropologists alike.

### Uric Acid as a Danger Signal

Uric acid is a breakdown product of purine metabolism and was
long considered nothing more than an excretable remnant. However, studies suggest that evolution has coopted urate to play a
potentially critical role in organismal immunity. It has long been
appreciated that the lysates of damaged mammalian cells can serve
as adjuvants—that is, can promote immune responses to antigens—
but the mediator of adjuvant action was not known. Using classical biochemical techniques, one group
demonstrated that a major
endogenous adjuvant found in damaged or dying cells is uric acid.
Thus uric acid may serve as a danger signal to promote immune
responses. As first proposed by Matzinger, a
danger signal
is an
intrinsically produced molecule, issued by an altered or damaged
cell, that alerts the immune system to the need for an immunologic response.
Viewed from this perspective, production of
uric acid in a virally infected cell, for example, might serve as an
upstream signal to promote activating antigen presentation (

### Uric Acid and Human Evolution

Most mammals have serum urate levels under 2 mg/dL. In contrast, humans and other primates, as well as some New World
monkeys, demonstrate typical serum urate levels between 4 and
6 mg/dL (
Fig. 96.2
). The genetic and biochemical basis for these
increases is well appreciated. During the Miocene era (approximately 10 to 25 million years ago), mutations in various primate
species inactivated the uricase gene, which codes for the enzyme
that degrades uric acid to allantoic acid (which, in contrast to uric
acid, is a highly soluble and readily excreted substance). Genetic
studies indicate that the uricase gene experienced inactivating
mutations not once but multiple times, across multiple hominoid
lineages (
Fig. 96.3
).
Although uric acid transporters in the kidney (see below) co-evolved to keep urate levels within bounds,
the net result was a rise in serum urate. Evolutionary biologists
hypothesize that increases in urate generation, and mismatches
in renal uric acid excretion, conferred a survival benefit for these

in humans and other hominoids, the levels of serum urate consequently achieved are usually insufficient to promote urate

circumstances under which additional increases in urate production, or impairments in urate excretion, can result in serum urate
concentrations exceeding the solubility threshold. Accordingly, we
next review the mechanisms of urate production and excretion,
as well as the events that may tip the scales toward pathologic
hyperuricemia.

## Determinants of Urate Levels

### Uric Acid Production and Excretion: Normal Levels and Hyperuricemia

Uric acid is a breakdown product of purines, and uric acid generation depends directly on both intrinsic purine production and
purine intake. In humans, uric acid is an end-product metabolite;
consequently, the depletion of uric acid depends directly on its
excretion. The balance between uric acid production and excretion determines the serum urate level. Most individuals maintain
a relatively stable serum urate concentration between 4 and 6.8
mg/dL and a total body uric acid pool of approximately 1000
mg.
Individuals with serum urate levels >6.8 mg/dL (e.g., hyperuricemia), even without clinical gout, may deposit MSU crystals
either occultly or in the form of appreciable masses (tophi), with
the consequence that the total body uric acid pool may be significantly higher in individuals with asymptomatic hyperuricemia
than in nonhyperuricemics.
These occult deposits of uric acid
(total body urate burden) may have implications for treatment

because they act as a “buffering reservoir” that resists initial treatment with urate-lowering agents.

### Uric Acid Production: Purine Synthesis and Breakdown

KEY POINTS

> Uric acid is the end product of human purine metabolism.

> Uric acid production depends on both purine biosynthesis and the

> metabolic processes that convert purines into uric acid.

Purine Biosynthesis
Purines are heterocyclic aromatic compounds consisting of conjoined pyrimidine and imidazole rings (
Fig. 96.4
). In mammals,
the most common expression of purines comes in the form of
deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and ribonucleic acid (RNA)
(containing the purines adenine and guanine), as well as singlemolecule nucleotides (adenosine triphosphate [ATP], adenosine
diphosphate [ADP], adenosine monophosphate [AMP], and
cyclic AMP and, to a lesser extent, guanosine triphosphate, guanosine diphosphate, guanosine monophosphate [GMP], and cyclic
GMP). Purines are also critical elements of the energy metabolism
molecules NADH, NADPH, and coenzyme Q. They additionally
serve as direct neurotransmitters; for example, adenosine interacts
with receptors to modulate cardiovascular and central nervous system function.
Purine biosynthesis is initiated on a backbone of ribose5-phosphate. The enzyme phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate (PRPP)
synthetase catalyzes the addition of a pyrophosphate moiety to
form the adduct PRPP (
Fig. 96.5
). Subsequently, the enzyme
glutamine-PRPP amidotransferase catalyzes interaction of PRPP
with glutamine to form 5-phosphoribosyl amine, the first committed step in purine biosynthesis (
Fig. 96.6
). 5-phosphoribosyl
amine then serves as the backbone for a series of molecular additions, ending in the formation of the purine inosine monophosphate (IMP). IMP is converted into either AMP or GMP, which
can then be phosphorylated into higher-energy compounds. Glutamine-PRPP amidotransferase and PRPP synthase are both subject to feedback inhibition by IMP, AMP, and GMP, providing a
mechanism to slow purine biosynthesis in the setting of purine sufficiency. Collectively, the process of purine biosynthesis is highly
energy dependent, requiring consumption of multiple molecules
of ATP. Thus purine biosynthesis not only directly increases the
substrate load for urate generation but also increases the turnover of already-formed purines that contribute to increased urate
levels.
Urate Formation and Purine Salvage
Purines generated by the previously described mechanisms are
susceptible to enzymatic catabolism, presumably to maintain
purine homeostasis (
Fig. 96.7
). Purines susceptible to degradation
include the monophosphate nucleotides GMP and IMP, which
are converted by nucleotidases (e.g., removal of the sugar phosphate moiety) to their purine base forms, guanosine and inosine.
Additionally, AMP can be converted into IMP and adenosine into
inosine by adenosine monophosphate dehydrogenase and adenosine deaminase, respectively, which can then undergo further
degradation via the inosine catabolic pathway. Further catabolism
of both guanosine and inosine is mediated by the common enzyme
purine nucleoside phosphorylase (PNP). Guanosine is converted
to guanine, whereas inosine is converted to hypoxanthine. Both
guanine and hypoxanthine are subsequently converted to xanthine by the enzymes guanine deaminase and xanthine oxidase
(also known as
xanthine dehydrogenase
), respectively. Xanthine
from either source is then converted directly to uric acid, again
by the action of xanthine oxidase. Lacking the enzyme uricase,
human and primate purine metabolism ceases with the production of uric acid.
Presumably because purine synthesis is energy expensive for
the cell, evolution has dictated that mechanisms exist to recover
purines before they irreversibly traverse the degradative pathway.
These recovery pathways, collectively known as
purine salvage
, are
intimately connected to the feedback regulation of purine synthesis. The major enzyme responsible for purine salvage, hypoxanthine/guanine phosphoribosyl transferase (HGPRT1, also known
as HPRT1), catalyzes the transfer of phosphoribose to either
hypoxanthine or guanine, to reform IMP or GMP, respectively
(
Fig. 96.8
). These products are then available for reinclusion in the
purine pool. A second salvage enzyme is adenine phosphoribosyl
transferase (APRT), which restores adenine to AMP. However, as
described earlier, most adenosine/adenine breakdown occurs via
conversion to inosinic acid. Purine salvage regeneration of IMP,
GMP, and AMP also drives feedback inhibition, slowing the de
novo generation of urate.

Urate Overproduction: Primary and Secondary Causes

KEY POINT

> Hyperuricemia may result from urate overproduction owing to primary or

> secondary causes.

Primary Urate Overproduction.
In rare patients, inborn
errors of metabolism lead to urate overproduction and subsequent
hyperuricemia. Several of these deserve mention. First, a small
number of individuals possess PRPP synthetase enzymes that are
hyperactive. The result is increased generation of PRPP, which can
accelerate purine biosynthesis by Michaelis-Menton kinetics.
The second well-described class of abnormality leading to urate
overproduction occurs within the purine salvage pathway. Deficiencies of HGPRT1 result in impaired purine salvage, and therefore increased substrate for uric acid generation. Additionally,
because purine salvage normally results in nucleotide monophosphate generation, patients with purine salvage deficiencies accumulate lower levels of nucleotide monophosphates, resulting in
loss of feedback inhibition of both PRPP synthase and glutaminePRPP amidotransferase. Thus purine salvage failure resulting from
HGPRT1 deficiency is compounded by secondary purine overproduction. In addition to mutations in HGPRT1 itself, mutations in
ALDH16A1
, a member of the aldehyde dehydrogenase
superfamily, may promote hyperuricemia by impairing the function of HGPRT1.
Two major variants of HGPRT1 deficiency have been described.

is an X-linked recessive disorder manifesting in childhood as

Several secondary causes can lead to urate overproduction. In
most cases, these conditions induce increased cell turnover, with
resultant purine generation and breakdown. Chief among these
must be counted diseases of erythropoietic, lymphopoietic, and
myelopoietic cell turnover, of both malignant and nonmalignant
varieties. Among the erythropoietic diseases causing hyperuricemia, autoimmune, and other hemolytic anemias (red cell destruction with compensatory increased red cell generation), sickle cell
disease,
polycythemia vera,
and ineffective erythropoiesis
(e.g., in pernicious and other forms of megaloblastic anemia,

leukemias, lymphomas, and paraproteinemic diseases such as
multiple myeloma and Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia are also
at increased risk for hyperuricemia.
In some cases, hyperuricemia and concomitant renal failure may be the first presentation
of these malignancies.
Indeed, the extent of hyperuricemia may
correlate with the degree of disease and cell turnover. Patients with
essential thrombocytosis may also be at increased risk for hyperuricemia.

primarily responsible for most cases of human hyperuricemia.

### Urate Excretion: Gastrointestinal and Renal Mechanisms

KEY POINT

> Urate excretion occurs via the gut and the kidneys. In the kidneys,

> a complex series of urate transport proteins mediates a net

> elimination of sodium urate.

In most humans, serum urate levels are maintained within a narrow range. Accordingly, mechanisms must exist to ensure disposal
of urate. The gastrointestinal (GI) tract and the kidneys each participate in urate excretion.
Gastrointestinal Excretion of Urate
Uric acid elimination via the GI tract has been recognized for
more than 50 years but has been inadequately studied. On
the basis of radiolabeled urate tracer studies, Sorensen estimated that in healthy individuals, the GI tract is responsible
for the excretion of approximately 35% of the daily uric acid
burden.
GI uric acid excretion may increase in the setting of decreased renal uric acid excretion.
Mechanisms of
uric acid excretion into the gut appear to include both exocrine secretion (saliva, gastric, and pancreatic juices) and
direct bowel secretory mechanisms and appear to involve a
urate transporter known as ATP-binding cassette transporter
G2 (ABCG2), which also plays a role in renal urate excretion
(see below).
The anion transporter NPT5 may also take
part in in GI uric excetion.
After excretion into the gut, uric
acid undergoes degradation by intestinal flora. Studies suggest that alterations in the intestinal microbiome, potentially
including reduced populations of the genus
Coprococcus
, may
contribute to hyperuricemic states, both by decreasing the rate
of intestinal urate degradation and through effects on molecules such as short-chain fatty acids that may regulate local
inflammation.
Renal Handling of Uric Acid
In most circumstances the kidney is primarily responsible for uric
acid excretion via complex multistep mechanisms. In the bloodstream, urate is completely or nearly completely unbound to
plasma proteins, thus nearly 100% of bloodstream urate undergoes ultrafiltration in the renal glomerulus. As discussed later,
decreases in glomerular function therefore reduce urate filtration
and promote rises in serum urate levels.
After ultrafiltration, urate (typically in the form of uric acid
owing to low intraluminal pH) in the proximal tubule undergoes
distinct but apparently simultaneous handling steps: (1) a
resorption step
, in which most of the filtered urate undergoes reclamation, and (2) a
secretion step
, in which resorbed urate is transferred
back into the tubule lumen. The net result is excretion of only
approximately 10% of the filtered load. In fully functioning nephrons, the amount of secretion is responsive to serum urate levels
such that rises in serum urate induce increased renal excretion and
restoration of total body urate homeostasis. Studies have emphasized the importance of organic anion transporters (OATs) and
other active and passive transport molecules in the movements of
urate in both directions across the proximal tubule.

Urate
resorption

Fig. 96.9

resorption in humans.
SLC22A12

exchanger to move urate from the tubule lumen into the epithelial cytosol. The major inorganic counterion for URAT1 activity is Cl
. However, organic anions such as lactate, pyrazinoate,
and nicotinate can substitute for chloride, with potential clinical consequences discussed later. The importance of URAT1 to
renal urate resorption is indicated by the fact that patients with
inactivating mutations of URAT1 excrete nearly 100% of their
filtered urate and demonstrate extremely low serum urate levels
(along with increased urinary uric acid levels and risk for uric acid
kidney stones).
Conversely, other mutations in the
URAT1

hyperuricemia, presumably through a gain-of-function mutation.
Thus URAT1 appears to be the most important of the uric acid
retaining transporters. Several well-established uricosuric drugs

KEY POINT

> Decreased renal excretion of urate, owing to intrinsic or secondary

> causes, results in elevated levels of serum urate.

Most patients with hyperuricemia underexcrete urate; that is, for
any given serum urate level, their degree of renal uric acid excretion is inadequate to lower systemic concentrations (
Fig. 96.10
).
The mechanisms of uric acid underexcretion are various and stem
from either primary or secondary renal effects.
Primary Urate Underexcretion.
In a subset of patients, hereditary defects of renal tubule uric acid excretion result in hyperuricemia. With the identification of the renal urate transporters,
the underlying basis for some of these defects has become apparent. For example, ABCG2 loss-of-function mutations promote
hyperuricemia, and up to 10% of gout cases in White Europeans may be attributable to hyperuricemia induced by defects in
the uric acid exporter ABCG2.
As noted previously, ABCG2
also appears to play an important role in GI uric acid excretion,
and ABCG2 dysfunction may therefore also contribute to uric
acid retention and hyperuricemia on a GI basis.
Conversely,
gain-of-function mutations in several transport-related proteins
(e.g., PDZK1, CARMIL, NHERF1) may promote hyperuricemia by enhancing URAT1 (i.e., renal uric acid resorption) activity.

underexcretion typically display normal renal filtration and normal serum creatinine levels.
Familial juvenile hyperuricemic nephropathy (FJHN), also
known as
medullary cystic kidney disease (MCKD)

group of autosomal dominant disorders characterized by early
hyperuricemia and progressive chronic kidney disease.
This

protein), the most prevalent protein secreted by the kidney.
The probable importance of uromodulin deficiencies to FJHN
has recently been underlined by the observation that although
patients with the MCKD1 and MCKD3 subtypes of FJHN have
mutations that are not in the uromodulin gene, they nevertheless
display a phenotype of decreased uromodulin expression.

Toxins.
Several toxins affect the kidney to promote hyperuricemia. Chief among these is lead.
Lead exposure is endemic
to Western society, but at times, lead exposure has been excessive (e.g., during the Roman empire). In the 20th century, a large
cohort of patients with lead-induced hyperuricemia (saturnine
gout) was recognized during and after the era of Prohibition,
predominantly in the southeastern United States and relating to
the home brewing of moonshine using lead-lined distilling vessels (typically, automobile radiators). In the kidney, lead poisoning
leads to interstitial and perivascular fibrosis and glomerular and
tubular degeneration.
Although patients with lead nephropathy initially experience only mild to moderate renal insufficiency,
their clearance of urate is excessively limited, indicating a tubular
defect in urate excretion.
Suggestions that lead exposure may
also promote purine turnover are provocative but less well supported.
Epidemiologic data suggest that moonshine nephropathy may be more prevalent than commonly assumed.
Other toxic substances that have been potentially associated
with hyperuricemia include cadmium and the polychlorinated
biphenyls (PCB) PCB-138 and PCB-153, although it remains
unclear if the mechanisms of hyperuricemia caused by these
toxic substances operate via renal or metabolic (or both) pathways.
Patients with chronic beryllium poisoning, usually as
a result of occupational exposure, may also suffer diminished renal
uric acid excretion and hyperuricemia.

### Diet and Uric Acid

KEY POINTS

> Diet significantly affects serum urate levels, both by serving as a source

> of purines and by altering the metabolic production and/or renal

> excretion of uric acid.

> Various foods affect serum urate levels, either by increasing or

> decreasing the serum level.

Purine-Rich Foods
Purine-rich foods comprise a major source of daily purine
load and hence a major source of generated urate. However,
not all purine-rich foods convey equivalent risk: seafood and
red meat, particularly organ meats, convey an increased risk
for hyperuricemia, whereas consumption of purine-rich, leafy
green vegetables apparently does not.
Some research has
emphasized that the effect of purine intake is modest, with
studies suggesting that alterations in purine intake may result
in, at most, changes in serum urate of about 1 mg/dL.
However, such studies have rarely accounted for the role of
renal urate excretion in the context of diet. Thus it is worth
considering whether, for a patient who is intrinsically unable
to excrete serum urate, an increased dietary purine load may
produce more profound increases in serum urate levels than
would be seen in a patient with normal excretory capacities.
Lowering serum urate by reducing purine consumption, while
recommended, may have unanticipated consequences. Too
strict avoidance of purine-rich foods may lead to compensatory
consumption of carbohydrates, thereby paradoxically promoting increases in serum urate (see later). Much research has suggested that promoting healthy dietary patterns, including the
Mediterranean and Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension
(DASH) diets, may holistically help lower both serum urate
and diet-associated comorbidities. Not all healthy diets may
be the same, however. Secondary analysis of one such study
showed that a DASH-style diet lowered serum urate more than
diets focused on carbohydrates or unsaturated fats.
In
contrast to purine ingestion, protein consumption does not
increase the risk of hyperuricemia and/or gout, a point of occasional confusion for practitioners because many high-purine
foods are also high in protein.
Fructose
Osler recognized the ability of fructose to provoke gout flares as
early as 1901.
Little was made of this observation, however,
until the 1960s and 1970s, when it was demonstrated that fructose loads, administered orally or intravenously, cause transient
rises in serum urate levels, particularly in gout patients.
These effects are reproduced with consumption of sucrose (which
contains fructose), but not glucose or galactose.
Fructose consumption does not provide purines directly but alters metabolism
to promote purine and hence urate synthesis (
Fig. 96.11
). The first
step in fructose metabolism is the donation of a phosphate (Pi)
from ATP to form fructose-1-phosphate (enzyme phosphofructokinase), generating ADP. ADP is converted to AMP (enzyme
adenylate kinase), which in turn is degraded via several steps to
uric acid. Additionally, fructose-1-phosphate serves as a phosphate
“sink” because the donated phosphate is no longer available for the
regeneration of ATP from AMP and ADP. Because both Pi and
ATP inhibit the purine degradation pathway (inhibition of AMP
deaminase and 5
′
nucleotidase, respectively), depletion of these
compounds contributes to the formation of uric acid.
Depletion
of ADP/AMP also impairs feedback inhibition of PRPP synthase
and promotes purine biosynthesis. Epidemiologic studies confirm
a role for fructose consumption in hyperuricemia; patients who
consume excessive fructose in the form of fructose-sweetened
soft drinks or fruit juices demonstrate both higher serum urate
levels and increased incidence of gout, an effect not seen among
consumers of sugar-free beverages.

dietary fructose is underscored by the fact that the rise in gout

Ingestion of alcoholic beverages is associated with increased incidence of gout and gout flares, and ample physiologic and epidemiologic data confirm that ethanol consumption promotes the

## Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia and the Biological Effects of Soluble Urate

KEY POINT

> Soluble urate is biologically active and, even in the absence of gout,

> adversely affects the cardiometabolic system.

The presence of an elevated serum urate without prior clinical gout
flares is defined as asymptomatic hyperuricemia. Historically, the
most commonly used values for hyperuricemia have been population based and set at >7 mg/dL for men and >6.0 mg/dL for women.
However, the use of >6.8 mg/dL, the saturation point of urate for
both men and women, has gained favor as a more physiologic marker.

### Soluble Urate and the Vasculature

Soluble urate, even in the absence of gout, has multiple potential
biologic effects. As previously noted, extra-cellular urate acts as a

potent antioxidant. In contrast, urate inside cells may have prooxidant effects. Also noted earlier, there is evidence that rises in
primate serum urate in the Miocene may have helped maintain
normal blood pressure. In our current, salt-replete era, elevated
levels of soluble urate may promote renal microdamage, contributing to hypertension and chronic kidney disease. Multiple population studies document a relationship between elevated urate and
hypertension.
Commensurate with a role for soluble urate in hypertension,
several studies indicate that urate lowering can alter blood pressure. Among adolescents with premature essential hypertension
and hyperuricemia, treatment with the urate-lowering agent
allopurinol normalized blood pressure, which reversed after allopurinol discontinuation.
In adults with long-standing established hypertension, more potent urate-lowering approaches
may be needed; in one study, the highly active urate-lowering
agent pegloticase reduced mean, systolic, and diastolic blood
pressure.

### Soluble Urate and the Kidneys

The potential effects of soluble urate on the kidneys are numerous.
In rats, elevated serum urate increases expression of renin/cyclooxygenase-2 in the juxtaglomerular apparatus, decreases nitric oxide
synthase in the macula densa, and induces microvascular injury,
macrophage infiltration, thickening of pre-glomerular arterioles,
and tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
In vitro, exposure of vascular smooth muscle cells to soluble urate increases smooth muscle
proliferation and reduces capacity for regulatory vasodilation.
Additionally, urate can affect the E-cadherin pathway and cause
transformation of tubular epithelial cells to mesenchymal cells,
which may promote renal fibrosis.

### Trained Innate Immunity

Landmark studies by Joosten and others have promoted the concept of trained, or learned, innate immunity.
In contrast to the
accepted paradigm that innate immunity is a fixed process, trained
innate immunity suggests that chronic or intermittent exposure to
bacterial molecules or internal phlogistons can induce epigenetic
and/or metabolic changes that render innate immune responses
persistently enhanced—in short, that the innate immune system
can be “reset” to respond more rapidly and vigorously in the setting of chronic challenge. Chief among the internal phlogistons
that have been identified is soluble urate.
Consistent with this
observation, patients with asymptomatic hyperuricemia have
elevated baseline inflammatory markers, even in the absence of
frank clinical inflammation (gout flares) (
Fig. 96.12
).

### Urate and the Regulation of AMP-Activated Protein Kinase

Additional studies have identified a role for intra-cellular noncrystalline urate in the promotion of multiple inflammatory and metabolic processes, through effects on the enzyme AMP-activated
protein kinase (AMPK). AMPK is a master regulator that acts in
multiple cell types. In hepatocytes, AMPK activation inhibits gluconeogenesis
and triglyceride synthesis, and AMPK inhibition
may promote fatty liver.
In macrophages, AMPK activation
downregulates inflammatory responses.
In contrast, elevated
levels of intra-cellular urate stimulate AMP deaminase, which
inhibits AMPK, leading to proglycemic, prolipidemic and proinflammatory profiles (
Fig. 96.13
).

## Crystal Formation: The Transition From Hyperuricemia to Gout

KEY POINT

> MSU crystal formation involves physicochemical processes but may be

> regulated by synovial fluid proteins and immunoglobulins.

Soluble urate does not induce gouty flares; only crystallized MSU
promotes acute inflammation. Urate crystallization is therefore a
critical intermediate step in the progression from hyperuricemia
to frank gout.
Uric acid is a weak organic acid. At physiologic pH (7.4),
approximately 98% of uric acid exists in the form of urate. At
concentrations greater than 6.8 mg/dL, urate exceeds its apparent
solubility limit in serum, potentiating the precipitation of needleshaped MSU crystals that can promote inflammatory responses.
In vitro models have shed light on the relationship between
MSU crystallization and environmental factors such as pH, temperature, salt content, vibration, and large molecules.
Urate
may more readily precipitate at both lower pH and lower temperature, which may explain why gout flares are most commonly
seen in the first metatarsophalangeal joint, a joint that is both circumferentially exposed (i.e., relatively low in temperature) and at
the farthest outpost of the systemic circulation (i.e., most likely to
be acidotic).
Crystal formation and gout flares most commonly occur
within the confines of the joint. Some investigators have suggested that soluble urate is excreted more slowly from the joint
than other serum elements, providing a possible mechanism for
increased urate concentration within the joint space.
Once
MSU crystals are formed, the fenestrated endothelium of the
synovium might permit the joint space to serve as a crystal
“trap,” preventing crystals from being dispersed and/or dissolved
in the wider circulation. Other investigators have emphasized
the possible role of cartilage itself in the precipitation of urate,
particularly in the setting of aging and/or osteoarthritis (OA).
Potential mechanisms through which aging cartilage might
facilitate MSU nucleation/crystallization include changes in the
proportions and/or chemical properties of cartilage glycosaminoglycans and proteoglycans, as well as increases in intra-cellular and extra-cellular lipid content of articular cartilage.
One recent report suggests that OA chondrocytes may actually
secrete urate into the joint space, promoting local urate excesses
in the synovial fluid.
Most recently, one group reported that
the synovial cell product lubricin may play an important role
in preventing urate crystallization and that lubricin deficiencies
may promote MSU crystallization, even among patients who are
not hyperuricemic.
Interestingly, several investigators have suggested that urate
crystallization may be an immune-assisted process. One group
proposed that immunoglobulin (Ig) G antibody-binding to MSU
monomers, previously assumed to be nonspecific, may promote
the stacking of MSU crystals despite dispersion forces that exist
in fluid and tissue.

## Acute Gout Flares: The Inflammatory Response to MSU Crystals

### Transition From Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia to Gout

For clinical purposes, the first acute flare defines the progression
from asymptomatic hyperuricemia to gout.
However, the basis
for this transition remains incompletely understood. Although
patients with higher serum urate levels are more likely to develop
frank gout, as many as 50% of patients with the highest levels of
serum urate may not develop gout over a 15-year period.
And
although MSU crystal tissue deposition is likely to precede rather
than follow incident gout, patients with asymptomatic hyperuricemia and imaging-documented MSU crystal deposition do not
necessarily progress to a first gout flare. Consequently, the transition from hyperuricemia to gout may depend on more than simply MSU crystal formation. Likely factors include the individual
patient’s intrinsic capacity to generate inflammatory responses to
MSU crystals, whether due to heredity or environmental effects.
As noted previously, soluble urate may drive persistent upregulation of (trained) innate immunity, which might contribute to
a more ready response to MSU crystals. Similarly, recent studies
have suggested a possible role for clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP), defined as clonally expanded, somatically mutated cells in patients without hematologic malignancy.
According to one group, the presence of CHIP is a risk factor for
incident gout, and patients with CHIP are approximately 70%
more likely to have gout than individuals without the condition.
Patients with CHIP have elevated levels of IL-1
β
, suggesting that
CHIP may function to amplify inflammatory (inflammasome)
responses to MSU crystals, a phenomenon that was supported by
a mouse model.

### Response to MSU Crystals

KEY POINT

> MSU crystals activate both complement and resident synovial

MSU crystals are potent triggers of inflammation. The phlogistic potential of crystalline urate in human gout was established
dramatically by Faires and McCarty, who self-injected MSU crystals into their knee joints and experienced iatrogenic inflammation.
In the clinic, the diagnosis of acute gout is definitively
made when polarized light microscopy of synovial fluid from an
inflamed joint reveals the presence of MSU crystals, neutrophils,
and particularly intra-cellular MSU crystals (confirming crystaldirected neutrophil phagocytic activity).
However, the inflammatory mechanisms of gout flares are complex and involve not
only neutrophils but other cell types, inflammatory mediators,
and well-organized sequences of events.
MSU Crystals and Complement Activation
Complement activation by the alternative pathway is a continuous
process in body fluids, in which C3 component activation in the
fluid phase is followed by rapid C3b deposition onto nearby surfaces.
On human cell surfaces C3b is routinely inactivated by
regulatory proteins. In contrast, the polyanionic surfaces of MSU
crystals provide opportunities for unconstrained C3b deposition
and activation of downstream complement components. Weissindependently demonstrated the abilmann and Doherty
ity of MSU crystals to activate complement from C2-depeleted
serum, confirming activation of the alternative pathway.
Interestingly, MSU crystals also appear to activate complement
by the classical pathway (i.e., via C1 activation). Crystal activation of the classical pathway may occur in two ways. First, MSU
crystals may activate the classical pathway by an immunoglobulin-independent, C-reactive protein (CRP)–dependent pathway.
Second, formed MSU crystals possess the ability to
bind antibodies, probably in a nonspecific manner, but sufficient
to activate the classical complement cascade.
Either complement cascade will lead to the generation of
C5a, a potent vasodilator and chemoattractant for inflammatory
cells such as neutrophils.
MSU crystal-mediated complement activation may also lead to generation of soluble complement membrane attack complexes, which may activate local cells
to promote inflammation.
Other pro-inflammatory proteins,
including fibronectin and kininogen, may also adhere to urate
crystals.

Cellular Responses to MSU Crystals
The processes by which leukocytes respond to MSU and other
crystals are multiple and complex and remain a focus of ongoing studies. In addition to classical cellular responses to immunoglobulins and complement, mechanisms appear to include crystal/
receptor interactions, crystal/membrane interactions, and effects
driven when crystals gain access to the intra-cellular cytoplasmic
space.
Cell-Surface Recognition of MSU Crystals.
Several studies suggest that Toll-like receptors (TLRs), which are critical for
innate immunity, respond to MSU crystals in much the same way
as they do to pathogen-associated molecular patterns. In TLR2-
and TLR4-knockout mice, decreased cytokine production and
neutrophil influx argue strongly for a role for TLRs in MSU crystal responses.
Impairment of MSU crystal–driven inflammation in CD14 knockout mice also supports a role for TLRs, since
CD14 is essential for TLR2- and TLR4-dependent signaling.
However, other researchers observed no effect of multiple TLR
knockouts on murine models of crystal-induced inflammation.
Based on accumulating data, it would appear that TLR effects are
more likely to prime rather than drive MSU-induced responses,
for instance in the now-recognized role of “Signal 1” in activating the NLRP3 inflammasome (see below). Interestingly, MSU
crystals may also interact with Clec 12a, a C-type lectin receptor,
which drives inhibitory rather than inflammatory signals.
Other studies emphasize the ability of MSU crystals to electrostatically interact with cholesterol.
Cholesterol-enhanced
regions of plasma membranes (lipid rafts) are rich in signaling
molecules and represent hot spots for cellular activation. Receptor-independent interactions between crystalline MSU and lipid
rafts have been demonstrated in myeloid cells, resulting directly
in cell activation: nonspecific aggregation of transmembrane
receptors within the rafts activates immunoreceptor tyrosinebased activation motifs, followed by activation of the signaling
molecule Syk.
Syk activation can then induce cell activation
including phosphoinositol-3 kinase signaling, cytoskeletal rearrangement, and crystal phagocytosis. Multiple other downstream
signaling pathways may also be activated, including the extracellular signal-regulated kinase, c-Jun N-terminal kinase, and p38

mitogen-activated protein (MAP) kinase; phospholipases C and
D; rho-family proteins; and nuclear factorκ
B. Depending on the
cell type in question, activation of these molecules will result in
specific phenotypes including cytoskeletal alterations, production
of cytokines and lipid mediators, and induction of phagocytosis
and superoxide generation.
As noted earlier, MSU crystals may become coated by immunoglobulins and other serum proteins and may serve as a substrate
for complement activation. Immunoglobulin- and complementcoated urate crystals may also activate cells via their ability to
engage immunoglobulin, complement, and possibly other cell-surface receptors. Consistent with this model, IgG-coated MSU crystals have been observed to incite greater inflammatory responses
than those that are uncoated, and C5a receptor antagonists inhibit
MSU crystal–induced peritonitis in a murine model.

### Resolution of the Acute Gouty Flare

One of the more interesting features of acute gouty arthritis is
that, particularly in the early stages of the disease, most flares are
self-limited.
Multiple effects have been invoked to explain this
phenomenon. MSU crystals may become coated with synovial
fluid proteins (e.g., apolipoproteins B and E), inhibiting their
ability to provoke inflammation.
The stress of the gouty flare
may promote adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) secretion;
in addition to its ability to induce glucocorticoid release, ACTH
can bind directly to melanocyte-stimulating hormone receptors to
provoke anti-inflammatory effects and reduce phagocyte responses
to MSU crystals.
Other investigations have emphasized the local, active
events through which inflammation may be resolved. For
example, in vivo studies using the MSU crystal–induced mouse
air pouch model suggest that local upregulation of expression
of PGD2 and 15d-PGJ2 (a spontaneous dehydration product
of PGD2) contributes to the resolution of crystal-mediated
inflammation, probably through the ability of these molecules
to activate the peroxisome proliferator activating receptor(PPARγ
) and, likely, the metabolic regulator AMPK.
MSU crystals also stimulate the expression of PPARγ
itself,
setting the stage for enhanced suppression of inflammation.
15d-PGJ2 inhibits secretion of IL-1
β
, IL-6, and IL-12 and
TNF from macrophages and downregulates the expression of
inducible nitric oxide synthase. Other targets of 15d-PJG2
include PGD receptors and the NFκ
B pathway. 15d-PGJ2
also inhibits CXC chemokine production, alters cellular adhesion molecules, and stimulates apoptosis of endothelial cells.
Other anti-inflammatory molecules (e.g., IL-10) are also
upregulated, and the potent anti-inflammatory resolvins and
lipoxins are almost certainly involved.
In considering the resolution of acute gouty inflammation,
it is important to recognize the biologic programs that delay the
production of anti-inflammatory molecules until the appropriate
phase in the sequence. For example, PGD2 and 15d-PGJ2 production initially declines on exposure to MSU crystals, apparently
facilitating the inflammatory response, but then rebounds to act
during the resolution phase.
Similarly, the generation of antiinflammatory lipoxins requires the accumulation of at least two
active cell types (either neutrophils and activated endothelium or
neutrophils and activated platelets), an evolutionary adaptation
that creates an intrinsic delay before the anti-inflammatory effects
are initiated, one that may be critical to allow initial inflammatory
responses to infections.
Consistent with a “time-delayed” process of gout flare resolution, other studies emphasize the roles of the influxing leukocytes
themselves in the resolution of the gouty flare. Several studies support the ability of late-influxing monocyte/macrophages to clear
both MSU crystals and apoptotic neutrophils that have ingested
crystals, resulting in decreased crystal burden.
Moreover,
these influxing macrophages appear to differentiate into an antiinflammatory, M2 phenotype (in contrast with the inflammatory
M1 phenotype of resident macrophages) and, upon phagocytosing crystals, to release TGFβ
to provoke an anti-inflammatory
effect.
As noted previously, neutrophils that have phagocytosed crystals can release NETs that may initially play a proinflammatory role in the gouty flare.
Subsequently, however,
these NETs provide a stable scaffold on which extravasated neutrophil enzymes, particularly neutrophil elastase, encounter and
degrade cytokines to promote inflammatory resolution (
Fig.
96.17
).
Macrophages may then play an additional role in resolution of inflammation by the eventual clearance of NETs.

## Chronic Gouty Arthritis and Tophaceous Gout

The natural history of untreated gout frequently includes eventual progression to a state characterized by chronic inflammation
and/or the establishment of macroscopic MSU deposits known as
tophi
(Video 96.1). Even during the asymptomatic, intercritical
periods of gout, low-grade chronic inflammation can persist, with
continuous phagocytosis of crystals by leukocytes.
In patients
with long-standing gout, intercritical inflammation can become
frankly apparent, and the cytokines, chemokines, proteases, and
oxidants that participate in acute inflammation can contribute to
chronic synovitis, cartilage loss, and bone erosion, signaling the
progression to chronic gouty arthritis.
Although tophi are composed first and foremost of MSU crystals, they are complex structures in which urate (1) is embedded
γ
in a matrix of lipids, proteins, and mucopolysaccharides and (2)
drives a persistent inflammatory state.
A tophus can alternatively be conceived of as a granuloma of mononucleated and
multinucleated macrophages arranged in three distinguishable
zones (
Fig. 96.18
). MSU crystals and debris constitute the central
zone. Surrounding the central zone is the corona zone, consisting of macrophages, multinucleated macrophage-like cells, plasma
cells, and mast cells. This biologically active corona is thought to
account for the ultrasonographic finding of an anechoic rim that
circumscribes a tophus.
Eventually, the corona and central zones may become encased by a connective tissue layer, the
fibrovascular zone. Within this zone, macrophages express surface
markers of recent migration, maturation, apoptosis, continuous
recruitment, and pro-inflammatory activation.
T and B cells
are scattered throughout both outer zones.
Although classically a late feature of gout, tophi may occur early in the disease,
particularly in patients with renal insufficiency and high serum
urates.
In some patients, tophi may develop even in the absence
of any episodes of flares.
Tophi are important not only for their role as reservoirs of crystalline urate but also for their ability to damage the tissues in which
they reside, including bone, cartilage, and tendons and other soft
tissue structures.
Mechanical factors, induction of lytic enzymes,
and synthesis of pro-inflammatory cytokines all contribute to the
tophus’s ability to promote erosion and joint destruction. Tophus
macrophages produce IL-1
β
, TNF, IL-6, IL-17, matrix metalloproteinases (MMP)-2, and MMP-9, and macrophage colonystimulating factor (M-CSF).
These molecules promote further
inflammation and tissue damage, as well as the maturation and
activation of osteoclasts that actively resorb bone. For example,
M-CSF interacts with M-CSF receptors on osteoclast progenitor
cells to promote osteoclastogenesis. Tophus expression of IL-1
β
and other cytokines has additionally been shown to inhibit the
viability and bone-maintaining effects of osteoblasts.
Tophus
T cells promote bone erosions through the elucidation of RANK
ligand, the major promoter of osteoclastogenesis and osteoclast
activation.
Additionally, nontophaceous MSU crystals on the
cartilage surface reduce chondrocyte viability, increase chondrocyte production of degenerative enzymes, and promote cartilage
damage.
Even in clinically asymptomatic patients, these inflammatory and erosive processes persist; damage may not be apparent
until late in the process.

## Gout and Comorbidities

It is now appreciated that patients with gout suffer multiple
comorbidities at a higher rate than the general population, including hypertension, kidney disease, diabetes mellitus, obesity, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, cardiovascular disease, and OA, among
others.
In fact, patients with gout suffer metabolic syndrome
at rates approximately twice those of the general population.
Several studies indicate that patients with gout suffer a higher
mortality rate than the general population, which has remained
elevated, even as mortality among patients with other rheumatic
diseases has declined.
The direction of causality behind these associations remains
incompletely elucidated, and there is no doubt that certain
comorbidities—for example, renal disease—predispose to hyperuricemia and therefore gout. On the other hand, an accumulating
number of physiologic and clinical studies suggest that gout may
predispose to, and/or help drive, at least some of its associated
comorbidities. For example, in separate population studies, the
presence of gout was shown to be associated with a future risk of
type 2 diabetes, whereas the presence of diabetes was not shown to
be associated with a future risk of gout.
The mechanisms through which gout might act to raise comorbid risk remain an area of intense study. It is likely that at least
some effects of gout on comorbidities are mediated through
hyperuricemia, given the biologic effects of soluble urate described
earlier. For example, animal models have demonstrated the ability of hepatic urate production to drive nonalcoholic fatty liver
disease, a phenomenon that can be abrogated by blocking urate
production with allopurinol.
Studies also suggest that elevated
urate levels may inhibit insulin synthesis, increase insulin resistance, and induce hepatic glucose production, while others that
have demonstrated elevated urate may directly promote atherosclerosis.
However, in multiple studies gout has been shown
to be associated with a higher prevalence of comorbidities than
asymptomatic hyperuricemia, suggesting that additional mechanisms, unique to gout, may drive comorbidities. One possibility is
that both the acute and chronic inflammatory states of gout “add
on” to the risks invoked by hyperuricemia. From the point of view
of chronic inflammation, it is interesting to note that treatment
of gout with the anti-inflammatory drug colchicine may reduce
both the incidence of MI among gout patients and the incidence
of new-onset coronary artery disease.
In one recent study
colchicine also reduces the rate of kidney disease progression in
patients with gout, again implying a potential pathophysiologic
role for chronic gouty inflammation.
In patients with gout,
endothelial function (a recognized predictor of future cardiovascular events) is impaired to a degree proportional to elevations
in both serum urate and CRP, implicating both hyperuricemia
and systemic inflammation in the defect.
Consistent with this
notion, gout-related endothelial dysfunction can be partially
reversed by guideline-concordant treatment with a combination
of anti-inflammatory and urate-lowering therapy.
Recent studies of gout flares (acute inflammation) suggest even more dramatic effects: In one study the risk of MI among gout patients
with pre-existing coronary disease was roughly doubled for 120
days after a gout flare,
a time period that correlates closely
with a persistent post-flare rise in inflammation as measured by
the neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (also an established marker of
cardiovascular risk).
In addition to effects on systemic inflammation, it is possible that the progression from hyperuricemia to
gout drives other, more specific mechanisms that could promote
comorbid disease. For example, while controversial, recent studies
using dual-energy computed tomography to image the vasculature
suggest that patients with gout may deposit MSU crystals directly
in the aorta and coronary arteries, which may drive local rather
than systemic vascular changes.
While gout may promote
some or all of the aforementioned comorbidities, it is interesting
to note that the presence of gout and/or higher uric acid levels has
been associated with lower incidence and/or better outcomes in a
number of neurodegenerative and/or neuroinflammatory diseases,
including Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s, and Huntington’s diseases, and
multiple sclerosis.
Once again, the data is somewhat ambiguous, and the direction of causality and mechanisms of association remain uncertain. A better understanding of the mechanisms
through which gout contributes to, or protects against, comorbidities could shed new light on the biologic mechanisms of gout
and create new opportunities for its clinical management—a topic
addressed in
Chapter 97
.
