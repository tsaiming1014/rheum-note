ERIC M. RUDERMAN AND JOHN P. FLAHERTY

> Fungi are an infrequent but clinically important cause of bone and joint

> infections.

> These infections are often indolent in onset and may masquerade as

> other disorders.

> Travel and immigration have affected the geographic localization

> of several important fungal infections, which may be seen in

> nonendemic areas.

> Although diagnosis may be assisted by clinical presentation and

## Introduction

Fungal infection is a relatively infrequent but important cause of
osteomyelitis and arthritis. Fungal diseases that commonly cause
osteomyelitis include blastomycosis, coccidioidomycosis, cryptococcosis, candidiasis, and sporotrichosis (
Table 115.1
). Fungal
arthritis is less common and also most often associated with these
same fungal pathogens but occasionally other species. The epidemiology of these fungal infections, their musculoskeletal presentations, and their treatment are considered in this chapter.
The epidemiology and clinical features of individual deep
mycoses may suggest the diagnosis in some cases, but their indolent presentation, which often resembles that of other noninfectious diseases, may be misleading. Travel and immigration have
blurred their geographic localization. Infection may be acute
and overwhelming in immunocompromised patients, for whom
disseminated fungal infections are a major risk. Anti-cytokine
and other immunosuppressive treatments for rheumatic diseases,
particularly those targeting TNF, are associated with disseminated
fungal infection,
as are HIV infection, pregnancy, and treatments for transplantation and malignancies.
Fungal arthritis has
also occurred following intra-articular injection of contaminated
preservative-free methylprednisolone.
For rheumatologists, disseminated fungal infections are an important diagnostic consideration in some patients and must be considered before starting
biologic treatments in those at risk; they may also complicate the
clinical course of other arthritides. The presentation of fungal
infections as a consequence of rheumatic disease therapy will also
be considered in this chapter.
Fungal infections are typically diagnosed by histologic examination or culture of involved tissues. Improved molecular diagnostic techniques may facilitate the diagnosis, provided the possibility
of fungal infection is considered and appropriate studies are
requested. Synovial fluid leukocyte counts and culture results vary
among fungal infections and in individual cases and may be misleading. Serologic testing may also assist in diagnosing and staging several fungal infections. Detecting fungal antigens and fungal
RNA in blood, urine, other body fluids, and tissue may help with
diagnosis in selected circumstances.

## Coccidioidomycosis

Coccidioidomycosis is endemic to the southwestern United States
and areas of Central and South America, but cases are increasingly
diagnosed in nonendemic areas because of travel and reactivation
of remote infection. The soil fungi
Coccidioides immitis
and
Coccidioides posadasii
generally cause a primary respiratory illness following inhalation of the infectious fungal spores or arthroconidia
from the environment. The risk of acquisition increases when soil
is disturbed and in windy conditions. A self-limited acute pneumonia may result, associated with systemic manifestations such
as arthralgia and erythema nodosum (valley fever), but infection
is often inapparent and only infrequently becomes chronic or
disseminated.
Human-to-human transmission does not occur.
Extrapulmonary infection is almost always caused by hematogenous spread from an initial pulmonary focus. The bones and joints
are frequent sites of dissemination, particularly in immunocompromised hosts.
Septic arthritis of the knee is common, generally arising from
direct infection of the synovium. Other joint infections are caused

features, serologic studies, histologic examination, and culture.
Enzyme-linked immunoassays (EIAs) for immunoglobulin M

made by the demonstration of granulomatous synovitis and typical spherules in a biopsy specimen, confirmed by positive culture.
Synovial fluid, when obtainable, might not demonstrate septic
leukocyte counts and could have a lymphocyte predominance.
Synovial fluid is rarely culture positive; culture of synovial tissue
may be more helpful. Radioisotope bone scans may be helpful to
identify areas of infection.
With early diagnosis of effusive synovitis, antifungal treatment
alone is appropriate. Treatment is usually initiated with oral azole
antifungal agents, most commonly fluconazole or itraconazole.
Amphotericin B is recommended if lesions are worsening rapidly or are in particularly critical locations, such as the vertebral
osteomyelitis. Lipid formulations of amphotericin B have demonstrated less nephrotoxicity and infusion-related side effects than
conventional deoxycholate amphotericin B and may be given at
doses higher than those tolerated with conventional amphotericin.
A recent meta-analysis showed similar efficacy with conventional
and lipid-based amphotericin B formulations, with a better safety
profile for the latter.
With more widely disseminated infection
or involvement of critical areas such as the spine and in highrisk hosts, the choice and duration of treatments are often complicated. Factors that favor surgical intervention are large-sized
abscesses, progressive enlargement of abscesses or destructive
lesions, presence of bony sequestrations, instability of the spine,
or impingement on critical organs or tissues (e.g., epidural abscess
compressing the spinal cord).
Newer antifungal agents such
as voriconazole and posaconazole show promise as alternative
therapy.
Long-term prophylaxis with fluconazole can limit
the risk for reactivation in immunosuppressed patients.
Coccidioidal synovitis may also occur as a consequence of
immune complex-mediated inflammation, a presentation that
may complicate either primary pulmonary or disseminated disease and is typically polyarticular. It is accompanied by fever, erythema nodosum or erythema multiforme, eosinophilia, and hilar
adenopathy. It abates in 2 to 4 weeks.

## Blastomycosis

Blastomycosis, caused by
Blastomyces dermatitidis,
is endemic
in the northern-central and southern United States. Infection is
acquired by inhalation of conidia associated with decaying vegetation or decomposing wood and typically results in sporadic or
less commonly clustered cases of acute or chronic pneumonia.
Affected individuals do not appear to have any distinguishing or
predisposing characteristics except for exposure to the organism
during work or recreation. Clinical presentation includes pulmonary, skin, and bone involvement. About half of affected patients
will present with fever; leukocytosis and an elevated sedimentation rate are frequently seen.
Bone pain, swelling, and soft tissue
abscesses are the most common manifestations of osteoarticular
disease.
Hematogenous dissemination is common; skin disease
and osteoarticular disease occur most frequently. Bone involvement occurs in 25% to 60% of disseminated cases, and arthritis is
estimated to occur in 3% to 5%.
In a study of 45 patients with
skeletal blastomycosis, 41 had osteomyelitis, while 12 presented
with septic arthritis.
In another series of 14 cases at a single institution, 12 presented as a bone mass; 3 of 4 cases of septic arthritis
in this series developed due to direct extension of osteomyelitis.
The skeletal areas most commonly affected are the long bones,
vertebrae, and ribs (
Fig. 115.1
).
Blastomycosis often mimics
malignancy with subtle constitutional symptoms, nodular lung
opacities, and lytic bone lesions.
Arthritis is usually monoarticular in the knee, ankle, or elbow
but may rarely be polyarticular.
Joint infection is an isolated
skeletal disorder in only a few cases; joint radiographs more commonly show punched-out bone lesions (
Fig. 115.2A
). Synovial
fluid is commonly purulent, but
B. dermatitidis
is not visualized
with routine Gram stain and does not grow on routine culture
media. Diagnosis is often delayed if specimens are not cultured
on fungal media. The synovial histologic examination shows
granulomas and abundant neutrophils—a so-called
pyogranulomatous tissue response.
Broad-based budding yeast forms may be
seen (
Fig. 115.2B
). The yeast can be difficult to visualize with routine hematoxylin and eosin stains, but special stains, such as the
Gomori methenamine silver (GMS) stain or periodic acid–Schiff
(PAS) stain, can be helpful. The diagnosis is also frequently made
from involved nonarticular sites. Urinary antigen testing may be
less sensitive than previously believed, particularly for nonpulmonary infection, but remains useful for monitoring response to
therapy.
For moderately severe or severe disease, treatment with
amphotericin B for 1 to 2 weeks or until improvement is noted,
followed by oral itraconazole for a total of at least 12 months, is
recommended. For mild to moderate disease, oral itraconazole for
12 months is recommended. Serum levels of itraconazole should

## Cryptococcosis

Cryptococcus neoformans,
the fungus causing cryptococcosis,
is geographically ubiquitous and is found in soil contaminated
by pigeon feces; a related species,
Cryptococcus gattii,
is associated with certain types of eucalyptus trees in tropical climates
and has been associated with an ongoing outbreak of disease in
British Columbia, Canada, and the Pacific Northwest region of
the United States. Risk of illness is increased in individuals with
defects in cell-mediated immunity, including advanced HIV infection, organ and stem cell transplantation, lymphoreticular malignant neoplasms, TNF inhibitor treatment,
and corticosteroid
therapy. Concomitant corticosteroid use is a significant risk factor
for cryptococcal and other fungal infections in patients receiving
TNF inhibitor treatment.
Cryptococcosis is acquired by inhalation, and illness varies
in acuity. It usually affects the lungs in its primary form, but it
sometimes disseminates hematogenously to a wide variety of sites,
including the CNS and skin. Bone infection is common, causing
osteolytic lesions in 5% to 10% of cases, but articular involvement
is reported rarely (
Fig. 115.3
).
As seen in blastomycosis, bone
lesions may mimic metastatic neoplasm (
Fig. 115.4
).
Cryptococcal arthritis presents as an indolent monoarticular arthritis in about 60% of reported cases and as polyarthritis in the remainder.
The knee is most commonly
involved. The majority of cases reported from the pre-AIDS
era also demonstrated radiographic evidence of periarticular
osteomyelitis. These patients were young adults, did not have
debilitating disease or other evidence of dissemination, and had
pulmonary involvement in only 50% of cases. Synovial tissue
showed acute and chronic synovitis, multinucleate giant cells,
prominent granuloma formation, and large numbers of budding yeast with special stains. Most recently reported cases are
associated with immunosuppression and disseminated infection. Interestingly, osteoarticular cryptococcal infections have

## Candidiasis

Candida
species are widely distributed in nature.
Candida albicans
is a normal commensal of humans, and other species can probably
live in nonanimate environments such as soil. Since the advent of
antibiotic therapy in the 1940s and related to the common use
of immunosuppression and parenteral lines, candidiasis has been
responsible for an increasing incidence of mucocutaneous and
deep-organ infections.
Osteomyelitis, although uncommon, is a
potentially serious complication of hematogenous dissemination
in both adults and children.
A recent review identified a history of preceding candidemia in half of osteomyelitis cases, while
it was the first proven site of infection in the other half.
It may
also occur from direct tissue inoculation during surgery,
and
bone infection may emerge after successful treatment of infection
at other sites. Bone infection is commonly located in two adjacent vertebrae or in a single long bone,
although a few patients
have had multiple sites of involvement. Candidal prosthetic joint
infections may also occur as a late consequence of total joint
replacement.
The usual clinical presentation is localized pain. Other symptoms and laboratory abnormalities vary. It can present months
after an episode of fungemia, and clinical manifestations tend
to be milder than bacterial infections at the same sites. This can
lead to long delays in diagnosis. Bone changes of osteomyelitis
are commonly demonstrated in radiographs of the symptomatic site. The diagnosis is established when a culture of involved
bone obtained by either open or needle biopsy has identified
a variety of
Candida
species.
C. albicans
causes the majority of
infections, but the role of nonalbicans species has been increasing in recent years.
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) testing

of blood may allow earlier diagnosis of disseminated
Candida
infection, although it is not yet widely available.
Treatment
with azoles (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole, posaconazole, or isavuconazole), echinocandins (caspofungin, micafungin, or anidulafungin), or amphotericin B formulations can be
effective.
Species identification and susceptibility testing
assist antifungal therapy selection (note that the nomenclature
of some species has recently been updated). For example,
Nakaseomyces glabrata
(formerly
Candida glabrata
) frequently demonstrate dose-dependent susceptibility to azole antifungal agents
(e.g., requiring higher doses of fluconazole for treatment) but
remain susceptible to echinocandins;
Pichia kudreiavzevii
(formerly
Candida krusei
) are often resistant to fluconazole and may
show reduced susceptibility to amphotericin but remain susceptible to newer azoles and the echinocandins;
Clavispora lusitaniae
(formerly
Candida lusitaniae
) are often resistant to amphotericin; and
Candida parapsilosis
may demonstrate reduced susceptibility to echinocandins (but they remain clinically active).
Candida auris
has emerged recently as a cause of outbreak of
nosocomial infection.
C. auris
is notable because some strains
are multidrug resistant and refractory to treatment. The use of
surgical débridement must be individualized. With vertebral
involvement but no neurologic complications, medical therapy
alone has been effective.
Candidiasis is an uncommon cause of monoarticular arthritis.
C. albicans
is the most common pathogen, although septic
arthritis due to other candidal species has occurred.
Reported
cases commonly involve a knee, occur in the context of multifocal
extra-articular
Candida
infection, and are accompanied by constitutional symptoms. Both children and adults have been affected.
Predisposing conditions include gastrointestinal and pulmonary
disorders, narcotic addiction, intravenous catheters, leukopenia, immunosuppressive treatment (including TNF inhibitors),
broad-spectrum antibiotics, and corticosteroids. Some involved
joints were previously affected by arthritis, and infection has followed arthrocentesis in isolated cases. In most cases, radiographs
reveal coincident osteomyelitis. Synovial fluid leukocyte counts
may vary;
Candida
species have been cultured from synovial fluid
in all cases but are not commonly identified on smear. Histologic studies of synovium show nonspecific chronic inflammation
rather than granulomas. Mucocutaneous
Candida
infection has
occurred in patients treated with the interleukin (IL)-17 inhibitors
secukinumab and ixekizumab, but the risk of infection appears
low and infections have been mild, resolving spontaneously or
with standard therapy.

## Sporotrichosis

Sporotrichosis is caused by
Sporothrix schenckii,
a saprophyte found
widely in soil and plants. Infection in humans occurs through
inoculation of the skin or, rarely, inhalation into the respiratory
tract; it is a source of infection among agricultural workers in tropical and subtropical areas. A zoonotic outbreak associated with
Sporothrix brasiliensis
transmitted by cats was recently reported
in Brazil.
It most commonly involves the skin and lymphatics
but may disseminate from the lungs to the CNS, eyes, bones,
and joints.
In immunocompetent hosts, a single site is typically
involved; in immunocompromised hosts, including patients on
anticytokine therapy, multifocal disease may occur.
In contrast to the relatively common occurrence of skin
infection, articular sporotrichosis is a rare disorder.
In
84% of patients in one series, there was no accompanying skin
involvement, suggesting entry through the lungs. Sporotrichosis
most often occurs in individuals with a chronic illness that alters
host defense, such as alcoholism or a myeloproliferative disorder.
Sporothrix
arthritis is most often indolent and infects a single joint
or multiple joints in equal proportions. The knee, hand, wrist,
elbow, and shoulder are most frequently involved; hand and wrist
involvement distinguishes this from other fungal arthritides.
Articular infection shows a propensity to spread to adjacent soft
tissues, forming draining sinuses. Constitutional symptoms are
unusual.
Radiographic changes vary from juxta-articular osteopenia to
the commonly observed punched-out bone lesions. When it can
be obtained, synovial fluid is inflammatory. Synovitis is characterized on gross evaluation by destructive pannus and on microscopic examination by granulomatous histologic features or, less
frequently, by nonspecific inflammation. Organisms are difficult
to identify in tissue, and the diagnosis is often made by positive
culture of joint fluid or involved tissue. Incubation at room temperature facilitates growth of the mycelial phase of
S. schenckii.
Serologic testing is not useful in the diagnosis of sporotrichosis. In
a small number of cases, sporotrichosis may disseminate to cause a
potentially fatal infection characterized by low-grade fever, weight
loss, anemia, osteolytic bone lesions, arthritis, skin lesions, and
involvement of the eyes and CNS.
These infections occur in
immunosuppressed patients with either hematologic malignancies
or HIV infection.
Amphotericin B and itraconazole have been effective in the
therapy of systemic sporotrichosis, including osteoarticular disease.
In contrast, fluconazole is only modestly successful in
osteoarticular sporotrichosis.

## Aspergillosis

Aspergillus
species are ubiquitous, but infection occurs only rarely
in immunocompetent individuals. In contrast, invasive infection
is an important life-threatening complication in immunocompromised adults and children.
It may spread directly from the
lung to adjacent vertebrae, disk spaces, and ribs (more often in
children) or through the bloodstream (

).
Rare cases
of monoarthritis with adjacent osteomyelitis are also reported. The
knee is the most commonly involved joint. The organism may
be observed in infected tissue (

EIA has been validated as a surrogate marker for invasive aspergillosis, and the (1

with combined surgical débridement and antifungal therapy is
an ongoing challenge.
Voriconazole has proven superior

now recommended for initial therapy.
Liposomal amphotericin B; alternative azoles including posaconazole and isavuconazole; and the echinocandins caspofungin, micafungin, and
anidulafungin have demonstrated efficacy in salvage situations.
Combination therapy has not demonstrated clear improvement in

## Histoplasmosis

Histoplasma capsulatum
is a soil fungus that causes endemic
disease in the Midwest and the southeastern United States.
Bone and joint involvement is rare but has been described in
the knee, wrist, and ankle. Immunosuppression, including the
use of TNF inhibitors, predisposes to disseminated histoplasmosis in adults and children, which may be confused clinically with sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, or reactive inflammatory
conditions.
Diagnosis depends on appropriate use of fungal staining and culture and antigen detection.
A case report
emphasizes the rare occurrence of fungal prosthetic joint
arthritis.
The more common osteoarticular involvement with
histoplasmosis is a hypersensitivity syndrome accompanying
acute pulmonary infection; it is characterized by self-limited
polyarthritis, erythema nodosum, and erythema multiforme.
Liposomal amphotericin B followed by itraconazole is the preferred treatment for severe infection and itraconazole for less
severe cases.

## Scedosporiosis

Scedosporium
species are environmental molds that have been
identified as fungal pathogens in both immunocompetent and
immunocompromised hosts. They may cause focally invasive and
disseminated infection after cutaneous inoculation.
Scedosporium
prolificans
has a predilection for bone and cartilage, leading to
both septic arthritis and osteomyelitis. Infections are difficult to
eradicate with surgery and antifungal agents, and the organism is
resistant to amphotericin.
Voriconazole or combination therapy is the currently recommended treatment approach.

## Treatment of Fungal Infection

Antifungal chemotherapy has improved over the past several
decades, first with the introduction of amphotericin B and then
with the oral antifungal agents flucytosine, ketoconazole, fluconazole, and itraconazole. More recent advances include the development of less toxic formulations of amphotericin B, liposomal
amphotericin B, and amphotericin B lipid complex. Voriconazole, posaconazole, and isavuconazole are broad-spectrum azole
antifungals; all have improved activity against aspergillosis, and
posaconazole and isavuconazole have activity against mucormycosis. The echinocandin antifungal agents caspofungin, micafungin,
and anidulafungin have emerged as alternative therapies for aspergillosis and as the treatments of choice for some
Candida
infections. For detailed treatment guidelines, several excellent reviews
are available.
In choosing an appropriate drug (
Table 115.2
)
and course of treatment, the clinician must consider the infecting
agent, clinical manifestations of the disease, immune status of the
host, anti-microbial resistance, drug side effect profile, and direct
and indirect costs of treatment. Treatment has become more complex because of immune system compromise in infected patients
treated for transplant rejection, autoimmune disorders, malignant
disease, and AIDS.
Itraconazole has become the first choice for treatment of the
endemic mycoses—blastomycosis, histoplasmosis, and sporotrichosis. A loading dose of itraconazole of 200 mg three times
a day for 3 days is recommended, followed by 200 to 400 mg
daily. Absorption of itraconazole is unpredictable, and blood levels of itraconazole should be measured to ensure adequate drug
exposure. Absorption of itraconazole requires stomach acid, so

## Fungal Infection as a Consequence of Anti- rheumatic Therapy

Many of the same fungal pathogens associated with osteoarticular infections may cause infection in patients treated with antirheumatic therapy, particularly biologic therapy. Animal models
of infection point to an important role for TNF in host defense
against many of these organisms, including
Aspergillus,
Candida,
Cryptococcus,
Coccidioides,
and
Sporothrix.
Coccidioidomycosis infection following TNF inhibitor therapy
has occurred in endemic areas in the southwestern United States.
In the largest published series, 12 of 13 cases followed therapy
with infliximab, and 1 was associated with etanercept.
All but
two of these cases were believed to represent new infection rather
than reactivation. In one medical center included in this series, the
relative risk of coccidioidomycosis infection was 5.23 with infliximab therapy compared with other anti-rheumatic therapies. In
all cases, coccidioidomycosis infection developed in the absence
of other known risk factors, including diabetes, pregnancy, and
HIV infection. Updated data from the same institutions, however, have suggested that biologic or nonbiologic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) treatment can potentially be
Coccidioidomyreinstituted during or after antifungal therapy.
cosis infection has also occurred in nonendemic areas, presumably
as a consequence of fomite exposure.
Disseminated histoplasmosis infections have been reported
following TNF inhibitor therapy in endemic areas in the Ohio–
Mississippi River valley regions of the central United States; as with
coccidioidomycosis infections, these occurred more frequently
following infliximab therapy than with the other agents.
The
reason for the larger number of cases with infliximab is unclear;
possibilities include a unique mechanism of action for infliximab,
larger numbers of patients treated, patient selection, concomitant
immunosuppressive therapy, or some combination of all of these.
In most cases, patients developing disseminated histoplasmosis
with TNF inhibitor therapy have been taking additional immunosuppressive therapy. In a series of 26 patients with histoplasmosis
and rheumatoid arthritis (RA), TNF inhibitors (including adalimumab, etanercept, and infliximab), methotrexate, and corticosteroids were all implicated as potential contributing factors.
In a case-control series using commercial health claims data, corticosteroid use was a significant risk factor for the development of
mycobacterial and fungal infections in patients treated with TNF
inhibitors; the majority of these infections were tuberculosis and
histoplasmosis.
Patients with histoplasmosis typically present
with cough, dyspnea, fever, and malaise and can rapidly become
quite ill. Histoplasma antigen may be identified in the urine in
92% of cases with disseminated histoplasmosis, and this test may
facilitate rapid diagnosis.
Exposure to
Histoplasma
may result
in asymptomatic latent infection; it has been difficult, however,
to determine whether symptomatic infections following TNF
inhibitor therapy represent reactivation or new infection. Both
pulmonary and disseminated infections with cryptococcal infections have occurred with TNF inhibitors, as have infections with
Aspergillus, Candida, Scedosporium,
and
Sporothrix.
P. jirovecii
pneumonia (PJP) is caused by a fungus originally
classified as a protozoan
(Pneumocystis carinii).
PJP, an opportunistic infection commonly seen in patients with advanced HIV
infection, has occurred with a number of anti-rheumatic therapies, including cyclophosphamide and low-dose methotrexate,
often in combination with corticosteroid therapy.
Interestingly, PJP as a consequence of low-dose methotrexate therapy
is a particular concern in Japan, where one group identified the
prevalence of asymptomatic carriage of
Pneumocystis
being as
high as 18.8% in the elderly.
These same authors have suggested that PCR testing may identify carriers at increased risk
for PJP during therapy.
The use of biologic therapy, including
rituximab
and TNF inhibitors,
has been associated with
the development of PJP. A recent analysis of data from the British Society of Rheumatology Biologics Register suggested that
the rate of
Pneumocystis
infection was significantly higher with
rituximab than with TNF inhibitor therapy, although the overall
incidence of opportunistic infection did not differ among biologic therapies.
PJP presents as fever, dry cough, and dyspnea.
The clinical presentation in patients receiving immunosuppressive therapy is typically more acute than in HIV-associated cases.
Organisms may be identified in sputum from HIV patients but
are seen less commonly in rheumatologic patients; presumptive diagnosis may be made on the basis of PCR testing for
P.
jirovecii
DNA. Elevated serum levels of
β
-d-glucan, a common
component of fungal cell walls, may aid in the diagnosis. One
series of 21 patients identified older age, pre-existing pulmonary
disease, higher corticosteroid doses, and low serum albumin and
IgG levels as potential risk factors for PJP in patients treated

with infliximab.
Chest radiographs characteristically show
bilateral diffuse infiltrates, while CT scans demonstrate groundglass opacities (
Figs. 115.6 and 115.7
); radiographic findings
may be difficult to distinguish from methotrexate pneumonitis.
Treatment includes supplemental oxygen and trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (TMP/SMX) or pentamidine isethionate. Highdose corticosteroids are commonly used as adjunctive therapy in
the treatment of HIV patients with severe PJP but are less well
studied in patients taking immunosuppressive therapy.
PJP infection is also of concern with nonbiologic immunosuppressive therapy. In particular, the use of cyclophosphamide
to treat systemic lupus, vasculitis, and other autoimmune diseases has been associated with a risk of developing PJP, although
the overall risk appears to be low. In a review of published data
on 76,156 cases of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) treated
with cyclophosphamide, the risk of PJP was 15.88 per 10,000
patients, or 0.158%.

Two recent
surveys of U.S. rheumatologists, however, found that just 50.4%
and 69.5%, respectively, routinely prescribed prophylactic antibiotics.
Academic rheumatologists and more recent graduates are more likely to prescribe prophylactic therapy. TMP/

There are no consistent
standards or recommendations for the use of prophylaxis for
fungal infection other than
Pneumocystis.
Unlike mycobacterial infection, biologic therapy of rheumatic disease has not been definitively associated with an
increased overall risk of fungal infection, although the lack
of a statistically significant association with these rare infections cannot disprove a possible connection.
Indeed, fungal
infections have occurred in a post-marketing biologic registry in France.
In general, patients with a history of fungal
infection or those with significant exposure to these organisms should receive biologic therapy only when alternatives are
not available or appropriate. Patients should be cautioned to
minimize exposure to sources of infection during therapy (e.g.,
avoid exposure to histoplasmosis in old buildings [demolition,
remodeling, cleaning], chicken coops, bird roosts, wood piles,
or caves [spelunking]) and avoid exposure to outdoor dust in
regions endemic for coccidioidomycosis. Patients receiving
concomitant corticosteroid therapy may warrant closer observation during therapy.
In general, there remains little practical role for screening for
latent fungal infection or for prophylactic therapy.
Serologic
tests and delayed-type hypersensitivity testing do not distinguish
past, resolved infection from latent infection, although repeat coccidioidomycosis serologies have been proposed as part of an algorithm for determining the potential safety of reinstituting biologic
therapy after treatment of an acute, nondisseminated infection.
In regions where coccidiodomycosis is endemic, some have proposed screening and managing asymptomatic infections prior to
prescribing TNF inhibitors, although there is, as yet, little data to
demonstrate that this reduces the risk of symptomatic infections
with therapy. Chest radiographs may identify calcified granulomas consistent with prior histoplasmosis infection, but this is a
nonspecific finding. In Japan, it has been suggested that TMP/
SMX therapy may normalize PCR testing in asymptomatic
P.
jirovecii
carriers treated with methotrexate and that this might
reduce the risk of developing pneumonia, but this has not been
studied in a U.S. population.
The lack of useful screening tests
makes early recognition of infection and institution of therapy
critical. Indeed, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
has mandated a “black box” warning regarding the risk of fungal infections on the product labels of TNF inhibitors, reflecting
the potentially fatal consequences of delayed recognition of such
infections.
