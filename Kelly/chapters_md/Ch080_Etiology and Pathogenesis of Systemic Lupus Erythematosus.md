## Introduction

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is one of the most widely studied diseases in medicine. Predominantly a disease of young women
in their childbearing years and with the potential to cause significant physical disfigurement, morbidity, and occasionally mortality, lupus is the focus of strong advocacy to support research that
will generate insights into disease pathogenesis and improve patient
outcomes. In fact, characterization of the immunologic contributors to lupus, the prototype systemic autoimmune disease, has been
the focus of particularly intense study since the flowering of the
discipline of immunology in the 1950s and 1960s. Efforts to define
the genetic variations that underlie susceptibility to lupus have supported the central role of the immune system in disease pathogenesis but have extended the view of lupus pathology beyond the
important role of autoantibodies and T cells to include a significant
contribution of the innate immune system to disease. An underlying role for the vasculature as a target of the immune system and
its products has gained renewed interest as an important component of lupus pathogenesis. Together, these recent advances provide
important insights into how the intersection of genetic diversity
with environmental triggers amplifies immune system activation
and target organ vulnerability to generate the classic manifestations
of lupus and its clinically significant comorbidities.

## Historic View of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Pathogenesis

Insights into the immunopathogenic mechanisms that account for
development of autoimmunity in patients with SLE and the tissue
damage that results in disease have developed over many decades.
Progress has been informed by the scientific tools available at the
time, along with the astute observations of clinicians and research
scientists. Descriptions of the clinical manifestations of SLE have
defined a multisystem disease that typically involves skin and joints,
with renal, cardiac, and/or neurologic pathology often documented
with histologic or imaging studies. Alterations of blood vessels in
many organs were recognized as an important component of the disease process in early studies, with the pathognomonic “onion skinning” of splenic arterioles, cellular infiltration and damage to renal
glomeruli, and vasculopathy in skin and brain expressed clinically by
manifestations such as nephritis, livedo reticularis, and stroke.
The
lupus erythematosus (LE) cell, first described in 1948, suggested that
engulfment of components of cell nuclei targeted by serum antibodies, a mechanism that remains an important concept in considerations of lupus pathogenesis, was active at sites of inflammation.
Characterization of antibodies specific for DNA and for
nucleic acid–associated proteins (e.g., histones and components of the spliceosome) in the sera of patients with SLE
directed the attention of researchers toward the immune system.
Pathologic studies of kidneys from patients with lupus
nephritis showing glomerular deposition of immunoglobulin
and complement components, combined with the elution of
anti-DNA autoantibodies from lupus kidneys, contributed
to the concept that autoantibodies, particularly anti-DNA

In spite of these considerable gains in elucidating the pathogenesis of SLE, the environmental triggers that initiate autoimmunity
in some individuals but not others are less well defined.
Clinical observations have pointed to exposure to sunlight, microbial
infection, and certain drugs as factors that can lead to initiation
or exacerbation of lupus. Whether there is a common molecular
change induced by these environmental triggers is not clear, but
ultraviolet (UV) light–mediated DNA damage, modification of
DNA methylation, and oxidation of mitochondrial DNA or other

Consortia of investigators have cooperated to share biologic
samples from large numbers of patients and control subjects to
define the genetic variants with a statistical strong association
with a diagnosis of SLE. These genome-wide association studies
(GWAS) have helped identify lupus-associated genes that encode
important components of molecular pathways that promote production of or response to type I IFN, alter the threshold for activation of lymphocytes, or generate immune stimuli.
In addition,
new concepts suggested by single-gene mutations that alter regulation of genome integrity and nucleic acid degradation, resulting in
lupus-like clinical syndromes, point to the significance of endogenous nucleic acids as candidate disease triggers.
Together, these scientific advances have led to drug development programs, with several resulting in approval of therapeutic
agents by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Belimumab, approved in 2011, was the first new therapy to be approved
for use in SLE since 1955. Others, including anifrolumab, FDA
approved in 2021, also target mechanisms affecting lupus disease
activity, with more in the pipeline. This chapter reviews recent
studies that have illuminated the immunopathogenesis of SLE.

## Genetic Contributions to Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Pathogenesis

Current concepts in genetic studies of complex human diseases,
including SLE, identify common variants with a small impact on
risk of disease, as well as low-frequency mutations that can have
a great impact on the risk for lupus or a lupus-like syndrome.
Observations of the occurrence of several individuals with SLE
within a family, along with a high frequency of concordance of
SLE in identical twins, have pointed to a strong genetic contribution to SLE. Data from a national health registry in Taiwan
suggest that the contribution of genetic heritability to occurrence
of SLE is 44%, with identical twins having a relative risk (RR) of
developing SLE of 316 and siblings a RR of 24 compared with
unrelated individuals.
More than 150 genetic variants, typically
identified by statistical analysis of single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in association with a diagnosis of SLE, identify
important molecular pathways implicated in lupus pathogenesis
(
Table 80.1
). A study based on the Immunochip SNP platform
identified more than 50 non–human leukocyte antigen (HLA)
risk variants and indicated that some of the variants differ between
Caucasians and African-Americans.
Additional loci have been
identified in studies that include Asian patients.
The risk for
development of SLE increases in a nonlinear manner with the
number of risk loci, and genetic risk scores have been developed
that are associated with early onset of disease, damage accrual, and
increased mortality.
While most lupus-associated variants are located in regulatory
rather than coding regions of the genome, many are near genes
encoding proteins implicated in immune system function, providing clues to important pathogenic mechanisms.
In parallel to
the requirements for immune system activation stimulated by foreign antigens, SLE-associated genes are involved in generation or
impaired clearance of self-antigen, activation of the innate immune
response, or activation of the adaptive immune response. Additional
genetic variants may affect target organ vulnerability (

## Female Predominance of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

A discussion of genetic contributions to SLE pathogenesis should
address the dramatic 9:1 female predominance of the disease. Of
all characteristic clinical features of lupus, it is the extreme sex
skewing that remains least understood. Hormonal contributions
to immune system activation are likely to represent a component
of the female predominance of the disease; estrogen can modulate lymphocyte and pDC activation, and prolactin is expressed at
increased levels in lupus serum.
In addition to the role of hormones, current concepts prioritize X chromosome–encoded genes
and the potential for alterations in X chromosome inactivation in
the female skewing of SLE. It is intriguing that Klinefelter’s syndrome, characterized by a 47,XXY genotype, is increased 14-fold
among men with SLE compared with men without SLE.
These
data suggest an X chromosome gene dose effect as an important
contributor to SLE pathogenesis. Identifying the X chromosome as a possible risk factor for SLE provides a basis for new
hypotheses regarding disease susceptibility. While the nature of
that risk remains undefined, the
TLR7
gene may be an important contributor to the augmented risk for SLE in females.
Murine studies support the impact of duplications of portions of
the X chromosome that encode the
TLR7
gene in activation of the
innate immune system, production of type I IFN, and generation
of autoimmunity, and a recent report of a gain-of-function variant
of
TLR7
in a young girl with SLE further supports the role of that
gene and its protein product.
Of note, data from study of a
humanized mouse model support roles for both estrogen and X
chromosome dosage in augmented pDC production of type I IFN
in response to TLR7 ligation.
The
TLR7
gene is located on a
region of the X chromosome in which escape from X chromosome
inactivation has been documented, and a study using single-cell
analysis indicates that biallelic expression of TLR7 variably occurs
in B cells from healthy women and is associated with increased
expression of TLR7 protein.
In addition to
TLR7, TASL
and
IRAK1,
both encoding components of the TLR7 signaling pathway, are also X chromosome genes that may promote development of SLE in females.
A potential pathogenic role for the distinct events that occur in
the ovary compared with those in the testes also deserves further
study. The onset of SLE typically occurs in the childbearing years,
after menarche and before menopause. A striking positive association between early menarche and SLE has been observed and
might be consistent with molecular and cellular events related to
ovulation.
In addition, ovarian reserve is diminished in women
with lupus irrespective of medical therapy.
Although not yet
fully understood, the carefully orchestrated demethylation and
remethylation of DNA that occur during oocyte maturation,
along with the expression of genomic retroelements, can mediate
oocyte atresia and may provide a source of stimulatory nucleic
acids that could drive immune activation.
The challenge in
pursuing such concepts is the obvious limitation on access to ovarian tissue for study, although murine models could be helpful.

## Environmental Triggers of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

The noted study from Taiwan indicates that heritability accounts
for approximately 44% of the risk of developing SLE, supporting
a strong genetic contribution but also allowing for both environmental factors and stochastic events (i.e., chance) in development
of SLE in an individual.
Although some environmental triggers
of disease have been identified on the basis of clinical observation
and epidemiologic studies, in general there is incomplete understanding of the range of factors that can induce disease and the
mechanisms by which they do so.
Socioeconomic factors
have been demonstrated to contribute to poor outcomes in lupus
patients.
The distinct contributions of low socioeconomic
status versus genetic factors that affect disease severity in certain
ethnic groups are difficult to dissect, although a recent study suggests that ancestry-related genetic factors may be most important
in determining lupus-related mortality.
Clinical manifestations that are often present at the time of
diagnosis, including fatigue and arthralgias, have suggested that a
virus might initiate the disease. In fact, epidemiologic studies demonstrating higher prevalence of antibodies specific for Epstein–Barr

virus (EBV) antigen in patients with SLE compared with the general population, as well as higher frequency of anti-EBV antibodies
in a military cohort before diagnosis of lupus, support a possible
role for that virus in disease pathogenesis.
In addition, SLE
patients demonstrate increased EBV DNA in their blood.
Several potential mechanisms might account for a pathogenic
role for EBV in SLE, including production of EBV-encoded
RNAs that induce type I IFN, risk allele-specific binding of
EBNA2 to genomic regulatory regions, utilization of B cell signaling pathways to promote B cell activation and differentiation,
and generation of autoantibodies reactive with DNA- or RNAbinding proteins through a molecular mimicry mechanism.
EBV-encoded small RNAs (EBERs) are expressed in cells latently
infected with EBV. EBERs induce expression of type I IFN after
binding to the dsRNA-dependent protein kinase (PKR) and activating signaling through a TLR-independent pathway.
EBNA2,
encoded in the EBV genome, can bind to genomic loci that have
been statistically associated with a diagnosis of SLE in a risk allelespecific manner, thus regulating the expression of relevant genes
that can alter immune system function.
This phenomenon
was also observed in relation to risk loci associated with other
autoimmune diseases, such as multiple sclerosis and rheumatoid
arthritis. A related mechanism implicates EBV transformation
of B cells in activating the long terminal repeats of endogenous
retroviruses, unleashing their capacity to serve as enhancers and
promoters.
Latent membrane protein 1, encoded by EBV, can
act as a mimic of cluster of differentiation (CD)40 and promote B cell dysfunction and autoimmunity in lupus-susceptible
mice.
A recent study demonstrates that antibodies specific for
the virus-encoded EBNA-1 protein can also react with dsDNA.
The molecular basis of this apparent cross-reactivity is not known,
although DNA can associate with EBNA-1 and might account
for the described antibody reactivity. T cell responses specific for
EBV have been studied in patients with lupus and were found
to be deficient, perhaps contributing to the increased numbers of
EBV-infected mononuclear cells and the increased copy number
of EBV DNA found in SLE patients.
Although EBV may or may not be an important etiologic agent
in SLE, recent studies of murine models of persistent infection
with lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus provide a useful system
for study of pathogenic mechanisms that appear highly relevant to
the immunopathogenesis of SLE.
Sustained production of type I
IFN and a shift from expansion of T effector cells to expansion of
T follicular helper cells accompanies the organ inflammation and
damage that characterize those models of persistent virus infection. In mice with prolonged production of IFN, blockade of the
type I IFN receptor abrogates tissue damage. Data from comparisons of immune system function in patients with HIV-1 infection
to macaques infected with the related but less lethal simian version of the virus further support a view of chronic production of
type I IFN as an important mechanism that promotes widespread
inflammation similar to that seen in patients with lupus.
These
models of chronic viral infection provide a guide for further study
of key mechanisms that sustain disease in patients with lupus.
There is also growing interest in a potential contribution of
endogenous bacteria in initiating or exacerbating autoimmunity and
SLE. Proposed roles for the microbiome in generating autoimmunity and inflammation relevant to SLE include T cell and antibody
cross-reactivity/molecular mimicry between commensal bacteria
expressing Ro-related epitopes and the human Ro antigen.
AntiRo antibodies are characteristic of SLE although not specific for
that disease. In a provocative study, translocation of commensal gut
bacteria to the liver in lupus-susceptible mice induced anti-DNA
antibodies, an IFN signature, and tissue inflammation.
Documentation of DNA specific for the implicated microbe,
Enterococcus gallinarum,
in liver samples from some patients with SLE
or autoimmune hepatitis provides some support for the potential
clinical relevance of the intriguing mouse findings. A study of ribosomal RNA sequences of bacteria present in fecal specimens from
SLE patients demonstrated enrichment of
Ruminococcus gnavus
and
an association of that microbe with disease activity and anti-DNA
antibodies.
Impaired mucosal barrier integrity may also contribute to systemic circulation of gut bacteria or their products.
Environmental toxins are of interest, yet their potential role
in lupus pathogenesis has not been comprehensively explored.
Data support current smoking as a risk factor for SLE, with a
dose response relating pack-years of smoking to risk, and the relationship between smoking and anti-dsDNA
SLE is particularly
strong.
As is currently proposed with regard to pathogenesis
of rheumatoid arthritis, smoking might provide an inflammatory
stimulus to epithelial or mononuclear cells in the lungs, promoting
protein modification or nonspecific inflammation. In contrast to
cigarette smoking, moderate alcohol consumption is a negative risk
factor for SLE.
Data from the Nurses’ Health Study identified
early menarche (age 10 or younger), use of post-menopausal hormones, and use of oral contraceptives as associated with a significant RR of incident SLE (RR, 2.1, 1.9, and 1.5, respectively),
although placebo-controlled trials showed that oral contraceptives
did not increase the rate of lupus flare, and hormone replacement
therapy in post-menopausal women did not increase the rate of
severe flare over 12 months and only modestly increased risk of a
mild or moderate disease flare.
Data support residential pesticides as a contributor to SLE in African-American women,
and
silica is also supported as a pathogenic factor in SLE on the basis of
its known capacity to serve as an adjuvant.
Data from analysis
of the Black Women’s Health Study additionally implicate obesity
during teenage years with later diagnosis of SLE,
and interesting data from the study of relatives of lupus patients identified less
than 7 hours/night of sleep as associated with transition to SLE.
Two well-described triggers of lupus, UV light and certain
drugs, are likely to promote lupus pathogenesis through their
effects on DNA. UV light induces DNA breaks that might alter
gene expression or lead to apoptotic or necrotic cell death. Even
in the absence of cell death, DNA breaks or prolonged maintenance of DNA–protein cross-links might provide an adjuvant or
antigenic stimulus to the immune system. In that regard, recent
data show UV-induced generation of cyclobutane pyrimidine
dimers and induction of IFN-stimulated genes in cells from individuals heterozygous for mutations in
RNASEH2
genes encoding
enzymes that remove ribonucleotides from genomic DNA.
Altered DNA methylation has been proposed as a likely mechanism of drug-induced lupus, as can be observed in the setting
of treatment with procainamide or hydralazine.
In the case
of hydralazine, the drug inhibits extra-cellular signal-regulated
kinase (ERK) pathway signaling, resulting in decreased expression of DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) 1 and DNMT3a, two
enzymes that mediate DNA methylation. Modification of ERK
signaling can be mediated by the effects of oxidizing agents on
protein kinase C delta, an upstream regulator of ERK.
Altered
DNA methylation and other epigenetic modifications can alter
gene expression or prime cells for increased reactivity to subsequent stimuli.
Decreased DNA methylation can also generate nucleic acids enriched in endogenous retroelements with
the potential to activate nucleic acid sensors, promoting immune

system activation.

Generalized lymphopenia is typical of SLE, but expansions of
specific T cell populations have been described. The T follicular
helper population, characterized by expression of ICOS, CXCR5,
and Bcl-6 and by production of IL-21, mediates important signals
that promote differentiation of autoantigen-specific B cells.
In
recent studies a related T cell subset termed T peripheral helper
cells (Tph), expressing high levels of PD-1, was identified early
in the course of SLE and is active in driving B cell differentiation.
The elevated levels of type I IFN in most SLE patients
are likely to contribute to the differentiation of Tph cells.
Type I IFN also promotes altered mitochondrial function in an
expanded population of CD8

In addition, analysis of

The regulation and function of Tregs, which have the capacity
to suppress immune responses, and Th17 cells, which promote
inflammation by production of IL-17, have been studied in recent
years, although most data derive from murine models.
Some
studies of lupus patients do show a relative depletion of Tregs and
increased Th17 cells and IL-17.
The functional impact of those
alterations in human lupus is still not clear, but it is presumed that
augmenting Treg numbers and/or function could be beneficial in
controlling clinical disease. Data are beginning to emerge from
studies of low-dose IL-2, an approach that may augment Tregs
and reduce disease activity.
While secretion of IL-2 by lupus
T cells is impaired, those cells produce cytokines that promote
macrophage activation, such as IFN-gamma, and those that contribute to B cell differentiation, including IL-21.
Production of autoantibodies specific for nucleic acids or
nucleic acid–binding proteins is a fundamental feature of lupus
pathogenesis, although the efficient presentation of specific selfantigens to T cells and production of pro-inflammatory cytokines
are additional functions of B cells that contribute to disease. Progress in investigation of the mechanisms that regulate activation
and differentiation of autoantibody-producing cells has identified
receptors, signaling pathways, and cytokines that drive those cells.
BLyS produced by myeloid cells, IL-10, and IL-21 are among the
candidate therapeutic targets that can modify the B cell differentiation program, as demonstrated by successful clinical studies of
BLyS blockade by belimumab.
In addition, efforts are underway to determine whether alterations in B cell tolerance mechanisms might contribute to autoimmunity in SLE.
The baseline activation status of lupus B cells, as well as their
response to antigen stimulation, is altered compared with B cells
from healthy individuals, with lupus-associated genetic variants
in several kinases, phosphatases, and adaptor molecules such as
BLK, BANK, and PTPN22 potential contributors to altered B
cell function. Studies of the risk variant of
PTPN22
in B cells
from healthy participants point to the influence of that lupusassociated variant on impaired counterselection of self-reactive
B cells.
LYN
deficiency in mice is associated with a lupus-like
phenotype, and similar deficiencies in the LYN kinase have been
described in lupus B cells.
The gene encoding the inhibitory
Fc receptor,
FCGR2B,
is polymorphic, and several variants have
been associated with SLE. SLE B cells show decreased expression
of this FcR, particularly on marginal zone B cells, supporting B
cell hyperactivation.
While studies are limited, there are some
data to support altered B cell tolerance mechanisms in lupus bone
marrow, with production of type I IFN in neutrophils or mesenchymal stem cells impairing B cell tolerance.
Current investigations of antibody-producing cells are defining several categories of B lineage cells that contribute to autoimmunity and disease. A recently described B cell population, the
so-called
age-associated B cells (ABCs)
, is dependent on the transcription factors T-bet and IRF5 and is enriched in patients with
lupus and other autoimmune diseases.
Signaling through
TLR7 is important for differentiation of those cells, extending the
significant role of that TLR in lupus pathogenesis from induction
of type I IFN in pDCs to an association with B cell function and
autoimmunity, and IL-21 is also required.
ABCs can differentiate into plasmablasts, producing anti-dsDNA autoantibodies, or
plasma cells, more likely the producers of autoantibodies specific
for RNA-binding proteins.
Long-lived plasma cells that are
maintained by chemokines and stromal cell products in protective niches in the bone marrow are proposed as sources of those
lupus autoantibodies, such as anti-Smith (Sm) and anti-Ro that
are chronically maintained at fairly constant levels. Those antibodies have been relatively refractory to modulation by immunosuppressive or B cell depletion therapy, although current trials of
CD19-targeted chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells may be
more successful
In contrast, circulating pre-plasma cells or
plasmablasts are sources of anti-dsDNA antibodies that fluctuate
in some patients in association with variations in disease activity
and are more amenable to anti–B cell therapy, such as belimumab,
targeting BLyS.
The T and B cell alterations that have been described in studies
of lupus immune function reflect multiple genetic variations that
play out in altering the threshold for cell activation and efficiency
of cell signaling. They are also the result of interactions among
those lymphocytes, along with antigen-presenting cells and their
products that, with time, amplify the overall level of immune activation. Increased production of type I IFN and presence of antinuclear antibodies in healthy family members of lupus patients
may reflect the consequences of genetic variations that are among
the factors that predispose to SLE. When the baseline setpoint of
immune system activity encounters environmental triggers, conditions are in place to generate the characteristic T and B cell autoimmunity that is required for tissue damage and disease. The triggers,
potentially viral, that generate the particular features of autoimmunity specific to SLE, the targeting of autoantibodies to nucleic
acids, and their associated proteins are as yet not well defined.

Anti-Ro, anti-La, and anti-RNP antibodies are
characteristic of SLE but are also seen in other systemic autoimmune diseases. Lupus autoantibodies can be categorized in relation to their targets, including DNA and DNA-binding proteins,
typically aggregated in nucleosomes that contain histones; RNA
and RNA-associated proteins, typically aggregated in cytoplasmic or nuclear RNP particles; phospholipids exposed in plasma
membranes and associated proteins such as
β
-glycoprotein I; and
cell membrane proteins, typically those expressed in blood cells.
Some of these self-antigens typically targeted by lupus autoantibodies are most likely accessed by antigen-presenting cells in the
form of cell membrane–enclosed blebs derived from apoptotic
cells or microparticles, small aggregates of cellular material derived
from cells and generated after flipping of phosphatidylserine to
the outer aspect of the cell membrane in the setting of cell activation or apoptosis. Although some patients who present with a
clinical picture characteristic of SLE do not have significant titers
of those autoantibodies, it is likely that those patients have undefined autoimmunity targeted at unspecified antigens.
It is not possible to identify the earliest step in the generation of
pathogenic autoantibodies with certainty, and in fact it is probable
that the generation of autoimmunity can begin in any number
of ways. Presentation of self-antigen by an excessively activated
antigen-presenting cell, stochastic activation of low-avidity selfreactive T cells that are present in healthy individuals but have the
potential to expand and drive differentiation of B cells with broad
specificity for self-antigens, or direct activation of self-reactive B
cells in the presence of activating TLR ligands, such as those provided by demethylated CpG-rich DNA from bacteria or viruses or
self-derived nuclear debris, might all be starting points for development of pathogenic autoimmunity. As noted earlier, microbial
epitopes present on endogenous bacteria or virus-encoded proteins that associate with host particles might also represent an initiating point for generation of autoimmunity.
The process
might begin with production of type I IFN by a virus-infected cell
or a cell with impaired degradation of intra-cellular nucleic acids,
altering the threshold for activation of the immune system by
subsequent exposures to self-antigens. Oxidative or UV-mediated
DNA damage by environmental stressors might synergize with
altered maintenance of genome integrity to jump start immune
activation. Less important than the precise starting point is the
observation that autoimmunity develops and builds with time.
The presence of autoantibodies can be observed more than 5 years
before clinical manifestations of disease appear, and the scope of
autoantibody specificities expands with time.
Anti-Ro antibodies typically present earliest, anti-dsDNA antibodies appear several
years later, and anti-Sm, the antibody specificity most specific for
a diagnosis of SLE, appears approximately at the time of clinical
diagnosis. This pattern, along with studies from murine models
that demonstrated determinant spreading of autoantibody specificities not only to distinct proteins within a nucleic acid–protein
particle but also to other self-antigen targets, provides clues to
lupus pathogenesis that are not yet understood but hold promise for future breakthroughs in elucidating important pathogenic
mechanisms.
A shift from a predominant polyclonal immunoglobulin (Ig)
M picture toward polyclonal IgG, driven by T cell help and cytokines, occurs with time in most patients with lupus and with
development of tissue pathology and damage. In fact, some IgM
antibodies that have self-reactivity are viewed as protective, with
the switch from IgM to IgG or IgA representing an important
point of altered immune regulation that contributes to the immunopathogenesis of SLE.
In addition to immunoglobulin class, with class-switched
antibodies better able to access extravascular spaces compared
with IgM antibodies, the amino acid sequence and charge of the
antigen-binding site can influence pathogenicity. Arginines in the
CDR3 region of anti-dsDNA antibodies are characteristic and
influence binding to their DNA target. Molecular mimicry is a
concept that has been applied to reactivity of antibodies specific
for a microbial protein that also react with self-antigens, but the
concept can also be applied to those antibodies that unexpectedly
bind to two distinct self-antigens. For example, some anti-dsDNA
antibodies were found to bind to a peptide that is a feature of
glutamate receptors on CNS neurons.
Beyond features of Ig
class and antigen-binding site that influence access to tissue and
affinity of binding, glycosylation and complement-fixing capacity
are important determinants of an antibody’s capacity to bind to Fc
receptors and promote complement activation, resulting in target
cell death or inflammation.
The role of SLE autoantibodies in the pathogenesis of the disease has traditionally focused on the deposition of immune complexes in skin, renal glomerulus, and other sites of tissue injury,
along with a potential contribution of direct targeting of antibodies to local or “planted” antigens. With the recognition that
nucleic acid–containing immune complexes can directly induce
cell signaling and new gene transcription after accessing endosomal TLRs, an additional important pathogenic role for autoantibodies as immune modulators has been defined.
Particular lupus autoantibody specificities have been associated
with specific clinical manifestations of disease. Perhaps the bestdeveloped characterization of the mechanisms by which an autoantibody mediates disease is the role of maternal-derived anti-Ro
antibody in neonatal lupus syndrome. After transplacental transfer of anti-Ro antibody, RNA-containing immune complexes are
thought to form, activating the production of cytokines that contribute to fibrosis of the cardiac conduction system.
This scenario, similar to that which mediates production of type I IFN by
pDCs, identifies nucleic acid–autoantibody immune complexes as
regulators of cytokines that mediate tissue damage. Anti-Ro and
anti-La antibodies, although common to several autoimmune diseases, are also characteristic in association with sicca symptoms,

subacute cutaneous lupus, and, as noted, in neonatal lupus. AntiRNP antibodies are seen in both SLE and mixed connective tissue
disease.
Antiphospholipid antibodies, with lupus anti-coagulant the
most informative antibody profile, contribute to placental damage and vascular thrombosis, as well as thrombocytopenia in the
antiphospholipid syndrome and in some patients with lupus.
Studies in a murine model and supported by data from patients
indicate that tissue damage and thrombosis depend on inflammation promoted by activation of the complement system by
the antiphospholipid antibodies.
Similar mechanisms might
be relevant in forms of lupus renal disease characterized by
microangiopathy.

## Mechanisms of Target Organ Damage

Clinical disease is ultimately a reflection of tissue damage mediated by the cells and soluble products of the immune system,
along with an exaggerated or aberrant tissue repair response.
The classic view of the mechanisms that result in tissue damage
involves activation of the complement system by immune complexes deposited in tissue, as well as release of products of phagocytes, including enzymes released from neutrophil granules and
reactive oxygen intermediates from macrophages. Recent studies
in mouse and human systems involving extensive analysis of renal
infiltrating cells and their transcriptomes at various points in the
disease process have identified monocyte populations that mediate inflammation and contribute to sclerosis and organ dysfunction.
Infiltrating T and B cells present in the kidney interstitium
can also contribute to renal damage. Data from both murine lupus
models and kidney tissue from patients with lupus nephritis demonstrate the cell-related transcriptomes associated with transition
from acute to chronic nephritis, with a robust renal tubule transcriptome associated with resistance to development of chronic
disease.
The role of the vasculature as a significant target organ
contributing to disease in lupus involves altered structure and
function of both venous and arterial vessels. Periarteriolar concentric onion skinning is seen in the spleen, microangiopathy
and associated microthrombi are observed in some organs, and
endothelial dysfunction is associated with premature atherosclerosis in lupus patients. Recent studies have focused on the
potential role of type I IFN on endothelial cells and endothelial
cell progenitor cells and have postulated that increased production of IFN is at least one contributor to impaired vascular
repair and cardiovascular disease in SLE.
A contribution
of type I IFN to vascular injury supplements the previously
described role of complement activation products C3a and C5a
and expression of endothelial cell surface adhesion molecules
E-selectin, vascular cell adhesion molecule 1, and intercellular
adhesion molecule 1 in lupus flares. A role for granulocytes
and pro-inflammatory lipids has also been proposed.
Podocytes are specialized epithelial cells and components of
the renal glomerulus that, when damaged, contribute to the
proteinuria characteristic of lupus nephritis.
Current studies
are investigating the signaling pathways and podocyte products that might represent therapeutic targets for patients with
lupus nephritis.
Mechanisms of CNS lupus have extended
the concept that autoantibodies with dual specificity for DNA
and the NMDA receptor contribute to excitotoxic cell death to
consider the contribution of cytokines to neuronal damage and
clinical manifestations of CNS damage.

## Conclusion

Gains in understanding the mechanisms of lupus pathogenesis have established the knowledge base that will underlie
future advances in development of targeted therapies to achieve
improved patient outcomes.
As technologies evolve, they will
be used by investigators to gain deeper insights into lupus disease mechanisms and to identify additional therapeutic targets.
Single-cell transcriptome and immune repertoire analysis, use of
spatial transcriptomics to characterize cell distribution within
involved tissues, and application of long-read sequencing for
characterization of genomic regions harboring lupus-associated
genetic variants can facilitate our understanding of mechanistic
and effector contributions to disease. The recognition of a central role for innate immune system activation, including the role
of type I IFN, in lupus pathogenesis is a major advance. Rapid
progress in characterizing the genetic variants that are associated with a diagnosis of lupus has provided strong evidence that
alterations in molecular pathways that regulate genome integrity,
nucleic acid degradation, TLR-dependent and -independent
innate immune signaling, and lymphocyte activation thresholds
and signaling efficiency are important mechanisms that determine disease susceptibility. The products of the immune system,
including autoantibodies and their immune complexes, cytokines, and complement components, along with pro-inflammatory mediators and reactive oxygen products released from
neutrophils and macrophages, remain key mediators of tissue
damage. However, an additional pathogenic role for nucleic
acid–containing immune complexes as immune modulators
through their important capacity to access and activate endosomal TLRs and insights into regulation of endogenous cytoplasmic nucleic acids are important concepts that can guide
future therapeutic approaches. Defining and then targeting the
components of the innate immune system involved in activation
of the TLR and TLR-independent pathways, along with modulation of pathogenic T cell help for B cell differentiation to autoantibody-producing cells, is a rational therapeutic strategy given
our current understanding of pathogenic mechanisms. It seems
likely that no one therapeutic approach will be sufficient to control this disease, but rather therapies that target components of
both innate and adaptive immune systems may be required to
achieve improved patient outcomes. In the end, prevention of
disease is an important goal. Efforts to apply the many lessons
learned relevant to pathogenic mechanisms of SLE to assessment
of patients at risk for disease development might result in design
of preventive strategies.
