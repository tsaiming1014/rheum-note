# Rheumatic Paraneoplastic Syndromes and Malignancies With Rheumatic Manifestation

LAURA C. CAPPELLI AND AMI A. SHAH

KEY POINTS

> Rheumatic paraneoplastic syndromes are rare but can have enormous

> clinical impact on diagnosis and outcome of malignancies.

> Knowledge about their typical features facilitates early diagnosis and

> timely initiation of anti-tumor therapy.

> If malignant cells can be eliminated successfully, the symptoms of the

> paraneoplastic syndrome usually subside.

> Paraneoplastic symptoms can have a substantial influence on morbidity

> and mortality of patients with cancer.

> Musculoskeletal paraneoplastic syndromes can provide insight into

> common immunopathological mechanisms of rheumatic and

> malignant diseases.

> Malignancies, particularly hematologic, can cause a variety of

> musculoskeletal symptoms that may be referred to rheumatologists.

> Similarly, treatments for cancer such as chemotherapy and radiation

> can also cause rheumatic symptoms. Knowing these associations

> can improve timely diagnosis and management.

## Introduction

The relationship between cancer and rheumatic diseases is complex and bidirectional with many potential mechanisms linking
the two entities. As discussed in
Chapters 37, 129, and 130
, a
persistent inflammatory state and damage from rheumatic diseases
or cytotoxic and biologic therapies employed to treat rheumatologic conditions may increase the risk of subsequent malignancy;
similarly, malignancy or its treatment may induce autoimmunity
or rheumatic manifestations. In this chapter, we describe salient
features of rheumatic paraneoplastic syndromes that should alert
clinicians to the possibility of an underlying malignancy. We also
describe musculoskeletal and rheumatic symptoms that may be
related to malignancy and/or its therapy.
Rheumatic paraneoplastic syndromes are rare but have characteristic features that can be of clinical importance for early detection and therapy of an occult malignancy (
Table 128.1
). They
are not directly caused by the tumor itself or its metastases but
are mediated by soluble factors, such as hormones and cytokines,
or humoral or cellular immune mechanisms against tumor cells.
Therefore the clinical manifestation of paraneoplastic syndromes
occurs distant from the underlying malignancy and can involve
joints, fasciae, muscles, vessels, and bones.
To classify a rheumatic disease as truly paraneoplastic, a causal
relationship between the malignant disease and the musculoskeletal pathology has to be demonstrated. For the argument of causality over mere coincidence, a set of criteria was proposed more
than 50 years ago, called the Bradford Hill Criteria.
In the case
of paraneoplastic syndromes, besides strength and specificity of an
association between tumor diagnosis and rheumatic symptoms,
their temporal relationship is the main critical issue. A syndrome
is generally considered paraneoplastic when its manifestations
appear either simultaneously or in some studies within 3 years
of the detection of the malignancy. The best evidence for causation is established in retrospect, when it is possible to completely
eliminate a tumor and the rheumatic symptoms undergo full
remission.
In addition to these distinct paraneoplastic syndromes, certain cancers may more directly lead to symptoms that are similar
to rheumatic diseases. Tumors can arise from mesenchymal cells
in bone, cartilage, muscles, or connective tissues, or the musculoskeletal system can become invaded by metastatic spreading
of tumors from other locations or infiltration by hematologic
malignancies.
Finally, certain cancer therapies can induce rheumatic manifestations. Of particular interest to the rheumatologist are musculoskeletal symptoms caused by aromatase inhibitors,
chemotherapy,
radiation, and Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG)–induced reactive arthritis,
as detailed in this chapter. Most recently, various
autoimmune phenomena have developed after treatment with
immune checkpoint inhibitors, as discussed in
Chapter 130
.

## Palmar Fasciitis and Polyarthritis Syndrome

Recognizing a potential paraneoplastic syndrome in a patient
presenting with arthritis is more straightforward when in addition to synovitis other characteristic features are present. This is
the case in palmar fasciitis and polyarthritis syndrome (PFPAS),
where inflammation of palmar and/or plantar fasciae leads to
a very distinctive clinical presentation (
Fig. 128.1A
). PFPAS
is characterized by a symmetric acute, diffuse, painful swelling
of both hands caused by polyarthritis of fingers and wrists in
combination with palmar fasciitis. This syndrome presents with
marked thickening and induration of palmar tissues and rapidly
progressive flexion contractures of the fingers. The findings upon
palpation are described by the term “woody hands.” Arthritis of
other joints can be present but is usually milder; plantar involvement occurs in about 20%.
Of reported cases of PFPAS, more
than half of them occur in association with ovarian adenocarcinoma or other tumors of urogenital organs. Frequently, tumor
markers such as CA125 or CA19-9 are positive and a diagnostic
clue.
The pathogenetic mechanisms of PFPAS have not been
resolved; however, a potential role of connective tissue growth
factor has repeatedly been proposed.
In addition, there exists
one anecdotal report about the induction of PFPAS by treatment
with a metalloprotease inhibitor.
In patients with suspected
PFPAS, physicians should consider checking CA125, CA19-9,
and/or prostate-specific antigen and performing imaging of the
abdomen and pelvis if no known malignancy has been identified in the affected patient. Treating PFPAS proves difficult if
no malignancy is found. Glucocorticoids and disease-modifying
anti-rheumatic drugs have little effect; physical and occupational
therapy may improve function.
If no malignancy is found at
the time of presentation, one should consider repeating screening in 6 to 12 months.

## Remitting Seronegative Symmetric Synovitis With Pitting Edema

Remitting seronegative symmetric synovitis with pitting edema
(RS3PE) occurs in an older population (over the age of 50), more
commonly in men, and is characterized by symmetric edematous
swelling of the dorsum of the hands and feet
(
Fig. 128.1B
). Pitting edema of the hands is present in over 80% of reported cases,
while less than 40% have pitting edema of the feet.
Response
to corticosteroids is usually excellent, with a good prognosis.
Few patients develop erosions on joint imaging (only 5.5% in
one systematic review).
In pooled data from various European
and American studies the malignancy rate was estimated to be as
high as 31%
; a more recent meta-analysis of 331 cases found
malignancy in 16%.
In a Japanese cohort of 33 patients with
RS3PE, eight developed cancer within 2 years after the onset of
their arthritis; the patients with cancer had a significant elevation
of their serum matrix metalloproteinase 3 levels.
In general, in
any patient with RS3PE, a poor response to corticosteroids should
be considered a red flag and prompt a search for an occult malignancy. Patients typically respond to lower doses of steroids over a
2- to 3-month course; the average was 16 mg prednisone-equivalent daily in one systematic literature review.
Rheumatologists
can make sure patients’ age-appropriate cancer screening is up to
date upon diagnosis of RS3PE and consider further imaging based
on any additional symptoms present.

## Pancreatic Panniculitis and Polyarthritis

Polyarthritis in association with a panniculitis resembling erythema nodosum occurs in patients with pancreatitis (hence the
acronym PPP) and high levels of serum lipase.
This syndrome
is more common in men than in women. The same manifestations can be seen in acinar cell carcinoma, a subtype of pancreatic
cancer, which also leads to high concentrations of lipase in the
circulation. This causes extensive necrotic lesions of subcutaneous
fat tissues with a surrounding inflammatory reaction. The polyarthritis is typically symmetric and most commonly affects the
ankles, knees, wrists, and metacarpophalangeal joints. A review
of 59 cases of PPP found pancreatic malignancy in 11.9%. In
more than 130 publications, PPP is consistently associated with
a dismal prognosis.
There is generally a poor response to nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs and corticosteroids; treatment
should be focused on the underlying pancreatic disease.

## Erythromelalgia

Erythromelalgia is a syndrome characterized by attacks of severe
burning, erythema, and warmth of the extremities. In most cases
erythromelalgia involves the feet
(
Fig. 128.1C
). Symptoms
are exacerbated when the extremities are placed in a dependent
position, during ambulation, or during exposure to increased
temperatures. This disorder can be idiopathic or secondary to
another disease like a malignancy.
Myeloproliferative disorders, such as polycythemia vera and essential thrombocytosis, are
the most common conditions associated with erythromelalgia
and precede its diagnosis onset.
In one retrospective study,
however, only about 9% of patients with erythromelalgia had a
concomitant myeloproliferative disorder.
Because of the association with myeloproliferative syndrome, a thorough hematologic
examination with complete blood cell counts with differential,
peripheral blood smear and flow cytometry, and serum protein
electrophoresis and immunofixation is recommended. If abnormal labs are present, consider bone marrow aspiration. The underlying pathophysiology of erythromelalgia remains unclear. Platelet
breakdown products and platelet microthrombus formation may
trigger inflammation, especially in the context of erythromelalgia
associated with myeloproliferative disorders.
In primary erythromelalgia, microvascular arteriovenous shunts have been proposed
to cause this syndrome.
In the largest published cohort, 168
patients were identified with erythromelalgia: 31.9% of patients
reported worsening of disease, 26.6% reported no change, 30.9%
reported improvement, and 10.6% reported complete resolution
of symptoms.
The most effective therapy seems to be the use of
daily aspirin, leading to a significant relief of symptoms. A host
of other therapies have been tried with varying success.
Topical therapies include lidocaine, amitriptyline, and ketamine.

## Hypertrophic (Pulmonary) Osteoarthropathy

Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy (HOA) or Marie-Bamberger syndrome is a classic musculoskeletal paraneoplastic syndrome and
has frequently been described and reviewed in the rheumatologic
literature. It is characterized by two distinctive symptoms: (1)
the clubbing of distal phalanges of fingers and/or toes and (2) an
inflammatory, proliferative periostitis causing bone and joint pain
and occasionally synovitis and effusion.
The predominant
locations are tibia and fibula, knees, and ankles. This periosteal
inflammation causes osteoblast activation, which is detectable by
increased tracer uptake in bone scans and lamellar ossification of
the periosteum along tubular bones in conventional radiography.
In retrospective analyses, the frequency of HOA in primary lung
cancer was close to 1%; however, HOA can also occur occasionally in other types of malignancies (mainly intrathoracic).
Because of the characteristic acral fibroblast and periosteal osteoblast activation, this syndrome was initially thought to be a variant of acromegaly. Now it has become clear that platelet-derived
growth factor or vascular endothelial growth factor produced by
tumor cells can both contribute to the pathogenesis of HOA.
Removing the tumor or other underlying cause often improves
symptoms but is not always effective.
In refractory disease,
bisphosphonates like zolendronic acid and pamidronate have been
shown to be effective in case reports.
Upon a new diagnosis of
HOA, physicians should consider obtaining high-resolution computed tomography of the chest to evaluate for thoracic malignancy.

## Eosinophilic Fasciitis

Eosinophilic fasciitis (EF) is a fibrosing disorder of the fascia and
skin that can be associated with malignancy.
It is often accompanied by peripheral eosinophilia, but peripheral eosinophilia or
tissue eosinophilia are not necessary for the diagnosis. EF typically causes erythema and edema of the skin initially followed by
woody induration.
This can lead to a peau d’orange appearance to the skin
(
Fig. 128.1D
). It primarily involves the limbs
and trunk, sparing the hands and face. Patients may experience
venous grooving due to induration of involved subcutaneous tissues (
Fig. 128.1D
). They often have myalgias and more rarely
can have inflammatory arthritis. Evaluation of suspected EF can
include checking muscle enzymes (CK, aldolase), complete blood
count with differential, magnetic resonance imaging of an affected
limb, and full-thickness biopsy down to fascia.
As many as 10%
of patients with EF have an associated hematologic disorder.
The hematologic disorders associated with EF include lymphomas, leukemias, immune-mediated cytopenias, and myeloproliferative disorders. In evaluating patients with new EF, clinicians
should ask about any associated symptoms such as night sweats,
fevers, or significant weight loss and obtaining a complete blood
count with differential on laboratory studies. Additionally, a
serum protein electrophoresis and flow cytometry to evaluate for
large granular lymphocyte leukemia can be performed. Treatment
of EF usually starts with corticosteroids at doses of 0.5 to 1 mg/kg
with a slow taper.
In patients who are unable to taper steroids,
methotrexate, mycophenolate, and intravenous immunoglobulin
have been used.

## Tumor-Induced Osteomalacia

Tumor-induced osteomalacia (TIO) is clinically indistinguishable from other forms of osteomalacia, with progressive bone
pain, spontaneous fractures, muscle weakness, and fatigue. The
biochemical pattern consists of hypophosphatemia, hyperphosphaturia, normal calcium and parathyroid hormone, normal to
low levels of calcitriol, and an increased alkaline phosphatase.
The reason for the imbalance in phosphate-calcium metabolism
is marked renal phosphate wasting, due to high concentrations of
circulating fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23, phosphatonin).
FGF23 inhibits the reabsorption of phosphate in the proximal
tubule and suppresses osteoblast differentiation and matrix mineralization in the bone.
In the majority of cases, FGF23 is produced by a mesenchymal tumor (mixed connective tissue variant),
with other histologies (hemangiopericytoma, giant cell tumor,
osteosarcoma) being considerably less frequent.
From over 300
cases of TIO, an intraosseous tumor was demonstrated in 40%,
and in 55% the neoplasm was found in the soft tissue. Only 8%
of the tumors were malignant.
A successful localization and
removal of the neoplasm usually results in a complete recovery
from all symptoms. The majority of patients in one systematic
review also required phosphate supplementation and administration of active vitamin D.

## Cancer-Associated Myositis and Scleroderma

Both myositis and scleroderma can present in the paraneoplastic
setting, that is, with tight temporal association between diagnosis
of the autoimmune disease and a malignancy. The clinical features
of myositis and scleroderma are described in detail elsewhere in
this book (
Chapters 85 and 86
).
In dermatomyositis, the temporal association of cancer and
myositis onset has been known for decades.
Dermatomyositis is more commonly associated with cancer than polymyositis
or immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy. In paraneoplastic
myositis, cancers are typically diagnosed within 3 years before
or after myositis diagnosis. The autoantibodies most highly
associated with paraneoplastic myositis are anti-transcriptional
intermediary factor 1-
γ
(TIF1-
γ
) and anti-nuclear matrix protein-2; anti-3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase
or anti-small ubiquitin-like modifier 1-activating enzyme antibodies may have an association with cancer, but data are less
clear.
Patients with anti-TIF1-
γ
antibodies had higher rates
of breast and ovarian cancer than the general population in one
study.
In scleroderma, patients with anti-RNA-polymerase III antibodies and anti-RNPC3 antibodies are more likely to have shortinterval cancer, that is, a cancer diagnosis within 3 years before or
after their onset of scleroderma.
The types of tumors seen may
vary based on scleroderma subtype. In anti-RNA Pol III–positive patients, those with diffuse skin disease had increased breast,
prostate, and tongue cancer risk and those with limited skin disease had increased lung cancer risk compared with the general
population.
For both of these diseases, a link between genetic alterations
in the tumor and a concordant autoimmune response has been
described. In scleroderma, patients with cancer and anti-RNA
polymerase III antibodies were found to have mutations or loss
of heterozygosity in the
POLR3A
gene in their tumors with the
development of mutation and wild type peptide-specific CD4+
T cell immune responses and cross-reactive autoantibodies.
In

dermatomyositis, genetic alterations in
TIF1
genes sequenced
from tumors were found in patients with anti-TIF1-
γ
antibodies
and cancer.

## Malignancies With Musculoskeletal Manifestations

Some malignancies may present or be associated with joint, muscle, and soft tissue manifestations. A very common musculoskeletal symptom of malignancies is bone pain, which may be seen in
many cancers if they metastasize to or originate in bone. Multiple
myeloma in particular can commonly involve bone pain, and it
may be a presenting symptom in patients. In one study, nearly half
of patients presented with bone pain in the primary care setting
months before their multiple myeloma diagnosis.
Patients with lymphoma may have articular symptoms for several reasons. Synovitis or joint effusions may occur due to associated gout, as a reaction of nearby lymphomatous spread, or due
to infiltration of lymphoma into the synovium.
Lymphomas
may also have bony involvement and can be associated with pathologic fractures and bone pain. Finally, patients with certain types
of lymphoma can also present similarly to Still’s disease (angioimmunoblastic T cell lymphoma
) or granulomatosis with polyangiitis (extranodal diffuse large B cell lymphoma
).
Leukemia may present with either polyarthritis or arthralgia,
though musculoskeletal symptoms tend to be more common in
children than adults.
Large granular lymphocyte leukemia is
associated with RA and may even drive its development.
Myelodysplastic syndromes can also present with a variety of autoimmune abnormalities such as inflammatory arthritis, vasculitis,
neutrophilic dermatoses, and autoimmune cytopenias.

## Cancer Treatments and Rheumatic Manifestations

It is also important to be aware that certain medications and other
treatments used for cancer therapy can trigger rheumatic manifestations. Aromatase inhibitors are used commonly in hormone
receptor–positive breast cancer, often for several years of maintenance therapy. A common problem for patients on aromatase
inhibitors is developing arthralgias. The arthralgias typically
involve the wrists, hands, or knees and are symmetric.
Trigger
fingers and carpal tunnel syndrome have also been reported. As
many as 20% of patients will discontinue aromatase inhibitors
due to joint pain.
A variety of chemotherapies have been associated with scleroderma-like disease, Raynaud’s phenomenon, and
digital ischemia. These include bleomycin, gemcitabine, carboplatin, and paclitaxel.
Intravesical BCG therapy, used to treat
bladder carcinoma, can trigger a reactive arthritis.
For the most
part, the reactive arthritis improves with cessation of the BCG
therapy, though there are reports of some patients having chronic
symptoms. Immune checkpoint inhibitors, one type of cancer
immunotherapy, can cause a variety of rheumatic immune-related
adverse events, as detailed in
Chapter 130
.
Finally, radiation therapy for cancer may cause several side
effects similar to other rheumatic diseases. Head and neck radiation in particular can cause xerostomia that may be confused for
Sjögren’s syndrome. It can also trigger morphea or other fibrosing
syndromes. Due to the association of fibrosing syndromes with
radiation, there has been some concern about the safety of patients
with pre-existing scleroderma receiving radiation therapy for cancer. In one study of radiation therapy for breast cancer in scleroderma patients, about 50% developed localized skin thickening
in the radiation port, but there were no generalized flares of the
disease.

## Conclusion

Musculoskeletal paraneoplastic syndromes often precede other
clinical manifestations of neoplasms and can facilitate timely
diagnosis of a malignant disease. After successful elimination of
all malignant cells, paraneoplastic symptoms usually subside,
but some patients may have persistent symptoms. Paraneoplastic
symptoms can also have significant impact on quality of life, morbidity, and mortality of patients with cancer. In recent years, some
of the underlying mechanisms of paraneoplastic syndromes have
been elucidated, improving our understanding of the pathogenesis of rheumatic as well as neoplastic diseases. Additionally, some
malignancies may present with musculoskeletal symptoms that are
more directly related to the cancer, especially lymphoproliferative
disorders. Finally, certain treatments for malignancy can lead to
rheumatic disorders.
