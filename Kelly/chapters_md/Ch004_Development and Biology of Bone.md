## Structure and Composition of Bone

Bone is a specialized connective tissue that assists in (1) locomotion by providing the insertion site of the muscles; (2) protection of the internal organs; (3) bone marrow possession;
and (4) metabolic functions, such as calcium and phosphate
storage. Bone consists of four cell types: (1) osteoblasts, (2)
bone-lining cells, (3) osteocytes, and (4) osteoclasts in addition to an extra-cellular matrix, which is composed of type
I collagen fibers and a number of noncollagenous proteins
including glutamic acid (Gla)-containing proteins, glycoproteins, and proteoglycans. The specific composition of the bone
matrix allows for its mineralization, which is a specific feature
of bone.
The two major types of bones are (1) flat bones, which develop
through intramembranous ossification, and (2) long bones, which
emerge from endochondral ossification. Intramembranous bone
formation is based on the condensation of mesenchymal stem cells
that directly differentiate into bone-forming osteoblasts. In contrast, during endochondral ossification of the long bones, the mesenchymal stem cells first differentiate into chondrocytes making
a cartilage intermediate followed by differentiation of osteoblasts
producing bone. Long bones consist of the following: (1) epiphyses, which are protrusions at the ends of the long bones; (2) diaphysis, constituting the bone’s shaft; and (3) metaphyses, which are
located between the epiphysis and the diaphysis (
Fig. 4.1
). The
metaphysis is separated from the epiphysis by the growth plate,
a proliferative cartilage layer that is essential for the longitudinal
growth of bones. After the growth is completed, this cartilage layer
is entirely remodeled into bone.
The external shape of bones is formed by a dense cortical shell
(cortical or compact bone), which is particularly strong along the
diaphysis, where the bone marrow is located. The outer and inner
surfaces of cortical bone are covered by layers of osteogenic cells,
termed the
periosteum
and the
endosteum,
which are involved in
the growth of width by bone apposition at the periosteal sites
and bone resorption at the endosteal sites. The cortical bone shell
becomes progressively thinner toward the metaphyses and epiphyses, where most of the trabecular bone is located. Trabecular bone
(also called
cancellous bone
) is a sponge-like network consisting of
a myriad of highly interconnected bony trabeculae. The metaphysis transfers load from the weight-bearing joint to the diaphysis of
the bone and is critical for maintaining bone strength.
Although cortical and trabecular bone are composed of the
same cells and the same matrix components, there is a substantial difference between these two forms of skeletal tissue. Cortical
bone almost exclusively consists of mineralized tissue (up to 90%),
allowing it to fulfill its mechanical requirements. In contrast, only
20% of trabecular bone is mineralized tissue, with the bone marrow, blood vessels, and a network of mesenchymal stem cells constituting the remainder of the bone. As a consequence, trabecular
bone shares a vast surface with the nonmineralized tissue, which
is the basis for the metabolic function of bone, necessitating a
high level of communication between the bone surface and the
nonmineralized tissue.

### Bone Vasculature

The metabolic function of bone is dependent on access to requisite nutrients, oxygen, hormones, growth factors, and neurotransmitters. Bone marrow resident cells and mesenchymal stem cells
respond to bone mediators but can also produce metabolic factors
that are relevant for body homeostasis.
The exchange of such
mediators into and out of the bone is mediated by a complex vascular system.
Oxygen is supplied by nutrient arteries; these enter the bone
mainly at the metaphysis, run longitudinally along the bone shaft,
and ramify toward the inner surface of the cortical bone, the endosteum (

interconnect with sinusoidal capillaries. This fenestrated venous
vessel type allows a fast exchange of metabolites between the blood

### Bone Matrix

The key protein component of bone is type I collagen. Collagen
fibers follow specific directions, forming the basis for the lamellar
structure of bone. This lamellar structure, which can be visualized
when examining bone under polarized light, allows dense packaging, resulting in optimal resistance to mechanical load. The lamellar collagen structures can be assembled in parallel (e.g., along the
cortical bone surfaces and inside the bony trabeculae) or concentrically around blood vessels embedded in the Haversian channels
of the cortical bone. Upon rapid deposition of new bone, such
as during fracture healing, this lamellar structure is missing, and
the bone is then called
woven bone.
Woven bone is consecutively
remodeled into lamellar bone, which is also considered “mature”
bone. The composition of the collagen backbone also assists in
the deposition of spindle- or plate-shaped hydroxyapatite crystals,
which contain calcium phosphate, thus allowing the calcification
of the bone matrix.
In addition to type I collagen, noncollagenous proteins also
exist in bone. Some of them, such as osteocalcin, osteopontin, and
fetuin, are mineralization inhibitors, which serve to balance the
degree of bone mineralization. Aside from their intrinsic function
in bone, noncollagenous proteins also exert important metabolic
functions, such as the control of energy metabolism by osteocalcin.

### Bone Cells

Osteoblasts
Osteoblasts are the bone-forming cells that derive from mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs). MSCs also form chondrocytes, myocytes,
and adipocytes. Osteoblasts are cuboid-shaped cells that form
clusters covering the bone surface. They are metabolically highly
active, synthesizing the collagenous and noncollagenous bone
matrix proteins that are excreted and then deposited between the
osteoblasts and the bone surface. This newly built matrix, which is
not yet calcified, is termed the
osteoid.
The lag phase between osteoid deposition and its mineralization is approximately 10 days.
Osteoblast differentiation depends on the expression of two key
transcription factors: runt domain transcription factor 2 (Runx2)
and its target osterix-1. These transcription factors confer MSC
differentiation into osteoblasts in response to external stimuli.
Prostaglandin E
(PGE
), insulin-like growth factor (IGF)-
1, parathyroid hormone (PTH), bone morphogenic proteins
(BMPs), and Wingless (Wnt) and Int-1 proteins are key stimuli
for osteoblast differentiation. For instance, PGE
is an important
anabolic factor for bone and induces the expression of bone sialoprotein and alkaline phosphatase in MSCs. BMPs and transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
foster osteoblast differentiation
by activating intra-cellular Smad proteins. Finally, Wnt proteins, a
family of highly conserved signaling molecules, are potent stimulators of osteoblast differentiation. Wnt proteins bind to surface
receptors, including frizzled and low-density lipoprotein receptorrelated protein (LRP)5 on MSC, thereby eliciting activation and
nuclear translocation of the transcription factor
β

to transcription of genes involved in osteoblast differentiation.
Wnt proteins act in close synergy with BMPs and crosstalk with
the receptor activator of nuclear factorκ
B ligand (RANKL)–
osteoprotegerin (OPG) system involved in the differentiation and
function of bone-resorbing osteoclasts.
During aging, bone marrow adipocytes, derived from bone
marrow MSCs, accumulate, and this accumulation correlates
with osteoporosis.

Osteocytes
Osteocytes account for 90% to 95% of cells within bone. One
cubic millimeter of bone contains up to 25,000 osteocytes that
are well connected with each other and the bone surface by small
tubes termed canaliculi. This large and dense communication network inside the bone shares similarities to the dendritic processes
within the nervous system. Osteocytes reside in lacunae and connect to other osteocytes and bone lining cells by interconnecting
filaments. Modeling suggests that this extensive network forms a
total of 23 trillion connections; estimates place the total length of
all osteocytic processes connected end-to-end at 175,000 km in
the human body.
Osteocytes are derived from osteoblasts, which
are subsequently entrapped in the bone matrix.
Upon terminal differentiation, osteocytes express specific genes not found in
osteoblasts, including sclerostin, a secreted molecule that binds
LRPs and blocks Wnt-stimulated bone formation.
Consistent
with its function as an inhibitor of bone formation, overexpression of sclerostin leads to low bone mass, whereas deletion of
sclerostin leads to increased bone density and strength. This effect
has been harnessed as a therapeutic strategy to increase bone mass
by targeting and inhibiting sclerostin with a monoclonal antibody.

### Fracture Repair

Healing of long bones requires multiple stages with production of
a callus with a cartilaginous component followed by osteoblastmediated replacement with bone. Numerous cell types coordinate
to achieve fracture union. The initial phase occurs with the formation of a fibrin-filled hematoma full of inflammatory cells including T and B cells, mast cells, macrophages, and neutrophils.
Neutrophils and macrophages debride the injured and devitalized
tissues, helping to prepare for tissue healing and new blood vessel
formation. As inflammation resolves, macrophages differentiate
into alternatively activated macrophages promoting angiogenesis and remodeling collagenase, as well as osteoclasts to resorb
broken bone. The fibrovascular phase follows and involves the
recruitment of endothelial cells to support angiogenesis, neovascularization, and MSCs, which produce chondrocytes and osteoblasts. At the fracture site, CXCR4-expressing MSCs are recruited

### Regulation of Bone Remodeling

Normal physiologic circumstances ensure a balance between bone
formation and bone resorption to maintain skeletal homeostasis.
This bone remodeling process requires a tight mutual regulation
of bone resorption by osteoclasts and bone formation by osteoblasts, a phenomenon called
coupling.
Coupling is regulated on
at least three different levels: (1) by a direct interaction between
osteoblasts and osteoclasts, (2) by systemic neuroendocrine control of bone metabolism, and (3) by local interactions between the
immune system and bone cells.

Direct Interactions Between Osteoblasts and Osteoclasts
A proper coupling between bone formation and bone destruction
is essential to maintain bone integrity (
Fig. 4.5
). This coupling
process involves two main mechanisms; the first is the expression
of the essential pro-osteoclastogenic cytokines by the osteoblast
lineage, and the second involves the ephrin ligand/ephrin receptor
bidirectional signaling.
Preosteoblasts are the main pro-osteoclastogenic cells in normal physiologic conditions and provide the
first level of coupling between bone formation and bone resorption. In response to Wnt signaling, osteoblasts slowly lose their
supportive activity for osteoclasts when they mature toward more
mineralizing cells and then become the bone-embedded osteocytes. They then secrete anti-osteoclastogenic molecules such as
OPG and Wnt inhibitors sclerostin, dickkopf-1 (DKK1), and
secreted frizzled-related protein 1 (SFRP1), which either block
osteoclast differentiation (OPG) or inhibit the further differentiation of osteoblasts. A second level of coupling involves the
expression of ephrin ligands on the surface of osteoclast progenitors that can bind to ephrin receptors and activate their tyrosine
kinase activity.
Two ephrin ligands regulate bone remodeling.
The first, ephrin-B2, binds to the receptor EphB4 on osteoblast
progenitors, increasing their differentiation and stimulating bone
formation. The second, ephrin-A2, acts in an autocrine manner by
binding to the EphA2 receptor on the osteoclasts, which promotes
their differentiation, and in a paracrine manner on the osteoblasts,
thus inhibiting their differentiation.

Osteoimmunology in Rheumatoid Arthritis
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is associated with dysfunction of the
close interaction between the immune system and bone and manifests as chronic inflammation disrupting the physiologic balance
favoring bone resorption over bone formation. Bone loss is an
early and common feature of RA and occurs in three forms during
the course of the disease (see
Chapter 72
):
• Localized bone loss in the form of articular bone erosions
• Periarticular osteopenia adjacent to inflamed joints
• Generalized bone loss involving the axial and appendicular
skeleton
Bone Resorption in Rheumatoid Arthritis: Osteoclasts.
Osteoclasts are bone tissue resident macrophages capable of bone
resorption. The critical role of osteoclasts in pathogenesis of bone
erosions in inflammatory arthritis has been elucidated in studies
using genetically modified osteoclast-deficient RANKL and c-Fos
knockout mice that are completely protected from bone erosions
despite having significant joint inflammation.
Increased bone
resorption in RA results from an increase in osteoclast activation,
lifespan, and numbers due to enhanced recruitment of osteoclast
precursors and increased differentiation of osteoclasts from their
precursor cells. Osteoclast differentiation is controlled by cytokines, M-CSF, and RANKL. During the process of physiologic
bone remodeling, RANKL is mainly released by preosteoblasts and
osteocytes; however, in arthritic joints, RANKL is dramatically
increased due to production by synovial fibroblasts and infiltrating immune cells, including activated T cells.
Under physiologic
conditions, osteoclast activity is balanced by the expression of
anti-osteoclastogenic OPG by stromal cells and RANKL by osteocytes, but this ratio is skewed toward RANKL in active RA (
Fig.
4.6
). A higher RANKL:OPG ratio predicts long-term radiologic
damage progression in RA patients.
Similarly, the therapeutic
inhibition of RANKL holds potential to prevent bone erosions
in patients with RA, without affecting inflammation status. A
placebo-controlled phase 3 clinical study demonstrated that
denosumab (a monoclonal antibody targeting RANKL) significantly reduced the progression of joint destruction and induced
increases in bone mineral density (BMD) in RA patients receiving
concomitant conventional disease-modifying antirheumatic drug
(DMARD) therapy.
Bone Resorption in Rheumatoid Arthritis: B Cells.
B cells are
significantly involved in the development of bone erosions in RA,
in large part due to effects of autoreactive anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA) and rheumatoid factor (RF). High serum
levels of ACPA and RF correlate with more pronounced bone
erosions in RA.
ACPA antibodies bind citrullinated vimentin
on osteoclast progenitor cells and synoviocytes, stimulating the
production of TNF and RANKL, resulting in enhanced osteoclast
differentiation (
Fig. 4.6
). The importance of these findings is supported through the observation that treatment of RA with the B
cell depletion reduces synovial RANKL levels.
Bone Resorption in Rheumatoid Arthritis: T Cells.
Activated
T cells, in particular Th17 and Treg cells, have emerged as primary
players in the pathogenesis of bone loss in RA. Th17 cells drive
osteoclastogenesis by producing IL-17, which in turn induces
RANKL expression by synovial fibroblasts, as well as RANK
expression on osteoclast precursors.
Additionally, IL-17 promotes osteoclastogenesis and bone resorption by inducing TNF
and IL-1 production by synovial macrophages, IL-6 by fibroblasts,
and suppressing anti-osteoclastogenic factors including IL-4.
Inhibition of Bone Formation in Rheumatoid Arthritis.
Abnormal bone remodeling in RA is due not only to increased
bone resorption but also to active suppression of new bone formation. Osteoblasts, bone-forming cells, are regulated by Wnt signaling via the LRP5/6 receptors.
Sclerostin and dickkopf1 (DKK1)
inhibit Wnt signaling and suppress bone formation. DKK1 is
significantly upregulated in RA compared with healthy controls
and correlates with disease activity.
Drugs that activate Wnt signaling, such as Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors, increase osteoblast
function and can induce repair of articular bone erosions in RA
patients.
Osteoimmunology in Psoriatic Arthritis
Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is unique in its presentation of synovial
inflammation with bone destruction accompanied by juxtaarticular new bone formation resulting in osteophytes and enthesophytes. TNF and the IL-23/IL-17 axis are involved in PsA
pathogenesis. Potential osteogenic pathways in PsA include the
Wnt, TGFβ
, BMPs, and PGE
pathways.
New bone formation, a hallmark of PsA, is mediated by IL-17 and IL-22 targeting
MSCs and PGE2, activating osteoblast differentiation.
IL-23 binding to its receptor, IL-23R, on lymphocytes and
myeloid cells activates the JAK/signal transducer and activator of
transcription (STAT) pathway to induce inflammation in PsA.
IL-23 promotes T cell differentiation into Th17 cells secreting
IL-22 and IL-17A that stimulate RANK expression on osteoclast
precursors driving osteoclastogenesis. Osteoclasts, synovial fibroblasts, lymphocytes, monocytes, and keratinocytes bind IL-17A
via IL-17RA receptor and become activated promoting inflammation and tissue damage. IL-17A coupled with TNF drives the production of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) in synovial fluid,
leading to cartilage breakdown.
IL-17A also drives RANKL
expression in preosteoblasts leading to bone erosions. Inhibiting IL-17A in PsA inhibits the radiographic progression of bone
erosions.
Similar to RA, both genetic and environmental factors contribute to PsA disease progression.
Genetic factors include variants
in genes coding for IL-12, IL-23R, IL-23A, TLR2, and STAT3,
just to name a few. A variety of epigenetic alterations, including DNA methylation, post-translational histone modifications,
and noncoding RNAs, are associated with PsA. The gut microbiome also plays a significant role in PsA with increased numbers

Osteoimmunology in Post-menopausal Osteoporosis
Cessation of ovarian function at menopause causes sharp declines
in estrogen levels, leading to immune system activation, which
affects bone homeostasis and drives the development of postmenopausal osteoporosis.
Prior to menopause, estrogen increases
OPG production, which serves to maintain the OPG/RANKL
ratio and inhibit osteoclastogenesis. In the setting of estrogen deficiency, bone resorption is uncoupled from bone formation due to
increased osteoclast numbers, while bone formation only marginally increases, leading to net loss of bone density. These findings
suggest that post-menopausal osteoporosis is a high-bone-turnover disease with a shift in the remodeling balance in favor of
resorption. By contrast, senile osteoporosis is often described as a
low-bone-turnover disease.

Osteoimmunology in Osteoarthritis
In the past, osteoarthritis (OA) was not thought to be an inflammatory condition, but evolving research has shown that low-grade
synovitis is frequent and affects a significant percentage of patients
(41% vs. 14% without OA).
A low level of inflammation, which
includes immune cells in the synovium, macrophage infiltration

of the infrapatellar fat depots, and proinflammatory cytokines, is
correlated with OA severity. The number of activated macrophages
present in the joint is strongly correlated with knee discomfort (
R
= 0.60,
p
= .0001), joint space narrowing (
R
= 0.68,
p
= .007),
and osteophytes (
R
= 0.66,
p
= .001) in patients with OA.
These
activated macrophages release proinflammatory cytokines such as
TNF, IL-1, and IL-6 in response to danger-associated molecular
patterns (DAMPs).
T cells, the second-most prevalent cell type
in synovitis, also interact with these macrophages. Although the
variability of OA makes it difficult to accurately characterize T
cell populations within synovium or synovial fluid, data suggest
that there are more effector CD4+ Th1 and Treg cells.
However,
compared with healthy joints, these Tregs have lower levels of the
anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10. Recruitment of mast cells and
activation of complement are two additional important aspects
of OA.
Accordingly, OA pathogenesis is driven by low-grade
inflammation that includes mast cells, activated macrophages, and
altered T cell populations.

## Conclusion

Bone is continuously remodeled by bone-resorbing osteoclasts
and bone-forming osteoblasts. This remodeling process allows the
optimal adaptation of the bone architecture to individual demands
and tight control of calcium homeostasis. Local factors control
the bone remodeling process on the basis of osteoclast-osteoblast
interactions, as well as by systemic immune and neuroendocrine
factors controlling the bone-resorbing and bone-forming cells.
