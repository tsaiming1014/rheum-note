## Introduction

Autoantibodies have proven to be very useful tools for diagnosis
and prediction in autoimmune rheumatic diseases. Emerging data
from several autoimmune diseases has demonstrated that the clinical evolution of disease from the pre-clinical phase to overt clinical
disease is marked by a change in the specificity of the immune
response, with autoantibodies directed against distinct antigenic
targets at different disease phases. Whereas numerous autoimmune rheumatic diseases traditionally have been marked by highly
phenotype-specific autoantibody responses (e.g., anti–doublestranded DNA antibodies in systemic lupus erythematosus [SLE]
and high-titer anti-topoisomerase-1 antibodies in diffuse scleroderma), the discovery of highly specific autoantibody markers of
rheumatoid arthritis (RA) lagged significantly behind these other
diseases. During the past two decades, enormous progress has
occurred in this area, largely fueled by the discovery that citrullinated proteins are specific targets of autoantibodies in RA. This
finding has highlighted post-translational modifications (PTMs)
as critical antigenic determinants targeted by autoantibodies in
RA, which has led to the identification of novel autoantibody systems and pathogenic mechanisms in this disease.
It is now recognized that autoantibodies in RA target a large
and diverse group of proteins (
Table 56.1
), which appear to reflect
distinct pathogenic mechanisms and contribute to the marked
clinical heterogeneity among patients with RA. In this chapter, we
review the known autoantibodies in RA, with emphasis on discrete
subgroups of autoantibodies that have distinct value for diagnosis, prognosis, and identifying pathogenic pathways. These subsets include citrullination-associated autoantibodies, antibodies to
acetylated or chemically modified proteins, and autoantibodies to
unmodified antigens that are shared with other autoimmune and
inflammatory diseases. The implications of these specificities for
diagnosis and prediction of disease outcome, as well as for understanding pathogenic events in the disease, are highlighted.

## Citrullination-Associated Autoantibodies

Citrulline residues in proteins are generated post-translationally
through the deimination of arginine residues. This process, known
as citrullination, is mediated by the peptidylarginine deiminase
(PAD) enzymes (
Fig. 56.1A
). Patients with RA can develop antibodies to the products of citrullination (i.e., citrullinated proteins) and the PAD enzymes themselves (i.e., PAD2 and PAD4).
Among autoantibodies found in RA patients, citrullination-associated antibodies have the highest specificity for RA, making them
unique clinical tools for disease diagnosis. These antibodies also
provide clues into disease mechanisms, suggesting that understanding the process of citrullination and factors that lead to the
reversal of immunologic tolerance to these proteins may provide a
foundation for understanding the etiology of RA.

Assays for ACPA Detection
The Anti–cyclic Citrullinated Peptide Antibody Assay.
In the
initial characterization of AKA/APF antibodies, a single C-terminal peptide derived from filaggrin (amino acids 306 to 324)
was used to generate nine variants in which five arginine residues
were changed to citrullines, either individually or in pairs, and
these peptides were assayed by enzyme-linked immunosorbent
assay (ELISA).
Multiple different peptides were required for
maximum reactivity, supporting that AKA/APF correspond to a
pool of antibodies with different specificities against citrullinated
sequences. Indeed, when data from all peptides were pooled, assay
sensitivity for RA increased from 20 to 48% for single peptides to
76% for the pool.
To improve antibody detection, a cyclic citrullinated peptide
of filaggrin (substituting the terminal serine residues with cysteines and cyclizing the peptide through the formation of a disulfide
bond) was engineered to generate a
β
-turn conformation more
likely to mimic the original antigenic determinant.
Using this
cyclic citrullinated peptide (later named CCP1) compared with
its linear counterpart, it was demonstrated that the cyclic structure
increased the sensitivity of the assay (68% vs. 49%, without affecting specificity).
In 2000, CCP1 became the antigen for the
first generation of ELISAs designed to detect immunoglobulin G
(IgG) antibodies against citrullinated autoantigens in the research
setting (
Fig. 56.2
), leading to the term
anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP)

Later, a
third generation anti-CCP IgG assay (CCP3) was made available
for the laboratory diagnosis of RA. CCP3 recognizes additional
citrullinated epitopes that are not identifiable with CCP2. Studies directly comparing second- and third-generation anti-CCP
assays found them to perform very similarly,
with a slightly
increased sensitivity of CCP3 in one study (e.g., sensitivities of

Table 56.1

Since anti-CCP IgA antibodies are also often found in patients
with RA,

assay, which detects both the IgG and IgA isotypes of anti-CCP
antibodies simultaneously. Although the anti-CCP3.1 assay is
slightly more sensitive than the anti-CCP2 IgG assay, the specificity is markedly lower due to a higher false-positive rate.
Since patients with RA are rarely positive for anti-CCP IgA
alone,
the additive contribution of IgG and IgA in CCP3.1
likely increases the overall signal intensity, even for low-titer antiCCP IgG positive samples, which are normally considered negative by the anti-CCP2 IgG assay. Indeed, using a higher cutoff,

the specificity of the anti-CCP3.1 assay increases and becomes
comparable to the anti-CCP2 IgG assay.

RA, these peptides have proven to be excellent surrogates to detect
the majority of ACPAs in patients with RA. Therefore anti-CCP
antibodies are considered equivalent to ACPAs when referring to
antibodies recognizing citrullinated antigens.

Clinical Relevance of Antibodies to Citrullinated Proteins
The clinical importance of autoantibodies against citrullinated
antigens in RA stems from the following favorable features:
1.
Anti-CCP antibodies have high sensitivity and specificity for the
diagnosis of RA.
In systematic reviews and meta-analyses, antiCCP antibody positivity has been shown to be as sensitive as,
but more specific than, rheumatoid factor (RF) in distinguishing RA from other forms of inflammatory arthritis (see the section “Rheumatoid Factor”).
2.
The presence of anti-CCP autoantibodies is an important predictor of RA development.
In retrospective studies, increased antiCCP positivity was observed in a period of 1 to 5 years prior
to the onset of clinical RA, with increased antibody levels more
strongly associated with an increased risk of progression to RA.
In one study of patients with undifferentiated arthritis who
tested positive for anti-CCP, RA developed within 3 years in
more than 90% of patients, in contrast to only 25% of patients
who tested negative for anti-CCP.
3.
Anti-CCP positivity is associated with a more severe and destructive disease course.
Some investigators have shown that both
RF and anti-CCP antibodies are independent predictors of
severity, whereas others have shown that anti-CCP antibodies
rather than RF are the better predictor of radiographic progression,
particularly in patients who were seronegative for RF
(see “Rheumatoid Factor”).
4.
Anti-CCP/ACPAs are associated with RA-related interstitial lung
disease (ILD) and cardiovascular disease (CVD).
ILD and
CVD are extraarticular manifestations with high mortality in
persons with RA. Although the mechanistic significance of
these associations is still unclear, one possibility is that these
antibodies are markers of systemic inflammation, which plays
a role in mediating lung and cardiovascular damage. Alternatively, the observation that citrullination occurs in lung, myocardium, and atherosclerotic plaque
has suggested that
these antibodies may be directly pathogenic, targeting extraarticular tissues containing citrullinated antigens. The presence
of anti-CCP antibodies in patients who have ILD but do not
have RA is intriguing and has focused attention on the lung
as a possible extraarticular site where RA may be initiated
(see the section “Environmental Factors Linked to the Development of Citrullination-Associated Autoantibodies”).
The early and accurate diagnosis of RA, together with effective treatment with disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs
(DMARDs), decreases accrual of joint damage in RA. Because
anti-CCP/ACPAs are markers of disease severity and are detectable early in the disease course, they are powerful tools to classify
patients with early-stage inflammatory arthritis who will benefit
from treatment.
Citrullination Is Dysregulated in Rheumatoid Arthritis
Peptidylarginine Deiminase Activity and Regulation.
Since
citrulline is not a classical amino acid encoded by a codon during protein translation, citrulline residues need to be generated
post-translationally once the protein has been synthesized. This
process, termed
deimination
or
citrullination
, is mediated by the
calcium-dependent PAD enzymes. PADs target arginine residues
in proteins and mediate hydrolysis of the arginine guanidinium

side chain, resulting in the generation of citrulline residues and
ammonia (
Fig. 56.1A
). The enzymes are specific for peptidylarginine (i.e., they cannot citrullinate free l-arginine) and can only
modify residues within polypeptide chains but not at their termini
(i.e., they are endodeiminases).
Five PAD enzymes have been identified in mammals, and
the
PADI
genes are located in a single cluster on chromosome
1p36.1.
For historical reasons, these isozymes have been designated PAD1 to PAD4 and PAD6. Human PAD4 was initially
named PAD5
but was later renamed PAD4 to reflect the fact
that it is a true ortholog of mouse PAD4. PAD1–3 are classically
viewed as cytosolic enzymes, with PAD4 primarily expressed in the
nucleus. However, recent evidence suggests a more complex picture, with surface expression of PAD4
and nuclear or secreted
PAD2 reported in certain cell types.
The PADs are highly
conserved and share 50 to 55% sequence identity
but exhibit
distinct substrate preferences and tissue expression.
The calcium
binding residues are highly conserved among PADs,
except
for PAD6,
which lacks some of these sites; the catalytic cysteine is also different in PAD6. Consistent with these observations,
PAD6 is the only PAD member with no demonstrable citrullination activity.
Among the PAD enzymes, evidence suggests that
PAD2 and PAD4 are the predominant PAD enzymes responsible
for the generation of citrullinated autoantigens in RA.
These PAD isoforms are found in the tissue and fluid of inflamed
RA joints, and both enzymes have been shown to citrullinate proteins that are targeted by ACPAs.
Unlike many other enzymatic PTMs, such as phosphorylation,
ubiquitination, methylation, and others, citrullination appears to
be an irreversible process.
The mechanisms involved in the
clearance or turnover of citrullinated proteins in cells also remain
unknown. Because citrullination reduces the net charge of proteins
by neutralizing one positive charge per arginine residue modified,
it can increase protein hydrophobicity, lead to protein unfolding,
and alter intra- and intermolecular interactions.
These structural changes can lead to alterations in protein activity, most commonly a loss of function.
Given that citrullinated proteins
are prominent targets of autoantibodies in RA, the mechanisms
that control excessive citrullination of physiologic targets or citrullination of nonphysiologic substrates are important to define.
The activity of the PAD enzymes and generation of citrullinated proteins can be regulated at multiple levels. One regulatory component is calcium.
The binding of calcium ions to
PADs induces a conformational change that generates active forms
of the enzymes.
While this requires millimolar amounts of
calcium in vitro,
PAD activation in cells is observed under
physiologic nanomolar calcium concentrations.
This
suggests that physiologic calcium conditions are suboptimal but
may select for high-efficiency substrates and limit aberrant citrullination events. Another important regulatory component of PAD
activity is the oxidative environment.
Reducing conditions, like those present in the cytoplasm, are necessary to maintain PAD activity due to the presence of free thiol cysteine in the
active site required for catalysis.
Because the extra-cellular environment is oxidizing,
this may protect against aberrant extracellular citrullination by inactivating PADs that may leak from
activated or dying cells. Indeed, PAD activity is not detected in
extra-cellular fluids in vitro unless reducing agents are exogenously
added.
Citrullinated extra-cellular proteins, however, can be
detected in healthy human serum and synovial fluid at low levels,
which are significantly enhanced in RA,
suggesting that additional mechanisms are involved in controlling extra-cellular PAD
activity and that such mechanisms are altered in RA.
Similarly,
reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by nicotinamide adenine
dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase inhibits the catalytic
activity of PAD2 and PAD4 and, conversely, PAD4-mediated
citrullination blocks ROS production by targeting components
of the phagocyte NADPH oxidase (p47
and p67
).
The activity of the PADs may also be negatively regulated through
autocitrullination, since the enzymes can citrullinate themselves as
well as substrates.
While there are multiple natural mechanisms that inhibit PAD activity, the accumulation of citrullinated
proteins in the RA joint demonstrates that these regulatory mechanisms are incompletely effective in patients with RA (see the
section “Mechanisms for Citrullination-Associated Autoantigen
Production in the Rheumatoid Joint”).
The Citrullinome in Rheumatoid Arthritis.
Citrullination is a
normal process. A recent study identified 209 proteins as targets
of citrullination across 26 of 30 human tissues, which together
constitute the healthy citrullinome.
The levels of citrullinated
proteins differ markedly across healthy tissues, with the brain and
lung containing the highest levels and the largest spectrum of
citrullinated proteins (
∼
60 proteins per tissue). In contrast, fewer
than 10 proteins were identified as targets of citrullination in more
than half of the other body sites.
Interestingly, the majority of
proteins were only found to be citrullinated in a single tissue,
implying that citrullination of unique proteins is organ specific,
that citrullination under steady-state conditions is tightly regulated, and that protein citrullination may regulate or be regulated
by distinct factors in different tissues.
Functionally, the majority of citrullinated proteins across normal tissues are associated
with broad categories of cytoskeleton organization and protein
synthesis.
This collection of citrullinated proteins importantly contrasts
with the inflamed rheumatoid joint, in which both the type and
the magnitude of citrullinated proteins are significantly amplified, a characteristic that has been termed
hypercitrullination
.
More than 250 citrullinated proteins have been identified in the
RA joint,
which collectively comprise the RA citrullinome.
The identity of citrullinated proteins in the RA joint includes
proteins found across healthy tissues, although the vast majority
are proteins not detected in the healthy citrullinome.
The spectrum of citrullinated proteins in RA reflects hypercitrullination of
cellular and soluble components enriched in the distinct inflammatory compartments in the rheumatoid joint. For instance, RA
synovial fluid contains high levels of citrullinated extra-cellular
proteins, such as components of the complement and coagulation
cascades.
Cells in synovial fluid (mainly neutrophils)
and the synovium (containing synovial and inflammatory cells)
are the source of intra-cellular citrullinated proteins,
which
are associated with categories of cytoskeleton organization and cellular stress.
In particular, it is intriguing that citrullination in
RA, but not in the healthy citrullinome, targets proteins found in
metabolic pathways,
suggesting that these proteins became
accessible to citrullination under inflammatory conditions.
The unparalleled amount and variety of citrullinated proteins in the RA joint, not found in any other healthy tissue or
organ,
likely creates the perfect immunogenic environment
for ACPA production and immune complex formation in RA.
Importantly, there is no evidence that a single citrullinated protein is the primary target of ACPAs or a candidate toward which
immunologic tolerance is initially lost in RA. Instead, given the
significant variability in the recognition of distinct citrullinated
polypeptides by different RA sera,
it suggests that the ACPA

response among individual patients with RA is not initiated by a
single antigen but by different citrullinated proteins, most likely
with common citrullinated motifs.

### Autoantibodies to Peptidylarginine Deiminase Enzymes

In addition to their prominent enzymatic role in the generation of
citrullinated autoantigens, PAD2 and PAD4 are targeted by autoantibodies in unique subsets of patients with RA.
Although
PADs can citrullinate themselves,
antibodies to the PADs
are distinct from ACPAs in that antibody recognition is independent of citrullination status.
The sensitivity of PAD4 antibodies for RA is in the 23 to 45% range, with a specificity of greater
than 95% (
Table 56.1
).
PAD4 autoantibodies are strongly
associated with anti-CCP antibodies and are independently associated with the presence of radiographic joint damage in multiple
studies. Recent studies have shown that anti-PAD4 antibodies of
various isotypes and IgG subclasses are present in patients and
can delineate unique disease features.
Despite the association with severe joint disease, the relationship between anti-PAD4
antibodies and treatment response are less clear. One small openlabel study reported an association of anti-PAD4 antibodies with
the progression of erosive disease despite treatment with TNF
inhibitors,
while a more recent retrospective analysis of sera
from a randomized controlled trial showed that anti-PAD4 antibodies predict a more favorable response to treatment escalation
in patients who failed first-line DMARD therapy.
A subset of anti-PAD4 antibodies that cross-react with the
PAD3 isoenzyme has been identified in 10 to 12% of patients
with RA (
Table 56.1
).
These antibodies also have the unique
ability to enhance PAD4 enzyme activity upon binding, which
may play a novel role in the pathogenesis of RA (see the section
“Anti-PAD4 Antibodies: Origin and Pathogenicity”). Anti-PAD3/
PAD4 cross-reactive antibodies were most strongly associated with
severe progressive joint damage and the highest risk of RA-associated ILD.
PAD2 has recently been identified as a target of autoantibodies in 18.5% of RA patients a single cohort study (
Table 56.1
).
In contrast to anti-PAD4 or anti-PAD3/PAD4 antibodies, antiPAD2 were found in patients with milder disease, including fewer
swollen joints over time, less risk of RA-associated ILD, and a
lower likelihood of progressive radiographic joint damage. These
antibodies were not associated with other RA serologies such as
ACPA, RF, or anti-PAD3/PAD4 antibodies. Although antibodies to the PAD enzymes are not yet used in the clinical setting,
this study suggested that, when used together, anti-PAD2 and
anti-PAD3/4 antibodies may provide prognostic value in predicting patients who will progress in their erosive joint disease over
time.

### Etiology and Pathogenesis of Citrullination- Associated Autoantibodies

While the etiology of RA remains to be defined, the presence
of citrullination-associated autoantibodies in the pre-clinical
phase of the disease suggests that dysregulated citrullination is an
important event during disease initiation. This notion has sparked
the idea that environmental factors that dysregulate citrullination, especially at mucosal surfaces, may promote the breech of
tolerance to citrullination-associated antigens and promote the
development of autoantibodies in genetically susceptible hosts
(
Fig. 56.3
). There is evidence that this leads to the establishment
of feed-forward loops of disease propagation in the joint, in which
ongoing immune responses drive the continued generation and
release of PAD enzymes and citrullinated autoantigens (
Fig. 56.4
).

### Pre-clinical Detection of ACPAs and Anti-PAD Antibodies

Several studies have demonstrated that autoantibodies may precede
the onset of clinical disease in both tissue-specific and systemic
autoimmune diseases, including RA.
Analysis of stored preclinical blood samples has established that autoantibodies precede
RA diagnosis in many persons in whom seropositive RA subsequently develops, often by 2 to 6 years.
In several studies, 20
to 60% of patients with RA were RF positive prior to diagnosis,
and 30 to 60% of patients were positive for anti-CCP.
With
one exception,
the prevalence of anti-CCP prior to diagnosis
was almost twice as high as RF. Interestingly, whereas RF positivity
was constant, anti-CCP could vary over time, even disappearing
in some individuals. In one study, pre-clinical anti-CCP positivity was strongly associated with the presence of radiographic erosions at or after RA diagnosis, whereas RF was not.
As has been
observed for anti-CCP, anti-PAD4 antibodies are present in a subset of individuals pre-clinically, prior to the onset of any disease
symptoms, and mostly develop after ACPAs.
The evolution of autoantibody epitope spreading in pre-clinical
RA has been addressed using a multiplex assay to detect ACPAs to
multiple protein antigens.
This study confirmed that ACPA positivity predicts progression to RA in asymptomatic individuals and
showed that the number of ACPA specificities accumulate over
time during the pre-clinical phase of the disease.
Moreover, the
expansion of the ACPA response precedes the elevation of many
inflammatory cytokines, including TNF, interleukin (IL)-6, IL12p70, and interferonγ
, suggesting that ACPAs may be involved
in driving the inflammatory process in RA.
Analyses of unaffected first-degree relatives of patients with RA have also shown an
increased prevalence of ACPA positivity compared with healthy
controls.
Moreover, reactivity to multiple ACPAs and the
presence of an increasing number of ACPAs in this group at risk
for RA is more likely associated with signs of joint inflammation
and progression to RA.
Together, these data highlight that
the development of autoimmunity against citrullination-associated antigens is often asymptomatic and generally precedes the
onset of clinical disease. This pre-clinical phase is potentially of
great importance because it may identify persons in whom important precursor conditions for the subsequent development of
RA have been satisfied and could represent a unique therapeutic
opportunity. The subsequent events that convert this RA precursor into a chronic, self-sustaining process are not yet known, but
defining them is of great importance.

### Environmental Factors Linked to the Development of Citrullination-Associated Autoantibodies

Although hypercitrullination is found in the RA joint once disease
is established (see the section “The Citrullinome in Rheumatoid
Arthritis”), it is unknown whether abnormal citrullination precedes the onset of clinical RA, a requisite to explain the presence
of ACPAs long before the onset of clinical disease. Although such
evidence will be very challenging to obtain, a body of data suggests that the joint is not the only possible site of hypercitrullination that might initiate the RA-specific immune responses to
citrullination-associated autoantigens. There is growing support
for the hypothesis that aberrant protein citrullination and breach
of immunologic tolerance to citrullinated antigens initially occurs
at mucosal sites such as the oral mucosa, lung, and gastrointestinal
tract.
In support of this hypothesis, anti-CCP antibodies of
the IgA class (an isotype associated with mucosal immunity) are
found in about one-third of patients with recent-onset RA
; and
anti-CCP antibodies can be detected in the sputum of individuals
at risk of developing RA (e.g., RA-free first-degree relatives).
If
the lung is an initial site of ACPA production, it is postulated that
joints only become targeted secondarily after the immune response
is initiated as a consequence of an inflammatory event triggered by
a common environmental exposure, such as infection, inflammation, or smoking (
Fig. 56.3
).
Interestingly, PAD4-activating
anti-PAD3/PAD4 IgA was identified in the induced sputum from
a subset of patients with established RA, but not at-risk individuals, suggesting that these antibodies may arise later in the disease
process and play an active role in lung disease.
Smoking is the most widely studied environmental factor
linked to RA and ACPA development. A history of smoking
in patients with SE alleles (see “Genetic Factors Linked to the
Development of Citrullination-Associated Autoantibodies”) has
been linked to the development of ACPA-positive RA in some
populations,
but there is growing appreciation that this
relationship is nuanced. Whereas HLA-DRB1*04 alleles have
been primarily associated with ACPA production in the general
RA population,
in smokers, HLA-DRB1*0101, *0102, and
*1001 alleles demonstrate the strongest association with anti-CCP
antibodies.
In addition, it has been shown that certain ACPA
fine specificities and specific anti-CCP isotypes may be responsible for driving this association.
Anti-CCP antibodies can
also be found in patients with ILD who do not have RA,
further suggesting that smoking may play a role in generating or
sustaining autoantibodies to citrullinated proteins. In this regard,
a study of smokers without RA has shown that cigarette smoking
increased expression of PAD2 with a modest increase in protein
citrullination in the lungs.
Despite the strong relationship between ACPAs and antiPAD4 antibodies, and increased risk for ILD in anti-PAD3/
PAD4-positive patients who were past or present cigarette smokers,
a history of cigarette smoking is not associated with the
development of anti-PAD4 or anti-PAD3/4 antibodies.
This
suggests a two-hit model of lung damage in which anti-PAD3/
PAD4 antibodies may synergize with smoking to induce more
severe lung disease. In addition, this data suggests a role for other
environmental factors, which have not yet been identified, in the
development of anti-PAD immune responses.
Infection, particularly at mucosal sites, may represent another
factor associated with autoantibody development in RA. Tantalizing similarities and epidemiologic associations exist between
periodontitis and RA, including the presence of bone erosions,
association with similar SE alleles and smoking, and enrichment
of severe periodontal disease in patients with RA.
These
connections have focused attention on the study of oral pathogens
as potential triggers of autoimmunity in RA.
The identification
that
Porphyromonas gingivalis (P. gingivalis)
produces and secretes
a PAD enzyme (termed PPAD) led to the initial hypothesis that
infection with this periodontal pathogen may be etiologically
linked to RA.
Unlike mammalian PADs, however, PPAD
citrullinates free l-arginine in addition to peptidylarginine and
does not require calcium or any other metal ion for activity. Importantly, PPAD has preference for (and is potentially restricted to)
citrullination of carboxy-terminal arginines (C-terminal exodeiminase).
Although some studies have supported the idea that
PPAD can citrullinate autoantigens targeted by ACPAs,
the
pattern of citrullination induced by this enzyme (i.e., C-terminal
citrulline residues) is not characteristic in proteins recognized by
ACPAs, which target citrulline residues within protein sequences
(i.e., endocitrullination).
Indeed, there is evidence supporting
that the presence of amino acid residues on the C-terminal side of
citrulline are essential for ACPA reactivity.
An alternative mechanism that has been proposed for the
involvement of PPAD in RA pathogenesis is by citrullinating its
own bacterial products, which may act as the inciting citrullinated
antigens. Although initial studies suggested that autocitrullination
of PPAD may be relevant in this regard,
further studies by mass
spectrometry and crystal structure analysis of PPAD isolated from
P. gingivalis
have not supported this hypothesis.
In addition, one study showed that proteins from a mutant strain of
P.
gingivalis
lacking PPAD activity were recognized similarly to the
wild-type strain by both ACPA-positive and -negative sera, suggesting that autocitrullinated
P. gingivalis
are not a major target of
ACPAs.
The recent finding of a
P. gingivalis
strain encoding a
mutant PPAD with endodeiminase activity, however, has renewed
interest in unique
P. gingivalis
strains that could play a role in RA
pathogenesis.
The periodontal microenvironment in patients with periodontitis is highly enriched in citrullinated proteins, mimicking patterns of hypercitrullination generated by PADs in the RA joint.
This has suggested that the citrullinome in periodontitis is generated by the abnormal activation of host, rather than bacterial, PAD
enzymes. Among different microbial species associated with severe
periodontitis,
Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans (Aa)
was
identified as the only pathogen that could reproduce the citrullinome found in periodontitis and RA.
However, in contrast to
P.
gingivalis
in which citrullination is attributed to its own PAD,
Aa
does not encode a PAD enzyme. Instead, the bacterium secretes a
pore-forming toxin (leukotoxin A) that targets neutrophils, inducing prominent calcium influx, osmotic lysis, and hyperactivation
of PAD enzymes in a process termed
leukotoxic hypercitrullination (LTH)
.
This dysregulated PAD activation was shown

to induce global citrullination of a wide range of proteins in the
neutrophil, mirroring hypercitrullination found in periodontitis
and the RA joint.
Mechanistically, LTH induced by bacterial toxins is similar
to that induced by host immune-effector pathways in the RA
joint (see the section “Mechanisms for Citrullination-Associated
Autoantigen Production in the Rheumatoid Joint”).
If tolerance is broken to the citrullinated products of
Aa
-induced
hypercitrullination, this process may initiate the subsequent
production of ACPAs.
Moreover, a recent study
found that oral bacteria targeted by ACPAs are citrullinated in
the mouth by extra-cellular host PADs,
supporting a role of
PADs released from dying cells, such as LTH, in the production of citrullinated bacteria targeted by ACPAs in periodontitis.
Importantly, as other bacterial species also produce pore-forming toxins,
it is possible that a similar mechanism may also be
relevant to other pathogens at various mucosal sites, including
the lung and gut.
Recent studies have shown that human cytomegalovirus
(CMV) can induce overexpression and activation of PAD2
and PAD4 in infected cells, promoting hypercitrullination of
both viral and host proteins as a mechanism of viral fitness.
Interestingly, pathogens promote their survival by interfering
with or hijacking host PTM pathways.
Thus, similar to
other PTMs, it appears that pathogens promote dysregulated or
abnormal protein citrullination as a mechanism to subvert antimicrobial immune responses of the host and thereby maximize
their progeny.
During this process, pathogens can induce
citrullination of unique proteins, which are not part of the normal citrullinome, generating novel patterns of citrullination and
likely neoantigens. For instance, the citrullinomes induced by
Aa
and CMV comprise proteins described within the healthy
citrullinome, as well as novel citrullinated proteins, many of
which are found in the RA joint.
The production of citrullinated neoantigens during infection and in response to environmental factors is an important mechanism that may explain
the initiation of an antibody response to citrullinated proteins in
susceptible individuals.
Although there is no clear evidence that Epstein-Barr virus
(EBV) and human papillomavirus (HPV) are citrullinated during
infection, it is interesting that a subset of ACPA-positive RA sera
has been demonstrated to cross-react with synthetic citrullinated
peptides from EBV EBNA-1 and HPV-47 E2
.
Defining the kinetics of appearance of these antibodies during disease
development, and demonstrating directly that these modified
proteins are generated during natural infection, will be important to determine whether this cross-reactivity is relevant to RA
pathogenesis.
These gene–environment interactions playing out in microenvironments outside the joint add important dimensions to
the framework within which RA pathogenesis must be investigated and understood. The list of pathogens that are associated with the development of RA-associated autoantibodies
and immune responses is growing,
some of which are
arthritogenic in animal models.
While pathogens may contribute to RA development, RA-associated immune responses
may also be beneficial in controlling infection.
Although it
is possible that none of the mechanisms currently defined represent a unifying mechanism of RA pathogenesis, the broader
view of potential immunizing environments and the search
for a frequent worldwide environmental exposure is of great
importance.

### Development, Specificity, and Pathogenesis of Citrullination-Associated Antibodies: Lessons Learned From Monoclonal Antibodies Derived From Patients With Rheumatoid Arthritis

ACPA Development and Specificity
Important insights into ACPA development, target specificity, and
pathogenicity have been gained through the study of monoclonal ACPAs derived from single B cells/plasmablasts isolated from
patients with RA.
The first lesson has been that ACPAs
are enriched in somatic mutations,
supporting the
notion that ACPAs are produced by constant exposure to citrullinated autoantigens. The second is that individual ACPAs, even
those cloned from seemingly antigen-specific B cells, are multireactive to many citrullinated antigens.
This feature can be explained by recognition of consensus peptide motifs
found across multiple citrullinated proteins (e.g., citrulline-glycine, glycine-citrulline, arginine-citrulline-aspartic acid), implying
that ACPAs target consensus binding motifs rather than unique
citrullinated proteins.
This may explain why the clinical and
pathogenic value of detecting ACPAs to “specific” citrullinated
proteins is limited. The third lesson is that distinct patterns of
mutations during affinity maturation of a single ACPA precursor
can generate diverse antibodies recognizing different citrullinated
antigens.
This means that, in theory, the entire polyclonal
repertoire of ACPAs in a patient could be generated from a
small number of B cell precursors through affinity maturation.
The fourth lesson is that ACPAs are glycosylated in the variable
domain,
an uncommon feature among antibodies,
which
results from the generation of N-linked glycosylation consensus
sequences during the process of somatic hypermutation.
While
this modification appears to have no effect on antigen recognition, it has been proposed that N-glycosylation provides a selective
advantage to ACPA-producing B cells to escape negative selection
in germinal centers.
The fifth and final lesson is that ACPAs
are cross-reactive to other PTMs with a similar shape, charge, and
hydrophobicity as citrulline, such as carbamylated lysine (homocitrulline) and acetylated lysine (
Fig. 56.1
).

ACPAs in the Pathogenesis of Rheumatoid Arthritis
Initial in vitro evidence suggests that polyclonal ACPAs from RA
serum can activate both the classical and alternative pathways of
complement
and that ACPA immune complexes drive macrophages to produced TNF via engagement of Fc
γ
receptors and
Toll-like receptor 4 (
Fig. 56.4
).
In addition, ACPAs
purified from RA serum using MCV can induce differentiation
of human osteoclast precursors into osteoclasts in vitro and promote osteopenia and osteoclastogenesis in lymphocyte-deficient
Rag1
mice after intraperitoneal injection with lipopolysaccharide.
More recently, mechanistic studies have been centered
on the analysis of monoclonal antibodies. Interestingly, although
monoclonal antibodies to citrullinated collagen type II generated
by immunization in mice can cause arthritis by cross-reactivity to
joint cartilage,
monoclonal ACPAs derived from RA patients
cannot alone induce arthritis in mice. In healthy mice, monoclonal ACPAs can induce arthralgia, tenosynovitis, or bone loss in the
absence of joint edema or synovitis.
In experimental arthritis,
studies have found that monoclonal ACPAs can display different
effects in vivo and can either enhance, ameliorate, or even inhibit
arthritis in mice.
Although initial studies suggested that
“anti-inflammatory” ACPAs are specific for citrullinated histones

2A and 4
(also known as anti-NET ACPAs),
subsequent studies showed that the anti- or pro-inflammatory effect
of ACPAs was not related to the targeting of specific citrullinated
proteins.
The mechanisms by which RA-derived monoclonal ACPAs modulate arthritis in mice is unclear, particularly
because autoantigen citrullination is not a mechanism involved
in the induction of experimental arthritis in mice. Therefore these
data need to be taken with caution before translating into the
human model. Nevertheless, the possibility that distinct ACPAs,
likely defined by targeting unique sets of antigens with common
citrullination motifs, may have different effects on modulating RA
is interesting and could make an important change in the pathogenic paradigm of ACPAs in RA.
Anti-PAD4 Antibodies: Origin and Pathogenicity
Anti-PAD4 monoclonal autoantibodies have been derived from
PAD4-specific memory B cells isolated from patients with RA.
Analysis of these antibodies has suggested that they arise from
PAD4-reactive precursors, implying that defective checkpoints
in B cell tolerance are likely involved in the initial production
of these antibodies.
Nevertheless, autoantibodies to PAD4
have evidence of somatic hypermutation, suggesting that in
patients with RA, these antibodies have undergone antigendriven affinity maturation. Although ACPAs and anti-PAD4
autoantibodies appear to originate by different mechanisms,
chronic exposure to immunogenic self-antigens appears to be
a common driver.
Functional analysis of RA-derived monoclonal anti-PAD4
autoantibodies showed that, like polyclonal IgG from RA patients
with anti-PAD3/PAD4 antibodies (see the section “Autoantibodies to Peptidylarginine Deiminase Enzymes”),
they were able
to increase PAD4-citrullination activity at low calcium concentrations. Biochemical studies have revealed that this enhancement is
mediated by an interaction with structural epitopes at calciumbinding sites at the interface between the N- and C-terminal
domains. This suggests that anti-PAD4 antibodies could possess
the unique ability to propagate the generation of citrullinated
proteins in the RA joint (
Fig. 56.4
) (see “Mechanisms for Citrullination-Associated Autoantigen Production in the Rheumatoid
Joint”), but further in vitro and in vivo characterization is needed
to determine the full pathogenic potential of anti-PAD4 antibodies in patients with RA.
Mechanisms for Citrullination-Associated Autoantigen
Production in the Rheumatoid Joint
Once citrullinated-associated autoantibody responses are formed,
the propagation of inflammation in the RA joint requires the continued generation of citrullinated antigens and presence of active
PAD enzymes (
Fig. 56.4
). Analysis of synovial fluid and cells
suggests that dysregulated PAD activation with ongoing protein
citrullination occurs both intra- and extra-cellularly in the RA
joint.
Several PAD-expressing cells (such as immune
cells and synoviocytes) and distinct mechanisms of cell death
(including autophagy, NETosis, necrosis, and cytolysis induced by
pore-forming proteins) have been implicated in the production of
citrullinated autoantigens in RA by inducing intra-cellular PAD
activation and the release of PADs into the extra-cellular environment.
In this regard, recent studies have implicated neutrophils as a major source of intra-cellular citrullination and soluble
PADs for extra-cellular citrullination.
These cells represent one of the most abundant cell types in RA synovial fluid and
constitutively express several PADs.
Two mechanisms have been proposed to drive the cellular generation of RA autoantigens in neutrophils: (1) formation of neutrophil extra-cellular traps (NETs),
an anti-microbial form of
cell death (also known as NETosis) in which granule proteins and
chromatin are extruded from the cell and form extra-cellular fibers
that bind pathogens,
and (2) membranolytic damage mediated
by pore-forming proteins.
NET formation is increased in
the joints of patients with RA and may be a relevant source of
inflammatory signals through the extra-cellular redistribution of
intra-cellular antigens.
While these may include citrullinated
autoantigens, a growing body of data has demonstrated that the
formation of NETs is a complex process resulting from the activation of different pathways that can be triggered by a broad range
of stimuli, only some of which are associated with citrullination of a limited number of substrates, mainly histones.
Hence, even though NETs in RA may be associated with PAD4
activation, the contribution of this process to generating the RA
citrullinome is unclear.
Thus, although citrullination and the
formation of NETs may be increased in the RA joint, and NETs
may contribute to RA pathogenesis,
these processes may not
necessarily be directly associated.
As described (see the section “Environmental Factors Linked to
the Development of Citrullination-Associated Autoantibodies”),
bacterial toxins have the capacity to activate PADs and induce
hypercitrullination, which could initiate the production of ACPAs
at extraarticular sites. Host pore-forming proteins, such as perforin and the membrane attack complex of complement, may
then be responsible for sustaining the production of citrullinated
autoantigens in the joints of patients with RA.
These host
effector pathways are active in the RA joint,
and have been
demonstrated to be potent activators of PADs and efficient inducers of the neutrophil hypercitrullination that mirrors the RA joint
citrullinome.
In addition to neutrophils, monocytes and fibroblast-like synoviocytes can also generate citrullinated autoantigens as a result of
autophagy.
However, this process does not lead to hypercitrullination but, rather, to the citrullination of
α
-enolase and vimentin.
Interestingly, increased expression of autophagy markers correlates
with ACPA titers, suggesting that autophagy may contribute to
generation of these citrullinated antigens in patients with RA.
The discovery that fibrinogen is heavily citrullinated in the RA
joint provided initial evidence that citrullination can also occur
extra-cellularly.
Soluble PAD2 and PAD4 have been detected
in the synovial fluid from patients with RA,
suggesting that
these enzymes are released from activated or dying cells, most
likely neutrophils.
In this regard, a recent report showed that
neutrophils constitutively secrete PAD2 and express a fraction of
PAD4 on their cell surface, which can be upregulated following
cell activation.
Although regulatory mechanisms exist to limit
aberrant PAD activity (see the section “Peptidylarginine Deiminase Activity and Regulation”), accumulation of PAD enzymes
in the inflamed RA joint could overcome these inhibitory factors. For example, although the oxidizing extra-cellular environment is inhospitable to PAD activity, a continued supply of
active PADs release from neutrophils infiltrating the inflamed RA
joint could fuel the generation of citrullinated proteins. Autoantibodies to the PAD enzymes themselves may also support extracellular PAD activity by stabilizing the active conformation of
PAD4. Human anti-PAD4 monoclonal and anti-PAD3/PAD4
cross-reactive autoantibodies were also shown to enhance PAD4
enzymatic activity by lowering the amount of calcium needed for
catalysis into the physiologic range.
Thus, PAD4 released

during neutrophil activation or death may encounter anti-PAD4
antibodies in synovial fluid with the capacity to sustain enzymatic
activity, contributing to extra-cellular citrullinated autoantigen
generation in RA. Together, the presence of effector pathways that
drive hypercitrullination and extra-cellular release of citrullinated
antigens and PADs likely create a feed-forward loop that keeps a
constant production of autoantigens for B cell stimulation, autoantibody production, immune complex formation, and tissue
damage (
Fig. 56.4
).

## Antibodies to Post-translational Modifications With Structural Similarity to Citrulline

### Antibodies to Carbamylated Proteins

Carbamylation or homocitrullination is a nonenzymatic posttranslational process in which lysine residues are converted to
homocitrulline. This process results from the reaction of cyanate
or carbamyl phosphate with the primary amine of lysine residues
(
Fig. 56.1B
).
Cyanate is present in the body in equilibrium with urea and, therefore, carbamylation accumulates in conditions that enhance cyanate levels, such as uremia, inflammation,
and cigarette smoking.
Carbamyl phosphate is generated from
ammonia and bicarbonate by the catalytic activity of carbamoyl
phosphate synthases.
Although homocitrulline is one methylene group longer than citrulline, these amino acids are similar in
shape, charge, and hydrophobicity (
Fig. 56.1B
). Different than
citrullination, however, carbamylation is generated in conditions
associated with oxidative stress
and is considered in the
group of PTM-derived products (PTMDPs).
PTMDPs are
irreversible, accumulate during life, and have critical influence on
protein structure and biologic functions, especially in the context
of aging, physiologic stress, and inflammation.
The study of anti-carbamylated protein (anti-CarP) antibodies
resulted from the search for PTMs that may explain the pathogenic role of citrullinated proteins in RA.
Because attempts
at using citrullinated proteins or antibodies targeting citrullinated
peptides to induce experimental arthritis yielded modest or negative results,
it was postulated that other factors might be
responsible for triggering arthritis. Since citrulline and homocitrulline are structurally similar, it was hypothesized that carbamylated proteins may be the “missing link” between citrullination
and RA.
In support of this hypothesis, initial studies demonstrated that mice immunized with carbamylated peptides developed erosive arthritis.
Moreover, these mice became susceptible
to a more aggressive form of erosive arthritis following a subsequent intraarticular injection of citrullinated peptides. While
the significance of these interesting findings has not been pursued experimentally, these observations sparked interest in defining whether patients with RA have antibodies to carbamylated
proteins.
Using carbamylated–fetal calf serum and carbamylated-fibrinogen as surrogate antigens in ELISA assays, IgG and IgA anti-CarP
antibodies were identified in more than 40% of patients with RA
(
Table 56.1
).
Although homocitrulline structurally resembles
citrulline, the finding that anti-CarP IgG and IgA antibodies are
present in approximately 35% of ACPA-negative patients and
that these antibodies are predictive of a more severe disease course
independent of ACPA status, initially suggested that these antibodies were not merely cross-reactive between the two PTMs.
However, the analysis of monoclonal antibodies has challenged
this notion, suggesting the possibility that ACPAs and anti-CarP
antibodies are cross-reactive antibodies (see the section “Implications of the Anti-Modified Protein Antibodies Concept”). AntiCarP antibodies appear many years before the diagnosis of RA
and predict the development of RA independently of anti-CCP
antibodies in patients with arthralgia.
Although the mechanism by which carbamylated antigens
are generated in RA is still unknown, it has been proposed that
inflammation may be the trigger of enhanced carbamylation.
Myeloperoxidase released from neutrophils in the RA joint may
promote the conversion of thiocyanate to cyanate, further enhancing carbamylation.
Nevertheless, despite the growing interest in anti-CarP antibodies, a major caveat is that their primary
protein targets in RA are still unknown. Recently alpha-1-antitrypsin was found to be carbamylated in synovial fluid from an
RA patient and was found to be recognized more strongly by RA
patient sera compared with control sera.
Additional studies are
needed to define the specific set of carbamylated autoantigens targeted in RA and their presence in RA target tissue in order to
better understand the clinical significance and pathogenic role of
anti-CarP antibodies in RA.

### Anti-acetylated Protein Antibodies

Lysine acetylation is a reversible PTM consisting of the transfer
of an acetyl group from acetyl-coenzyme A to the
ε
-amino group
of lysine residues.
Although acetylation can occur nonenzymatically,
in the majority of cases, it is regulated by a balance
between lysine acetyltransferases and lysine deacetylases (
Fig.
56.1C
).
Several thousand proteins have been identified as targets of acetylation in different cell lines and tissues from humans
and mice.
Lysine acetylation is a dynamic process used in
nearly all intra-cellular compartments to regulate the function and
interactions of proteins involved in distinct cellular processes, such
as epigenetic regulation, cellular proliferation, and mitochondrial
function, among others.
The identification of anti-acetylated protein antibodies
(AAPAs) followed the search for novel antibodies targeting PTMs
with structural similarity to citrulline and/or homocitrulline.
Since acetylated lysine (KAc) resembles homocitrulline (except
for the side-chain terminal amine, which is replaced by a methyl
group) (
Fig. 56.1
), it was hypothesized that this modification may
also be recognized by antibodies in RA.
However, in contrast
to the discovery of ACPAs and anti-CarP antibodies, in which
their detection was based on modified antigens with potential biologic relevance,
AAPAs were initially detected using a peptide
sequence that does not exist in proteins.
To investigate the presence of AAPAs, a 12 amino acid peptide
sequence derived from MCV (used to detect ACPAs) was changed
to include KAc instead of citrulline at position 7.
The rationale
of using this peptide to screen for AAPAs may have followed the
idea that antigen recognition by these antibodies is only dependent on the presence of KAc and that ACPAs do not bind KAc.
However, it is important to consider that efficient antigen binding by ACPAs is not only dependent on the modified residue but
occurs only in the context of specific amino acids neighboring
the citrulline.
Therefore, using a peptide containing an
ideal ACPA recognition motif in which citrulline was changed
for a structurally similar residue (e.g., KAc or homocitrulline), it
is likely to be recognized by subsets of ACPAs that tolerate this
change in the antigen, rather than validating the existence of a

new subset of antibodies in RA.
Indeed, when KAc is introduced at position 2 instead of position 7 into MCV (i.e., outside
the ACPA recognition motif), antibody reactivity to this peptide
was importantly reduced.
Moreover, analysis of RA-derived
monoclonal antibodies supports the notion of cross-reactivity of
ACPA subsets with peptides/proteins containing KAc
(see the section “Implications of the Anti-Modified Protein Antibodies Concept”).
A similar approach has been used to detect AAPAs, but instead
using acetylated ornithine (which is one methylene group shorter
than KAc) rather than citrulline at position 7 within the MCV
peptide.
Similar to the MCV peptide containing KAc, ACPApositive sera reacted with the peptide containing the acetylated
ornithine residue,
suggesting that binding to this peptide is
also observed with some ACPAs. Importantly, since ornithine is
not an amino acid present in proteins naturally, this modification
is not relevant as a source of autoantigens in RA.
Although the initial data suggesting the existence of AAPAs in
RA were ambiguous,
further studies have pursued this modification in RA using peptides from proteins containing naturally
occurring KAc sites (i.e., histones).
In one study, reactivity
to KAc-Histone 2B was almost exclusively detected in individuals with anti-CCP-positive RA,
supporting cross-reactivity with
ACPAs. Thus, whereas the detection of AAPAs may be useful as
a surrogate to identify RA subsets and antibody-positive RA in
some patients negative using the CCP assays,
rigorous evidence is lacking to confirm that AAPAs are an independent antibody specificity in RA, as initially suggested.

## Antibodies to Malondialdehyde- Acetaldehyde Adducts

Similar to carbamylation, the generation of malondialdehydeacetaldehyde adducts (MAAs) is nonenzymatic. During oxidative stress caused by a variety of stimuli, including inflammation
and exposure to alcohol, ROS triggers peroxidation of membrane
lipids, resulting in the formation of reactive malondialdehyde
(MDA) and acetaldehyde (AA).
These products can covalently modify proteins through carbonylation of amino acids carrying free amine groups (i.e., lysine, arginine, histidine) and, to
a lesser extent, amino acids with amide groups (i.e., asparagine,
glutamine), resulting in the formation of stable MDA-AA protein
adducts, referred to as MAA (
Fig. 56.1D
).
These adducts
have been implicated in contributing to inflammation and cellular injury associated with cardiovascular and alcoholic liver
disease.
Interestingly, RA synovial tissue, but not tissue from patients
with osteoarthritis, is enriched in MAA-modified proteins, which
co-localize with citrullinated proteins, leading to the search of
anti-MAA antibodies in RA.
Using MAA-modified albumin
as a surrogate antigen, anti-MAA antibodies are found in up to
44% of patients with RA.
Although anti-MAA antibodies
are strongly associated with anti-CCP and RF, it is interesting
that anti-MAA antibodies of the IgG isotype are found in 33%
to 88% of patients (depending on the cohort) who are negative
for anti-CCP antibodies,
suggesting that these may be useful biomarkers, particularly in people with ACPA-negative RA
(
Table 56.1
). In addition, anti-MAA IgG and IgA are detected
in pre-clinical RA but appear later than anti-CCP2 or RF, suggesting that anti-MAA antibodies may play a role in the transition from subclinical to clinical RA.
MAA-modified proteins in
RA synovial tissue includes serum proteins (i.e., albumin, hemoglobin, IgG gamma chain, transferrin, and apolipoprotein A1),
the acute-phase protein
α
1-glycoprotein-1, the enzyme carbonic
anhydrase-1, and cytoskeletal proteins (actin and vimentin),
which are candidates targets of anti-MAA antibodies in RA.
Anti-MDA IgG levels significantly correlate with disease activity by DAS28-ESR, and with high serum levels of TNF, IL-6, and
C-reactive protein.
Moreover, using a large cohort of patients
with RA to compare antibodies to different PTMs (i.e., CCP2,
anti-CarP, AAPAs, and anti-MAA), patients with more active disease by DAS28 had higher levels of IgG anti-MAA antibodies,
which was not observed for any other autoantibody or RF isotype tested.
Nevertheless, compared with other autoantibodies, IgG anti-MAA antibodies were not significantly elevated in
RA-risk individuals, suggesting that anti-MAA antibodies likely
contribute to established and active disease.
In addition to the
RA joint, MAA is highly expressed in RA-ILD lung tissue,
and
anti-MAA IgA and IgM antibodies have been associated with ILD
in patients with RA.
Together, these data suggest that MAAmodified proteins, which colocalize with citrullinated proteins
in RA target tissues, likely enhance autoantibody-induced damage in RA. It is important to highlight that anti-MAA antibodies
are not specific for RA but have also been found in patients with
osteoarthritis, SLE, alcohol-induced liver disease, diabetes, and
CVD.
Therefore, these antibodies may not exclusively
be markers of joint damage but may be generated as a result of
comorbidities associated with RA.
In contrast to anti-CarP antibodies and AAPAs, current evidence supports that anti-MAA antibodies are not cross-reactive
with ACPAs. MAA adducts have no structural similarity to
citrulline residues (
Fig. 56.1
), and the shape and charge of MAA
adducts are unlikely to fit into the ACPA paratope.
Moreover,
while anti-MAA antibodies are associated with ACPA positive
disease, these antibodies are not directly related to anti-citrulline
reactivity.
More importantly, monoclonal ACPAs have no reactivity with MAA-modified proteins and, similarly, RA-derived
monoclonal anti-MAA antibodies have no reactivity with citrullinated sequences.
Interestingly, although anti-MAA monoclonal
antibodies have not been characterized as extensively as ACPAs,
current evidence suggests that, unlike ACPAs, anti-MDA/MAA
antibodies primarily target the modified amino acid residue without the need of interacting with the neighboring amino acids,
resembling the immune response to a hapten.
Haptens are
small molecules that cannot induce an antibody response unless
they are attached to a larger carrier.
In this scenario, even
though anti-MAA antibodies in RA were generated with and react
against modified self-antigens, these antibodies may not represent
autoantibodies per se but rather anti-hapten antibodies.

## Implications of the Anti-modified Protein Antibodies Concept

The concept of anti-modified protein antibodies (AMPAs) was
introduced to unify three seemingly independent but crossreactive classes of autoantibodies targeting PTMs in RA: ACPAs,
anti-CarP antibodies, and AAPAs.
The definition of AMPAs
offers some advantages for diagnosis and mechanisms in RA.
Because a small proportion of ACPA-negative RA (detected by
anti-CCP assays) is positive for anti-CarP antibodies and/or
AAPAs, the model of AMPAs permits classifying a larger number of patients as seropositive RA regardless of the antibody

specificity.
Moreover, because these antibodies are highly
cross-reactive among citrullinated, carbamylated, and acetylated
peptides, their classification as AMPAs implies that these antibodies are mechanistically related and likely generated by common
pathogenic mechanisms.
Against the AMPA concept, however,
is the lack of evidence supporting that ACPAs, anti-CarP antibodies and AAPAs are indeed independent antibodies, particularly for
the AAPA group (see the section “Anti-Acetylated Protein Antibodies”). Rather, these distinct subsets of antibodies may correspond to heterogenous ACPAs, some of which can bind to PTMs
with structural similarity to citrulline, mostly when the modified
residue is found within an ACPA binding motif. In support of
this notion, the study of RA-derived monoclonal antibodies has
shown the existence of ACPA subsets with the additional capacity to efficiently bind carbamylated and acetylated sequences.
Since carbamylation is an immunogenic PTMDP, however, it cannot be excluded that this modification may play a role, via crossreactivity, in the initial induction of ACPAs in RA.
It is certain that by interacting with other protein modifications—for instance, carbamylated and acetylated proteins—
ACPAs may extend their pathogenic effect in RA through
cross-reactivity.
Similarly, using specific carbamylated and
acetylated peptides may overcome potential limitations in the
detection of some antibodies using CCPs and to identify ACPA
subsets that may target unique cross-reactive epitopes. However,
this nomenclature, while conceptually useful, does not necessarily imply the existence of etiologically distinct antibody subsets
that cross-react with diverse antigen modifications in RA. This
is particularly relevant since the initial definition of AMPAs was
done to unify cross-reactive antibodies reactive to citrullinated,
carbamylated, and acetylated antigens, and the further inclusion
of anti-MAA antibodies as AMPAs,
which do not recognize
these three modifications,
may not be appropriate.

## Other RA Autoantibodies

### Rheumatoid Factor

RFs are autoantibodies that target the Fc region of IgG. Besides
their potential relevance to autoimmune disease pathogenesis,
RFs hold a special place in rheumatology because they were the
first autoantibodies discovered in the autoimmune rheumatic diseases. In 1940, Erik Waaler found that sera from patients with
RA inhibited the hemolysis of antibody-sensitized sheep erythrocytes and, instead, cause marked agglutination of these cells.
He also noted that this effect was thermostable and associated
with the globulin fraction of the serum, demonstrating that it was
not dependent on complement activation. It was suggested that
this process resulted from the existence of a factor (“the agglutination activating factor,” later called rheumatoid factor), which
was increased in patients with RA.
Nevertheless, despite the
high prevalence of a positive agglutination reaction in sera from
patients with RA (35% positive), Waaler did not consider it of
diagnostic value for RA because some patients with other diseases
(such as cancer and infections) were also positive (
∼
5%).
Later,
this phenomenon was rediscovered in 1948 by Rose and colleagues
while performing complement fixation tests for rickettsialpox, and
they suggested the potential of this test in the diagnosis of RA.
These assays were later shown to be detecting IgM antibodies from
patients with RA that recognized the Fc portion of IgG.
Numerous modifications of the agglutination assay have evolved
(called the Waaler-Rose agglutination test), which led to the use of
IgG-coated latex beads instead of sheep erythrocytes.
Subsequent developments established RF assays in radioimmunoassay,
ELISA, and nephelometry formats, with increased convenience
but similar performance in terms of sensitivity and specificity.
RFs are positive in
∼
70% of RA patients but have been shown
to have lower specificity for RA compared with anti-CCP antibodies (85 vs. 95%, respectively) (
Table 56.1
).
RFs may be positive in healthy control subjects (1% in younger persons and up to
5% in persons older than age 70) and in patients with numerous
other non-RA diseases (including other rheumatic diseases such
as Sjögren’s syndrome and cryoglobulinemia), and chronic infections.
The pre-test probability of diagnosing RA therefore
greatly influences the performance of the RF test, with sensitivities
and specificities both in the 50 to 90% range, depending on the
patient groups studied.
The existence of IgG and IgA RFs
and evidence of somatic hypermutation have suggested that some
RFs in RA are T cell dependent.
Although some studies have
suggested that IgG and IgA RFs increase the specificity of RFs for
RA,
a recent meta-analysis showed that assays of the different
RF isotypes performed very similarly in terms of sensitivity and
specificity to standard RF assays.
Importantly, patients with RA also vary in terms of timing of
the appearance of RF, with RF preceding symptomatic disease in
a significant subpopulation
and following disease onset
with variable kinetics in other patients. Indeed, earlier onset of RF
in patients with RA has been associated with more severe disease,
highlighting a possible contribution of these antibodies to disease
amplification.
The observation that RF only appears after disease onset in some patients suggests that it may mark distinct,
sequential events in pathogenesis, which are very close together
in the subgroup in whom RF occurs early, or are separated in
time in patients in whom RF follows symptoms. The mechanisms
that drive the production of RF in persons with RA are not fully
understood. The presence of somatic hypermutation in RFs and
the evidence that these antibodies are produced by plasma cells in
the rheumatoid synovium have suggested that the RF response is
driven locally by antigens.
However, it is still unclear how
IgG becomes a target of the autoimmune response in the rheumatoid joint. In contrast to other autoantigens in RA, there is
no evidence that the IgG targeted by RFs requires modification
(e.g., PTM) for antibody recognition. Nevertheless, it is still possible that the initial events that break tolerance to IgG may be
initiated through an abnormally modified molecule, as occurs for
other autoantigens in RA. The possible mechanisms whereby RF
may play an amplifying role in RA are numerous and include formation of immune complexes as well as amplification of cellular
antigen capture, signaling, and effector functions, among others
(
Fig. 56.4
).
More recently, in vitro studies have suggested that
RFs increase the capacity of ACPA immune complexes to activate
complement and stimulate pro-inflammatory cytokine production by macrophages.
Thus, although RF may not be specific for RA, these autoantibodies may enhance the pathogenicity
of RA-specific autoantibodies such as ACPAs.

### Anti-RA33 Autoantibodies

Anti-RA33 antibodies were discovered in 1989 as a novel autoantibody specificity recognizing a nuclear protein with an apparent
molecular mass of 33 kDa.
These autoantibodies were detected
in 35% of patients with RA but were also detected in a small number of patients who had other autoimmune or degenerative rheumatic disorders (
Table 56.1
). In later studies, the RA33 antigen

was identified as the A2/B1 protein of the heterogeneous nuclear
ribonucleoprotein complex (hnRNP-A2/B1).
Further characterization of this antibody showed that it was not specific for RA
but was also found in approximately 20% of patients with SLE
and in 40 to 60% of patients with mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD).
However, in SLE and MCTD, anti-RA33
usually occurred together with antibodies to U1–small nuclear
ribonucleoproteins (snRNPs) or Smith (Sm) antigen. Therefore
anti-RA33 without concomitant anti–U1 snRNP autoantibodies
were found to have specificity of 96% for RA.
Additionally,
anti-RA33 antibodies in RA, SLE, and MCTD are distinguished
by their recognition of different conformation-dependent epitopes in hnRNP-A2/B1.
Moreover, in the absence of RF and
ACPAs, anti-RA33 antibodies in patients with very early arthritis
(<3 months’ duration) are associated with a relatively mild non
erosive disease course, potentially identifying patients with a good
prognosis who will respond well to treatment with DMARDs.
Stimulation assays using hnRNP-A2 showed proliferation of
RA-derived T cells in almost 60% of patients with RA, but only
20% of the control subjects, with substantially stronger responses
observed in patients with RA.
Interestingly, immunohistochemical analyses have revealed pronounced overexpression of
hnRNP-A2 in the synovial tissue of patients with RA, placing the
antigen at sites of disease in RA.
An intriguing feature of RA33 is that this antigen is also citrullinated in the RA joint and is a target of ACPAs in RA.
Thus,
patients with RA can have antibodies to both the native and
citrullinated forms of RA33. Moreover, four transcript variants
encoded by the same gene have been identified in cells from RA
synovial fluid (designated hnRNP-A2, B1, A2b, and B1b), with
B1b being most strongly recognized.
In a single cohort, antibodies against citrullinated hnRNPB1b were identified in 44%
patients with RA, whereas antibodies against the native protein
were detected in 10.7% of patients. Only 6% of patients with RA
had both antibody specificities.
The presence of autoantibodies
against the same antigen in two different forms (i.e., native and
citrullinated) may reflect an evolving immune response to structural immunogenic changes in autoantigens that occur during the
development of the disease.

### Anti-glucose-6-Phosphate Isomerase Antibodies

Glucose-6-phosphate isomerase (G6PI) is another autoantigen of potential interest in RA, which is a glycolytic enzyme
residing in the cytoplasm of all cells. Anti-G6PI antibodies
were first discovered in the K/BxN mouse model of arthritis,
in which pathogenic immunoglobulins that recognize G6PI
are formed.
These antibodies are directly pathogenic
in mice, as passive transfer of anti-G6PI antibodies alone
can transfer arthritis to healthy recipients.
The pathogenic
potential of this antibody prompted its study in human RA.
However, although initial studies reported a high frequency of
such antibodies in sera of patients with RA,
these results
have been the subject of debate.
Overall, anti-G6PI antibodies are found in 12 to 29% of patients with RA in different
cohorts and are more prevalent in patients with active disease
(
Table 56.1
). Patients with psoriatic arthritis, undifferentiated
arthritis, and spondylarthropathy also develop anti-G6PI antibodies at similar frequencies (12 to 25%), and similar titers
are detected in a proportion (5 to 10%) of control subjects or
patients with Crohn’s disease or sarcoidosis.
The potential
relevance of these antibodies in RA is still uncertain.

## Conclusion

The autoantibody repertoire in patients with RA is diverse, and
new antigen specificities continue to be discovered. Autoantibodies
in RA are important tools for disease diagnosis and for identifying
clinically informative patient subgroups. They are also serologic
clues for understanding disease pathogenesis. Autoantibodies
identified to date fall into four major categories: citrullinationassociated autoantibodies; antibodies to PTMs with structural
similarity to citrulline (i.e., homocitrulline and acetyl-lysine);
anti-MAA antibodies; and those that recognize largely native
proteins that are shared with other diseases. Understanding how
autoantigens are generated and defining the genetic and environmental factors linked to autoantibody development offer valuable
insights into disease mechanisms. To date, citrullination-associated antibodies are the most specific for the diagnosis of RA, and
specific genetic and environmental factors associated with their
development have been defined. The discovery of these antibodies
has identified dysregulated protein citrullination as a key pathogenic mechanism and potential therapeutic target in this disease.
The finding that ACPAs are cross-reactive with carbamylated and
acetylated proteins is intriguing and demands understanding of
the significance of such cross-reactivity. The possibility that antiCarP antibodies and AAPAs are simply subsets of ACPAs requires
major consideration. The analysis of RA autoantibodies and their
target antigens at sites of disease initiation and propagation may
enable the development of additional novel strategies for the treatment and prevention of RA.
