# Bisphosphonates

ARTHUR C. SANTORA, II, AND KENNETH G. SAAG

KEY POINTS

> Bisphosphonates (BPs) share a similar chemical structure that results

> in rapid distribution to hydroxyapatite on bone surfaces or renal

> excretion after either oral absorption or intravenous infusion.

> BPs on bone surfaces are “targeted” to osteoclasts that dissolve and

> concentrate BPs in resorption lacunae prior to absorbing them.

> Inhibition of osteoclast-mediated bone resorption is useful for the

> treatment of osteoporosis, prevention of bone loss, and treatment

> of Paget’s disease of bone, cancer metastases in bone, and

> hypercalcemia of malignancy.

> The nitrogen-containing BPs inhibit bone resorption at doses a

> hundred to several thousand times lower than those that inhibit

> mineralization.

> Following discontinuation of BP treatment, bone resorption increases in

> two phases: (1) due to loss of BPs on bone surfaces (with a half-time

> of about 1 month) and (2) due to loss of BPs trapped within bone

> formed during prior treatment (with a half-time of about 5 years).

> The magnitude of reduction of bone resorption more than 6 months

> after treatment is discontinued is a function of dose and duration of

> prior BP treatment.

> BPs have common nonserious symptomatic adverse effects and rare,

> more serious adverse effects that appear associated with prolonged

> skeletal retention and protracted suppression of bone resorption.

## Introduction

Bisphosphonates (BPs) were first synthesized over 150 years ago,
as inhibitors of insoluble calcium salt formation. Their principal
application was industrial, as anti-scaling agents.
Their initial use
in humans was in toothpaste to prevent calcified tartar accumulation.
BPs were explored in nonclinical and clinical studies as
inhibitors of ectopic calcification and calcium-containing kidney
stone formation and for targeting radionuclides to sites of bone
mineralization. The
Tc-methylene BP complex (
Tc-MDP)
binds to hydroxyapatite on bone surfaces and has been the standard “bone scan” imaging agent since the 1970s. Efforts to treat
and prevent heterotopic ossification with a BP met with very limited success. No beneficial effects of BPs on either vascular calcification or kidney stone disease have been established in human
studies. Nonclinical studies of BPs identified a “side effect”—inhibition of osteoclast-mediated bone resorption
—that has been
exploited for the treatment of osteoporosis and Paget’s disease
of bone and prevention of bone loss. While etidronate inhibits
both resorption and mineralization, newer BPs selectively inhibit
bone resorption at much lower doses than those that inhibit bone
mineralization. This chapter reviews the clinical pharmacology of
BPs currently used for the treatment and prevention of osteoporosis and Paget’s disease, the clinical trial data that support their
use as treatments of these diseases, and a review of their potential adverse effects. The clinical pharmacology is also relevant to
the use of BPs for the treatment of cancer metastatic to bone and
hypercalcemia of malignancy.

## Clinical Pharmacology

BPs were developed to treat metabolic bone diseases characterized
by abnormally high rates of bone resorption, the most common
of which are osteoporosis and Paget’s disease of bone. BPs also
have a role in the treatment of cancer metastatic to bone including
hypercalcemia of malignancy that may result from lysis of bone by
enlarging skeletal metastases. While the clinically relevant objectives of treatment may differ by disease, the underlying clinical
pharmacology resulting in inhibition of osteoclast-mediated bone
resorption is common.

## Chemistry of Bisphosphonates

BPs are analogs of pyrophosphate in which the central oxygen of
the P-O-P structure of pyrophosphate is a central carbon of the
P-C-P structure of bisphosphonaten.
BPs retain a relatively high affinity for hydroxyapatite found in
bone but are not hydrolyzed by phosphatases in humans or other
vertebrates.

### Distribution and Metabolism

Following absorption or intravenous (IV) administration, BPs
rapidly distribute to the extra-cellular space and are cleared from
plasma with a T
of 1 to 2 hours.
In nonclinical studies, approximately 50 to 60% of the administered dose is found in bone and
40 to 50% eliminated in urine.
There is no known metabolism
or intra-cellular soft tissue accumulation of BPs.
The kinetics of BPs in bone may be described by a threecompartment model: in bone extra-cellular fluid (ECF), on the
bone surface, and within the bone matrix. The flow of BPs to
bone is through afferent arteries and from bone to systemic ECF
is through the efferent veins; there is no known diffusion of BPs
from bone surfaces or matrix directly to or from the systemic ECF.
Due to the high affinity of BPs for hydroxyapatite on bone surfaces and very high bone surface area, the great majority (>99%)
of BPs that flow into bone are initially retained on bone surfaces.
Nonclinical studies demonstrated that [
H]alendronate was found
almost exclusively on bone surfaces and was concentrated under
active osteoclasts 4 hours after IV administration.
However, 7
weeks later, very little [
H]alendronate remained on bone surfaces,
and most was found within recently mineralized bone matrix. BPs
trapped within mineralized bone remain there unless freed by new
osteoclastic bone resorption. BPs are not metabolized and once
resorbed and released into bone ECF they may (1) bind to another
bone surface or (2) leave bone and reenter the systemic circulation.
BPs recycled from bone into blood are either terminally eliminated
in urine or reenter bone anywhere in the skeleton.
Elimination of BPs in urine occurs through both glomerular filtration and tubular secretion, the latter via a transport system shared
by alendronate and etidronate.
While the great majority of oral BPs
pass through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract without being absorbed,
less than 1% of IV alendronate is excreted through the GI tract.
The elimination kinetics of most BPs have been studied in animals with no notable qualitative differences observed. The terminal elimination half-life (T
) of alendronate rats was 200 days in
rats and 3 years in dogs.
Elimination of risedronate in humans
has been evaluated in relatively short studies, with only 28 days of
follow-up. Terminal elimination T
in humans has been reported
only for alendronate. Excretion of alendronate after IV administration daily for 4 days was tracked by measuring 24-hour excretion for up to 2 years.
The terminal elimination T
estimated
from the daily urinary excretion of alendronate measured monthly
from the end of month 8 to month 18 after dosing averaged 10.5
to 10.9 years. An intermediate elimination T
of 36 days was
estimated based on urinary alendronate excreted measured 1 to 6
months after dosing. In a short-term (28-day) study of risedronate
excretion, the “terminal exponential half-life” was reported as 561
hours (23.4 days).
The intermediate elimination T
—about 3
weeks for risedronate and 5 weeks for alendronate—probably represents its half-life on the surface of bone.
The half-life of alendronate in bone is shorter than the terminal
elimination half-life from the body because approximately 50%
of the drug recycled from bone is returned to bone and bound
to a different bone surface. Accumulation of a BP during longterm treatment may be modeled based on the average daily dose,
duration of treatment oral bioavailability, fractional uptake of the
absorbed BP into bone, retention in bone after approximately 6
months, and terminal elimination T
. In the case of alendronate
with an oral bioavailability of 0.64%, and 33% of total absorbed
dose retained in the body (6 months after administration), the skeletal accumulation would be about 21
μ
g per day with after a 10 mg
average daily oral dose (same as 70 mg once weekly). The total skeletal accumulation of alendronate is estimated to be about 40 mg
after 5 years and 75 mg after 10 years with either 10 mg daily or 70
mg once weekly. This model may also be used to estimate whether
prior BP treatment is likely to be sufficient to reduce bone resorption and prevent bone loss during a “drug holiday” (interruption of
treatment) after multiyear use (see below). If treatment is stopped
after 10 years of alendronate 70 mg per week, skeletal release of
alendronate into the circulation is approximately the same as that
produced by an oral dose of 2.5 mg per day.

## Molecular Mechanism of Action

The molecular mechanisms of action of BPs were established several years after they were approved for the treatment of Paget’s
disease or osteoporosis. BPs may be grouped into two classes based
on their mechanism of action. The non-nitrogen-containing BPs
including etidronate may be incorporated into adenine nucleotides that are nonhydrolysable analogs of adenosine triphosphate
(ATP). They may also inhibit aminoacylation of transfer RNA.
The result of these effects is cell death.
Nitrogen-containing
BPs (N-BPs) are not incorporated into ATP analogs. N-BPs specifically inhibit farnesyl diphosphate synthase,
a key enzyme
in the mevalonate pathway, leading to cholesterol synthesis. Inhibition of farnesyl diphosphate synthase reduces the isoprenoid
lipids geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP) and farnesyl diphosphate, leading to insufficient geranylgeranylation of regulatory
proteins required for normal cytoskeletal function and osteoclast
survival.
Low doses of N-BPs produce reversible inhibition of
osteoclast function while high doses result in apoptosis. Non-NBPs do not inhibit farnesyl diphosphate synthase.

### Mechanism of Action at the Cell and Tissue Level

Osteoclasts are uniquely sensitive to the effects of BPs because they
are exposed to much higher concentrations of BPs than other cells.
There are several reasons: (1) BPs are targeted to bone surfaces
because of their high affinity for hydroxyapatite, (2) osteoclasts solubilize BPs on bone surfaces when they acidify resorption lacunae
and trap them within the lacunae where their local concentration
may reach 1 mM,
and (3) BPs are hydrophilic, charged anions
that do enter cells by either diffusion or active or passive transport
across their plasma membranes. However, BPs enter osteoclasts by
endocytosis along with other contents within resorption lacunae.
BPs on bone surfaces have no recognized direct effect on osteoblasts, quiescent lining cells, or pre-osteoblasts primarily because
these cells do not solubilize alendronate on bone surfaces, do not
form resorption lacunae and concentrate BPs released from the
bone surface, and have much lower levels of endocytosis than do
mature osteoclasts. When pre-osteoclasts on a bone surface coated
with a BP become active osteoclasts, they accumulate BPs that
inhibit their general metabolic activity. In nonclinical studies,
[
H]alendronate is found within osteoclasts 12 to 15 hours after
it is administered.
As osteoclasts accumulate, BPs’ ruffled borders adjacent to the resorption lacuna are lost, their acidification
is inhibited,
and their resorption slows.
Alendronate in solution had little or no effect on isolated osteoclasts in vitro until the
concentration reached 0.5 mM.
Inhibition of bone resorption

by isolated osteoclasts in vitro is reversible. While sufficiently high
doses of N-BPs may cause osteoclast apoptosis, lower and moderate doses do not.
Nonclinical and clinical studies of BPs included detailed histologic examination of bone after short- and long-term treatment.
None of the N-BPs that are selective inhibitors of bone resorption
have been associated with clinically meaningful inhibition of bone
mineralization and/or osteomalacia. Treatment of patients with
extensive Paget’s disease with zoledronic acid may result in rapid
mineralization of pagetic bone that exceeds nutrient (calcium and
phosphate) availability, resulting in hypocalcemia, hypophosphatemia, secondary hyperparathyroidism, and transient delayed
mineralization.
In contrast, the nonselective BP etidronate may
directly inhibit bone mineralization at the standard doses for treatment of Paget’s disease.
Bone histomorphology of osteoporotic patients treated with
alendronate and other N-BPs shows greatly reduced rates of bone
formation that reach a stable plateau after about 1 year and remain
at the same low level during long-term treatment.
Decreased
bone remodeling during BP treatment might be associated with
similar decreases in both resorption and formation. While the
decrease in bone remodeling is large versus that observed in the
placebo group of untreated osteoporotic women in these studies,
bone remodeling in osteoporotic post-menopausal women may
be three times greater than that found in young pre-menopausal
women.
Thus the absolute rates of bone remodeling during
treatment of osteoporosis with oral BPs
are generally within the
range found in young pre-menopausal women.

### Mechanism of Action on Bone Strength

The objective of treatment of osteoporotic patients and people
who are losing bone (e.g., as a result of menopause, hypogonadism in men, or with administration of chronic glucocorticoids) is
to reduce their risk of fracture, and several BPs have been shown
to reduce the risk of vertebral and nonvertebral (including hip)
fractures during treatment for 3 to 6 years. There are several hypothetical mechanisms that may account for these favorable effects.
BPs generally prevent bone loss associated with menopause in
women, hypogonadism in men, and in persons on glucocorticoids
and, in doing so, prevent a decrease in bone strength resulting
from a decrease in bone mass. However, if this were their only
effect, it would take 10 years of treatment to prevent the anticipated 10% perimenopausal loss of bone mass
and 50% lower hip
or spine fracture risk (based on bone mineral density [BMD] vs.
fracture risk in epidemiologic studies).
As the reduction in risk
of spine and hip fractures occurs within 6 to 18 months,
when
changes in BMD are relatively small, their short-term effects on
bone strength are likely mediated through the effect of decreased
remodeling on bone microarchitecture. In clinical trials, this is
associated with a reduction in levels of biochemical measures of
bone remodeling such as N-telopeptide (a marker of bone resorption) and propeptide of type 1 procollagen. Moreover, improvement in trabecular microarchitecture has been observed during
long-term treatment with alendronate.

The vast majority of BP use is for the prevention and treatment
of osteoporosis, mostly in post-menopausal women, but also in
men and among men and women on glucocorticoids. Based on
good efficacy, relative safety, low cost, and convenient dosing,
guidelines place BPs as the first-choice therapy in post-menopausal women and men with osteoporosis.

BPs, particularly IV zoledronic acid, have dramatically changed
the landscape of management of Paget’s disease of bone. A single
IV infusion is often credited with a dramatic reversal of atypical
bone remodeling, and for some patients retreatment may not be
necessary for years, if not indefinitely (also see

### Gastrointestinal

Oral BPs can cause nausea, dyspepsia, esophagitis, and, rarely,
esophageal or gastric ulcerations.
It is rarely possible to determine the offending drug when BPs are used with aspirin or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs that commonly produce gastric
ulceration. Symptoms of heartburn and dyspepsia may occur in the
absence of mucosal lesions. The mechanism may be due to a direct
toxic effect on the esophageal mucosa when a tablet is retained in
the esophagus (pill esophagitis). Reflux of acidic gastric solutions of
BPs may also cause esophageal irritation; BPs should be used with

caution in patients with gastroesophageal reflux disease. Exercising
caution when taking an oral BP by imbibing the drug with a full
glass of water and remaining upright for at least 30 minutes postdose can partially mitigate esophageal adverse reactions. Weekly
dose regimens also permit minor esophageal injury to heal between
doses. Less than 20% of patients stop oral BPs due to GI intolerance.
A meta-analysis suggested an increased risk of esophageal
cancer,
but this concern was offset by data from a case-controlled
study of the risk of upper-GI cancer using the General Practice
Research Database and other meta-analyses with a more extensive
group of data sets that did not support such an association.

### Acute Phase Response Reactions

With the use of IV BPs, an “acute phase reaction” (APR) may occur.
APRs can include myalgias, arthralgias, abdominal/bone pain, malaise, and occasional pyrexia.
Mild and usually transient (typically
lasting less than 4 days) reactions may be present as often as 30% of
the time and more significant flulike malaise and musculoskeletal
pain occur in less than 10% of people. Symptoms are attenuated
with acetaminophen and tend to be less severe or absent with IV
BP readministration or among persons who have previously received
oral BPs. APRs are associated with circulating gamma-delta T cells
and may be more common in younger individuals.
Arthralgias
and bone pain are occasionally reported with oral BPs as well.

### Osteonecrosis of the Jaw

Osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) is defined as an area of exposed
bone in the maxillofacial region that does not heal within 8 weeks
and excludes osteoradionecrosis due to radiation therapy.
Medication-related ONJ requires a history of BP, receptor activator
of nuclear factor
κ
B ligand (RANKL) inhibitor or angiogenesis
inhibitor treatment.
The incidence, clinical presentation, and
management of ONJ differ greatly in patients with osteoporosis treated with BPs (or denosumab) and patients with cancer
metastases in bones receiving much higher doses. ONJ was first
described in cancer patients treated with IV pamidronate and
zoledronic acid.
The incidence of ONJ in cancer patients treated
with high-dose zoledronic acid or denosumab is approximately
1% to 2% per year.
During long-term treatment, cumulative incidence approached 10% in the setting of malignancy.
In contrast, ONJ in patients treated with oral or IV BPs at standard osteoporosis doses is a rare event. The incidence is estimated
at approximately 1 out of 10,000 exposed patients.
ONJ
may occur in the absence of BP treatment and the background
incidence of ONJ in untreated osteoporotic patients is unknown.
The pathophysiology of anti-resorptive-related ONJ has not
been established and a number of potential mechanisms have
been hypothesized.
The majority of cases of ONJ follow invasive dental procedures such as tooth extraction, dental implants,
or other jawbone surgery. Mandibular bone is more commonly
involved than maxillary bone. Periodontal or other dental disease
(extensive treated caries) are risk factors. Effective treatment of
periodontal disease, restoration of caries, or extraction of teeth
that cannot be restored should be completed prior to the start
of BPs or RANKL inhibitors. When ONJ is suspected, patients
should be referred to oral surgeons familiar with the differential
diagnosis (e.g., infectious osteitis or osteomyelitis) and treatment
of ONJ. While most cases of anti-resorptive-related ONJ are mild
and resolve with conservative measures (e.g., anti-microbial rin
ses),
long-term antibiotic treatment may be required when
infection is present. Interruption of treatment with BPs is recommended until the mouth is fully healed. Restarting anti-resorptive
drug therapy should be considered based on individual patients’
benefit and risk.
Controversy exits regarding the role of BP
discontinuation antecedent to invasive dental procedures. While
there are no studies that indicate that the risk of ONJ is reduced
when BPs are interrupted prior to invasive dental procedures, the
sustained inhibition of resorption of these drugs makes a short
break in therapy prior to dental work less meaningful in terms of
a detrimental effect on osteoporosis risk in most settings.

### Atypical Femoral Fracture

An atypical femoral fracture (AFF) is a fracture that occurs in the
subtrochanteric region of the femur distal to the lesser trochanter
with at least four of five additional features: minimal or no apparent trauma; a fracture line that originates in lateral cortex and is
transverse; complete fractures may extend to the medial cortex
while incomplete fractures only include the lateral cortex; and
noncomminuted or minimally comminuted or associated with
localized periosteal and/or endosteal thickening at the facture site
(“beaking” or “flaring”).
A prodromal syndrome of bone pain
is often associated with an incomplete AFF. AFFs are rare within
the absence of BP exposure (0.3 to 0.7/100,000 person-years) but
appear significantly associated with BP (and denosumab) use based
on large observational studies.

treatment; however, long-term management is unclear.
As the absolute risk of AFFs is much lower than the absolute
risk of typical hip fractures during treatment with BPs, approximately 100 hip fractures are prevented for each AFF.

### Musculoskeletal Pain Syndrome

Musculoskeletal symptoms may occur, typically beginning 1 to 3
months after the start of treatment with oral BPs. During postmarket surveillance, severe and intermittent bone, joint, and/or
muscle pain without evidence of active inflammation have been
reported in patients taking BPs. Most symptoms remit within 2
weeks after drug withdrawal and a subset of these patients may
have recurrent symptoms when rechallenged.

Use in Children and Pregnancy Considerations
BPs should not be used during pregnancy. Nonclinical reproductive toxicology studies demonstrated that high doses of BPs
throughout pregnancy cause maternal hypocalcemia and dystocia
associated with maternal death and subsequent fetal death. Maternal hypocalcemia may be associated with delayed ossification
of the fetal skeleton. Except for zoledronic acid that may cause
central nervous system, visceral, and external malformations in
animals, other BPs are not recognized as teratogenic. As they are
contraindicated during pregnancy, there are relatively few reports
of the outcome of pregnancy following BP use during pregnancy.
There are reports of planned use during pregnancy in patients
with osteogenesis imperfecta and hypercalcemia of malignancy.
Negative fetal outcomes were uncommon and were generally
attributed to concomitant use of other drugs. Several reviews of
the literature indicate no clear evidence of fetal or maternal harm
following generally brief exposure to these drugs.
Because
of the skeletal retention and gradual skeletal release following
treatment discontinuation, there are concerns about the use of
these drugs in pre-menopausal women of childbearing potential. Anecdotes exist suggesting no clear associations with fetal
abnormalities among women who have been past BP users and
have had subsequent pregnancies, many of whom have also been

inadvertently during early pregnancy likely have slightly worse
fetal outcomes overall, owing in part to the underlying disease for
which glucocorticoids are required. However, very cautious use
of BP is recommended in younger women with glucocorticoidinduced osteoporosis.

### Duration of Therapy and Bisphosphonate “Drug Holidays”

The duration of therapy recommended in approved product labeling is generally the same as the duration of the placebo-controlled
studies that demonstrated fracture risk reduction, for example,
4 years for alendronate and 3 years for risedronate. Rare adverse
events appear to be associated with continued treatment such as
ONJ or more frequent during protracted BP usage such as AFF
do not appear to persist following discontinuation of treatment.
Moreover, some benefits persist even after BPs are stopped—
including modest sustained reduction in bone remodeling,
relative stability of BMD (particularly at the spine), and a modest
protracted fracture benefit
—have motivated consideration of
taking a break from BP therapy, often termed a drug holiday. A
task force of the American Society of Bone and Mineral Research
(ASBMR)
(see
Fig. 67.4
) has suggested that after three doses of
IV BPs
and 5 years of oral therapy, a break in therapy should
be considered among patients at lower future fracture risk for up
to 1 to 3 years.
Guidelines developed by the American Association of Clinical Endocrinologists are similar.
However, for some
patients who remain at high risk of fractures,
it is unwarranted
to stop all anti-fracture medications. Instead, switching to a lower
dose of the same BP or an osteoanabolic drug is a consideration.
In patients who discontinue BPs, their risk of fracture should be
monitored periodically and increase within 2 years, particularly
for those BPs (such as risedronate) that are less avid for bone.

While a decrease in lumbar spine or total hip BMD that exceeds
the precision error of the methods is commonly used for this purpose, a new fragility fracture or increased risk of falls may also
signal the need to restart a BP or another a different osteoporosis
drug.
