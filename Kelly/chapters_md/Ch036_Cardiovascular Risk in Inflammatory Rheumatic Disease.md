# Cardiovascular Risk in Inflammatory Rheumatic Disease

ANNE GRETE SEMB, ANNE KEROLA, SIRI LILLEGRAVEN,
AND EIRIK IKDAHL

KEY POINTS

> Excess rates of cardiovascular disease (CVD) have been reported among

> patients with a variety of inflammatory rheumatic diseases.

> CVD mortality and morbidity—in particular, ischemic heart disease

> and heart failure—are significantly higher among people with

> rheumatoid arthritis (RA), systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), and

> other autoimmune disorders compared with people in the general

> population of the same age who do not have these diseases.

> Although the prevalence of some traditional CVD risk factors is elevated

> in people with inflammatory rheumatic diseases, these elevations

> alone do not adequately explain the excess CVD risk.

> The systemic inflammation and immune dysfunction that characterize

> rheumatic diseases appear to be major drivers of the increased CVD

> risk in these patients.

> The relationship between rheumatic drugs and CVD risk is difficult to

> disentangle because of confounding by indication/contraindication.

## Introduction

For nearly half a century, excess rates of cardiovascular (CV) disease (CVD) have been reported among people with inflammatory
rheumatic diseases.
More recently, the discovery of the inflammatory and immune mechanisms underlying atherosclerosis has
spurred renewed interest in the association between CVD risk and
the rheumatic diseases. In this chapter, we review biologic mechanisms underlying CVD comorbidity in the rheumatic diseases and
discuss the risks of these diseases, with a focus on people with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).
We also discuss the contribution of traditional and nontraditional
CVD risk factors (CVDRFs) to the observed excess CVD risk.
CVD risk in other rheumatic diseases will also be briefly reviewed.

most common and best studied of the autoimmune rheumatic
diseases. The immune underpinnings of CVD and of RA share
many similarities. Circulating acute-phase reactants, such as CRP,
are elevated in people with RA and are risk markers for heart disease in the general population. Emerging evidence suggests that

People with RA are at increased risk of developing HF compared
with the general population.
In the Rochester RA cohort,
the cumulative incidence of HF (defined according to the Framingham criteria) at 30-year follow-up was 34%, compared with
25% in the non-RA cohort (
Fig. 36.3

Even after adjustment
for demographics, CV risk factors, and IHD, patients with RA
had almost twice the risk of developing HF as subjects without
RA (HR, 1.87; 95% CI, 1.47 to 2.39). This increased risk of HF
appeared to be predominant in the subgroup of patients with
rheumatoid factor (RF) who were RF

to general population comparator subjects.
A more recent study
again confirmed an increased risk for HF among RA patients
compared with patients without rheumatologic conditions. The
excess risk was independent of traditional cardiovascular risk factors, including prevalent coronary artery disease.
Of note, the

may be a result of more aggressive RA treatment, including the use

Cardiovascular Mortality in Rheumatoid Arthritis
Mortality of patients with established RA is higher than that of
the general population. A meta-analysis of 24 mortality studies in
people with RA, which were published between 1970 and 2005,
reported a weighted combined all-cause standardized mortality
ratio (met-SMR) of 1.50 (95% CI, 1.39 to 1.61) with similar
increases for IHD (met-SMR, 1.59; 95% CI, 1.46 to 1.73) and
stroke (met-SMR, 1.52; 95% CI, 1.40 to 1.67) and for men (metSMR, 1.45) and women (met-SMR, 1.58).
Approximately
50% of all deaths in subjects with RA are attributable to CV causes,
including IHD and stroke,
and CVD appears to occur earlier
in people with RA. A large community-based prospective cohort
study that used UK Biobank data confirmed higher rates of CVDrelated mortality in multiple inflammatory conditions, including
RA, and reported, once again, that these events can occur early in
the disease course.
The latter observation is consistent with the
recent hypothesis of accelerated aging in people with RA.
Moreover, people with RA frequently experience “silent” IHD and/or
silent MI—that is, showing no symptoms at all before a sudden
cardiac death. Sudden cardiac deaths are almost twice as common
in patients with RA as in the general population (HR, 1.99; 95%
CI, 1.06 to 3.55).
One group recently demonstrated mortality
due to CVD to be increased among Norwegian RA (and axSpA)
patients compared with the general population.
The excess CVD mortality related to RA may be confined to,
or at least be substantially higher in, subjects who are RF

Controversy persists regarding how soon after symptom onset

follow-up—that is, follow-up starting from the time of symptom
onset, from a physician’s diagnosis of RA, or from the date of fulfillment of ACR criteria or other diagnostic criteria. The latter
may not occur until some years after the first symptoms appear. In
the Norfolk Arthritis Register (NOAR), the excess CVD mortality is detectable beginning around 7 years after symptom onset.
In a Dutch inception cohort of 1049 patients with RA recruited
between 1985 and 2007, excess mortality became apparent at
around 10 years after diagnosis (with all subjects having <1 year
symptom duration).
Emerging evidence indicates that mortality may be improving among people with RA, but CV mortality has also improved in the general population; thus the relative
increase in CV mortality in patents with RA compared with the
general population has not changed over time.
A 2009 metaanalysis that included 17 studies (91,916 patients) also showed no
trend toward improving CVD mortality with time.
A follow-up
analysis from the Mayo Clinic published in 2021 assessed trends
in incidence of cardiovascular disease (CVD) and mortality following incident CVD events in patients with RA.
On the other
hand, importantly, this analysis
showed that the incidence of

the gap in CVD occurrence between patients with RA and the
general population is closing.

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Ischemic Heart Disease in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
Accelerated atherosclerosis and an increased risk of CAD are well
established in SLE.
The prevalence of atherosclerotic vascular
events varies from 1.8% early in the course of the disease to more

than 27% later in the course of SLE.
The majority of studies
reported increased risk of MI ranging from twofold to greater
than tenfold in various SLE patient groups compared with the
general population.
This increased RR of MI is particularly apparent in younger patients with SLE. The most striking
example comes from the University of Pittsburgh lupus cohort, in
which women with SLE aged 35 to 44 years were more than 50
times more likely to have an MI compared with women without
SLE in the Framingham Offspring study (RR, 52.43; 95% CI,
21.6 to 98.5).
Furthermore, the majority (67%) of women with
SLE were younger than 55 years of age at the time of their first
cardiac event. In addition, a more than twofold increased risk of
hospitalizations for MI have been reported in young women with
SLE between the ages of 18 and 44 years compared with people
without SLE (OR, 2.27; 95% CI, 1.08 to 3.46).
As in RA, it
appears that the increased risk of CVD among people with SLE
may begin before the diagnosis of SLE (OR, 3.7; 95% CI, 1.8 to
7.9). One report in a population-based study of 70 patients who
had SLE compared with 2565 patients who did not have SLE
supported similar findings.
The risk of CVD and mortality also
increased tenfold among women with SLE who had successful
deliveries, despite the rarity of CVD among women of reproductive age.
More recently a large case-control study (5018 patients with
SLE and 25,090 age- and sex-matched controls) was conducted
in Israel examining the association between ischemic heart disease and SLE.
Multivariate analysis demonstrated that SLE was
strongly associated with IHD (OR, 3.77; 95% CI, 3.34 to 4.26),
confirming that SLE patients (especially women) suffer from an
excess risk of IHD and that SLE is an independent risk factor for
IHD.
In a study of patients undergoing coronary revascularization
procedures, no significant differences were found in the mean percentage of coronary stenosis and total occlusion in subjects with
and without SLE.
Except for the increased likelihood of lesions
confined to the left anterior descending artery in subjects with
SLE versus subjects without SLE (42% versus 19%;
P
= .003), the
pattern of coronary involvement, including artery dominance and
prevalence of multivessel disease, appeared similar in subjects with
SLE versus subjects without SLE. However, the study reported
significantly worse CV outcomes at 1 year after PCI in subjects
with SLE versus subjects without SLE, including higher risk of
MI (16% versus 5%;
P
= .01) and repeat PCI (31% versus 12%;
P
= .009) in people with SLE, even after adjustment for important
covariates.
Given the increased vulnerability of coronary atherosclerosis in people with SLE, which is associated with the risk
of occlusive events irrespective of the size of the plaque, these findings suggest an increased risk of unfavorable CV events in people
who had SLE compared with people who did not have SLE with a
seemingly similar pattern of coronary involvement.
A large population-based study from 1996 to 2000 of patients
hospitalized in California with acute MI showed that in-hospital
mortality and length of stay were essentially similar in patients
who had SLE compared with patients who did not have SLE; the
study adjusted for age, race, ethnicity, type of medical insurance,
and Charlson Index.
In contrast, data from the 1993 to 2002
U.S. Nationwide Inpatient Sample showed significantly increased
rates of in-hospital mortality (RR, 1.46; 95% CI, 1.31 to 1.61)
and prolonged hospitalization (RR, 1.68; 95% CI, 1.43 to 2.04)
for acute MI in people who had SLE compared with control subjects after adjusting for age, sex, race/ethnicity, income, and HF.
Differences in methodology (e.g., a smaller sample size, older age
of patients and controls in the earlier study, potential ascertainment bias, and a shorter observation period in the later study) may
partially explain these contrasting results.
Considering the presence of myocardial involvement, chronic systemic inflammation,
vasculitis, and hyperviscosity syndrome in SLE, worse outcomes
after acute coronary events and associated interventions can be
expected.
Further CVD risk in other rheumatic diseases will also
be briefly reviewed. Additional studies are warranted.
Given these well-documented excess risks, studies have called
for research assessing the predictive ability of biomarkers, imaging techniques, and other monitoring tools to address the initial (potentially reversible) stages of atherosclerosis.
Promising
results have been reported for CV magnetic resonance (CMR) as a
tool to detect subclinical cardiac involvement in SLE
; however,
these findings have not been replicated.
Heart Failure in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
The risk of HF and related hospitalization in people with SLE
appears to be substantially increased.
In particular, young
women with SLE between the ages of 18 and 44 years have more
than a 2.5-fold increased risk of hospitalization for HF compared
with people who do not have SLE, even after adjustment for age,
race, insurance status, hospital characteristics, and the presence
of hypertension (HT), diabetes mellitus (DM), and chronic renal
failure.
A recent large multicenter population-based study further elucidated risk factors for heart failure, identifying 523 (2.3%)
HF cases among 1466 children, 2163 young adults, and 19,349
adults age 25 to 44 with SLE.
In this cohort 50% or more of HF
diagnoses in children and young adults presented near the time of
initial SLE diagnosis, reminiscent of similar observations in RA.
Also, several risk factors for the development of HF later in the
disease course were identified, including baseline cardiac or renal
involvement and antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, as well as
modifiable CV risk factors such as HT. The study also reported
significant racial disparities in HF risk and indicated that early
antimalarial use may be associated with a decreased risk of HF.
The nature of HF in SLE is likely multifactorial and only partly
attributable to atherosclerosis.
The presentation of HF in people with SLE may vary from severe overt HF to insidious myocardial involvement.
Finally, mortality in patients with SLE who
have HF is significantly higher than in patients without HF (18%
versus 6%,
P
< .001) and approximates 3.5-fold compared with
patients who have HF in the general population.
Cardiovascular Mortality in Systemic Lupus
Erythematosus
Mortality in SLE follows a bimodal pattern, with an early peak
in mortality (within 1 year of diagnosis) and a later peak occurring more than 5 years after diagnosis. Reported survival in the
first 5 years of SLE has improved considerably from about 50%
in the 1950s to greater than 90% in the 1990s.
However, this
improvement may be due, at least in part, to earlier diagnosis
and improved ascertainment of mild cases. In the Toronto lupus
cohort of 1241 patients recruited between 1970 and 2005, the
SMR improved from 13.84 (95% CI, 9.78 to 19.76) during
1970 to 1978 for those who entered the cohort in that decade to
3.81 (95% CI, 1.98 to 7.32) during 1997 to 2005 in people who
entered the cohort in that period.
The SMR during 1997 to
2005 was very similar for patients regardless of their disease duration; it ranged from 3.23 for those who had entered the cohort
in 1970 to 1978 to 3.93 for those who had entered the cohort in
1988 to 1996. Similarly, evidence from Olmsted County showed

an SMR of 2.70 with significant improvement in survival in
recent decades.
In a study of 434 female patients with SLE from
Seoul, Korea, who were followed up from 1992 to 2002, the SMR
was 3.02 (95% CI, 1.45 to 5.55).
A recent population-based
cohort study of 70 patients with incident SLE in 1991 to 2008
reported nearly twofold increased mortality and increased CVD
rates. In addition, increased mortality and CVD event rates were
found perioperatively in women with SLE who were undergoing
surgical procedures, even for low-risk procedures (OR, 1.54; 95%
CI, 1.00 to 2.37).
A community-based prospective cohort study
from the United Kingdom showed increased CV-related mortality
in SLE and emphasized the public health value of early screening
and effective preventive strategies.
More recently a carefully conducted, 10-year case-control
study from the Karolinska Institute showed that patients with
mild SLE have a three- to fourfold increased risk of incident CV
events and mortality compared with controls with the same levels
of traditional risk factors and intima-media measures (by carotid
ultrasound).
These findings appear to be related to accumulated
disease damage and antiphospholipid syndrome. Notably, the
early observations of a bimodal pattern in mortality was not seen
in this study. Rather the findings were more in line with longterm follow-up studies that revisited the concept of the bimodal
pattern of mortality in SLE and showed the importance of both
disease severity and moderate to severe atherosclerosis for death of
any cause in patients with SLE after a prolonged duration of the
disease.

### Traditional Cardiovascular Risk Factors in Rheumatoid Arthritis: Occurrence and Impact

In the general population, five major CVDRFs (i.e., HT, elevated total cholesterol [TC] levels, smoking, obesity, and DM)
account for 80% of the risk for MI
and half of the CV mortality.
Moreover, a recent meta-analysis found that these five
major CVDRFs also increased the risk of CVD in patients
with RA.
One possible explanation for the increase in CVD
in people with RA could be that the traditional CVDRFs are
more common in people with RA or that they are as common
but more deleterious. The five CVDRFs (i.e., HT, elevated TC,
smoking, obesity, and DM) were comparable across patients
with RA, ankylosing spondylitis (AS), and psoriatic arthritis
(PsA), except for more HT and obesity in the older age categories.
In addition, the excess CVD risk may also be explained by
the adverse impact of the inflammatory and immune changes of
RA on the vessel wall. It is most likely, however, that traditional
risk factors and inflammation are intimately interconnected
and perhaps act synergistically (
Fig. 36.6
).
Indeed, one study
showed that progression of a new CVD event in subjects with
early RA may be predicted by traditional CVDRFs but precipitated by high disease activity.

Disparities in Cardiovascular Risk
Currently, there are controversial results on the influence of sex in
the risk of CV morbidity and mortality in patients with RA and
other inflammatory joint diseases (IJDs). RA is more common in
women, whereas AS is more frequent in men, and the sexes are
equally represented in PsA. This different prevalence according to
sex of the IJDs makes it difficult to conclude on the actual influence of sex on the risk of CVD and heart complications in these
chronic inflammatory rheumatic diseases. Further studies addressing this specific issue are still needed.
A study on socioeconomic status on CV risk screening and
management revealed that at least 25% of the patients with high
CV risk did not follow the recommendation to contact their general practitioner for a follow-up. Higher education decreased the
odds to follow recommendations for a follow-up. The diagnosis of
SpA, AS, or PsA significantly increased the odds of having a consultation at general practice with a relevant action compared with
RA patients. Sex, age, diagnosis, cohabitant status, and employment status did not influence whether the high-risk patients consulted their general practitioner.
Smoking in People With Rheumatoid Arthritis
The link between tobacco smoking and CVD events is well
known and is identified both in the general population and in
patients with RA.
Smoking is a known risk factor for the
development of RA, in particular RF
and ACPA
RA.
In a
meta-analysis of four case-control studies of traditional CVDRFs
in RA that included 1415 patients with RA, the prevalence of
smoking was found to be significantly higher than in control subjects (OR, 1.56; 95% CI, 1.35 to 1.80).
A known interaction
exists between smoking,
HLA-DR1
shared epitope (SE) alleles,
and the production of ACPA
and between smoking, ACPA,
and the SE in premature CVD mortality in RA.
There are some
discrepancies regarding smokers with RA having higher disease
activity.
In the A TransAtlantic Cardiovascular Consortium for Rheumatoid Arthritis patients (ATACC-RA) register, in
which 3311 RA patients were followed for a median of 3.5 years,
smoking cessation resulted in comparable disease activity as nonsmokers and having a significant lower disease activity compared
with smokers. Furthermore, smoking cessation was a predictor of
reduced CVD event rates in patients with RA.

Hypertension in Rheumatoid Arthritis
HT is common in people with RA, but it remains unclear whether
it is more common than in the general population. Whereas some
studies have found HT to be an important risk factor for CVD
in people with RA,
a recent meta-analysis of seven case-control
studies (1053 patients with RA) found the prevalence of HT to
be the same in people with RA as in control subjects (OR, 1.09;
95% CI, 0.91 to 1.31).
However, evidence exists for underdiagnosis and undertreatment of HT in people with RA.
Multiple other factors may influence blood pressure control in people
with RA, including physical inactivity, obesity, specific genetic
polymorphisms, smoking, and some anti-rheumatic medications,
including nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), corticosteroids, leflunomide, and cyclosporine. In a recent systematic
review from 2021, the conclusion was that overall, there is a paucity of research investigating factors associated with incident HT
among RA patients in longitudinal studies. The findings indicated
that regular exercise and methotrexate (MTX) may protect against
HT, while corticosteroids and COX-2 inhibitors may increase risk
of HT in RA patients. Physicians should consider these risks when
prescribing anti-rheumatic medication to their patients.

of genetic regulation of lipid metabolism, particularly in the context of gene–environment interactions, has not been addressed
in the RA population. This factor may be particularly important
because lipid alterations appear to predate the diagnosis of RA.
Although the relationships between lipids and CVD in RA are
complex, the value of primary lipid screening in people with RA
is undisputed. Unfortunately, evidence suggests an unacceptably
low rate of primary lipid screening and of statin use in people with
RA.
This has not improved over the years, as shown in
a recent large international survey including RA patients from 19
countries between 2014 and 2019.

Medications and Cardiovascular Risk
Medications used for the treatment of rheumatic diseases may
also affect CVD risk. Because of the common use of NSAIDs and
concerns surrounding CVD risk with their use, this subject has
been extensively studied. Although some evidence indicates that
NSAID use is not associated with increased CVD risk in people
with RA,
a meta-analysis of 31 trials in 116,429 patients concluded that there was little evidence to suggest that any of the
investigated drugs (i.e., naproxen, ibuprofen, diclofenac, celecoxib, etoricoxib, rofecoxib, or lumiracoxib) were not safe in relation to CVD.
In contrast, use of DMARDs (methotrexate and
hydroxychloroquine in particular) and/or biologic agents may
decrease CVD risk.
A recent large meta-analysis evaluating
b-DMARDS on risk of CV event, including 55 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and 17 cohort studies in patients with RA,
psoriasis, and SLE, found that b-DMARDS reduced CVD events.
Although these results were mostly driven by the reduced events
in RA patients,
this outcome likely reflects effective long-term
control of systemic inflammation. Although these findings are
intriguing, they cannot be considered definitive evidence that
these agents reduce CVD risk because of confounding by indication and/or contraindication. Reports of the effect of corticosteroids on CVD risk are conflicting, but low doses (<7.5 mg/day)
may be beneficial, and high doses may be detrimental.
A
study in an early RA, treatment-naïve RCT cohort with no known
CV history and maximum of one traditional risk factor (excluding diabetes) revealed that abnormal vascular stiffness (which predicts major CV events independently of traditional clinical risk
scoring models in patients without known CVD) and evidence
of diffuse myocardial fibrosis and reduced left ventricular mass.
First-line etanercept with methotrexate or initial methotrexatetreat-to-target strategy improved vascular stiffness. However, RA
treatment response and cumulative disease activity did not appear
to add to this improvement in vascular stiffness.
JAK inhibitors (consisting of tofacitinib, baricitinib, and upadacitinib) are
a class of targeted synthetic DMARDs that are increasingly used
for management of patients diagnosed with moderate to severely
active RA.
In an RCT comparing tofacitinib with a TNF
inhibitor in a cardiovascular risk–enriched population, risks of
major adverse cardiovascular events (MACEs) and cancers were
higher with tofacitinib. Several adverse events were more common
with tofacitinib.
Concerns regarding venous thromboembolism
related to JAK inhibitor treatments have been raised, but evidence
is not conclusive.
Consequently, in the longer term, large studies are needed to accurately quantify thromboembolic risks attributable to JAK inhibitors and other drugs used to treat RA and to
differentiate these risks from those attributable to RA itself and its
comorbidities.
During the past few decades, mounting evidence on CVD risk
in RA has led to advances in CVD risk management in this patient
population.
Cardiovascular outcome trials (CVOTs) evaluating
modern DMARD medications versus placebo are not ethically
sound and will never be done. However, several DMARDs, especially methotrexate, TNF inhibitors, and IL-6 inhibitors, seem
to improve various biomarkers of CVD and are associated with
reduced risk of CV events in observational studies among RA
cohorts.

### Traditional and Nontraditional Cardiovascular Risk Factors in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

While the traditional CV risk factors may not fully explain the
increased CV risk observed in SLE patients, they are frequent and
need to be addressed to prevent CV events.
For instance,
SLE patients have increased levels of elevated LDL cholesterol
and triglycerides, as well as high prevalences of sedentary lifestyles,
smoking, premature menopause, chronic renal impairment, HT,
hyperhomocysteinemia, metabolic syndrome, and insulin resistance.
Indeed, SLE patients with HT or dyslipidemias have
higher risk of experiencing an MI or stroke.
Since SLE is
a relatively uncommon disease, scientific documentation of the
effects of common CV preventive medication is scarce, and existing studies often have designs that are prone to confounding.
Based on the scarce available evidence, the authors of the first
European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR)
recommendation for CV risk management in SLE patients,
published in 2022, advocates for a blood pressure (BP) target of
<130/80 and that one should follow recommendations for the
general population in dyslipidemia treatment.
The association between systemic inflammation and CV risk
is well known and has been described in detail previously in
this chapter. High cumulative disease activity appears to be an
important cause of the high CV event rates reported in SLE
patients.
Although some studies have not found an association between recent assessments of SLE disease activity and future
risk for CV events,
this is likely because SLE is a chronic

remitting–relapsing disease and because atherosclerotic CV disease takes years to develop. In fact, previous studies have shown
that prolonged disease remission is associated with better CV outcomes, and in the EULAR recommendations for CV risk management in SLE, maintaining low disease activity is recommended
to reduce the CV risk.
Although no specific immunosuppressive medicine could be recommended due to limited scientific
knowledge, the lowest possible glucocorticoid dose should be used
to minimize adverse cardiometabolic effects.

## Cardiovascular Mortality, Morbidity, and Risk Factors in Other Rheumatic Diseases

### Ankylosing Spondylitis

The risk of CV disease in people with AS is less well defined than
for RA. The available data point toward increased mortality rates
in AS patients compared with the general population.
It appears that increased frequencies of CVD is a main driver of
the increased mortality in AS patients, although they seem to be
at lower risk than RA patients.
Traditional CVD risk factors, including dyslipidemia and HT, are commonly found in AS
patients, and there exists much room for improvement in terms of
introducing and intensifying preventive measures to obtain lipid
and blood pressure treatment goals.

### Psoriasis and Psoriatic Arthritis

Increased rates of CVD morbidity in cutaneous psoriasis and PsA
patients relative to the general population have been reported,
although, as in AS patients, the documentation is less strong than
for people with RA.
While a great deal of heterogeneity exists in
the studies in this field, two meta-analyses have reported a pooled
odds ratio (95% CI) of MI and stroke of 1.19 (1.11 to 1.27) and
1.46 (1.26 to 1.69) in cutaneous psoriasis, while it was 1.68 (1.31
to 2.15) and 1.2 (1.05 to 1.41) in PsA.
Several studies have
reported a striking increase in the metabolic syndrome, meaning
a co-occurrence of several traditional CVD risk factors including
obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and HT, in patients with
cutaneous psoriasis and PsA.
While there is an unmet need
for traditional CVD-preventive measures in PsA patients, the frequency of various CVD events remains high even after adjusting
for traditional CVD risk variables.
Interestingly, while there is a small but significant increase in
CVD related mortality in patients with cutaneous psoriasis, there
are now controversies regarding whether the rates of CVD mortality are raised in PsA patients compared with the general population.
Although the underlying reasons for these rather
surprising results have yet to be settled, it may be due to improved
PsA therapies, and there may still be excess mortality due to CVD
in patients with more aggressive disease.

### Giant Cell Arteritis, Takayasu’s Arteritis, and Polymyalgia Rheumatica

Although there are some conflicting data on the risk of cardiovascular disease in giant cell arteritis (GCA),
recent analyses
indicate an increased mortality from CVD.
The increased
risk of vascular events is due to both direct and indirect effects of
GCA, a relationship that can be complex to delineate.
For
Takayasu’s arteritis, a meta-analysis found the pooled prevalence
of stroke to be 8.9 and 3.4, respectively, suggesting up to a tenfold
increase in severe cardiovascular events in this patient group.
Traditional CV risk factors are also more common in patients
with Takayasu’s arteritis.
Previous treatment recommendations
for large vessel vasculitis (GCA and Takayasu’s arteritis) have indicated that aspirin should be used for primary prevention in these
diseases, but recent data have led to newer recommendations that
treatment with platelet inhibitors should follow recommendations
used in the general population.
A few studies have reported increased risks for CVD in patients
with polymyalgia rheumatica, which are similar to the risk levels
reported in other inflammatory diseases.
However, two systematic reviews found the studies to be heterogenous, suggesting
that more research is needed to definitively determine whether the
risk of CVD is increased in polymyalgia rheumatica.

### Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibodies (ANCA)– Associated Vasculitis

ANCA-associated vasculitis is a heterogenous group of diseases
including granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA, previously
termed Wegener’s granulomatosis),
microscopic polyangiitis (MPA),
and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA, previously termed Churg–Strauss syndrome).
A
population-based study demonstrated a threefold increased risk
of CVD and an eightfold increased risk of cerebrovascular events
in patients with ANCA-associated vasculitis without increased
prevalence of CV risk factors compared with the general population.
The increased risk for CVD has been confirmed in a
number of studies.

### Dermatomyositis and Polymyositis

Cardiac involvement is well recognized as a clinically important
manifestation of myositis.
In a recent meta-analysis patients
with dermatomyositis and polymyositis had a 2.4 times increased
risk of CVD, particularly males diagnosed in the previous 5
years.
One study found the incidence of cardiovascular events
in a combined population of dermatomyositis and polymyositis to
be comparable to that of patients with RA in the first 5 years after
diagnosis.
A report utilizing the Nationwide Inpatient Sample
found that one-fifth of dermatomyositis hospitalizations in the
United States were associated with an atherosclerotic CV diagnosis or procedure; the report also found that patients with dermatomyositis and CVD have twice the risk of in-hospital death
compared with control subjects and patients who have dermatomyositis without CVD.

### Osteoarthritis

Although osteoarthritis is the most common rheumatic disease,
little is known about the link between osteoarthritis and CVD.
While the link between systemic inflammation and CVD is not
as apparent in this degenerative disease as in the IJDs, excessive
pro-inflammatory cytokine production also plays a role in the
pathogenesis of osteoarthritis.
Furthermore, genetic, metabolic,
and neuroendocrine factors, as well as lifestyle factors, including
sedentary lifestyles, are possible links between CV disease and
osteoarthritis.
Previous studies have found that patients with
osteoarthritis have a high prevalence of CVD risk factors such as
HT, dyslipidemia, BMI, and increased fasting glucose levels.
Indeed, a previous meta-analysis reported significantly higher

CVD rates in osteoarthritis patients than in controls; perhaps
most striking was the doubled risk of experiencing heart failure or
ischemic heart disease.
Whether this increased risk is simply a
result of high burdens of CVD risk factors or whether osteoarthritis is also an independent risk factor for CVD remains unknown.

### Pediatric-Onset Rheumatologic Diseases

CVD has been difficult to study in patients with pediatric-onset
rheumatologic disease, such as juvenile idiopathic arthritis, pediatric-onset SLE, and juvenile dermatomyositis, as CV events are
rare at young ages.
However, traditional and disease-related
CVD risk factors are evident in children with rheumatic diseases,
with juvenile SLE, for example, being associated with premature
atherosclerosis beginning in childhood, early myocardial infarction, and stroke.
Data on hospitalizations in the United
States showed juvenile dermatomyositis to be associated with
significantly higher odds of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular
comorbidities, with adolescents, girls, and racial/ethnic minorities
being at highest risk.
Surrogate measures of CVD, such as flow-mediated dilation,
carotid intima media thickness, and pulse wave velocity, have been
used to explore CVD in children with pediatric-onset rheumatologic diseases. Studies of these measures of subclinical CVD and
of lipids have shown increased levels among children with juvenile
idiopathic arthritis, pediatric-onset SLE, and juvenile dermatomyositis.
It is likely that these patients will experience significant CVD morbidity and mortality in adulthood. More studies
are needed to better define the increased risk of CVD among
patients with pediatric-onset rheumatologic diseases, to design
and test preventive strategies to circumvent this eventuality, and
to improve the long-term outcomes of these patients. Although
data on the increased risk of CVD among patients with less widely
studied inflammatory rheumatic diseases are still emerging, CVD
has been recognized as an important contribution to increased
mortality and morbidity in this patient population and represents
an area of ongoing research.

## Managing Cardiovascular Risk in Rheumatic Disease

### Coordinating Care: Cardiology and Rheumatology

Despite the large and growing body of knowledge showing
increased risk of CVD in people with RA and other rheumatic diseases, even when controlling for traditional CV risk factors,
little translation into clinical care has occurred.
Inflammation
and immune mechanisms underline the atherosclerotic process.
Because traditional risk factors alone do not explain the elevated
CVD risk in patients with rheumatic diseases, addressing the control of inflammation is essential to decrease the CVD risk.
Thus close collaboration between the rheumatologist and the
cardiologist is vital.
Not only does the rheumatologic
condition need to be treated aggressively to decrease the inflammatory burden but the traditional CV risk factors must also be
identified and appropriately treated. Specialized preventive cardiorheuma clinics that specifically address the needs of rheumatologic
patients allow these patients to be followed up in a more rigorous
fashion, with more uniform evaluation and longitudinal follow-up.
Examples of such clinics include the Preventive Cardio-Rheuma
clinic at the Department of Rheumatology at Diakonhjemmet
Hospital in Norway and the Cardio-Rheum clinic at the Division
of Cardiovascular Diseases at the Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota. Both models represent deep and ongoing collaborations
between the clinical disciplines of rheumatology and preventive
cardiology. Integral to both models are (1) extensive patient and
provider education regarding CVD risk in the unique setting of
autoimmune diseases, such as RA; (2) comprehensive CVD risk
assessment including history, physical examination, and use of
relevant risk assessment scores, as well as noninvasive and (when
indicated) invasive measurement techniques; and (3) development
and monitoring of an aggressive plan for CVD risk reduction,
considering not only traditional CVD risks but also tight control
of systemic inflammation and RA disease management. Each program also includes a clinical research component aimed at continually evaluating and improving the preventive care paradigms.

## Cardiovascular Disease Risk Assessment

### Risk Calculators

As previously discussed, traditional CVD risk factors such as
dyslipidemia, HT, and smoking are common inflammatory
rheumatic disease entities. However, such traditional CV risk
factors alone cannot account for the increased risk of CVD in
these patients.
Moreover, traditional CV risk factors appear
to behave differently in patients with inflammatory rheumatic
diseases compared with the general population, suggesting that
risk scores based on traditional CV risk factors alone are unlikely
to accurately estimate CVD risk in these patients. Indeed, risk
calculators (e.g., the Framingham Risk Score [FRS]) have underestimated CVD risk twofold in patients with RA.
The Systematic COronary Risk Evaluation (SCORE), QRISK II, and
Reynolds Risk Score (RRS) CVD risk calculators also underestimate future CV events in patients with RA, except for those in
the highest risk classes.
For SLE patients, several studies
have found that SCORE and FRS performs poorly,
even
after applying a multiplier of 2.
More recently, the CVD risk
prediction scores have also been tested on hard CVD endpoints
in smaller cohorts of AS and PsA patients, with unsatisfying res
ults.
The 2012 European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines for
CVD prevention were the first to recognize immunologic diseases,
such as RA, as CVD risk factors.
Later, the presence of RA
has also been implemented in CVD risk algorithms such as the
United Kingdom CV risk calculators and the QRISK2 and 3, as
well as in the Norwegian NORRISK2.
The EULAR recognizes that patients with IJDs have increased
risk for CVD and make recommendations for management of
CV risk factors, which they base largely on expert consensus.
The EULAR task force recommended a multiplication factor
of 1.5 to the calculated CVD risk by SCORE for all patients
with RA. This multiplier and other RA- and SLE-specific risk
calculators have generally underperformed or have a lack external
documentation of improved prediction accuracy compared with
risk calculators used in the general population.
Attempts
to apply the 1.5 EULAR RA multiplier in AS and SLE patients
have also been disappointing.
Further development of
accurate CVD risk calculators for patients with chronic inflammatory rheumatic diseases and external validation of previously
developed calculators are needed to improve CVD risk prediction in these patients.

### Risk Scores and Risk Markers: Biomarkers and Tests for Subclinical Disease

One reason for underestimation of CVD risk in people with RA
may be the high frequency of asymptomatic atherosclerosis,
which is easily visualized upon ultrasound of the carotid arteries. In
addition, carotid artery plaques in patients with RA are predictors
of a future acute coronary syndrome.
RA-specific factors, such
as disease duration and disease activity, have been associated with
plaque size and vulnerability in patients with RA.
Moreover, in
the European guidelines on CVD prevention,
the presence of
carotid artery plaques is considered a CVD equivalent, which suggests that including ultrasound of carotid arteries in the CVD risk
evaluation (class IIa, level of evidence: B, GRADE: strong) would
increase the proportion of correct risk stratification.
Due to the
high pre-test probability for detection of carotid artery plaques
by use of ultrasound in patients with RA and the clinical consequence of indication for statin treatment if a carotid plaque is
present, this procedure could be of additional value for CVD risk
evaluation. Ultrasound of the carotid arteries to identify atherosclerosis has reclassified a considerable proportion of patients with
RA into a more appropriate CVD risk group in accordance with
current guidelines.
Other tests for subclinical disease that might be useful for
assessing CVD risk in patients with RA include aortic pulse wave
velocity and lipoprotein(a).
Soluble biomarkers are also appealing because they are typically less costly and have more widespread availability.
However, they also may be more difficult to
interpret in patients with rheumatic disease because of their dual
association with CVD and rheumatic disease (
Fig. 36.10
).
In
conclusion, available CVD risk calculators developed for the general population inaccurately estimate CVD risk in patients with
RA but currently represent the best utility for CVD risk evaluation in these patients.

### Managing CVD Risk in Patients With Rheumatic Disease

Patients with RA are significantly less likely to receive CVDpreventive measures compared with the general population.
A study showed that despite receiving similar treatment, patients
with RA who had an MI experienced worse long-term outcomes
than did patients with an MI who did not have RA.
This deficiency in CVD risk management is amply confirmed in an investigation of CV risk factor control in 836 patients with RA by
one study.
Among the most striking observations is that in the
644 patients without established CVD or DM, inadequate blood
pressure and lipid control were documented in 36% and 55% of
the participants, respectively. Findings from a Preventive CardioRheuma clinic revealed that 63% of the patients referred were in
need of CVD-preventive medical intervention.
Statins have established effectiveness in the primary prevention
of CV events in the general population and at-risk subpopulations
(e.g., patients with DM). These agents have both lipid modifying
and anti-inflammatory effects.

RA patients are still undertreated in terms of secondary CVD prevention.
An important CV risk factor in the general population is lipid
levels, although lipid levels may appear to be less important for
patients with inflammatory rheumatic diseases.
However,
meta-analyses of RCTs have shown that patients in the general
population benefit from lipid-lowering treatment regardless of
baseline lipid levels.
Clinical experience regarding use of CVDpreventive medications in IJDs is scarce. Results from a Preventive Cardio-Rheuma clinic for patients with IJDs revealed that
lipid targets were attained using fewer than three consultations

in 90% of the patients without serious adverse events.
These
results indicate that treating patients who have RA with statins
and achieving recommended lipid goals are safe. Prospective, longitudinal data are needed to demonstrate whether patients with
RA need to achieve the same low lipid targets as people without
RA or whether a relatively smaller or larger reduction in lipids will
result in the same CVD protection as in people without RA.
Modern RA therapy and the multiple comorbidities lead to
polypharmacy, thus increasing the possibility of drug to drug
interactions with CVD-preventive medications, such as statins.
Interaction between anti-rheumatic treatments, such as NSAIDs
and synthetic or biologic DMARDs, with CVD-preventive medications is also an area of uncertainty. Neither inflammation (by
CRP or ESR levels) nor anti-rheumatic medications, such as
NSAIDS, prednisolone, or synthetic or biologic DMARDs, influenced the dose of statin needed to obtain lipid recommended targets.
The influence of statins on clinical response and B cell
depletion after rituximab treatment in people with RA is inconclusive.
An increased CVD risk associated with NSAID
use is recognized in the general population,
but in a recent
Danish nationwide study, the CVD risk associated with use of
NSAIDs in people with RA was modest and significantly lower
than in people without RA.
Furthermore, NSAIDs interfere
with the thrombocyte inhibitory effect of aspirin, which complicates secondary CVD prevention.
Observational studies have
provided indications that use of synthetic and biologic DMARDs
is associated with reduced CVD risk in people with RA.
However, it is unknown whether this effect is related to the reduction in inflammation opposing the increased levels of atherogenic
lipids that occur with biologic DMARDs and improvement of
HDL function.
Studies to clarify these issues are needed.
CVD prevention in patients with RA is fraught with concerns
of drug- and disease-related complexities. Therapies of rheumatic
conditions have evolved enormously in the past two decades, as
have CVD prevention strategies. The available literature suggests
substantial underuse of CVD-preventive pharmacotherapy in
the general population.
More than half of all patients did
not reach their LDL treatment goals in general practice. These
findings are also supported by the Eurospire IV report, which
indicates that clinical implementation of secondary prevention
treatments remains suboptimal.
Similar undertreatment and
failure to obtain recommended lipid and blood pressure targets
have been shown in patients with RA.
Large international survey of risk factors (SURF) in various high CVD risk rheumatic
patients are underway, and the SURF-RA demonstrated considerable geographic differences in estimated CVD risk and preventive
treatment. Low goal attainment for lipid-lowering treatment was
observed, and only half the patients obtained their blood pressure
goals.
Despite a high focus on the increased CVD risk in RA
patients over the last decade, there is still substantial potential for
improvement in CVD-preventive measures.
Efforts to address the high CVD risk in patients with rheumatic disease should be a focus in the cardiology community and
among rheumatologists. It is hoped that such a focus will result
in more patients with RA being evaluated for CVD risk and the
institution of preventive measures when necessary.
There are several new medications for CVD prevention; the
most promising are alirocumab/evolocumab (proprotein convertase subtilisin/kexin type 9 [PCSK9] inhibitors), which are recommended for patients with primary hypercholesterolemia who
do not achieve lipid goals with statins.
Recently it has been
highlighted that since PCSK9 is lower in RA patients, it has been
hypothesized that there may be a greater expression of CD36 and
its pro-inflammatory ligands. Further studies are needed to elucidate this in relation to the use of PCSK9 inhibitors in IJD patients.
Caution should be taken if unexpected adverse effects are experienced in patients with RA treated with PCSK9 inhibitors.

### Patient Awareness of Cardiovascular Disease Risk

Communicating future risk and the need for preventive treatment
is one of the most challenging aspects of the patient/health professional interaction. Individuals with RA at highest risk for CVD
report low awareness and perceived risk of this comorbidity. This
represents a knowledge gap in need of intervention but must be
tailored to patients’ needs. An understanding of the system- and
individual-level barriers preventing CVD awareness is needed.
Only then will approaches to improve CVD screening and management in RA be successful. Furthermore, although programs for
assessing CVD risk in people with RA have been developed, it
will take a great effort to fully disseminate them. These strategies
include education of health care professionals, written material,
and online and group education of patients and their families.
Even starting with simple information and brochures as a precursor to implementing a full education program is a step forward. It
is of the utmost importance for patients with rheumatic diseases
to understand that effective CVD risk management comprises not
only adequate treatment of conventional CV risk factors but also
tight control of rheumatic disease activity.

## Conclusion

CVD remains a major problem for people with systemic inflammatory diseases. Systemic inflammation and its interplay with
traditional and nontraditional CV risk factors appear to have a
major role. Optimal control of traditional risk factors is imperative but insufficient to reduce CVD risk for people with rheumatic
diseases. Tight control of systemic inflammation will be required
for optimal results.
Future work should focus on further delineating the underlying biologic mechanisms involved, developing
and evaluating risk assessment tools and biomarkers, and above all
developing prevention and treatment strategies specific to rheumatic disease populations. On might learn from CVD-preventive
programs from other high-risk patient populations, such as those
with diabetes mellitus.
