# Behçet’s Disease

CHRISTINE S. AHN, LINDSAY C. STROWD,
AND JOSEPH L. JORIZZO

KEY POINTS

> Behçet’s disease is a chronic, multisystem disease characterized by oral

> and genital aphthae and other systemic features.

> The pathophysiology is multifactorial and includes environmental,

> infectious, immunologic, and genetic factors.

> The diagnosis is established using the International Criteria for Behçet’s

> Disease, which includes oral aphthae, genital aphthae, ocular

> lesions, and cutaneous lesions.

> Cutaneous lesions demonstrate a neutrophilic vascular reaction on

> histopathology.

> Treatment is based on the degree of systemic involvement and has

> expanded to include specific immune targeting agents, such as biologics.

> Prognosis is variable, but most patients experience a chronic relapsing

> and remitting course.

## Introduction

Behçet’s disease (BD) is a complex, multisystem auto-inflammatory disease. Originally described in 1937 by Turkish dermatologist Hulusi Behçet, BD was characterized as a triple-symptom
complex that included recurrent oral ulcers, genital ulcers, and
uveitis.
Later studies of this entity expanded understanding of
BD as a chronic relapsing, multisystem disease with vascular, musculoskeletal, gastrointestinal, neurologic, pulmonary, and cardiac
involvement.

## Epidemiology

BD is observed worldwide, with the highest prevalence along the
“Silk Route,” extending from Japan to the Middle Eastern and
Mediterranean countries.
The prevalence of BD is highest in
Turkey, with up to 420 per 100,000 inhabitants affected, followed
by Iran, Israel, China, and Korea.
The lowest prevalence of
BD is seen in the United Kingdom, Germany, Portugal, and the
United States, ranging from 0.12 to 6.4 per 100,000 people.
The mean age of onset is in the third to fourth decades of life;
BD can be observed in children and in adults above the age of 50,
although in these patients BD tends to follow relatively benign
clinical courses. Men and women are equally affected, although
some epidemiologic studies have shown a male predominance of
BD in Middle Eastern and Mediterranean countries and a female
predominance in Japan and Korea.
BD in men tends to follow a
more severe clinical course with greater disease-associated morbidity, particularly with vascular involvement.

## Pathogenesis

The current knowledge of BD suggests that the disorder is an
autoimmune process triggered by infectious or other environmental agents in genetically predisposed individuals.

### Genetics

The inheritance pattern of BD is not fully understood, although
familial aggregation studies suggest a strong genetic component.
Individuals with a first-degree relative with BD are at increased
risk of developing the disease, with 31% of individuals reporting positive family history.
In children with a parent with
BD, the clinical manifestations tend to present earlier in life.
This is known as
genetic anticipation,
due to progressive increases
in nucleotide repeats through consecutive generations.
The
first reported genetic association with BD was a human leukocyte antigen (HLA) in the Japanese population, HLA-B51.
Over-representation of HLA-B51, a split product from HLA-B5,
has been associated with development of BD in multiple studies. HLA-B51 presence is seen in only about 20% of individuals
with BD, indicating that other genetic factors play a role in the
development of BD.
In addition to HLA-B51, a wide range of
gene polymorphisms have been described in association with BD,
many of which encode proteins involved in immune regulation
and inflammation (

5.78, which has been observed in multiple studies across different

The

52.2% in Southern Europe, 31.7% in Northern Europe, 44.4%

### Immunologic Mechanisms

Activation of the immune system and enhanced expression of
pro-inflammatory cytokines play key roles in the pathogenesis
of BD. The major lymphocytes implicated in BD are T lymphocytes, with subpopulations of
γδ
T cells, cytotoxic T cells,
Th1 cells, regulatory T (Treg) cells, and Th17 cells acting as key
players.
In mucosal immunity,
γδ
T cells act as the first line
of defense. Originally,
γδ
T cells were thought to be the primary
T lymphocyte involved in the immunologic mechanisms of BD,
as increased activation of
γδ
T cells as well as accumulation of
γδ
T cells at sites of inflammation have been observed.
However, increased activation of NK T lymphocytes, Th1
lymphocytes, and, more recently, Th17 lymphocytes has been
implicated.
Increased numbers of activated NK cells and
increased production of Th1 cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, IL-12,
TNF, and interferon [IFN]-
γ
) in the peripheral blood of BD
patients, as well as within oral and genital BD ulcers and in
gastrointestinal lesions, have been observed. Current data have
linked Th17 cell activation and IL-17 production to BD disease
activity. Increased serum levels of IL-17, IL-23, and IFNγ
have
been observed in patients with BD. Higher serum levels of IL-17
occur in patients with active uveitis, oral ulcers, genital ulcers,
and articular symptoms.
Endothelial factors are also known to play a role in the pathogenesis of BD. Decreased levels of prostacyclin in the serum of
BD patients and increased concentrations of nitric oxide in the
serum, synovial fluid, and aqueous humor of individuals with
BD indicate endothelial dysfunction.
Endothelial cells, which
maintain the integrity of the vessel lumen, are one of the main
targets in BD, and their activation leads to vascular inflammation
and thrombosis.
Other immunologic factors thought to play a role in BD
include heat shock proteins (HSPs) and alterations in neutrophil
and macrophage activity.
HSPs may trigger both innate and
adaptive immune responses in patients with BD. HSPs transfer
antigenic peptides to antigen-presenting cells (APCs) that are
recognized as endogenous antigens, leading to activation of the
innate immune response and Th1 stimulation. HSPs may increase

expression of vascular endothelial growth factor by T cells, contributing to endothelial cell damage and subsequent vasculitis.
Neutrophils in patients with BD demonstrate increased phagocytosis, superoxide production, chemotaxis, and production of
lysosomal enzymes.
Abnormal neutrophil activation is stimulated by APCs, which stimulate the Th1 response, while Th17 cells
contribute to the upregulation of the neutrophil inflammatory
response.
The elevated concentrations of pro-inflammatory
cytokines such as IFNγ
, TNF, and IL-8 contribute to the primed
state of neutrophils in BD.
Increased neutrophil activity leads
to tissue injury in the form of the neutrophilic vascular reaction,
seen in lesions such as aphthae, pustular cutaneous lesions, and
erythema nodosum–like lesions. Accordingly, infiltrates of neutrophils are found histologically in cutaneous BD lesions and
within venous and arterial lesions. Downregulation of Treg cells
in patients with BD suppresses antigen-specific T cell responses,
potentially contributing to immune tolerance.

### Infection

Environmental exposures contribute to the development of BD.
Infections have been identified as possible environmental triggers
of BD, including
Streptococcus sanguis
and herpes simplex virus
(HSV) type 1.
Streptococcal infections have been implicated
based on observations of higher concentrations of
S. sanguis
in the
oral flora of patients with BD, higher incidence of infections such
as tonsillitis and dental caries, and the therapeutic benefit of antibiotics on mucocutaneous and arthritic symptoms.
Other bacteria including
Escherichia coli, Staphylococcus aureus, Mycoplasma
fermentans,
and
Helicobacter pylori
have also been identified as possible triggers via lymphocyte activation. In addition to bacteria,
an unbalanced microbiome or molecular mimicry triggers inflammation in BD. The innate immune system’s pro-inflammatory
response, via Toll-like receptors, neutrophils, NK cells, or
γδ
T
cells, represents a crucial step in BD pathogenesis.
HSV-1 has been detected in the saliva, intestinal ulcers, and
genital ulcers by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) in patients with
BD at higher rates than compared with healthy controls. Additionally, a BD-like mouse model was developed after inoculation
of mice with HSV, and it demonstrated HSV DNA sequences
within cutaneous and gastrointestinal lesions. There is no clear
understanding of the role of infectious agents as specific etiologic
factors, but some hypothesize that infection may trigger aberrant
immunologic activation and function through different mechanisms including molecular mimicry.

## Clinical Features

### Mucocutaneous Manifestations

BD was originally considered primarily a dermatologic condition,
with the classic triad of symptoms including recurrent oral ulcers,
genital ulcers, and uveitis. Oral aphthae (

are often the initial feature and are considered a major criterion
for BD. They most commonly occur on the mucosa of the lips,
buccal mucosa, tongue, and soft palate and develop in crops of
3 to 10 or more lesions, although individual lesions may occur.
Aphthae begin as erythematous papules, vesicles, or pustules,
which rapidly progress into shallow ulcers with rolled borders and
a gray to yellow pseudomembranous necrotic base with surrounding erythema. They can heal spontaneously without significant
scarring over a period of weeks but are associated with pain and

### Other Systemic Manifestations

Central nervous system (CNS) involvement, seen in 5% to 10%
of patients with BD, is more common than peripheral nervous
system involvement. The most common manifestations include
brainstem or corticospinal tract syndromes (also known as
neuroBehçet’s disease
), venous sinus thrombosis, increased intracranial
pressure, isolated behavioral symptoms, and isolated headache.
Parenchymal or brainstem involvement is associated with a worse
prognosis.
Gastrointestinal involvement in BD is characterized by
mucosal inflammation and ulcers that resemble orogenital
aphthae. They occur most commonly at the ileocecal region,
colon, and esophagus. Large aphthae can lead to perforation of the gastrointestinal mucosa. Presenting symptoms
include dysphagia, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and melena. It
is important to distinguish BD from inflammatory bowel disease, as the gastrointestinal and cutaneous manifestations of
BD overlap significantly with inflammatory bowel disease.
Hepatic vein thrombosis is a potential serious complication
of BD, which can lead to hepatic venous outflow obstruction and Budd–Chiari syndrome.
Cardiac involvement is
uncommon in BD, with few reports of myocardial infarction, pericarditis, endocarditis, valvular lesions, and coronary
vasculitis.

## Diagnosis

BD is a diagnosis of exclusion. The oral and genital ulcers of BD
can carry a wide differential, but the diagnosis should be considered if other causes of complex aphthosis have been excluded.
Complex aphthosis can be divided into primary idiopathic and
secondary aphthosis. Secondary complex aphthosis is seen in
conjunction with vitamin deficiencies, inflammatory bowel disease, Sweet’s syndrome, and PFAPA syndrome (periodic fever,
aphthous stomatitis, pharyngitis, and cervical adenitis) and from
medication or food exposures. Other mimickers of BD ulceration lesions include HSV infections, reactive arthritis, syphilis,
immunobullous diseases, and MAGIC syndrome (mouth and
genital ulcers with inflamed cartilage). Although not required
by diagnostic criteria, workup for BD should include the necessary serologic and imaging studies to exclude these other disease
entities.

### Diagnostic Criteria

Clinicians and investigators must rely on clinical criteria because
no pathognomonic laboratory findings exist. In 1990 the International Study Group (ISG) for BD created diagnostic criteria
to help guide clinicians. In 2006 the International Criteria for
Behçet Disease (ICBD) was revised to include vascular involvement. The most current version of the ISG diagnostic criteria
requires one major and two minor criteria in the absence of other
clinical explanations. The only major criterion is recurrent oral
ulceration greater than three times in one 12-month period, and
minor criteria include recurrent genital ulceration, eye lesions,
skin lesions, and a positive pathergy test (
Table 94.3
). The ICBD
included a scoring system with 2 points awarded to the presence of oral aphthosis, genital aphthosis, and ocular lesions,
and 1 point each for other skin lesions, neurologic symptoms,
and vascular manifestations. Although pathergy testing is not
required, if performed and positive, an additional point can be

nosis of BD. The sensitivity of this scoring system is reported as
94% and specificity as 92%. With a total score of 5 or greater,
ICBD criteria demonstrates a sensitivity of 97.5% and 99.6%
and has the highest diagnostic consistency with ISG criteria. The
performance of ICBD and ISG criteria have been established by
referring to expert or panel consensus diagnosis to serve as the
gold standard.
The O’Duffy–Goldstein criteria require the presence of oral
aphthae plus at least two of the following: genital aphthae, synovitis, posterior uveitis, cutaneous pustular vasculitis, and meningoencephalitis. Patients who have only two findings, with one being
recurrent oral aphthae, are considered to have an incomplete form
of BD. Additionally, the inclusion of acneiform lesions in the
ICBD criteria should be considered with caution, as acneiform
lesions are a common, nonspecific finding observed in both adolescents and adults. Histologic confirmation of vessel-based histology within acneiform lesions to exclude acne vulgaris or rosacea
is important. In addition to the ICBD criteria, application of the
O’Duffy–Goldstein criteria to exclude patients with inflammatory
bowel disease and enteropathic arthritis is recommended.

### Histopathology

Histopathologic evaluation of oral and genital ulcers demonstrate
findings similar to those seen in complex aphthosis, which is
characterized by ulceration and granulation tissue with a mixed
inflammatory infiltrate composed of lymphocytes, histiocytes,
neutrophils, and occasionally thrombi within vessels. On direct
immunofluorescence, deposition of IgM, IgG, C3, and fibrin can
be seen, consistent with an immune complex vasculitis.
HSV,
acid-fast bacteria, and fungal stains should be negative.
Papulopustular lesions of BD demonstrate a diffuse dermal
infiltrate of neutrophils, lymphocytes, and histiocytes, which can
be accompanied by findings of cutaneous small vessel vasculitis.
Small vessel vasculitis features neutrophilic infiltrates, karyorrhexis, and extravasation of erythrocytes surrounding dermal
capillary walls, with or without fibrinoid necrosis. Significant
neutrophil infiltration is seen in almost all early lesions, as well
as in the skin pathergy reaction.
Epidermal ulceration or pustule
formation is variable, depending on the stage of the lesion.
In biopsy specimens of synovial membranes, there is a neutrophilic reaction with occasional plasma cells and lymphocytes.
Leukocyte counts within synovial fluid ranges from 300 to 36,200
cells/mm
, with a predominance of neutrophils and normal glucose levels.

## Treatment

Initial evaluation and management of patients with BD require a
multidisciplinary approach. In addition to dermatologic management, other specialties must be involved in the management of
potential ophthalmologic, neurologic, gastrointestinal, urogenital,
and pulmonary involvement. Treatment of BD depends on the
organ system(s) involved and severity of involvement, frequency
of recurrences, disease duration, age at disease onset, and sex. Due
to the chronic course of BD, the aim of treatment is to reduce
or prevent recurrences, prevent irreversible organ damage, and
improve quality of life. Immunomodulatory and immunosuppressive agents are the mainstays of treatment, although there is a
general lack of large, randomized controlled clinical trials to guide
the treatment of BD.

### Mucocutaneous Disease

For the treatment of localized or mild mucocutaneous disease,
topical corticosteroids and topical immunomodulators are used
most frequently. Topical corticosteroids have demonstrated efficacy in decreasing pain and duration of oral and genital ulcers.
Pimecrolimus, a topical calcineurin inhibitor, has been studied

alone and in combination with oral colchicine for the treatment
of genital ulcers. In one study, topical pimecrolimus was effective in reducing the duration of ulcers, while in another study,
pimecrolimus decreased the severity of pain, but the recovery
period of lesions was not significantly reduced.
Alternative
topical agents can be helpful in conjunction with topical corticosteroids and immunomodulators. Anti-inflammatory washes
such as benzydamine hydrochloride and topical prostaglandin
E
gel (0.3 mg) twice daily can help decrease pain, and topical
prostaglandin E
helps prevent formation of new aphthae in one
study.
Anti-microbial and antiseptic agents can help reduce
anti-microbial load, with some agents providing additional benefit through anti-inflammatory effects. Listerine
mouth rinse,

The addition of oral colchicine, dosed 0.6 to 2.4 mg daily, can
decrease the size and frequency of oral and genital aphthae.
Dapsone (50 to 150 mg/day) is an alternative treatment for oral
and genital ulcers, either alone or in combination with colchicine.
Apremilast, an oral phosphodiesterase-4 inhibitor
used for the treatment of psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis, has
been studied in a randomized, placebo-controlled trial in patients
with BD with active mucocutaneous involvement and no major
organ involvement. In this study, there was a significant reduction in the number of oral ulcers and associated pain at 12 weeks
of treatment. Compared with the placebo group, more patients
treated with apremilast had a complete response with no ulcers
or a partial response with a minimum of 50% reduction in the
number of ulcers. Overall disease activity and quality of life also
improved among patients treated with apremilast. The most common adverse effect associated with apremilast was gastrointestinal symptoms, similar to what has been demonstrated in trials
for psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis.
In 2019, apremilast 30 mg
twice daily was approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of BD.
In patients with severe mucocutaneous disease who have failed
the localized or conservative treatments outlined above, other
immunosuppressive and/or immunomodulatory agents are used.
Systemic corticosteroids are often administered for acute attacks to
suppress inflammation. However, corticosteroids are not effective
in preventing relapsing, and they are not sustainable as long-term
therapy due to the adverse effects associated with long-term use.
Thus they are typically combined with a corticosteroid-sparing
immunosuppressive agent such as azathioprine (2.5 mg/kg/day),
cyclosporine (3 to 5 mg/kg/day), mycophenolate mofetil (1 to 2
g/day), thalidomide (100 mg/day), or methotrexate. Other agents
that can be used include pentoxifylline (400 mg three times daily)
and low-dose isotretinoin (20 mg/day).
Due to the expanded knowledge of the immunologic mechanisms of BD, biologic therapies have become increasingly utilized
for the treatment of severe or recalcitrant mucocutaneous disease.
Anti-TNF therapies infliximab and adalimumab have been variably effective for the treatment of mucocutaneous lesions of BD.
Etanercept (25 mg/day twice weekly) is the only TNF agent that
has demonstrated efficacy in suppressing cutaneous lesions of
BD in a randomized clinical trial.
Anti–IL-1 agents that have
been effective in some reports include anakinra (anti–IL-1R) and
canakinumab (anti–IL-1
β
), although in one study, oral and genital
ulcers were the only component of BD that remained refractory
to treatment with anakinra. Secukinumab, an IL-17 antagonist,
was studied in a small preliminary study for the treatment of
refractory mucocutaneous and articular BD. In this study, one
patient was treated with 300 mg/month, and all other patients
were treated with 150 mg/month. The patient treated with 300
mg/month reached a complete response within 3 months. The
results in patients receiving 150 mg/month were variable, with
one patient reaching complete response, one patient achieving
response and then relapsing, and two patients not achieving complete response. When patients were switched to 300 mg/month
dosing, all reached complete response within 3 months. These
findings were in contrast to prior studies that had demonstrated
limited response.

### Systemic Disease

Patients with systemic disease require immunosuppressive therapy, particularly to prevent morbidity and mortality resulting
from untreated disease. Systemic corticosteroids are often used as
first-line measures but typically in combination with an additional
immunosuppressive agent such as azathioprine, IFNα
, cyclosporine, cyclophosphamide, or chlorambucil.
For ocular manifestations of BD, azathioprine has been considered the first-line corticosteroid-sparing therapy. Recent evidence
supports the use of anti-TNF agents infliximab or adalimumab
as first- or second-line corticosteroid-sparing therapy for patients
with ophthalmologic disease. In two large noncontrolled trials
studying infliximab for recalcitrant uveitis in BD, patients experienced significant improvement in visual acuity after initiating
treatment with infliximab.
In patients with vascular involvement, immunosuppressive
therapy is the mainstay of therapy, and aggressive treatment is recommended due to the morbidity and mortality associated with
uncontrolled vascular disease in BD. Neurologic manifestations of
BD are typically treated with a combination of corticosteroids and
other immunosuppressive agents such as azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, methotrexate, and cyclophosphamide. However,
when first-line agents are ineffective or poorly tolerated, anti-TNF
agents or IFNα
may be used as alternative treatments. There are
also reports of other biologic agents such as tocilizumab, anakinra,
and canakinumab effectively improving symptoms of neuroBD.
For gastrointestinal involvement, anti-TNF agents
appear to be most helpful, with adalimumab demonstrating good
results.

## Prognosis

BD has a variable course and is characterized by relapses and
remissions. Diagnosis can be difficult, and there is often a delay in
diagnosis after the initial presentation of mucocutaneous lesions.
Major causes of morbidity and disability are ocular involvement
and neurologic disease. Overall, the prognosis has improved over
recent decades with the use of more aggressive therapeutic measures. The mortality rate varies based on study cohorts. Higher
mortality rates have been reported in Turkey (up to 9.8%), related
to sudden death by vascular complications or major vessel disease
(44%) and CNS involvement (12%).
With a better understanding of the pathophysiology of the disease, newer immunomodulatory agents have been tried in the treatment of BD. There

is conflicting evidence of the efficacy of these agents, and most
studies are limited by small patient numbers and low population incidence. Larger controlled clinical trials will help dictate
treatment algorithms and potentially improve access to newer
therapeutics.
