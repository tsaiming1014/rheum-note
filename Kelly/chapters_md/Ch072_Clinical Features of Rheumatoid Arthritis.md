## Epidemiology and Disease Burden

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic, systemic autoimmune
condition with protean manifestations. The primary expression of
disease occurs in the synovial tissues and is characterized clinically
by symmetric polyarticular inflammation, which can lead to progressive joint damage. As a result, RA can be associated with substantial functional disability, morbidity, and accelerated mortality,
which pose a substantial societal burden.

### Disease Frequency

The prevalence of RA has been estimated at 0.5% to 1% in most
developed countries, although epidemiologic estimates vary
widely depending on geographic region and methodological
approach. The Global Burden of Disease 2017 study conducted a
rigorous systematic review and metaregression, estimating a global
age-standardized prevalence of 0.25%. North America, Western
Europe, and the Caribbean (0.3% to 0.4%) exhibited the greatest
regional prevalence of RA in contrast to Southeast Asia, Oceania, and Western Sub-Saharan Africa (0.1%).
The highest rates of
RA incidence are observed among individuals of Native American
ancestry, with a reported disease prevalence approaching 5% to
7% in Pima and Chippewa Indians in the United States, respectively.
There is a much lower prevalence in select African regions,
thought to be the result of a lower population frequency of the
human leukocyte antigen
(HLA)-DRB1
shared epitope–containing allele,
which is the single strongest known genetic risk factor for RA. A 40-year population-based study from Rochester,
Minnesota (1955 to 1994), demonstrated an overall age- and
sex-adjusted annual incidence of 44.6 cases per 100,000 personyears.
In this study, incidence rates were approximately twofold
higher in women than men, with peaks in disease onset observed
at younger ages in women (55 to 64 years) compared with men
(75 to 84 years). Notably, these authors reported marked temporal
trends, including a 47% decline in overall RA incidence during
the study period. A more recent cohort (1985 to 2014) from the
same population suggested that the incidence of RA has been stable, but seropositive RA incidence rates have modestly decreased,
while seronegative RA incidence rates have increased. These results
underscore the potential impact of environmental exposures (e.g.,
cigarette smoking) in mediating risk, and the potential relevance
of birth cohort effects.
Based on this population, the lifetime risk
for RA among U.S. adults has been estimated at 3.6% for women
and 1.7% for men.
Globally, the incidence of RA has increased
by 8.2% with a 7.4% increase in prevalence from 1990 to 2017.

In many patients, arthralgias herald the end of pre-clinical disease and often precede the onset of synovitis detected by physical examination. Advanced imaging modalities, namely MRI
and musculoskeletal ultrasound (MSUS), have shown promise in

detecting subclinical inflammation that predicts the transition to
clinical RA. In a study of 136 ACPA-positive patients, of whom
42% subsequently developed RA over a median follow-up of 18
months, more than 95% of patients had gray-scale synovitis in
at least one joint on MSUS, but only 33% had power Doppler
findings present.
Power Doppler was particularly informative of
progression to inflammatory arthritis of the same joint, with a
10-fold higher risk if any power Doppler signal was detected. Similarly, MRI features of subclinical inflammation (synovitis, bone
marrow edema, tenosynovitis, and intermetatarsal bursitis) of
the metacarpophalangeal (MCP), wrist, and metatarsophalangeal
(MTP) joints are frequently detected in patients with clinically
suspect arthralgia and predict the development of inflammatory
arthritis, independent of other clinical risk factors (such as age,
joint involvement, CRP, and ACPA positivity).
Notably, a
prospective study of 149 patients with clinically suspect arthralgia demonstrated that the predictive ability of MRI inflammation
is similar in magnitude to that of ACPA positivity (hazard ratio
[HR] 5.07 for MRI inflammation and 6.43 for ACPA positivity).
Much less is known about the transition to clinical RA in
seronegative patients, in part because of typical definitions for preRA that require autoantibody positivity. During this transition to
clinical RA, seronegative individuals appear to have less frequent
lower extremity involvement, shorter time from symptom onset
to presentation with arthralgias, more tender joints, and a longer
time to development of arthritis than seropositive individuals.

## Clinical Presentations of Early Rheumatoid Arthritis

The early presentation of RA includes systemic and articular signs
and symptoms. Extra-articular features are more commonly seen
in later disease, although occasionally they may precede articular
manifestations. Systemic findings include fatigue and, less commonly, weight loss or low-grade fever. Articular symptoms most
often begin with stiffness, pain, and swelling involving the small
joints of the hands and feet in a symmetric distribution, including
the MCP, proximal interphalangeal (PIP), and MTP joints. Understanding that RA-related synovitis can involve almost any synovial
joint in the body, the condition characteristically spares the distal
interphalangeal (DIP) joints (affecting <10% of cases)
and also
spares the thoracic and lumbosacral spine. Joint activity in RA rarely
remits completely in an involved joint when developing in another.
This quality of arthritis distinguishes RA from conditions such
as rheumatic fever, serum sickness, or palindromic rheumatism,
which can be characterized by a true migratory arthritis pattern.
With increasing efforts to diagnose and treat RA earlier, rheumatology consultations for inflammatory arthritis are occurring at earlier stages of disease, often before fulfilling classic disease criteria.
Among patients with an early undifferentiated inflammatory arthritis, 30% to 40% will experience a spontaneous remission, whereas
classifiable RA will eventually develop in approximately one in four.
In addition to ACPA positivity, other features predictive of later RA
classification in patients with early undifferentiated inflammatory
arthritis include polyarthritis, symmetric joint involvement, and the
presence of radiographic erosions.

### Patterns of Onset

RA onset typically follows one of several different temporal patterns, ranging from an acute or “explosive” polyarticular disease
onset to a far more insidious inflammatory arthritis that can be
oligoarticular and additive during the initial course.
Insidious Onset
Estimated to affect 55% to 65% of patients,
an insidious disease onset represents the most common early presentation of RA.
The small joints of the hands, wrists, and feet are most commonly
affected.
The joint pattern is often symmetric and is accompanied by morning stiffness, a cardinal sign of inflammatory arthritis
that may last as long as several hours. Less commonly, onset is
characterized by a slowly evolving monoarticular or oligoarticular presentation, with a predilection for larger joints, such as the
elbows, knees, or hips. This presentation mimics a seronegative
spondyloarthropathy, but, with time, most patients experience
symptoms in additional joint areas, including the contralateral
joint or other small joints, resulting in a pattern of involvement
more typical of RA. In older patients (e.g., >65 years), an insidious onset can be dominated by systemic features, including fever,
weight loss, fatigue, marked acute phase responses, and occasional
proximal girdle involvement, which together parallel the signs
and symptoms of polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) or remitting
seronegative symmetric synovitis with pitting edema (RS3PE).
This type of presentation should also prompt consideration of an
occult malignancy.

Acute or Intermediate Onset
Estimated to affect between 8% to 15% of all cases, RA less frequently manifests as an explosive polyarticular arthritis that can
be intensely inflammatory and may involve both small and large
joints at presentation. With acute onset, the pain may be so limiting that the initial evaluation occurs in an urgent ambulatory
setting. This dramatic, but uncommon, disease onset appears to
have a predilection for the elderly and has been described as one
of the characteristic presentations in “elderly onset” or “lateronset” RA. An intermediate disease onset, in which symptoms
develop over days to weeks, affects approximately one in five
incident cases.
Atypical Onset
In a minority of patients with RA, the initial presentation involves
a simple bursitis or tendonitis that may evolve into tenosynovitis.
Such patients may also be seen with early carpal tunnel syndrome
from wrist involvement and subsequent median nerve compression. As with insidious presentations, the disease in individuals
with an atypical presentation often evolves over time to the more
typical symmetric small joint findings of RA.
Other Patterns of Onset
Palindromic Rheumatism.
Palindromic rheumatism is an
uncommon form of disease onset characterized by relatively
brief but intense episodes of joint inflammation. Often, by the
time patients are seen by a rheumatologist, they may already be
asymptomatic because of the self-limited nature of palindromic
flares, which can last from a few hours to a few days.
Joint
involvement with palindromic onset is often monoarticular,
with maximal inflammation achieved within hours. Affecting
almost any joint, this onset most commonly affects the patient’s
knees, fingers, or shoulders. Because of the highly inflammatory and often monoarticular presentation, palindromic RA can
closely mimic crystalline arthropathy or septic arthritis. Serologic testing, including RF and/or ACPA, is often helpful in differentiating palindromic rheumatism from other inflammatory

arthritides.
If followed over time, one-third to one-half of
patients with initial palindromic rheumatism evolve to develop
a daily pattern of joint involvement more typical of RA.
This
disease evolution appears to be more common among those with
hand involvement and ACPA positivity.
The disease in the
remainder of patients tends to continue to be palindromic or
to evolve into a definable crystal-related arthropathy (suggesting
that early misclassification is common).
Arthritis Robustus.
As with palindromic onset, arthritis robustus is a relatively uncommon RA subtype that was first described
in 1973.
The typical patient profile is an older man, with an
occupational history notable for manual labor. Patients seen with
arthritis robustus often have a markedly inflammatory arthritis, usually involving the hands and feet. Nevertheless, affected
patients often have few if any symptoms, giving the impression in
some cases of a patient who is “blissfully unaware” of the condition.
The challenge in the care of these patients is they often do
not communicate complaints, rendering the physical examination
and laboratory assessments key components in physician management decisions.
Extra-articular Disease as Initial Presentation.
Several reports
have shown that extra-articular disease manifestations rarely represent the initial disease manifestation in RA. This relatively uncommon phenomenon has perhaps been most widely observed with
the appearance of rheumatoid nodules preceding demonstrable
synovitis.
“Rheumatoid nodulosis” has been classically described
by the combination of subcutaneous rheumatoid nodules, subchondral bone cysts, and recurrent joint symptoms, an entity with
a favorable prognosis most often seen in the context of RF seropositivity.
Other forms of extra-articular disease have also been
observed to precede the development of synovitis in patients with
RA, most notably lung disease. Retrospective studies of RA–associated interstitial lung disease (RA-ILD) cohorts have evaluated
the onset of articular versus lung disease manifestations, finding
the lung disease to predate articular disease in 10% to 20% of RAILD patients.

### Diagnosis

The characteristic patient with RA reports pain and swelling of
the small joints of the hands, wrists, and feet, with prolonged
morning stiffness, often lasting more than an hour. This typical
articular presentation, the presence of any extra-articular manifestations, and laboratory and radiographic assessments are critical in
differentiating RA from other inflammation-driven diseases. This
is in comparison with osteoarthritis (OA), which is a far more
prevalent “low-grade” inflammatory articular disease (
Table 72.1
).
Early in the disease course, patients with RA may experience difficulty using their hands with routine activity and pain in the forefeet with weight bearing or walking. Patients commonly report a
“gelling” phenomenon following periods of rest, which improves
with activity. Systemic symptoms such as fatigue and low-grade
fever may also be present, but polyarthritis accompanied by a
high fever (e.g., >38.5° C) strongly suggests an alternative etiology. The joint examination often reveals swelling (or synovitis),
tenderness, increased warmth, and limited range of motion. Other
findings may include palmar erythema and subcutaneous nodules
on extensor surfaces.
Adding to the complexity of early diagnosis, other rheumatic
diseases included in the differential diagnosis of RA may share
many of these findings. Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), primary Sjögren’s syndrome, and viral diseases, such as hepatitis B,

## Course and Complications of Established Rheumatoid Arthritis

### Articular Manifestations

With its primary manifestation involving synovial tissues, the
signs and symptoms of RA are primarily driven by active synovitis
that results in joint swelling, stiffness, palpable warmth, and pain.
The classic joint distribution in early disease includes the small
joints of the hands and feet (MCP, PIP, and MTPs) (
Fig. 72.3A
).
With time, intermediate (wrists, elbows, and ankles) and large
(hips, shoulders, and cervical spine) joints may become involved.
With more advanced and longer duration of disease, RA may also
involve atypical joints, including the temporomandibular joint
(TMJ), cricoarytenoid, and sternoclavicular joint. In contrast to
OA, RA rarely affects the DIP joints and seldom targets the thoracic or lumbosacral spine.

Feet and Ankles
As with the hand and wrist, the foot and ankle may also be considered as a single functional unit. For example, hindfoot abnormalities that involve the ankle and/or subtalar joint directly
affect forefoot pressures with loading and weight bearing. Thus
optimal correction of an existing forefoot deformity is reliant
upon simultaneous correction of any residual hindfoot defect.
Ankle and foot involvement is common in RA, with forefoot
involvement observed in as many as 90% of patients at some
point in the disease process and serving as the initial disease
manifestation in approximately 15% of patients. In addition to
MTP synovitis, juxta-articular inflammation including intermetatarsal bursitis, submetatarsal bursitis, and tenosynovitis is
increasingly recognized as a common manifestation in patients
with early RA.

deformities of the PIP joints. Cystic collections that represent outpouchings of flexor tendon sheaths often develop under the MTP
joints.

Cervical Spine
Reported in as many as 80% of patients with RA, although estimates vary widely, cervical spine involvement appears to be most
common in those with long-standing and poorly controlled disease. Reviewed by Joaquim and Appenzeller,
risk factors for
cervical spine involvement in RA include erosive disease, glucocorticoid use, treatment failure, and early age at disease onset.
Importantly, the development of cervical spine involvement and
its complications may be abrogated by intensive treatment. One
study showed that patients treated aggressively with combination
disease-modifying therapy were at lower risk for cervical spine disease than patients treated with less aggressive monotherapy.
Of the cervical spine findings observed in RA, the most serious
clinical presentations relate to atlantoaxial (C1–C2) subluxation,
cranial settling, and subaxial subluxation. Of these, atlantoaxial
subluxation is the most common radiologic abnormality of the
cervical spine observed
and may include:
• The atlas (C1) moving anteriorly on the axis (C2) (prevalence
27%)
• The atlas moving posteriorly, laterally, or rotary on the axis
(prevalence 0.1% to 0.7%)
• Subluxation of the atlas vertically in relation to the axis (prevalence 11%)
The earliest and most common symptom of atlantoaxial subluxation is pain radiating into the occiput. Two other serious but less
common clinical patterns include (1) slowly progressive spastic
quadriparesis with painless sensory loss in the hands and (2) transient episodes of medullary dysfunction associated with vertical
penetration of the dens and probable vertebral artery compression. In the latter case, paresthesias may occur in the shoulders
or arms during movement of the head. With vertical C1–C2
subluxation, patients may complain of facial pain with decreased
sensation in the V1 and V2 distributions of the fifth cranial nerve.
A recent meta-analysis suggests that the incidence of anterior
atlantoaxial subluxations is decreasing over time, but rates of vertical subluxation, subaxial subluxation, and cervical myelopathy
remain stable.
Neurologic symptoms resulting from spinal cord involvement
often have little relationship to the degree of C1–C2 subluxation
present. When present, symptoms range from simple paresthesias to altered consciousness, loss of sphincter tone, and other
well-defined signs of cord compression. Signs of cord compression portend a poor prognosis, often heralding the rapid progression of myelopathy with an approximate 50% 1-year mortality
rate.
Risks are further magnified in the context of patient falls,
whiplash injury, or the need for general anesthesia with intubation requiring cervical manipulation. The incidence of sustained
neurologic deterioration following surgical correction of C1–C2
disease may be as high as 6%, suggesting that a conservative management strategy may be optimal for a substantial proportion of
asymptomatic patients.
As with neurologic symptoms, physical examination findings
are nonspecific and unreliable in detecting C1–C2 subluxation.
Physical findings may include the loss of cervical lordosis, resistance to passive spine motion, or the abnormal protrusion of the
axial arch felt with the examining finger on the posterior pharyngeal wall. In symptomatic patients, cervical spine films in flexion
should be taken only after radiographs have been taken to rule
out odontoid fracture or high-grade atlantoaxial subluxation.
Advanced imaging modalities, including CT and especially MRI,
have proven to be particularly useful in defining pathology affecting this region in RA.
Although not lined by synovium, discovertebral joints of the
cervical spine can be involved, leading to osteochondral tissue
destruction and disk space narrowing that is visible on lateral
radiographs. Patients with cervical spine involvement may report
significant neck pain, although passive range of motion may be
preserved in the absence of significant muscle spasm. At least two
possible mechanisms have been proposed to explain the cervical
spine involvement characteristic of RA: (1) extension of inflammation from adjacent synovial-lined neurocentral joints (the
joints of Luschka) into the discovertebral area and (2) chronic cervical instability initiated by apophyseal joint destruction, leading
to vertebral misalignment or frank subluxation.
Intermediate and Large Peripheral Joints
Intermediate and large joint involvement is more indicative
of long-standing and often poorly controlled disease. Elbow
involvement is reported in approximately one-third to one-half
of patients. The earliest clinical manifestation is extension loss,
a finding that frequently goes unnoticed by the patient. Bilateral
olecranon bursal swelling is observed in RA and may be encountered in gout or pseudogout, although typically unilaterally.
Shoulder involvement affects not only the glenohumeral joint
but also the subacromial (subdeltoid) bursa, the distal third of the
clavicle (related to adjacent inflammatory bursitis), and rotator
cuff tendons (
Fig. 72.9A
). In contrast to other joints, shoulder
involvement is often quite painful, with symptoms referred to the
deltoid region. Patients commonly complain of significantly disturbed sleep with painful awakenings in side sleeping positions.
Rotator cuff dysfunction is common in RA and may relate to the
proximity of proliferative synovium to the rotator cuff insertion
on the greater tuberosity. Resulting in substantial morbidity, rotator cuff tears have been demonstrated to affect approximately 20%
of patients with RA, with another one-fourth having evidence of
frayed tendons.
Because the rotator cuff acts as a downward stabilizer of the humerus in the glenoid, rotator cuff dysfunction can
lead to superior migration of the humeral head from unopposed
forces of the deltoid and resultant secondary degenerative arthritis
of the glenohumeral joint.
Hip and knee involvement also results in substantial disability. In contrast to the hip, knee involvement in RA is usually
readily identified on examination, with joint effusions that can
be large and associated with increases in intra-articular pressures.

of knee involvement. Increased intra-articular pressure, particularly those accompanying flexion, can lead to an outpouching
of the posterior components of the joint, resulting in a popliteal or Baker’s cyst. In extreme circumstances, a popliteal cyst

either the lateral or medial malleolus. MRI and ultrasound are

### Extra-articular Manifestations

Recognizing that RA is a systemic inflammatory disease is requisite for optimal management. Inflammation in RA involves
not only articular structures but also potentially extra-articular
tissues, leading to the development of subcutaneous nodules
and other mucocutaneous findings, as well as serositis and cardiopulmonary, neurologic, ocular, and hematologic complications. Extra-articular manifestations are observed in up to 50%
of patients with RA and generally portend a poor prognosis,
including increased morbidity and mortality.
In a singlecenter series, mortality risk in patients with RA with extra-articular manifestations was more than twice that of patients without
extra-articular disease (relative risk [RR], 2.49; 95% confidence
interval [CI], 1.43 to 4.03), and cardiovascular disease (CVD)
accounted for 70% of all deaths in this group.
Fortunately,
clinically important extra-articular complications are decreasing,
likely as a consequence of better treatment practices. Nevertheless, some of our treatments may further complicate assessments
for extra-articular complications in RA, because disease-remitting therapies can lead to adverse effects that closely mimic
extra-articular disease manifestations. For example, glucocorticoids exacerbate both osteoporosis and CVD risk, conditions
already over-represented in patients with RA. The focus of this
section will be on the “disease-specific” extra-articular manifestations of RA and not complications related to therapy, which are
covered elsewhere in this textbook.

men.

The presence of livedo reticularis, with its characteristic lacey
appearance (most prominent on the lower extremities), should
raise a concern for a medium-vessel vasculitis or an associated connective tissue disease, such as SLE or antiphospholipid antibody
syndrome (APS).
The prevalence of secondary Sjögren’s syndrome (autoimmune
exocrinopathy) in RA has been estimated at 17%,
resulting in
symptoms of keratoconjunctivitis sicca (dry eyes) or xerostomia
(dry mouth). These symptoms are accompanied by objective evidence of lacrimal or salivary gland dysfunction.

Ocular
In addition to keratoconjunctivitis sicca, episcleritis and scleritis
complicate the disease course in less than 1% of patients with RA.
Episcleritis typically presents as a painless red eye without vision
loss or frank discharge, the latter more indicative of conjunctivitis.
Inspection reveals engorged blood vessels that stop at the limbic
border. Scleritis also results in scleral injection without discharge,
but, in contrast to episcleritis, this is typically accompanied by
boring pain that can be precipitated by palpation of the globe
and areas of dusky discoloration. Manifesting with variable levels
of involvement, scleritis can be localized and superficial or more
generalized and can extend into deeper tissue layers. In severe
cases, deeper involvement can be characterized by granulomatous
scleral resorption leading to scleromalacia perforans (
Fig. 72.11
).
Although episcleritis can usually be managed with topical antiinflammatory agents, scleritis requires prompt evaluation by an
ophthalmologist, and treatment routinely requires systemic or
intraocular immunomodulating therapy. In some cases, the sclera
can become thinned, even in the absence of overt inflammation,
and lead to scleromalacia.

is caused by the deposition of cryoproteins (RF–IgG complexes),
which can lead to perforation of the anterior chamber. Iritis and
uveitis can occur in RA but are more commonly seen with other
rheumatic conditions (e.g., spondyloarthritis, sarcoidosis, Behçet’s

Bone
In addition to periarticular bone loss and local erosions, RA is a
well-recognized cause of systemic bone loss, placing patients at
a substantially higher risk for the development of osteoporosis
and fracture. This risk is further heightened in a dose-dependent
fashion in the context of glucocorticoid therapy. In a prospective
series of 395 patients with early RA, more than one-third experienced a fracture over just 5 years of follow-up.
In addition to
glucocorticoid use, risk factors for bone loss and fracture in RA
included older age, disability, longer disease duration, decreased
grip strength, low body mass, and increased inflammation. Systemic inflammation in RA results in the increased expression of
growth factors, including macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) and receptor for activation of nuclear factorκ
B
ligand (RANKL), and pro-inflammatory cytokines that promote
osteoclast differentiation and ultimately bone resorption.
More
recently, antigen-specific ACPAs have been implicated as direct
promoters of the osteoclastogenesis process and bone loss that
characterizes, and occasionally precedes, RA.
These findings
are bolstered by animal work showing that immune responses to
citrullinated self-antigen may serve as a trigger for declines in bone
mineral density and bone quality.

Muscle
Weakness is a common patient complaint in RA. Although this
may be related to articular complaints such as joint pain and/
or instability, true muscle pathology, including sarcopenia, can
also occur in RA as a result of deconditioning, inflammatory
processes, or type II muscle atrophy complicating glucocorticoid
use. Most typically with long-standing, active disease, loss of
lean body mass, primarily muscle, signifies
rheumatoid cachexia,
which is strongly associated with functional disability and mortality risk (reflecting the appropriate naming by Paget nearly 150
years ago; the direct translation is “bad condition”).
There
have also been reports of inflammatory myopathy accompanying RA presenting with muscle tenderness and increased muscle
enzymes. Muscle biopsies demonstrate focal accumulations of
lymphocytes and plasma cells in a condition referred to as
nodular myositis.
Necrotizing myositis has also been reported as a rare
complication of RA.
Hematologic
Hematologic abnormalities are common in patients with autoimmune disease, and RA is no exception. Patients with RA frequently
manifest a mild, normocytic normochromic anemia that tends to
correlate with increased disease activity and elevated inflammatory
markers. Patients with chronic inflammation have an increased
synthesis of ferritin and hemosiderin, abnormal retention of iron
from senescent red blood cells, and increased lactoferrin, ultimately
contributing to the binding and lowering of serum iron. Although
mild anemia is common, the presence of more severe anemia (i.e.,
hemoglobin <10 g/dL) suggests an alternative etiology. Mixed anemia poses a frequent diagnostic challenge in RA. For instance, a
patient might have concomitant microcytic anemia (iron deficiency
or, less commonly, anemia of chronic disease) and macrocytic anemia (vitamin B
or folate deficiency, methotrexate use), a clinical scenario that is characterized by a heterogeneous red blood cell
population (reflected in an increased red cell distribution width).
In addition to its utility in evaluating causes of anemia, ferritin is
an acute phase reactant and is therefore affected by inflammation.
In general, a serum ferritin of less than 50 ng/mL is indicative of
iron deficiency, whereas values exceeding 100 ng/mL are indicative
of adequate iron stores, regardless of RA disease activity.
Thrombocytosis is frequent with active RA, with platelet counts
serving as an indirect measure of systemic inflammation. Platelet
counts are positively correlated with both clinical and laboratory
parameters of disease activity in RA, including ESR, CRP, and
several acute phase proteins.
Thrombocytopenia, on the other
hand, is rare in RA in the absence of treatment-related bone marrow suppression or Felty’s syndrome. As with other rheumatic and
autoimmune conditions, eosinophilia may accompany active RA.
Associated with the presence of the
HLA-DRB1*0401
allele,
Felty’s syndrome comprises an uncommon but severe disease subset, defined by the triad of RA, splenomegaly (present in >90% of
cases), and leukopenia. The syndrome classically occurs in patients
with long-standing disease, characterized by RF and ANA seropositivity and the presence of rheumatoid nodules and articular
deformities, often in the context of minimal synovitis. In addition
to rheumatoid nodules, these patients often have other extra-articular manifestations and are at an increased risk for bacterial infections and chronic nonhealing ulcers, leading to skin infections.
The risk of bacterial infection is greatest in patients with more
pronounced neutropenia (e.g., absolute neutrophil count [ANC]
<100/mm
). Low serum complements may also be seen in Felty’s
syndrome, with bone marrow biopsy typically yielding either normal or hyperplastic cell lines. In addition to associations with the
HLA-DRB1*0401
allele, Felty’s syndrome is characterized by the
presence of autoantibodies directed against citrullinated histones
that bind to activated neutrophils and neutrophil extracellular
traps (NETs).
Large granular lymphocyte (LGL) syndrome may also complicate the course of RA and may be related to Felty’s syndrome. In
addition to marked neutropenia, patients typically demonstrate
expansion of LGL cells in both the blood and bone marrow, but
splenomegaly is less common than in Felty’s.
LGL cells represent
in vivo activated cytotoxic T cells (CTC) or natural killer (NK)
cells, and they proportionally represent 85% (CTC) and 15%
(NK) of LGL syndromes, respectively.
LGL affects as many
as 1% of patients with RA but is also seen in other inflammatory
diseases (SLE, Sjögren’s syndrome), viral infections, and hematologic malignancies (myelodysplastic syndrome). As opposed to
aggressive and fatal subsets of LGL leukemia, the lymphocytosis
associated with RA tends to follow an indolent course. In addition
to rare malignant transformation (characterized by monoclonality), LGL places patients with RA at risk of infection. Although
seemingly counterintuitive in the context of marked neutropenia,
methotrexate remains the treatment of choice for both Felty’s and
LGL syndromes complicating RA. Other second-line therapies
include cyclosporine, cyclophosphamide, glucocorticoids, and
granulocyte colony-stimulating factor. Splenectomy is reserved
for those patients with Felty’s syndrome and documented splenomegaly who are recalcitrant to medical management.
Malignancy
RA patients have a 10% higher risk of malignancy than the general population. The risk varies based on cancer type, with RA
patients disproportionately afflicted by lymphoproliferative
(particularly Hodgkin’s disease and non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma
[NHL]), lung, and skin malignancies.
As with assessing the
etiology of other extra-articular manifestations, delineating the
simultaneous contributions of underlying RA from the effect of
immunosuppressive agents used to treat the disease is difficult.
Lymphoma risk in RA appears to be closely associated with RA
disease activity and severity, even in the absence of RA treatments. In a large case-control study involving more than 11,000
patients with RA (many of whom were treatment naïve), those
with higher levels of inflammation had a more pronounced risk
of lymphoma, with an odds ratio of greater than 25 among those
with the highest cumulative disease activity.
Patients with Felty’s
syndrome had an approximate 12-fold risk for lymphoma compared with patients with RA without this extra-articular manifestation.
In the absence of immunosuppressive therapy, RA results
in the clonal expansion of CD5
cells, decreases the number and
function of T-suppressor lymphocytes and NK cells, and inhibits
T-suppressor function directed against the prooncogenic Epstein–
Barr virus (EBV), which are all potential contributing factors to an
increased lymphoma risk in RA. These immunologic changes may
also explain the general state of immunosuppression that characterizes RA even in the absence of immunomodulation therapy.
Much less is understood regarding the mechanisms linking RA
with other cancers, although higher serum cytokine expression
was associated with an increased risk of lung and overall cancer
incidence and mortality in a prospective RA cohort.
Mounting evidence suggests that the immune system is closely involved
with cancer neogenesis and progression through processes that

have been termed immunosurveillance and immunoediting; several cytokines well characterized in RA are also detected in the
tumor microenvironment.
Vasculitis
A small- to medium-vessel vasculitis can complicate the natural
course of RA, a manifestation that is more common in men and
associated with RF positivity (high titer), smoking, long-standing
disease, and radiographic damage. Clinically, an important clue
to the development of vasculitis is low serum complements with
associated circulating immune complexes and cryoglobulins, along
with the presence of other extra-articular manifestations, including subcutaneous nodules.
Perhaps owing to improvements in
treatment, the incidence of rheumatoid vasculitis has decreased
substantially in recent years, with a study from a prospective vasculitis registry showing a greater than fivefold decline in incidence
between 1990 and 2010.
Rheumatoid vasculitis can present in a variety of ways,
including:
• Distal arteritis with splinter hemorrhages, nail-fold infarcts,
and possible gangrene (
Fig. 72.12
)
• Cutaneous ulceration, including pyoderma gangrenosum
• Palpable purpura
• Peripheral neuropathy with either a mononeuritis multiplex or
a sensory-related stocking-glove polyneuropathy
• Coronary arteritis
• Pulmonary capillaritis
• Arteritis involving viscera, similar to polyarteritis nodosum
(although lacking microaneurysms)
• Rheumatoid pachymeningitis (rare), confined to the dura and
pia matter
As with other forms of vasculitis, the signs and symptoms of systemic vasculitis in RA stem from vascular occlusion and resulting
ischemic injury. Organ involvement is variable, with the skin and
peripheral nervous system being the most frequently involved;
involvement of the lungs, kidneys, gastrointestinal tract, and CNS
is rare. Intestinal involvement typically manifests as abdominal pain
with bowel infarction representing a surgical emergency. Digital
gangrene, intestinal ischemia/infarction with bleeding or perforation, and mononeuritis multiplex are indicative of severe vasculitis
and are associated with poor patient outcomes.
With diagnostic
biopsy or tissue resection, histopathology in rheumatoid vasculitis typically demonstrates a “panarteritis” with fibrinoid necrosis.
Obliterative endarteritis can be seen with distal gangrene, with
immune complex deposition in the involved vessels reported.
As with other vasculitides, the treatment of rheumatoid vasculitis
depends on the extent and severity of organ involvement. Conservative approaches, consisting of conventional disease-modifying
anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs), can be used with splinter hemorrhage or palpable purpura in isolation, whereas more aggressive regimens, including high-dose glucocorticoids and cytotoxic
therapies, should be reserved for organ-threatening involvement.
Treatment success with biologic DMARDs, including TNF
inhibitors and rituximab, has been reported. Prognosis remains
relatively poor for rheumatoid vasculitis, with no improvement in
survival over time and 5-year mortality in recent cohorts ranging
from 26% to 50%.

Pulmonary involvement in RA is relatively common, targeting the
pleura, parenchyma, and airways. Although reported, pulmonary
arterial hypertension appears to be relatively uncommon in RA.

Several risk factors are associated with pulmonary complications

Pleural Disease.
Clinically apparent pleuritis affects approximately 5% of RA cases, whereas postmortem examination suggests
this number is substantially higher, up to 70%. Pleural effusions
can be large and result in symptomatic dyspnea. Pleural fluid analysis in RA typically reveals exudative fluid, with mixed cell counts
(predominance of monocytes), high protein and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) levels, low glucose concentrations (resulting from a
defect in the transport of glucose across the pleura), and low pH.
Low pH and low fluid glucose (10 to 50 mg/dL) are highly characteristic of RA-related effusions, although these values can also
be observed in infectious etiologies (particularly tuberculosis).

most serious form of pulmonary involvement in RA.

age, autoantibody (ACPA and RF) positivity, and severe RA.

coupled with a forced vital capacity of <80% predicted was associated with an over fourfold increased risk of death.
HRCT, more
sensitive than chest radiography, typically demonstrates bibasilar
reticular or reticulonodular patterns in both lung fields, with a
characteristic honeycombing appearance and traction bronchiectasis in more advanced disease. Lung biopsy, which has traditionally been considered the gold standard for diagnosing ILD,
is infrequently performed. This likely reflects the fact that HRCT

immunomodulatory therapies are often initiated independent of

diseases, where nonspecific interstitial pneumonia (NSIP) is more
frequent. NSIP, characterized on HRCT by ground-glass opacities
without honeycombing, is the next most common pattern of ILD
observed in RA. Even with limited disease, pulmonary function
tests frequently reveal a decreased diffusion capacity with evidence
of restrictive ventilator defects (e.g., diminished vital capacity) and
lung volumes (e.g., total lung capacity) becoming evident with
more extensive ILD.
Caplan’s syndrome
represents a rare complication of RA in
which extra-articular ILD acts in concert with a pneumoconiosis
(related to coal, silica, or asbestosis exposure) to produce a rapidly progressive obliterative granulomatous fibrotic syndrome.
Lymphoid interstitial pneumonitis (LIP), a benign lymphoproliferative disorder of the lung, has been observed in patients with
RA and Sjögren’s syndrome.
Characteristic HRCT findings
of LIP include ground-glass changes accompanied by thickened
bronchovascular bundles, small ill-defined pulmonary nodules,
and scattered thin-walled cysts within the lung parenchyma in
a perivascular or subpleural location. Consistent with a lymphoproliferative process, approximately 80% of patients with LIP
have evidence of a polyclonal or monoclonal IgM gammopathy.
Cryptogenic organizing pneumonia (COP; also known
as
idiopathic bronchiolitis obliterans with organizing pneumonia
) is
another rare pattern of ILD present in RA; it appears to be more
responsive to immunosuppression than UIP. Complicating the
evaluation of RA patients with parenchymal lung findings, most
DMARDs used in the treatment of RA have been associated with
drug-induced pneumonitis.

in RA patients.

over 50% more likely to develop in individuals with RA than
in controls, even after adjusting for pertinent covariates, most
importantly smoking.

obstructive lung disease and was more closely linked with RA

Cardiovascular
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of mortality
in RA.
In addition to associations with atherosclerosis and
coronary artery disease, extra-articular cardiac manifestations also
relate to cardiomyopathy, serositis, and valvulitis. CVD risk in RA
patients appears to be beyond that explained by traditional CVD
risk factors alone (such as hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes)
and results from the complex interaction of both traditional and
RA-related risk factors (
Fig. 72.14
).
Atherosclerosis.
Patients with RA are disproportionately
affected by atherosclerotic CVD,
including angina, myocardial infarction (MI), stroke, and the need for coronary revascularization. In a meta-analysis of observational studies including
41,490 patients, individuals with RA were found to be at a 48%
higher risk of incident CVD events, with a pooled RR of 1.68
(95% CI, 1.40 to 2.03) specifically for MI and 1.41 (95% CI,
1.414 to 1.4) for cerebrovascular events.
Additionally, a metaanalysis of 111,758 patients noted a 50% increase in CVD-related
mortality, with a 52% increase in death from cerebrovascular disease as well as a 59% increase in death from ischemic heart disease
among patients with RA compared with non-RA controls.
The premature atherosclerosis and resulting CVD risk observed
appears to be a consequence of the systemic inflammatory burden posed by RA.
Several studies have now demonstrated that
greater levels of RA disease activity, including both ESR and CRP,
are robust predictors of major adverse cardiovascular events.

Heart Failure.
A large, prospective study identified a twofold
higher risk of heart failure (HF) among RA patients compared
with individuals without RA.
The heightened risk of HF in
RA has been demonstrated independent of ischemic CVD and
traditional CVD risk factors, implicating RA-related risk factors as
mediators of HF in this population.
Indeed, higher RA disease activity and elevated measures of systemic inflammation were
associated with increased risk of HF, in particular nonischemic
HF, in two large RA cohorts.
These findings are consistent
with studies in non-RA populations supporting the role of systemic inflammation in the development of ventricular stiffening,
diastolic dysfunction, and HF with preserved ejection fraction.
In addition to a heightened risk of HF development, RA patients
are at risk for poorer HF-related outcomes, including a nearly
twofold increased risk of mortality in the year following a HF
diagnosis.
Pericarditis.
Pericarditis is the most common cardiac manifestation of RA and may be seen in as many as 50% of patients
at autopsy. Clinically apparent disease is far less common, with
cardiac tamponade serving as a rare consequence. With RA
involvement, pericardial fluid demonstrates findings similar to
that described with RA-related pleuritis; an exudative sample
characterized by cellular infiltrate, high protein and LDH concentrations, and low glucose and low pH levels. Rarely, constrictive pericarditis can complicate the course of RA and may require
pericardiectomy or pericardial stripping for definitive treatment.
Myocarditis.
Myocarditis is rare but may be related to granulomatous disease or interstitial myocarditis. The granulomatous process, when present, pathologically resembles rheumatoid nodules
and is highly specific for RA.
Conduction Defects.
Atrioventricular (AV) block is an
unusual complication of RA but if present is probably related
to direct granulomatous involvement. Complete heart block has
been described and is also more common in patients with seropositive nodular disease.
Because RA is a chronic inflammatory
process, patients with long-standing, poorly controlled disease are
at risk for amyloidosis, which can result in both cardiomyopathy
and conduction disturbances.
Endocardial Inflammation and Granulomatous Aortitis.
Cross-sectional echocardiographic studies have demonstrated
a high prevalence of aortic and mitral valve abnormalities in
RA, including calcifications, thickening, and nodular involvement.
While the epidemiologic burden of valvular disease in
RA has been infrequently studied, a 75% increased risk of valvular
heart disease-related death was recently observed in a national RA
cohort.
In severe RA, granulomatous disease of the endocardium
can spread to the base of the aorta, resulting in aortitis and valvular incompetency, which can rarely necessitate emergent valve
replacement.
Neurologic
Most commonly, neurologic disease in RA is a result of local
compression, such as carpal tunnel syndrome and atlanto-axial
instability with cervical myelopathy (see section on “Articular
Manifestations”). Noncompressive involvement of the central and
peripheral nervous system can develop, although rarely as a direct
result of RA. In a case-control study examining the risk of inflammatory CNS events with prior exposure to TNF inhibitors, RA
was associated with a higher risk for both demyelinating and nondemyelinating CNS events with TNF exposure compared with
other autoimmune diseases including ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis, psoriasis, and inflammatory bowel disease.
Cases
of rheumatoid pachymeningitis have been described, presenting
as an aseptic meningitis with plasma cells and rheumatoid nodules on meningeal biopsy.
In addition, cognitive impairment
and dementia are over-represented in patients with RA.
Beyond
traditionally recognized risk factors, such as hypertension, CVD,
depression and anxiety, more active RA may additionally increase
the risk of incident dementia.

### Disease Outcomes and Prognosis

Disease Outcomes
Because of its systemic and polyarticular nature, RA can negatively affect physical functioning and health-related quality of life.
Compared with individuals without arthritis, patients with RA
in one study were 36% more likely to report fair or poor general
health, twice as likely to report a health-related activity limitation, and almost 30% more likely to require assistance with personal care.
Likely reflecting the strain that living with a chronic
disease renders, impaired quality of life as a consequence of RA
is not limited to physical health domains but also encompasses
mental health domains. In a long-term study of RA patients with
disease onset less than 1 year from enrollment, 39% were unable
to work after just 10 years of follow-up.
Rates of work disability were highest among older patients, those with lower education, manual laborers, and those reporting more baseline physical
impairment. Altogether, with the prevalence of RA increasing in
an aging population, disability-adjusted life-years related to RA
have increased.
Correspondingly, RA-related expenditures in the
United States are staggering. In 2013 alone, related health care
costs were estimated to have exceeded $10 billion, and societal
costs approached $45 billion.
In addition to detrimental
effects on physical functioning and quality of life, RA portends an
increased risk of premature mortality. In a meta-analysis of eight
studies of RA patients within 2 years of disease onset at the start
of follow-up, a pooled standardized mortality ratio (SMR) of 1.48
(95% CI, 1.19 to 1.83) was estimated.
Prognosis
Several factors have been associated with poor outcomes in RA
including reduced survival and increases in disability, related
health care costs, the accrual in structural joint damage, and the
need for total joint arthroplasty. Some of the factors that have
been associated with poor outcomes include:
• Autoantibody (RF and ACPA) status
• Measures indicative of greater disease activity and poorer functional status at baseline
• Extra-articular disease
• Disease duration
• Diagnostic and treatment delays
•
HLA-DRB1
shared epitope status
• Cigarette smoking and heavy alcohol use
• Occupational factors and socioeconomic status
• Erosive disease
• Comorbid illnesses, including depression, post-traumatic stress
disorder, and others
• Altered body composition, namely sarcopenia and excess adiposity
• Older age
• Select medication use (glucocorticoids worsened survival, and
methotrexate improved survival)
Past ACR treatment guidelines have “risk-stratified” patients
based on characteristics such as RF or ACPA positivity or the

presence of radiographic erosions. It is important to recognize,
however, that although these factors may be statistically correlated
with select outcomes in RA, none of them (either alone or in combination) have been demonstrated to provide robust predictions
of any outcome at the level of the individual patient.
Temporal Trends in Outcomes
The landscape of RA has dramatically changed over the last three
decades. With earlier diagnosis and treatment, aggressive treatment to a target of disease remission or minimal disease activity,
and expanding therapeutic choices, many patient outcomes have
dramatically improved. RA patients are more likely to achieve
treatment targets (low disease activity or remission) and have
improved functional status and quality of life.
Occurrence of
extra-articular disease manifestations and rates of joint replacement and hospitalization are declining. Despite these improvements and favorable trends in all-cause mortality, the gap in
mortality rates for specific causes of death between RA patients
and the general population has not been consistently narrowing.
Patients with RA continue to experience a heightened risk of allcause, respiratory, and CVD-related mortality, with nearly 70%
of excess deaths attributable to cardiopulmonary disease.
Additional surveillance of RA patients with disease onset in the current
era will be needed to determine whether improvements in mortality rates are on the horizon.
