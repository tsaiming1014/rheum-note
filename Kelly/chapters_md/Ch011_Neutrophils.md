## Introduction

Neutrophils (polymorphonuclear neutrophils) are a key part of
the innate immune response and the first line of defense against
invading microorganisms.
They belong to the family of haematopoietically derived leukocytes that share the features of a multilobulated nucleus (thus polymorphonuclear) and characteristic
intracytoplasmic granules (hence termed
granulocytes
). Based on
the cytochemical staining properties of their respective granules,
three classes of polymorphonuclear leukocytes can be identified:
neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils. Whereas neutrophil granules stain with neutral dyes, eosinophil granules stain with acidic
dyes such as eosin, and basophil granules stain with basic dyes.
In a standard polychromatic Wright stain of a peripheral blood
smear, the cytoplasm of neutrophils appears blue-pink, and this
helps to differentiate them from the other granulocytes.
Neutrophils are the most abundant type of white blood cell in
humans, typically constituting greater than 50% of all circulating
leukocytes. They are a major cell type involved in acute and some
forms of chronic inflammation. The importance of neutrophils
in bacterial defense is illustrated by patients who have hereditary defects in neutrophil numbers or function and are prone to
repeated and often life-threatening infections. In contrast, tissue
concentrations of neutrophils during periods of homeostasis are
low but increase in response to infection or other triggers. Over
the past decade, neutrophils have reemerged as presumed culprits
in various aspects of pathogenesis of rheumatologic diseases. Neutrophils develop and terminally differentiate in the bone marrow
and are then released into the circulation, where they patrol and
sense danger signals. Under conditions of homeostasis, neutrophils are also found in the lung, spleen, and liver, and they follow tightly regulated circadian rhythms that allow for disarming
strategies prior to their migration to various tissues. Neutrophils
use a complex armamentarium of strategies to immobilize and
kill microbes, including phagocytosis and bacterial degradation
through synthesis of reactive oxygen species (ROS); release of
anti-microbial peptides from granules; and the synthesis of neutrophil extra-cellular traps (NETs), which are lattices of chromatin
bound to granule peptides that have the ability to immobilize and
potentially kill various microorganisms. In this chapter, we review
neutrophil development, structure, and function; the role of neutrophils in protecting against infection; and the role and impact of
neutrophils in the pathogenesis and pathophysiology of immune
deficiency and autoimmune and autoinflammatory diseases.

## Neutrophils: Basic Principles

dedicated to neutrophil production. Every day approximately 1

### Morphology and Contents

The multilobed nature of the neutrophil nucleus reflects the condensation of chromatin, and it was believed that mature neutrophils may be incapable of transcription. However, recent evidence
demonstrates that neutrophils retain the capacity for both constitutive and stimulated RNA and protein synthesis. On exposure to
microbial agents, human neutrophils rapidly change their chromatin folding with subsequent changes to nuclear architecture
and activation of proinflammatory genes.
These changes are
unique to the stimuli faced with the closed chromatin opening up
in one of three mechanisms where the accessible chromatin occurs
only in the promoter region or only in the distal sites or at the
promoter and distal sites.
This plastic transcriptional expression
takes place under an intricate accessible chromatin regulation.
Perturbation in this tight regulation can lead to susceptibility to
autoimmune and inflammatory diseases. Variants associated with
binding of myeloid master regulator PU.1 in primary neutrophils
across 100 volunteers showed genetically driven differences in cell
count and immune disease susceptibility.
Neutrophil granules have been classified into three types: azurophilic (primary) granules, specific (secondary) granules, and gelatinase (tertiary) granules (

proteins contained in these granules are a cornerstone of innate
immune responses. In addition to their direct anti-microbial
effects, granular contents play important roles in amplifying or
dampening innate and adaptive immune responses.

### Diapedesis

The process of neutrophils passing through blood vessels is called
diapedesis
. The exact mechanism of diapedesis is still not completely
understood. While many believe that it occurs by disruption of
cell–cell junctions between endothelial cells,
some evidence has
also shown that neutrophils can pass directly through the endothelial cells by generation of pores in them (
Fig. 11.4
).
Interactions between adhesion molecules called platelet-endothelial cell
adhesion molecules found on both neutrophils and endothelial
cells is critical for diapedesis. Neutrophils also upregulate a6b1, an
integrin that mediates binding to laminin, which is a key component of perivascular basement membrane.
Other proteins that
are also upregulated during transmigration of neutrophils through
the endothelium include CD47, also known as integrin-associated
protein and CD99. After passing through the endothelium, neutrophils then go through the basement membrane through transient disruption of the patency of this membrane.

### Chemotaxis

The directional motion of neutrophils across a molecular gradient is called
chemotaxis
. This is achieved by formation of membrane ruffles, known as
lamellipodia,
followed by anchoring of
these extensions to the substrate and withdrawal of the trailing
edge. This movement is accomplished through rearrangement of
the actin cytoskeleton after sensing of chemotactic gradients that
signal through G protein–coupled receptors and phosphoinositide
3-kinases. Chemoattractant receptors concentrate at the leading
edge, the so-called “headlight phenomenon.” As the cell moves
forward, receptors on the formerly leading edge are swept to the
tail and internalized.

### Degranulation

The process of mobilization of granules in neutrophils is called
degranulation
. Degranulation helps kill pathogens and modulate immune response in infectious and noninfectious diseases.
Release of granular proteins can be extra-cellular when the vesicles
fuse to the plasma membrane or intra-cellular when the vesicles
fuse to the phagosome (detailed in the “Phagocytosis” section)
to kill extra-cellular and intra-cellular microbes, respectively. For
extra-cellular degranulation, there is a hierarchy by which vesicles
fuse to the plasma membrane and spill their contents.
Secretory vesicles mobilize first, followed by gelatinase granules, specific
granules, and azurophil granules.

### Phagocytosis

Phagocytosis is one of the mechanisms used by neutrophils to
eliminate infectious organisms, dead cells, and other particles.
It requires direct contact and is activated by recognition of
pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), small molecule
motifs found on/in bacteria or viruses. PAMPs are recognized by
extra-cellular or intra-cellular Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and other
pattern recognition receptors, which get activated and stimulate phagocytosis by stimulating structural and conformational
changes.
Another process that greatly enhances phagocytosis
is opsonization, where the surface of the target is decorated with
immunoglobulin, complement components, or both, resulting in
modification and better recognition.

Another way that neutrophils can kill bacteria is by generating
toxic oxygen metabolites like nitric oxide, superoxide anion (O

### Neutrophil Extra-cellular Traps

Neutrophil extra-cellular traps (NETs) are typically generated and
released through a distinct programmed inflammatory cell death
process, although mechanisms of NET formation that do not
result in cell death have been described. NETs are structures composed of externalized, concentrated nuclear and granule contents
that contain many potent anti-microbial molecules that can trap,
immobilize, inactivate, and kill microorganisms.
They also have
the ability to stimulate other neutrophils and immune cells. NET
formation can be triggered by various stimuli including microorganisms and their products, cytokines, autoantibodies, immune
complexes, crystals, chemicals/drugs, and platelets.
While the exact mechanism that leads to NET formations
continues to be refined, the process usually depends on ROS
production, either through NADPH oxidase machinery and/or
mitochondria. ROS can prevent actin polymerization and cause
the release of many proteases (MPO and neutrophil elastase) that
cleave histones and other structural proteins, including the poreforming gasdermin D, which subsequently leads to a decrease in
membrane stability and an increase in the concentration of calcium in the cytoplasm.
Once initiated, there is disassembly of
the neutrophil cytoskeleton, assembly of granular proteins on both
genomic and mitochondrial DNA, and post-translational modification of histones, including citrullination by the peptidyl arginine deiminase (PAD) family of enzymes, particularly PAD4.
These modifications can disrupt the electrostatic interactions of
DNA and histones that lead to changes in the chromatin structure
and promote decondensation of nuclear material.
This is followed by degradation of cell membrane and extrusion
of NETs in the extra-cellular space (
Fig. 11.6
and Video 11.1).
Post-translationally modified (citrullinated and carbamylated)
proteins found in NETs have direct pathogenic action on target
cells and can modify immunogenicity and vasculopathic effects
compared with unmodified versions, suggesting their key role in
the pathogenesis and generation of autoantigens and promotion
of autoimmune response in predisposed individuals.

## Heterogeneity

Recent studies show that circulating neutrophils are not as homogenous as previously considered. Using single-cell RNA sequencing, neutrophils in peripheral blood can broadly be classified in
four subsets based on their gene expression profile.
The majority of neutrophils express genes associated with maturity. Nearly
20% are relatively immature, and another subset shows minimal
transcriptional activity. Of note, there is a neutrophil subset that
expresses a higher level of type I interferon (IFN) stimulated genes
compared with other subsets. Women appear to have an IFNregulated neutrophil subset that respond more to this group of
cytokines than the male subset, indicating that female neutrophils
may have a more active intra-cellular machinery due to differences in maturation.
This subset is also expanded in patients
with severe COVID-19 and can be decreased by steroid treatment.
Although the role of this IFN-responsive neutrophil subset in health and disease remains to be determined, it is possible
that these neutrophils are primed cells that patrol the blood and
may be readily available to fight off infections. Similarly, in those
genetically predisposed or in the presence of other priming factors
(like sex hormones), some individuals may have more activated
neutrophils. This could explain sex differences in many systemic
autoimmune diseases as well as robust response to many infectious
diseases by females. Indeed, human neutrophils from young adult
women are more mature and activated than those from young
adult men, with an increased tendency to form NETs.
These
sex differences in neutrophils are likely driven by sex hormones as
they are not present in pre-pubescent children or individuals with
Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY).
A subset of proinflammatory neutrophils described in inflammatory conditions, based on its density during separation of
blood components, is called low-density granulocyte (LDG).
These neutrophils are pathogenic and differ from normal-density
neutrophils in several phenotypic and functional parameters. For
example, LDGs produce higher levels of proinflammatory cytokines like IFNs and are primed for increased NET formation.
Presence of LDGs correlates with several clinical phenotypes
including renal disease and vasculopathy.
A recent closer look
at LDGs using bulk and single-cell RNA sequencing and flow
cytometry has shown that this group of cells, described by density
gradient, is a mix of cells in various transcriptional states with
differences in maturation and functions,
although the majority
are intermediate mature cells with proinflammatory features. The
role of LDGs in various autoimmune diseases is discussed in more
detail below.
Other neutrophil subsets like myeloid-derived suppressor cells
have also been reported in cancer and sepsis but are beyond the
scope of this chapter.
LDGs do not display myeloid-derived
suppressor cell activity despite expressing some of the markers
associated to those suppressive phenotypes.

## Role of Neutrophils, Low-Density Granulocytes, and Neutrophil Extra-cellular Traps in Rheumatic Diseases

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Evidence over the past decade has established that the innate immune
system, specifically the neutrophils, play a crucial role in the initiation and propagation of aberrant immune responses, including organ
damage and vasculopathy, seen in systemic lupus erythematosus
(SLE) (
Fig. 11.7
).
Patients with SLE show altered neutrophil populations and association of neutrophil-specific genes with more active
disease. A multitude of abnormalities in phenotype and function,
including increased cell death, impaired ability to phagocytose, and
aberrant oxidative activity, have been reported in neutrophils from
patients with SLE.
In the bone marrow, neutrophils contribute to synthesis of type I IFNs and B cell dysregulation through
B–cell activating factor (BAFF)–mediated effects.
Lupus patients have increased circulating levels of LDGs,
which have been associated with distinct clinical manifestations,
including skin and renal involvement, vasculopathy, arterial
inflammation, and coronary artery disease.
Lupus LDGs
have distinct transcriptomic, epigenetic, and functional attributes
compared with normal dense neutrophils from lupus or healthy
controls.
They differ in protein content and have distinctive modulation of their cytoskeleton, which leads to differences
in biochemical properties, potentially making them predisposed
to getting trapped in organs like lungs and causing tissue damage.
They are also key drivers of the type I IFN gene signature characteristic of SLE and contribute to the synthesis of type I IFNs and
other proinflammatory cytokines. Recent evidence suggests that
LDGs from SLE patients are not as homogenous as previously
thought and there are at least two main subpopulations of intermediate-mature and immature neutrophils with differences in
transcriptional, epigenetic, and functional readouts, like phagocytosis, response to type I IFNs, NET formation, and chemotaxis.
Neutrophils (particularly LDGs) from SLE patients, compared
with those from healthy controls, exhibit increased propensity
to form NETs that can cause direct tissue damage.
NETs

Oxidation of

sizable portion of SLE patients, which could explain the impaired

are associated with kidney involvement and vascular damage.

lupus LDGs.

increased in lupus skin, vessels, kidneys, and placenta in association with inflammation and organ damage.

### Anti-neutrophilic Cytoplasmic Antibody– Associated Vasculitis

In patients with anti-neutrophilic cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–
associated vasculitis (AAV), antibodies against granular proteins in
cytoplasm of neutrophils like MPO and PR3 serve as serologic
biomarkers of diagnosis and severity.
These antibodies may
also activate neutrophils that have already been primed by proinflammatory stimuli, like TNF, G-CSF, lipopolysaccharide, or
C5a, leading to degranulation and production of oxygen radicals
that promote destructive necrotizing vascular and extravascular
inflammation.
This triggers an inflammatory amplification
loop in the vessel wall that further attracts and activates neutrophils. Neutrophils from AAV patients also have enhanced NET
formation in vitro.
Studies suggest increased NET formation
may be associated with ANCA generation, as several ANCA targets are part of the DNA–granular protein complexes that form
NETs.
NET remnants, like MPO–DNA complexes, and
neutrophil granule proteins, such as calprotectin, have been found
in increased levels in sera from AAV patients. Levels of NET remnants correlated with disease activity and neutrophil counts.
Furthermore, NETs and NET structures have also been detected
on immunostaining of several affected tissues like vascular
thrombi and kidneys from patients with AAV.
As in SLE,
increased LDGs and their transcriptional signature is seen in AAV.
Moreover, LDG signature in peripheral blood is associated with
disease severity and decreased treatment responses to agents like
rituximab and cyclophosphamide.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is characterized by the presence of
highly specific autoantibodies that target post-translationally
modified proteins like anti-citrullinated protein antibodies
(ACPAs) and anti-carbamylated protein (anti-CarP) antibodies.
Immune complexes of citrullinated antigens and ACPAs
display increased immunogenicity and arthritogenicity.
Neutrophils are abundant in the inflamed joints of patients with RA,
especially in synovial effusions in early stages of disease.
Patients
with RA have increased levels of NET complexes in circulation,
which correlates with ACPA levels and systemic inflammatory
markers.
In the tissue, increased NET formation is also seen
in synovial fluid neutrophils, synovial tissue, rheumatoid nodules,
and skin. An interesting finding is the presence of NETs in sputum and their association with IgA and IgG ACPA responses in
relatives of RA patients and in individuals at high risk of developing RA. Reduced endocytic capacity of sputum macrophages and
association of NET remnants and IgA ACPA with inflammatory
cytokines like IL-1, IL-6, and TNF was also found in subjects at
risk of developing RA. These findings suggest a potential mechanism for ACPA generation in the mucosa and the role for dysregulated neutrophil responses in the lung in early RA.
Neutrophils may also play a role in the presence of extracellular citrullinated autoantigens found in the joints of patients
with RA and the subsequent generation of ACPAs. Many citrullinated autoantigens are part of the chromatin complex that is
extruded from neutrophils during NET formation, forming a
source of extra-cellular citrullinated proteins.
NET formation
also releases active peptidyl arginine deiminase (PAD) isoforms
that could citrullinate extra-cellular histones and fibrinogen.
Indeed, neutrophils overexpress PAD2 and PAD4 and these
enzymes are seen in close association with neutrophil infiltrated in
the synovium of patients with RA.
Moreover, NETs activate RA synovial fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS) that results in release of proinflammatory cytokines,
chemokines, and adhesion molecules.
NET products can
be internalized by FLS, with subsequent upregulation of class II
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules and presentation of NET-derived peptides by FLS to CD4 T cells, leading
to antigen-specific T cell responses, ACPA responses, and joint
damage. NET-derived elastase can also directly damage cartilage
matrix, leading to release of membrane-bound PAD-2 by FLS.
The cartilage fragments thus generated are subsequently citrullinated, taken up by FLS and then presented to antigen-specific
CD4+ T cells. Immune complexes containing citrullinated cartilage
components can activate macrophages to release proinflammatory cytokines.
Thus, in a genetically predisposed individual,
increased NET formation can lead to generation of key autoantibodies and disruption in adaptive immunity that leads to a vicious
cycle, further promoting NET formation and amplification of
inflammatory responses in joint and other tissues.
NETs also externalize carbamylated (car-P) antigens (like
car-histones and car-LL37) that correlate with their respective

anti-CarP antibodies in RA patients and are associated with more
aggressive erosive RA. Carbamylated proteins bound to NETs
are internalized by FLS and loaded on class II MHC for T-cell
presentation. Anti-CarP histone–IgG immune complexes promote osteoclast differentiation and osteoclast-mediated matrix
resorption.
There are also reports of circulating LDGs in RA patients with
correlation with disease activity.
However, in contrast to
SLE, LDGs seen in RA patients do not seem to have increased
levels of induced NET formation. They also express lower levels of
TNF receptors, with decreased responsiveness to TNF. These findings suggest a functional heterogeneity of these cells, with much
still to understand about the role of LDGs in RA.

### Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome

Neutrophils from patients with antiphospholipid antibody syndrome (APLS) have an activated, proinflammatory phenotype.
There is increased expression of interferon stimulated genes and
of surface adhesion proteins like P-selectin glycoprotein ligand
1 (PSGL1, aka SELPLG or CD162) that promotes the formation of vascular thrombi by enhanced binding of neutrophils to
the endothelial cells of the vascular wall.
Neutrophils from
APLS patients form spontaneous NETs ex vivo. NETs are integral
components of thrombi and are important activators of the coagulation cascade.
Patients with primary APLS have elevated levels
of both cell-free DNA and NET remnants in circulation and in
affected tissues.
Sera and IgG from patients with APLS can also
stimulate NET formation from control neutrophils, which is considered to be ROS- and TLR4-dependent. Sera from patients with
APLS also has decreased ability to degrade NETs, likely due to
presence of antibodies against NETs and neutrophil remnants.
LDGs have also been reported in APLS; however, their role in its
pathogenesis remains to be determined.

### Idiopathic Inflammatory Myositis

Recent reports show the role of neutrophils in the pathogenesis
of various idiopathic inflammatory myositis (IIMs). Neutrophilspecific molecules, like neutrophil elastase, PRTN3, and serine
proteases, were found to be elevated in circulation as well as in
tissues.
Neutrophil gene signature was present in the muscle
tissues of patients with IIM and associated with the IFN gene signature as well as extent of muscle damage.
Affected tissues from
IIM patients show increased neutrophil infiltration with NET
formation. In some patients with IIM, increased levels of circulating NETs were associated with specific disease manifestations
and disease severity. Similar to SLE, and likely due to presence of
serum antibodies to DNASE1, NET degradation is also impaired
in those with IIM.
Patients also have increased levels of LDGs
with increased NET formation ability.
Additionally, myositisspecific autoantibodies like MDA5 can also directly induce NET
formation in vitro. These NETs, in a manner dependent on citrullinated histones, can interfere with the biology of myoblasts and
myotubes, leading to an impairment in the function of these cells
in striated muscles.

### Psoriasis

Neutrophil infiltration is a hallmark of the initiation phase of psoriatic lesions. Neutrophil-derived MMP-9 likely induces cutaneous vasodilation and hyperpermeability by activating cutaneous
vascular endothelial cells, facilitating psoriatic lesion development.
LDGs have also been found to be elevated in psoriasis
and associated with skin disease severity.
They associated with
noncalcified coronary plaque burden and included endothelial cell
damage in vitro. Interestingly, platelet-associated biologic pathways were upregulated in psoriasis LDGs along with colocalization
of platelets with LDGs in circulation. This LDG–platelet interaction associated more strongly with noncalcified coronary plaque
burden than LDGs alone, suggesting a role of activated platelets
as potential stimuli for increased NET formation in LDGs from
patients with psoriasis.

### Gout

Acute gouty arthritis, caused by deposition of monosodium urate
(MSU) crystals in the joints, is characterized by presence of numerous neutrophils in the affected synovial fluid.
In an acute
episode, neutrophils may lead to direct damage of joint structures
through discharge of intra-cellular lysosomal contents directly
into the joint fluid during attempted phagocytoses of MSU crystals. MSU crystals are also potent inducers of NADPH oxidasemediated NET formation. Interestingly, recent studies show that
MSU-induced NET formation can also help in the resolution of
acute inflammation by creation of granuloma and NET aggregates
that can degrade inflammatory cytokines like IL-1b.

### Other Diseases

Deficiency of adenosine deaminase 2 (DADA2)
is a rare, recessively inherited monogenic autoinflammatory vasculitis due to a
biallelic mutation in the
ADA2
gene that encodes for adenosine
deaminase 2, an enzyme that breaks down extra-cellular adenosine
and is mainly expressed by myeloid cells.
The clinical presentation of DADA2 is broad, and manifestations include early-onset
systemic inflammation, vasculitis, early-onset stroke, cytopenias,
and immunodeficiency.
Mutation in
ADA2
leads to decreased
enzyme activity, resulting in increased levels of extra-cellular adenosine that binds to A1 and A3 adenosine receptors on the neutrophils to trigger NET formation. Patients also have increased
LDGs in circulation that are predisposed to form NETs. These
NETs activate macrophages that subsequently produce proinflammatory cytokines like TNF. NETs have also been found in affected
organs from patients with DADA2, further reinforcing the dysregulation of neutrophils in this disease.
Pyogenic arthritis, pyoderma gangrenosum, and acne syndrome
(PAPA syndrome)
is a rare autosomal-dominant, autoinflammatory
disease caused by mutations in the
PSTPIP1
gene that encodes
proline-serine-threonine phosphatase-interacting protein 1.
Patients with PAPA syndrome have variable presentation that
includes neutrophil-predominant arthritis. Mutations in
PSTPIP1
alter its interaction with pyrin and the inflammasome, leading to
increased serum levels of IL-1b and other neutrophil-associated
proteins, such as MMPs.
PAPA patients show increased NET
formation and reduced NET clearance, increasing their half-life.
Additionally, NET remnants were also found in skin biopsies
from PAPA patients. These neutrophils are more responsive to
IL-1b and inhibition of IL-1b (with anakinra, an IL-1 receptor
antagonist) can block NET formation. These observations suggest
a link between IL-1b and neutrophil responses.
Vacuoles, E1 enzyme, X-linked, autoinflammatory, somatic syndrome (VEXAS)
is a recently described monogenic, adult-onset,
X-linked disease caused by somatic mutation in the
UBA1
gene

that encodes ubiquitin-like modifier activating enzyme 1 and is
expressed on cells of myeloid lineage.
Patients with VEXAS are
usually older men that present with treatment-refractory severe
systemic inflammation and pronounced neutrophilic infiltrates
in tissues with clinical features of vasculitis, polychondritis, and/
or myelodysplasia. The mutation inactivates E1 ubiquitin ligase
activity of the enzyme, which is important for protein homeostasis through promotion of proteasomal degradation of damaged or
unwanted proteins in a cell. Inactivation of
UBA1
results in disruption of intra-cellular architecture in neutrophils that can often
be visualized as cytoplasmic vacuoles. Neutrophils in this disease
maintain their phagocytic activity; however, they have increased
NET formation. Our understanding of this disease is still evolving, and further research may help untangle the role of ubiquitylation and other-post-translational modifications in neutrophil
functions.
Several drugs have been attributed to induce autoimmunity. Recent evidence suggests a role of NET formation in some
drug-induced vasculitis
. Vasculitis purpura with neutropenia and
ANCAs is seen in some active illicit users of cocaine contaminated
with levamisole.
This phenomenon is likely due to levamisole as
it induces formation of NETs through engagement of muscarinic
subtype 3 receptor. Patients actively using levamisole-adulterated
cocaine have autoantibodies against NET components in sera.
Additionally, NETs were found to be present in skin biopsies of
areas with vasculitis in these patients.
Other studies have shown a role of neutrophil activation and
increased NET formation in vasculitis induced by drugs like propylthiouracil (PTU),
procainamide, and hydralazine.
Interestingly, the NETs generated by these drugs differ. For example,
NETs generated by exposure of neutrophils to PTU are relatively
resistant to digestion by DNase 1, whereas those generated by
exposure to hydralazine require PAD4 activation and do not interfere with DNase 1 degradation. These variations in types of NETs
could possibly explain the differences in phenotypes because these
drugs.

## Targeting Neutrophils and Neutrophil Extra- cellular Traps in Systemic Autoimmune Diseases

Given the key role that neutrophils and NETs play in the pathogenesis of wide-ranging systemic autoimmune diseases, there is
increasing interest in targeting them for therapeutic purposes.
Drugs that can decrease formation, half-life, or release of NETs
could be beneficial. Since ROS production is key in NET formation, inhibitors of NADPH oxidase or mitochondrial ROS production or ROS scavengers, like N-acetylcysteine (NAC), have
been explored in vitro and in vivo with mixed results in murine
models and SLE patients.
Inhibition of various granule
protein functions, like MPO, in mouse models of immune complex-mediated vasculitis decreased neutrophil recruitment, NET
formation, and inflammatory cytokine formation and is a potential target.
TLR inhibition may also have similar effects, with
in vitro studies on human neutrophils showing TLR4 inhibition
resulted in decreased NET formation and ROS production.
Another protein of interest are the PAD enzymes, as targeting
them has shown a decrease in NET formation and autoantibodies
and protection from organ and vascular damage in mice models
of lupus.
Degrading already formed NETs to help improve their removal
is another potential approach. DNase1L3 is an enzyme involved
in degradation of extra-cellular DNA, like in NETs. Humans
and mice with genetic defects in this enzyme have lupus features
with autoantibody production, suggesting a possible use of this
enzyme in systemic autoimmune disease patients with high levels
of NETs.
Other drugs that can destabilize NET structures by
affecting the actin cytoskeleton could also decrease the half-life of
NETs.
Blocking IL-17 can decrease neutrophil trafficking and NET
formation and may diminish lupus nephritis.
Interruption of interactions between endothelial ligands and neutrophilexpressed kindlin-3 and B2 integrin could lead to a decrease in
neutrophil recruitment and NET release in tissues.
Similarly,
blocking various chemokines may limit pathogenic tissue infiltration of neutrophils.
Reduction of circulating NETs and
improvement in lupus biomarkers was also seen by reducing
autoantibodies and immune complexes by a combination of
monoclonal antibodies targeting CD20 (rituximab) and BLyS/
BAFF (belimumab).
Neutrophils with elevated IFN signature are primed for NET
formation. Blocking of IFN signaling through use of mAb (anifrolumab) targeting the type I IFN receptor (IFNAR1) has shown
promising results in two lupus trials and is now approved by the
U.S. Food and Drug Administration.
Anifrolumab also decreased
NET formation in SLE subjects compared with placebo.
Similarly, anti-malarials like hydroxychloroquine are weak type I IFN
inhibitors and can decrease NET formation in vitro.
Also, inhibitors of Janus kinases (JAKs), signaling molecules for several proinflammatory cytokines like IFNs, can directly modulate neutrophil
biology. Tofacitinib, a pan-JAK inhibitor, reduced NET formation
in vitro and in vivo and decreased disease manifestations in lupusprone mice.
In a phase Ia/IIb clinical trial of tofacitinib in SLE
patients, LDGs and NETs were significantly decreased.

## Conclusion

Neutrophils play a key role as the first line of defense against
foreign organisms. Through chemotaxis, phagocytosis, oxidative
burst, or NET formation, neutrophils protect our bodies from
many forms of infection and also send signals to help develop
more directed responses. However, growing evidence over the
past decade has shown that dysregulated neutrophil responses are
responsible for several autoimmune and autoinflammatory conditions. Rogue neutrophil responses can lead to direct tissue injury,
create an inflammatory milieu, and generate autoantigens. Recent
studies have also recognized the diverse and functionally distinct
subsets of neutrophils. Targeting pathogenic neutrophil subsets or
dysregulated neutrophil functions, like NET formation, are areas
of exciting research with hopes of development of novel therapeutic strategies.
