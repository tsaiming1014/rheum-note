KEY POINTS

> Acute bacterial arthritis is a medical emergency that warrants rapid,

> accurate diagnosis and immediate treatment along with appropriate

> consultation with specialists as needed.

> Most cases of native joint infection are the result of hematogenous

> spread.

> Staphylococcus aureus

> is the most frequent microorganism in adult

> nongonococcal septic arthritis.

> The initial selection of an antibiotic regimen should be broad enough

> to take into account host factors, clinical characteristics, likely

> causative microorganisms, and regional antibiotic sensitivity data

> pending confirmation of bacteria by culture and sensitivities.

> An infected joint must be adequately drained, and an antibiotic course

> that is sufficiently long to cure the infection must be instituted.

> Surgical drainage should be considered but only if needle

> aspirations are unsuccessful or impractical.

> Poor prognostic factors in bacterial joint infection include old age,

> underlying rheumatoid arthritis, use of immunosuppressive therapy,

> and infection in a prosthetic joint.

> Debridement, antibiotics, and implant retention are reasonable options

> for selected patients with early prosthetic joint infections (PJIs).

> Late PJIs require antibiotic treatment directed at the isolated

> microorganism and the complete removal of the infected prosthesis

> before reimplantation of a new prosthesis in a one-stage or two-

> stage operation.

> Reducing the risk of a PJI involves a thorough pre-operative evaluation

> and peri-operative use of antibiotics. Clinical evidence does not

> support the use of antibiotic prophylaxis for most dental procedures.

## Epidemiology

Septic arthritis is a medical and surgical emergency associated with
significant morbidity and mortality, especially in immunocompromised individuals. Delaying diagnosis and effective treatment
can lead to irreversible joint damage, disseminated infection, or
death. In children, the most affected joint is the hip, followed by
the knee and the ankle. In adults, the knee, hip, and ankle are
the most common sites of infection followed by the elbow, wrist,
shoulder, sternoclavicular joint, sacroiliac joint, and foot joint.
Polyarticular involvement may be seen in 10% to 20% of cases
(see
e-Fig. 112.1
).
The overall incidence of septic arthritis varies depending on the
country. In the United States the incidence is 4 to 10 per 100,000
individuals.
In western Europe, it is 4 to 12 per 100,000 patientyears, and in Australia, it is as high as 29 cases per 100,000 in the
aboriginal population.
In children, bacterial joint infections have
an incidence of 4 cases per 100,000 children per year, occurring
more frequently in males under 5 years of age.
The populations
commonly affected include neonates, persons with hemophilia
complicated with hemarthrosis, and persons with sickle cell disease, human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), and malignancy on
active chemotherapy.
The incidence of bacterial arthritis in adults is 5 to 12 cases per
100,000 people and is more common among those with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) or a prosthetic joint, with up to 70 cases per
100,000 individuals.
The incidence varies based on the presence
of risk factors including older age, diabetes mellitus, HIV, osteoarthritis (OA), and immunosuppressive therapy.
The incidence
has increased likely due to the aging population, with OA requiring orthopedic procedures (i.e., intra-articular injections, joint
replacements, etc.), increased use of potent immunosuppressant
agents, and intravenous (IV) drug use in the opioid epidemic era.
Other risk factors that increase the risk of septic arthritis include
skin infection, history of septic arthritis, cirrhosis, hypogammaglobulinemia, end-stage renal disease on hemodialysis, crystal
arthropathies, and low socioeconomic status
(
Table 112.1
).

### Pathogenesis

Acute septic arthritis is usually monoarticular due to a contiguous source from skin or bursa infection, direct inoculation from
trauma or animal bites, procedures (arthrocentesis, steroid injection), or hematogenous seeding from a bloodstream infection.
Joint infections occur when microorganisms gain access to the
highly vascularized synovium. The normal joint is resistant to
invasion because of local and systemic host defenses. Nevertheless,

the synovium lacks a protective basement membrane, making it
vulnerable to infection through hematogenous spread, the most
common route of infection. Once bacteria reach the well-vascularized synovium, the low fluid shear conditions and the production of host matrix proteins allow bacteria to adhere to the tissue,
causing infection.
S. aureus
is the most common cause of nongonococcal septic arthritis, and it has multiple virulence factors
that are associated with its ability to bind the connective tissue
and access the joint, evade the host defenses, and cause damage to
the joint
(
Fig. 112.1
). It has a thick peptidoglycan cell wall that
acts as a protective barrier against the immune response from the
host. Joint damage occurs from the combination of bacterial adhesion and toxins, the inflammatory cascade by the host immune
response, and ischemia.
S. aureus
reaches the synovium with the
assistance of microbial surface components recognizing adhesive
matrix molecules (MSCRAMMs). These molecules are part of the
cell wall peptidoglycan of
S. aureus
and bind to host matrix proteins including collagen, fibrinogen, elastin, vitronectin, laminin,
and fibronectin.
Among the most important
S. aureus
virulence factors are
collagen-binding protein, clumping factor A and B, fibronectinbinding protein A and B, capsular polysaccharide, staphylococcal
protein A (SpA), toxic shock syndrome toxin-1, and enterotoxins.
Other virulence factors that help
S. aureus
evade the host immune

### Clinical Features

The classic presentation of septic arthritis is new-onset pain, swelling, warmth, erythema, decreased range of motion of the affected
joint, and occasionally fever. Bacterial arthritis of the large joints
is more common, especially the knee and hip, the former in adults
and the latter in children. People with septic arthritis often have
underlying illnesses that increase the risk of joint infection, with
RA being an important risk factor. Patients with RA and other
inflammatory or crystal arthropathies that increase the risk of bacterial arthritis have baseline pain, swelling, and range-of-motion
limitation of the joints which makes it sometimes difficult to differentiate an arthritis flare from a septic joint.
Most patients with bacterial arthritis are febrile and have
decreased appetite, general malaise, weakness, and hesitancy to use
the affected joint. In neonates or young children, septic arthritis
is more difficult to identify as they usually do not present with
typical signs and symptoms of infection. Some of the signs that
can help and raise suspicion of a septic joint in this population
are irritability, failure to thrive, decreased oral intake, tachycardia,
and fever. On physical exam, the neonate may flex, abduct, or
externally rotate the hip to relieve the intra-articular pressure.
Children may be hesitant to extend or flex the other affected joints
and may have weight-bearing limitations. In adults, the presentation is classic, although, in immunocompromised patients, typical signs may be absent. Thirty to 60% of cases of septic arthritis
in healthy individuals present with fever; therefore the absence
of fever does not rule out bacterial arthritis.
The onset of symptoms can vary depending on the virulence of the organism causing
the infection and the individual’s immune status. More virulent
organisms, such as
S. aureus
, will cause symptoms within 1 week.
Monoarticular infection is present in 80% to 90% of the cases.

### Diagnosis

Synovial Fluid and Blood Analysis
The diagnosis of bacterial arthritis starts with a high level of suspicion considering clinical features of a painful, swollen, erythematous joint in the absence of an alternative explanation
for inflammation. Whenever bacterial arthritis is suspected, the
most important diagnostic procedure is arthrocentesis with synovial fluid analysis. For joints that are deep and more difficult to
aspirate, ultrasound-guided or fluoroscopy-guided needle aspiration should be performed. Delay in diagnosis or long duration
of symptoms before seeking medical attention and the presence
of bacteremia, leukocytosis at presentation, worsening renal function, and polyarticular infection are among the factors associated
with poor outcome.
The synovial fluid analysis starts with the evaluation of the
appearance of the fluid (see
Fig. 112.2
). A normal synovial fluid
should look clear and viscous and will have few white blood cells
(WBCs). Fluid from an infected joint may look purulent or turbid. Historically, leukocyte count in the synovial fluid has been
used to differentiate between septic arthritis and other inflammatory arthropathies. The most common criterion for septic synovial
fluid WBC is >50,000/mm
, with most cases close to 100,000/
mm
with >90% polymorphonuclear (PMN) cells. Unfortunately,
this information alone is not enough to make a diagnosis, as
atypical infections and immunosuppressed patients tend to have
a lower WBC count, and other inflammatory arthropathies may
have a higher WBC in synovial fluid.

arthritis are listed in (

).
As there are multiple reasons for abnormal synovial fluid, culture continues to be the gold
standard for the diagnosis of bacterial arthritis. When specimens
are obtained for cultures, a Gram stain is performed (see
Table
112.4
for sensitivity and specificity).
The sensitivity of the
cultures is improved by the inoculation of blood culture bottles
with synovial fluid, and cultures are incubated for at least 4 days.
If suspicious of an atypical organism, this might be extended to 14
days to allow slow-growers such as
C. acnes
to grow, although this
organism is more commonly associated with PJIs.
Blood cultures should also be obtained prior to antibiotic therapy. They are positive in 30% to 50% of cases of bacterial arthritis; in 10% to 15% of cases, blood culture is the only method of
identifying the pathogen. Extra-articular sites of infection such as
pneumonia, urinary tract infection, or cellulitis may offer guidance regarding the possible pathogen causing septic arthritis.
Synovial fluid glucose, protein, and lactate dehydrogenase offer
limited diagnostic value for bacterial arthritis. Leukocyte esterase,
released by leukocytes during inflammation, has been studied as
a diagnostic marker for PJI and to differentiate between septic or
aseptic body fluids such as cerebrospinal fluid, pleural fluid, and
peritoneal fluid.
Other markers in the synovial fluid such as procalcitonin and
presepsin have been recently studied for both native and PJIs. Procalcitonin is the peptide precursor of calcitonin, while presepsin is
produced from CD14 after bacteria are phagocytosed by monocytes. These two biomarkers have been found to be elevated in the
bloodstream in the presence of a bacterial infection. Synovial procalcitonin has been evaluated as a marker of septic arthritis including native and PJIs, and low synovial procalcitonin (<0.5
μ
g/L)
had a negative predictive value of 90%, whereas a value above 4.5
μ
g/L had a positive predictive value of 82%.
The utility of synovial fluid presepsin as a tool for early diagnosis of septic arthritis
has been studied and is elevated in patients with native and PJIs
compared with patients with OA. The sensitivity and specificity
were higher than those of blood presepsin.
Additional studies have been done comparing presepsin in
synovial fluid and blood with other inflammation markers such
as C-reactive protein (CRP) and procalcitonin in patients with
native septic joint and crystal arthropathies. Synovial fluid pre
sepsin has good sensitivity and specificity for joint infection,
85.7% and 85.2%, respectively. Synovial lactate and IL-6 are
additional markers of septic arthritis, and lactate had the highest predictive value with a sensitivity of 75% and a specificity of
87%, especially with values >10 mmol/L. IL-6 had the highest
sensitivity of 92% (with levels <7000 pg/mL) and a specificity of
64%.
Even though these markers have been studied recently and
their utility is promising, they have not yet all been incorporated
into guidelines. There are multiple other markers that have been
studied as predictors of infection, although with variable sensitivity and specificity when evaluated individually. Inflammatory
markers such as erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and CRP
are usually elevated in serum, but sensitivity is low.

Imaging
Plain radiographs in septic arthritis are usually normal early in
the course of the infection, but baseline films should be obtained
to look for evidence of other diseases and contiguous osteomyelitis. Radiographs often show nonspecific changes in inflammatory
arthritis, including periarticular osteopenia, joint effusion, soft tissue swelling, and joint space loss. In more advanced infections,
periosteal reaction, marginal or central erosions, and destruction

of septic arthritis. Dislocation or subluxation of the femoral head

using technetium 99m is often done in children to identify an associated metaphyseal osteomyelitis or avascular necrosis of the femoral

### Treatment

Treatment of septic arthritis must begin immediately after the
clinical evaluation is complete and all appropriate cultures are
taken. A serious clinical suspicion of a joint infection warrants
the initiation of empiric antibiotic therapy before culture confirmation is available. Delays in treatment allow the infection to
become more established in the joint and permanently damage
the articular cartilage. Untreated, there is the opportunity for the
joint infection to spread to other body sites via the hematogenous
route and become more widespread and more difficult to cure.
The principles of treatment of an infected joint, whether natural or prosthetic, follow those of treatment of an infected body
cavity in which antibiotics must be used in conjunction with
adequate drainage of the infected closed space. The clinical circumstances and the preliminary laboratory data aid the selection
of antibiotic agents. Host factors, any extra-articular sites of infection, and the Gram-stained smear of the synovial fluid are the best
early guides for the antibiotic agents with which to start.

It is generally accepted that prompt and adequate drainage of the
septic joint is essential to decrease the risks of substantial loss of
articular function; however, the best approach to drain the joint
remains controversial.
From retrospective studies, daily aspiration of an infected
joint showed better functional outcome than open surgical
drainage, although the former had higher overall mortality.
An explanation for higher mortality could be the higher comorbid conditions of patients who had daily aspirations than the
ones who were more fit and underwent open surgical drainage.
If the synovial fluid cell count and PMN percentage decrease
with successive aspiration, the anti-microbial therapy is probably
effective.
If needle aspiration is technically difficult (as in the
hip or the shoulder) or does not provide thorough drainage of the
joint, the joint effusion does not resolve promptly, if sterilization
of the joint fluid is delayed, the infected joint is already damaged
by pre-existing rheumatoid disease, or infected synovial tissue
or bone needs debridement, surgical drainage should be considered sooner rather than later.
Arthroscopy is emerging
as an alternative to arthrotomy with the advantage of reduced
surgical morbidity. Wound healing is faster, and rehabilitation
time is shortened.
A retrospective analysis from the United
Kingdom suggested that most patients can be treated medically
(with repeated aspirations) and do not require surgical drainage
(either by arthroscopy or arthrotomy).
Although statistically
not significant, medical therapy resulted in more complete cure
and less deterioration of functional status at time of discharge.
Another study, which included 20 adults with native hip joint
septic arthritis, concluded that symptom duration, especially if it
is longer than 3 weeks before presentation, was a statistically significant predictor of the need for excision arthroplasty.
These
results highlight the need for careful case selection for surgical
intervention.
Durations of anti-microbial therapy ranging from 2 weeks
to 6 weeks is recommended for nongonococcal septic arthritis,
depending on the type, sensitivity of the microorganism, and
presence of contiguous osteomyelitis. The duration of antibiotic
administration can be 2 weeks for uncomplicated infection by
susceptible microorganisms or 4 to 6 weeks for more extensive
infection in an immunocompromised host or in the setting of
abnormal joints (e.g., severe OA). Prospective data on the optimal
duration of antibiotic treatment for native-joint septic arthritis is
scant. A single-center, randomized, unblinded, noninferiority trial
found that microbiological recurrences and antibiotic-associated
adverse events were similar with 2 weeks of anti-microbial therapy
post-surgical drainage in nonimmunocompromised adults with
nongonococcal septic arthritis involving the hand and wrist compared with 4 weeks. Over 80% of the patients enrolled in this
study had small joint involvement (of the hand, wrist, and metatarsal joints), arising most commonly through direct, traumatic
inoculation; this contrasts with most cases of septic arthritis that
involve large joints, arise from hematogenous seeding of the vascular synovial membrane during bacteremia, and affect patients
that are older and have more comorbidities. These differences in
the host and pathogenesis were noted in an earlier retrospective
analysis of hand and wrist septic arthritis that also described high
cure rates with a short duration of treatment (median total antibiotic treatment duration of 14 days).
At present, generalization to most septic arthritis patients is difficult, but further studies
are underway (SHASAR, NCT0371692) that may support the
efficacy and safety of shorter courses of anti-microbial therapy in
other groups of septic arthritis patients.

### Gonococcal Arthritis

With the increase in the incidence of sexually transmitted infections in the United States,
Neisseria gonorrhoeae
infections are
increasing. There was a 63% increase in gonococcal infections in
young individuals in 2014–2018.
Women are typically asymptomatic with primary infection in contrast to men.
N. gonorrhoeae
is the most common cause of monoarticular septic arthritis in
sexually active young adults, historically more common in women
than in men. The spectrum of gonococcal disease includes genitourinary infection, proctitis, pharyngitis/tonsillitis, conjunctivitis,
and bacteremia with disseminated infection causing dermatitis,
tenosynovitis, septic arthritis, and rarely endocarditis and meningitis.
If untreated, disseminated infection occurs. Risk factors
such as pregnancy, intrauterine devices, pelvic surgeries, SLE, and
other complement deficiencies have been associated with disseminated infection. According to the Surveillance for Disseminated
Gonococcal Infection, Active Bacterial Core surveillance (ABCs)
system in the United States from 2015 to 2019, additional risk
factors were identified that might represent the initiation of a
shift in epidemiology with increased numbers of male individuals
infected, people with HIV, and drug or alcohol abuse.
The mechanism of infection in gonococcal arthritis is through
hematogenous seeding.
N. gonorrhoeae
colonizes the mucosal

membranes in the pharynx and urogenital tract and has multiple
virulence factors that allow it to adhere to the cell membrane,
invade the cells, and compete with the microbiota for nutrients
to survive. These virulence factors interact with the host immune
system, especially the complement system, through two important mechanisms: binding and inactivating the complement cascade avoiding opsonization and binding to the factor H through
lipooligosaccharide and porin attempting to protect itself from
identification as a pathogen by the complement system. Another
mechanism of survival is modulating apoptosis and the inflammation cascade to survive inside the macrophages and polarizing
macrophages to impair T cell production
(see
e-Table 112.2
).
Patients with terminal complement deficiencies, which affect the
complement membrane attack complex (MAC), are at increased
risk of
Neisseria
spp. infections. Up to 13% of patients with disseminated disease are diagnosed with a complement deficiency,
according to a recent study from Canada.
Certain drugs such
as eculizumab and ravulizumab (indicated for hemolytic uremic
syndrome, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria, and myasthenia gravis) selectively bind to complement protein C5, inhibiting
cleavage to C5a and C5b. They prevent terminal complex C5b-9
generation and MAC formation, hence increasing the risk of
Neisseria
spp. infections and dissemination (see
e-Fig. 112.3
).
It
is recommended that patients receiving eculizumab get routine
testing for sexually transmitted infections and penicillin as prophylaxis for
N. meningitidis
given the life-threatening nature of
this infection. Because of increasing anti-microbial resistance, prophylaxis for gonorrhea has not yet been established by guidelines;
however, there are ongoing trials and increasing data regarding the
potential benefits of using doxycycline for post-exposure prophylaxis in high-risk populations. The risk of dissemination persists
for up to 3 months after discontinuation of the drug due to its
long half-life.
Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI) occurs after cellular
invasion by bacteria, resulting in transcytosis of the epithelium. It
can occur 3 weeks after a genitourinary or pharyngeal infection,
with the site of infection playing a role in the risk of bacteremia.
Pharyngeal gonococcal infection is usually asymptomatic; therefore patients may go untreated for weeks, increasing the risk of
dissemination. The same concept is considered for asymptomatic
women. Menstruation and the post-partum period also increase
the risk of DGI due to changes in the vaginal pH and cervical
mucus. The clinical presentation of DGI usually involves the
skin and the joints (especially knees, elbows, and distal joints)
and rarely presents with cardiovascular or neurologic complications (see
e-Fig. 112.4
). Recognizing clinical findings of DGI will
aid with the diagnosis of a patient with
Neisseria
spp. infection
presenting with fever, arthritis, tenosynovitis, vesiculo-papular
or pustular rash, or migratory polyarthralgia. Gonococcal infections do not develop immunity; therefore individuals are at risk of
recurrent infections with reexposure.
The two most common presentations observed are arthritisdermatitis syndrome and localized septic arthritis, with absent
skin lesions on the latter. The diagnosis is based on clinical presentation and isolation or detection of the organism from sterile
sites. It is important to obtain blood cultures, synovial fluid for
analysis, and cultures or molecular testing (PCR or nucleic acid
amplification test). It is also recommended to obtain samples from
other nonsterile or mucosal sites (pharynx, urethra, cervix, and
anorectum) for cultures and or molecular identification in cases of
DGI. Cultures from the skin have an extremely low yield and are
not recommended. The yield of blood cultures is higher in cases
of arthritis-dermatitis syndrome (positive in about 30%–50% of
the cases). Possible explanations for low sensitivity could be that
patients may have a lower burden of bacteria in the bloodstream
or the fastidious nature of this organism.
The appearance of
N. gonorrhoeae
on Gram stain is unique,
intra-cellular gram-negative diplococci (see
e-Fig. 112.5
). Gram
stain cannot differentiate among species or pathogenic versus
commensal species. When isolated from sterile sites, however, it is
considered a pathogen. With molecular testing methods, identification is more likely, even if the organism does not grow on cultures. The main benefit of cultures relies on the ability to perform
anti-microbial susceptibility testing. Synovial fluid evaluation is
essential for the diagnosis of gonococcal arthritis. The appearance of the fluid tends to be less purulent compared with other
bacterial infections, and the WBC count on synovial fluid ranges
from 30,000 to 50,000/mm
. Synovial fluid cultures are positive
in about 50% of cases of gonococcal arthritis. Molecular testing is
an important aid for diagnosis in cases of culture-negative arthritis
with high suspicion of gonococcal infection. Given the fastidious
growth of this bacteria, it is recommended to inoculate aerobic
and anaerobic blood culture bottles with synovial fluid (10–15
mL) or to place the specimen on conventional broth and solid
media to increase the yield of the cultures.
Other serum markers
such as CRP and ESR are usually elevated. In cases of negative
cultures, NGS should be considered (see “Diagnosis” section).

### Treatment

The drug of choice for gonococcal arthritis in children and adults
is ceftriaxone. IV antibiotic therapy is recommended for a total of
7 days with the option of transitioning to oral therapy if susceptibility testing is available. Anti-microbial susceptibility testing is
important given the increase in anti-microbial resistance. Recently
the Surveillance for Disseminated Gonococcal Infections, ABCs
system evaluated, from 2015 to 2019, the anti-microbial susceptibility of
N. gonorrhoeae
isolated from cases in California and
Georgia. Most isolates were susceptible to azithromycin, cefixime,
and ceftriaxone.
There is, however, an increase in resistance to
ciprofloxacin (33%), penicillin (38%), and tetracycline (24%).
The emergence of multidrug-resistant gonococci is a major concern, especially the FC
clone. It is resistant to ceftriaxone and
has decreased susceptibility to azithromycin. In England resistance of
N. gonorrhoeae
to azithromycin increased from 4.7% to
8.7% between 2013 and 2020. Ceftriaxone resistance remained
low, although did increase from 0.3% to 1.4%. Ciprofloxacin and
tetracycline resistance had a high percentage of 44% and 65%,
respectively. Cefixime remains highly effective.
For patients with
gonococcal arthritis with cardiovascular complications, such as
endocarditis, the duration of therapy is 4 weeks (IV therapy) while
for central nervous system (CNS) infection (meningitis), the
duration of therapy is 2 weeks. With newer diagnostic techniques
and the ability to detect genes associated with resistance, antibiotic therapy could be transitioned to the oral route to shorten the
length of stay for hospitalized patients with DGI without cardiac
or CNS complications.

## Prosthetic Joint Infections

Total joint replacement for advanced arthritis is one of the major
advances in medicine in the 20th century and continues to improve
in the 21st century. Infection of prosthetic joints is an uncommon
but devastating complication of joint replacement surgery. More
than 800,000 total knee arthroplasties (TKAs) and 450,000 total
hip arthroplasties (THAs) were done in the United States in 2020.
Arthroplasties are increasing, and projections are that nearly 4 million arthroplasties (3.5 million TKAs and 572,000 THAs) will be
done each year in the United States by 2030. In addition to hip
and knee arthroplasties, shoulder, elbow, and ankle replacements
are becoming more common, particularly in patients with RA.
The U.S. infection rate of arthroplasties is about 1% for knees and
hips. I U.S. infection rate is higher for elbow (3.3%) and lower
for shoulder (1.3%) arthroplasties compared with hip and knee
arthroplasties.
Infection rates are much higher in patients
undergoing revision of a prosthetic joint compared with primary
arthroplasty (3% for hips and 6% for knees).
The risk of infection is approximately twofold higher in patients with RA compared with those with OA.
The risk of PJI is related to many factors. The most important
risks for infection include (1) a diagnosis of osteonecrosis (hazard ratio [HR] 3.65), (2) a diagnosis of post-traumatic arthritis
(HR 3.23), (3) male sex (HR 1.89), (4) an American Society of
Anesthesiologists score of 3 or more (HR 1.65), (5) body mass
index (BMI) of 35 or more (HR 1.47), and (6) diabetes mellitus (HR 1.28). Surgical risk factors included quadriceps-release
exposure (HR 4.76) and the use of antibiotic-laden cement (HR
1.53).
Certain patient populations are at increased risk of infection because of comorbid conditions (e.g., diabetes mellitus and
RA). Other risk factors include long operative time, use of disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs, and smoking
(see

Orthopedic implants adversely affect host defenses. Prosthetic
devices impair opsonic activity and diminish the ability of neutrophils to kill bacteria. PMN leukocytes release lysosomal enzymes
and superoxide into the area surrounding the prosthesis, resulting
in tissue damage and local devascularization. Phagocytes may be
focused on removal of the foreign body such that fewer cells are
available to fight infection. Finally, polymethylmethacrylate bone
cement can inhibit neutrophil and complement functions, and the
heat produced by the polymerization of polymethylmethacrylate
can damage adjacent cortical bone and result in a devascularized
necrotic area, which is ideal for bacterial growth. After implantation,
prosthetic joints are immediately coated by host proteins, including albumin, fibrinogen, and fibronectin.
S. aureus

numerous host protein-binding receptors (MSCRAMMs), is a
common pathogen in infection of prosthetic joints. Patients with a
prosthetic joint in whom

bacteremia develops have about
a one in three chance of experiencing an infection of the implant.
Another phenomenon crucial to development of infection is
the ability of organisms to form biofilms on the surface of the
prosthetic device. A biofilm is defined as an assemblage of microbial cells that is irreversibly associated with a surface and enclosed
in a matrix of primarily polysaccharide material.
Organisms
grow on indwelling medical devices, potable water system pipes,
and living tissues.

PJIs are divided into early onset (<3 months after placement), delayed (3–24 months post-surgery), and late onset (>24
months after placement). Early and delayed infections are usually related to surgical contamination at the time of the implantation, whereas late infections usually result from hematogenous
seeding of the joint. Most infections occur within the first 2
years of implantation. Because of its high virulence,
S. aureus
accounts for most early and late infections. Small-colony variants of
S. aureus
may be responsible for persistent and recurrent
infections of prosthetic implants.
These subspecies of
S. aureus
are difficult to treat because they grow slowly and are relatively
resistant to cell wall–active anti-microbial agents, anti-folate
drugs (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole), and aminoglycosides.
There is some concern that the practice of using gentamicinimpregnated polymethylmethacrylate beads to treat PJIs may
select for the small-colony variant species.
Delayed infections are usually caused by less virulent microorganisms such
as coagulase-negative staphylococci and
C. acnes.
Because these
low-virulence organisms are common skin contaminants, it is
important to interpret culture results carefully.
Clinically, the most common symptom in patients with PJI
is pain of the affected joint. Differentiating pain from mechanical loosening of the prosthesis from pain related to infection can
be difficult. Typically, a patient with mechanical loosening but
no infection has pain only with motion, whereas a patient with
infection experiences pain at rest and with motion. Warmth at the
implant site, effusion, erythema, and fever are frequently associated with early and late, but not delayed, infections. This difference in clinical presentation likely represents the virulence of the
most common organisms associated with the three categories of
infection.

### Diagnosis

The presence of a sinus tract with purulent discharge suggests
involvement of the implant and is an indication for removal of
the prosthesis. In the absence of a sinus tract communicating
with the prosthesis, the diagnosis of PJI rests upon a constellation
of clinical features, serum and synovial fluid laboratory parameters, microbiological identification, histologic interpretation, and
intraoperative findings. Different organizations have developed
criteria to aid in the diagnosis of PJI, including most recently
the 2018 International Consensus Meeting (ICM) criteria (see
e-Fig. 112.7
),
the 2019 American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons guidelines, and the 2021 European Bone and Joint Society
(EBJIS) criteria. The EBJIS criteria for diagnosis of PJI have been
revised and are shown in
Fig. 112.4
.
These definitions were compared in a single-center retrospective study,
which found that the EBJIS definition in the preoperative setting had better performance in predicting definitive
diagnosis after surgery, with fewer patients having an inconclusive diagnosis, compared with the 2013 Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA)
and 2018 ICM definitions. Although
recommendations vary somewhat across different guidelines
in the specific laboratory evidence supportive of PJI, generally
serum ESR and CRP remain first-line tools in the pre-operative
setting.
The diagnosis of PJI can be challenging, particularly if there
was prior exposure to antibiotic therapy. Specimens that have been
studied and are adequate for diagnosis include serum, synovial
fluid, sonicate fluid, and periprosthetic tissue. Swabs of the joint
have low sensitivity and should be avoided.
Serum
Biomarkers that are useful from serum are WBC count, ESR,
CRP, IL-6, procalcitonin, TNF, and more recently, D-dimer. These
markers have different sensitivity and specificity (see
Table 112.4
).
Most recent studies have shown that even though these markers
can be elevated after surgery, IL-6 returns to normal within a few
days after the surgery compared with CRP and ESR, which may
remain elevated for 3 weeks and months, respectively. Although
ESR and CRP are often elevated in chronic inflammatory conditions, such as RA, limited evidence suggests that no difference
exists in the thresholds of ESR, CRP, or synovial fluid WBC count
with differential to diagnose PJI in patients with and without
inflammatory arthropathies.
Other challenges to the interpretation of systemic biomarkers in suspected PJI arise in the setting of
prior anti-microbial use
and infection with low-virulence microorganisms that elicit less inflammation.
Serum procalcitonin can be a useful marker with a specificity
of 90% shown in a recent meta-analysis using a cutoff of >0.5 ng/
mL, although sensitivity was low at 46%. Sensitivity was further
improved when a cutoff of >0.2 ng/mL was used.
D-dimer, which
is a product of fibrin degradation, has been shown to be elevated
in infectious processes and to be a predictor of poor outcomes.
The first prospective study that reported high sensitivity (89%)
and specificity (93%) of serum D-dimer in PJIs was published by
one group.
The serum D-dimer test has promise as a marker for
the diagnosis of PJI, although its utility is limited in patients with
coagulation disorders and inflammatory arthritis. In the absence
of these conditions, it is one of the most effective serum markers
for the detection of PJI, although it is difficult to determine the
optimal cutoff.
While D-dimer has been included as a minor
criterion in the 2018 ICM definition of PJI, additional investigation is required to establish a threshold for its use in the acute setting and further refine its role in the diagnosis of PJI.
Synovial Fluid
Synovial fluid WBC counts of >3000/mm
is part of the Musculoskeletal Infection Society (MSIS) criteria for PJI. Leukocyte
esterase is also included in those criteria, although the test strips
have not been evaluated for synovial fluid. Other important markers in synovial fluid are IL-6 and alpha-defensin; the latter is an
anti-microbial peptide produced by neutrophils, which has been
studied as an important marker of PJI in synovial fluid. Intraoperative alpha defensin with values above 5.2 mg/mL had a sensitivity and specificity >95%,
and CRP at values >9.5 mg/L has
a sensitivity of 85% and a specificity of 95%.
The final diagnosis is made with the identification of a pathogen
through cultures or molecular testing. For PJIs, five to six samples
(tissue, not swabs) from periprosthetic tissue should be collected
for cultures. Per MSIS criteria, at least two specimens with positive cultures, with the same organism, is sufficient evidence of PJI.

Serial plain radiographs may be helpful; the presence of subperiosteal bone growth and transcortical sinus tracts is specific

Medical therapy of patients with PJIs is challenging. Organisms existing in biofilms are much more resistant to anti-microbial
agents for several reasons. Most anti-microbial agents penetrate
the biofilm layer adequately but do not inhibit growth of bacteria
within the biofilm matrix. Biofilm-associated organisms, including the small-colony variants of
S. aureus
, grow much slower (i.e.,
are in stationary-phase growth) than organisms in suspension. As
a result, anti-microbial agents such as vancomycin, penicillins,
and cephalosporins, which act on rapidly dividing organisms, are
not effective in treating device-related infections.
Rifampin and
fluoroquinolones (particularly moxifloxacin) may be more effective because they are active against organisms in the stationary
phase of growth.
IDSA guidelines recommend rifampin in
combination with another agent for the treatment of rifampinsusceptible strains of
S. aureus
.
There is evidence that clinical
outcomes are improved in patients receiving rifampin, although
the data are based on retrospective analysis.
The role of
newer anti-staphylococcal antibiotics—such as linezolid, daptomycin, telavancin, ceftaroline, dalbavancin, oritavancin, and tigecycline—is unclear at this time.
There is some evidence that
oritavancin may be active against biofilm-associated small-colony
variants of
S. aureus.
Treatment of late PJIs is complex. In most patients, effective
therapy requires a combination of antibiotics with the removal of
the orthopedic device. Failure to remove the infected prosthesis is
frequently associated with a high rate of relapse, probably related
to biofilm formation on the orthopedic implant. Removal of the
joint prosthesis, debridement of infected bone, and placement of
a new prosthesis during the same operation have been associated
with a high rate of recurrence of infection, but studies indicate
that single-stage revision and debridement with retention of the
prosthesis may be effective in certain situations.
Patients
who have had symptoms of pain and swelling of the joint for less
than 8 days or less than 3 weeks
and who have a stable prosthesis with little soft tissue damage and no sinus tract are candidates
for debridement, antibiotics, and implant retention (DAIR) if the
pre-operative synovial fluid cultures are negative or if the cultures
grow an easily treatable organism (see
Fig. 112.5
).
The treatment of choice for most patients is a two-stage process
,
involving removal of the infected prosthesis and debridement of
infected bone, stabilization of the joint by using an antibiotic-impregnated methyl-methacrylate spacer, and 6 weeks of IV antibiotics (first
stage), followed by reimplantation of a second orthopedic implant
(second stage). Using this approach, the success rate is approximately
72% to 95%; the success rate is slightly higher for hip versus knee
arthroplasties. Risk factors for treatment failure include prior joint
revision, presence of lymphedema with infection, RA, presence of a
sinus tract, and infection with MRSA.
Rarely, antibiotic treatment
is continued indefinitely in a patient in whom the risk of removing
the infected prosthesis is too great, the prosthesis is not loose, and the
organism responsible for the infection can be reasonably suppressed
by the use of an oral antibiotic agent.

### Prevention

There is consensus that pre-operative evaluation of a patient for
occult infection, such as periodontal disease, is warranted, and
corrective steps to eradicate any infection are essential before joint
replacement. The role of antibiotic prophylaxis to prevent late PJI
before diagnostic or therapeutic procedures that lead to transient
bacteremia, especially dental treatment, has been controversial. Two
case-controlled studies determined that there was no increased risk
of infection of prosthetic joints following low- or high-risk dental
procedures.
A large case-crossover study including 9427 latePJI hospital admissions in England, where anti-microbial prophylaxis has never been recommended, found no temporal relationship
between invasive dental procedures and subsequent development of
late PJI.

diseases, chronic kidney disease, diabetes, smoking, nasal colonization with MSSA or MRSA, and obesity with BMI
≥
35 kg/
m
.
In a multicenter, randomized controlled trial with at least
2 years of follow-up of patients undergoing two-stage THA or
TKA revision for infection, patients who received microorganismdirected oral anti-microbial therapy for 3 months post-operatively
were found to have fewer joint infections than those who did
not.
While promising, further research is necessary before this
practice becomes the standard of care.

## Conclusion

Bacterial arthritis is a serious condition in patients with native
joints or prosthetic joints. The number of patients with prosthetic
joints is expected to reach 4 million by 2030. As such, there will be
an increase in the number of PJIs. The increasing use of biologic
agents in the field of rheumatology and other internal medicine
areas will inevitably result in an increase in the number of infections in patients with native joints. There are new molecular testing modalities that will likely improve the ability to make quick
and timely diagnoses in patients with bacterial arthritis. It will
be incumbent upon all specialties (internists, hospitalists, rheumatologists, orthopedic surgeons, infectious diseases, and microbiologists) to collaborate to make management of patients with
bacterial arthritis a seamless process.
