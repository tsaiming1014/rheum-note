KEY POINTS

> Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)–associated arthritis is a member of

> the spondyloarthritis (SpA) family of disorders.

> Several hypotheses have been formed to explain how gut inflammation

> may be linked to joint inflammation: (1) dysbiosis in the gut

> may lead to systemic inflammation; (2) there may be shared

> genetics that could explain physiologic aberrations in differing

> tissues; (3) overexuberant Th17 pathway activation as a result of

> dysbiosis, genetic abnormalities, or another mechanism leading to

> inflammation in susceptible tissues; or (4) translocation of activated

> inflammatory cells from the gut to the joint.

> Previous classification of IBD-associated arthritis includes “type I” and

> “type II” categories, but it is better classified as axial or peripheral

> SpA. As such, Assessment of Spondyloarthritis International Society

> classification criteria for axial SpA and peripheral SpA can be helpful

> in guiding diagnosis.

> IBD-associated arthritis has similar manifestations to other SpA family

> disorders and may include axial or peripheral disease including SpA

> features such as enthesitis and dactylitis. Uveitis and psoriasis can

> also occur.

> The treatment of IBD-associated arthritis includes therapies typically

> used for SpA but depends on whether the patient has Crohn’s

> disease or ulcerative colitis.

> Certain therapies often prescribed for SpA do not have effectiveness for

> IBD (e.g., etanercept) and some may worsen it (e.g., secukinumab).

> Similarly, some therapies with efficacy in IBD (e.g., ustekinumab)

> do not have efficacy for axial disease. Thus the activity of IBD and

> inflammatory arthritis, and other extra-articular and extraintestinal

> manifestations, must be considered in therapy selection, and these

> decisions should be made in collaboration with a gastroenterologist.

## Introduction

In recent years, there has been increasing recognition of the role
of the gut in the pathophysiology of systemic diseases. In particular, a growing body of literature supports the association between
gut pathology and various rheumatic diseases, in particular joint
disease. Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)–associated arthritis
(sometimes referred to as enteropathic arthritis) is one such disease
that exhibits a clear relationship between the gut and joints. In
this chapter, we give an overview of IBD-associated arthritis and
discuss the pathophysiology of the gut mucosa and the gut microbiome in relation to both spondyloarthritis (SpA) and IBD. This
is followed by a discussion of the clinical manifestations and treatment of IBD-associated arthritis. We then address other arthropathies associated with gut pathology.

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease–Associated Arthritis

The spondyloarthritidies are a group of inflammatory arthritidies
with shared clinical features such as axial spondylitis and sacroiliitis, peripheral inflammatory arthritis (often asymmetric but
sometimes a symmetric, rheumatoid arthritis [RA]–like presentation), enthesitis, and dactylitis.
The spondyloarthritidies include
ankylosing spondylitis (AS), psoriatic arthritis (PsA), reactive
arthritis (ReA), and IBD-associated arthritis. Additionally, synovitis, acne, pustulosis, hyperostosis, and osteitis (SAPHO) syndrome and Bechet’s disease share some features with SpA.
Peripheral arthritis in patients with IBD was first described
in 1929 by investigators.
They also described a propensity for
arthritis to flare with exacerbations of colitis. For several decades,
patients with this condition were often classified as having RA.
Then, at the end of the 1950s multiple authors described sacroiliitis in patients with IBD.
In 1964 the American Rheumatism
Association classified arthritis associated with IBD as an independent clinical condition.
In 1976 Wright and Moll included
enteroarthritis as part of the SpA group of diseases.
Beyond the typical relationship between IBD and clinical
syndromes that occur in SpA, asymptomatic gut inflammation
is relatively common in these diseases. One cohort analysis of

65 patients with SpA found evidence of asymptomatic microscopic
bowel inflammation in 46% of patients.
Another study found
that one-third of patients with AS and PsA have microscopic gut
inflammation but no signs or symptoms of IBD. Similarly, among
patients with IBD, asymptomatic sacroiliitis is frequently discovered on pelvis imaging performed for assessment of the patient’s
IBD.
To date, relatively little is known about IBD-associated arthritis,
and it remains an understudied disease entity. Additionally, there
is widespread disagreement about how to classify IBD-associated
arthritis and its place in the context of other enteropathic arthropathies. In this chapter, we provide an overview of what is known
and where disagreement lies or where little data exist. We examine
the pathophysiology of SpA and IBD and where these two diseases
intersect. Additionally, we examine the current treatment modalities available and the contemporary clinical management approach.

### Maintaining Balance in the Gut

The mucosal barrier plays an important role in maintaining gut
homeostasis. The mucous layer (secreted by goblet cells) provides
a physical and chemical barrier. One of the critical roles of this
barrier is to keep the gut microbes at a safe distance from intestinal
epithelial cells (IECs).
Within the mucus are anti-microbial peptides secreted by Paneth cells and immunoglobulin (Ig) A secreted
by plasma cells to support homeostasis. Immune cells within the
lamina propria aid in immunosurveillance. In a normal state, all
of these components exist in harmony. In other words, there is a
state of immune tolerance: epithelial cells secrete interleukin (IL)-
33, T-regulatory cells secrete IL-10, and retinoic acid receptorrelated orphan receptorγ
t+ innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) secrete
IL-22. ILCs play a particularly important role in maintaining this
balance; lack or dysfunction of these cells leads to dysbiosis. Additionally, the balance between Th17 cells and T-regulatory cells also
seems to be important for maintaining homeostasis.

### Barrier Disruption

The mucus layer of the gut is an important physical and chemical barrier. Disruption of the mucosal barrier or protein polymorphisms associated with perturbation of the mucous layer are
associated with altered immune responses in the gut and stimulate
more wide-reaching altered immunity. Dysregulated mucus production has been reported in the intestine of both Crohn’s disease
and SpA.
When barrier injury occurs, microbes are given direct
access to the cellular microenvironment, penetrating through the
mucus layer. It remains unclear if injury to the layer must first
occur or whether certain microbes or dysbiosis cause this injury;
both are plausible pathways. Germ-free mice, however, do not
develop microscopic or gross colonic inflammation; thus microbes
are required for this process to occur. Exposed to microbes from
the other side of the mucosal barrier, IECs then launch an inflammatory response, sending out a “danger signal” by secreting IL-1
and IL-6. Subsequently, dendritic cells secrete IL-12 and IL-23,
stimulating Th1 and Th17 responses. This leads to the secretion
of IL-17 and interferon (IFN)
γ
. B cells may also begin to secrete
commensal specific IgG. Taken together, these factors may result
in local tissue damage (i.e., IBD or subclinical gut inflammation).

### Dysbiosis in Inflammatory Bowel Disease and Spondyloarthritis

In IBD, there is a decrease in gut diversity and specifically a loss of
microbes that may normally promote gut homeostasis.
Microbes
that induce inflammation, such as
Prevotellaceae
, may be increased.
Similar alterations in the gut microbiome have been observed
in patients with SpA.
Patients with axial SpA, compared with
controls, have been found to have associated increases in numerous bacterial taxa, including
Ruminococcus gnavus
and the genus
Dialister
,
that correlated with SpA disease activity in patients with
bowel inflammation. Genetic risk such as human leukocyte antigen
(HLA)-B27 may partly explain changes in bacterial diversity.

### From the Gut to the Joint

Why might inflammation or immune activation in the gut lead
to synovitis and/or enthesitis? A number of potential mechanisms
and several theories exist. First, there may simply be shared genetics with similar environmental triggers. Next, increased intestinal
permeability allows for exposure of microbial antigens to mucosal
immune cells. This may then lead to accumulation of microbial
antigens systemically, which then stimulate joint inflammation.
Alternatively, exposure of the mucosal immune cells to microbial
antigens may lead to a systemic inflammatory response that triggers inflammatory arthritis. Finally, another hypothesis is that
immune activation leads to translocation of inflammatory cells
from the gut to synovium, suggested by studies showing both
increased
shared macrophages and T cells in the gut and joint,
affinity of mucosal T cells for synovial proteins,
and expansion
of T cells within the circulation, joint, and eye sharing receptors

Shared Genetics
HLA-B27 positivity is present in 50% to 60% of patients with
spondylitis associated with IBD. However, the association is not
as strong with peripheral arthritis associated with IBD.
The role
of HLA-B27 has been postulated for many years to contribute
to development of both gut inflammation and joint inflammation, in part because of the cell’s dysfunction when encountering an intra-cellular pathogen.
Instead of loading the protein
for antigen presentation appropriately, the clunky HLA-B27 can
form homodimers leading to an unfolded protein response and
ultimately an inflammatory “danger” response (IL-1, IL-6, TNF,
IL-23, and IL-17 production) or autophagy (resulting in a similar
inflammatory cascade).
Studies in HLA-B27 rat models suggest that microbes induce inflammatory changes similar to those
observed in subjects with IBD. Additionally, in healthy patients,
differences in microbiota between HLA-B27 positive and negative
siblings have been observed, suggesting a role for genetic background on gut microbiota composition.
Dysbiosis may occur
as the result of infections (i.e.,
Campylobacter
and other infections
long associated with ReA) or antibiotics. Finally, recent data support a long-held hypothesis that HLA-B27 can present bacterial peptides that trigger T cell cross-reactivity with those in host
tissues.

## Clinical Manifestations and Diagnosis of Inflammatory Bowel Disease–Associated Arthritis

### Introduction

Inflammatory arthritis is the most common extraintestinal
manifestation of IBD.
IBD-associated arthritis occurs in both
Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC).
Overall, the

prevalence of inflammatory arthritis in IBD patients ranges widely,
though generally from 13% to 33%.
Patients with IBD-associated inflammatory arthritis share many of the features of SpA.
Most epidemiologic studies have historically broken IBD-associated arthritis into peripheral and axial disease. For example, a
meta-analysis found that in patients with IBD, the prevalence of
AS, sacroiliitis, and peripheral arthritis were 3%, 10%, and 13%,
respectively.
However, the prevalence of axial disease may be
higher due to the presence of asymptomatic sacroiliitis, which is
frequently not brought to the attention of the provider treating
bowel disease.
Conversely, among patients with SpA, the prevalence of IBD is lower. One large meta-analysis found that IBD
occurs in approximately 6.8% of patients with AS.
Because both IBD and SpA often have an insidious onset, it
can sometimes be difficult to determine which process started
first. Given the overlap among these conditions, the nomenclature
becomes challenging. Is it IBD-associated arthritis or SpA with
IBD? Most use the term
IBD-arthritis
to refer to patients who first
developed IBD or in whom the IBD drives therapy. Overall, the
pathophysiology is currently presumed to be similar, and thus the
terminology differences are likely not important or meaningful.
Regardless of the term used, the two processes commonly coexist. In a study of the extraintestinal manifestations in the Swiss
Inflammatory Bowel Disease Cohort, peripheral arthritis and
axial disease were present prior to the diagnosis of IBD in 20%
and 40% of patients, respectively.
One population-based study
found that in AS patients, the highest risk of developing IBD was
within the first year of diagnosis.
Certain characteristics of bowel
inflammation are correlated with more active joint disease. For
example, arthritis is more likely to occur in patients with large
bowel disease and in patients with abscesses, pseudomembranous
polyposis, and perianal disease.

### Clinical Manifestations

Joint disease in IBD-associated arthritis resembles that seen in
other types of SpA and includes inflammatory arthritis (peripheral, axial, or both), enthesitis, and dactylitis. Any combination
of these manifestations can be seen, as commonly occurs in other
types of SpA. In addition, other extraintestinal manifestations of
IBD and extra-articular manifestations of SpA, such as uveitis,
erythema nodosum, and pyoderma gangrenosum, can be seen in
affected patients.
Peripheral arthritis is among the more common manifestations of IBD-associated arthritis. The peripheral arthritis seen
in patients with IBD was historically categorized into two phenotypes: type I and type II. This classification schema first arose
from investigators at the Oxford IBD clinic.
Published in 1998,
their study was a review of case notes of all the patients with IBD
seen in their clinic, including 976 patients with UC and 483 with
CD. They noticed a pattern among their patients and categorized
them as having either pauciarticular (type I) or polyarticular (type
II) arthritis (

joint distributions, and frequency of other complications to further characterize each type.
This schema has since become the
standard in discussions about IBD-associated peripheral arthritis,
particularly among gastroenterologists, and as such is frequently
cited in the literature. The distinction between type I and type
II arthritis is relevant for an understanding of the literature, and
therefore we describe the two types in more detail. However, it
should be noted that this classification scheme is no longer used

Axial disease seen in IBD-associated arthritis is similar to that
seen in SpA in general. It can range from asymptomatic sacroiliitis,

to inflammatory low back pain with or without sacroiliitis, to true
AS. Symptoms include inflammatory low back pain and buttock
pain. Patients usually describe back pain that has an insidious
onset. Morning stiffness is a prominent feature, and pain is usually worse with rest and relieved with movement. Symptoms can
be intermittent. Patients may describe awakening toward the later
portion of the night due to back pain. Other important features
include the persistence of symptoms for greater than 3 months
and onset before the age of 45. It has traditionally been thought
that axial disease activity is not associated with bowel disease activity and that flares occur independently. However, some studies
have suggested an association between AS clinical activity scores
and the development of CD, as well as with disease activity scores
in CD.
Another study found that patients with IBD who
develop axial SpA are more likely to have a chronic and active
IBD disease course.
Surgical bowel resection has no impact on
axial disease.
Enthesitis and dactylitis are common and are signature features
of SpA. Enthesitis can occur at the Achilles, plantar fascia, patellar tendon, lateral and medial epicondyles, and a variety of other
enthesitis sites throughout the body. Enthesitis is clinically identified by putting pressure on the site of the tendon insertion and
eliciting pain (these clinical measures may have moderate sensitivity but low specificity); enthesitis can also be measured using
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or ultrasound.
Erosions
and/or new bone formation may occur at the site of enthesitis.
Dactylitis is inflammatory swelling of fingers or toes caused by
tenosynovitis and full-thickness inflammation of many of the tissues in the digits. Dactylitis and enthesitis occur less frequently in
IBD-associated arthritis than other types of SpA, such as PsA.
However, one study suggested dactylitis may be more common
than previously thought in patients with IBD-associated arthritis,
with a prevalence of 15% in one cohort.
In this study, dactylitis
was more common in IBD patients with CD and in those with
peripheral arthritis.
SAPHO syndrome is another musculoskeletal disorder that
is associated with IBD. SAPHO is sometimes thought of as part
of the SpA family and there does seem to be an association with
HLA-B27.
One review found that IBD was present in 4.8% of
patients with SAPHO, while SAPHO was present in 0.2% of
patients with IBD.
For a more detailed discussion of SAPHO,
please refer to Chapter 104.
Finally, hypertrophic osteoarthropathy is another type of joint
disease seen in IBD.
Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy is an excessive proliferation of skin and bone at distal parts of the extremities. Periosteal proliferation and synovitis can occur in late stages.
Patients can develop bulbous deformity at the tips of the digits.
These deformities are a poor prognostic sign for bowel disease
severity in IBD patients.
Hypertrophic osteoarthropathy is similarly associated with uncoupling of osteoclast and osteoblast activity and new bone formation, both key features in AS.

### Screening for Spondyloarthritis in Patients With Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Because of the increasing recognition that earlier identification
and treatment of SpA leads to improved outcomes, screening for
SpA in patients with IBD has received increasing attention. A few
screening tools have recently been developed. There are several
questionnaires that have been developed that may be useful tools
to assist in the early detection of inflammatory back pain and/or
AxSpA in patients with IBD.

## Assessment and Monitoring of Disease Activity

Activity levels of both joint and bowel disease should be monitored
regularly and objectively in patients with IBD-associated arthritis.
For the purpose of this discussion, we focus on monitoring
joint disease. However, the level of bowel disease activity should
always be considered when making decisions about treatment.
Selecting the best therapy for these patients is often challenging,
and partnership between a rheumatologist and gastroenterologist
is critical.
In general, as in other types of SpA, monitoring should objectively record the patient’s clinical status and this measure should
be followed at each visit.
Measures that are used in SpA in general are useful in measuring the activity of IBD-associated arthritis. Such measures include the Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease
Activity Score (ASDAS), the Bath Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease
Activity Index (BASDAI), and a more recently developed patient
reported outcome measure, the ASAS Health Index (ASAS HI).
Please refer to Chapter 76 for a more detailed description of measuring disease activity in patients with SpA. Patient-reported outcomes (such as BASDAI and ASAS HI) are also useful measures
that can be monitored longitudinally and are often more practical
in the clinical setting. The Routine Assessment of Patient Index
Data (RAPID3) is a commonly used patient-reported outcome
measure used across diseases in United States practices that was
found to correlate well with BASDAI in the assessment of disease
activity in patients with AS
and PsA.
Recent studies have investigated additional methods that
may help specifically monitor disease activity in patients with
IBD-associated arthritis, though these are not currently used in
the clinical setting. The use of fecal biomarkers, such as calprotectin, are promising noninvasive tests for the evaluation of disease
activity and mucosal healing in patients with IBD.
They may
also have a role in assessing arthritis and for predicting disease
course. In one study, elevated fecal calprotectin accurately identified SpA patients with bowel inflammation and more joint inflammation on MRI, and patients with higher baseline levels had a
better response of their arthritis to treatment with TNF inhibitors.
However, available evidence is mixed. Some studies have
shown calprotectin levels to be an independent marker for radiographic spinal progression in patients with AxSpA, whereas others
have found no association between calprotectin level and measures
of clinical disease activity in AS patients.
Capsule endoscopy (a procedure in which the patient swallows
a tiny camera that records images of the entire length of the GI
tract) was shown to be useful for the detection of both CD and
subclinical small-bowel inflammation in patients with AS and may
be more sensitive than traditional colonoscopy due to the ability
to view areas not picked up by colonoscopy.
However, it should
be noted that we understand little about the role of subclinical
bowel inflammation in AS patients, and many would argue there
is little to be gained from detecting and monitoring subclinical
disease until further evidence is available.
There are also other novel biomarkers under development such
as sclerostin,
serum levels of beta-2-microglobulin,
and more.
However, these potential biomarkers are still in their infancy and
require more study in patients with both IBD and various forms
of inflammatory arthritis.

## Management

The management of IBD-associated arthritis should focus on
the well-being of patients and their disease manifestations. Ideally there should be a multidisciplinary treatment team in which
patients are co-managed by a rheumatologist and a gastroenterologist. Patients have shorter delays in diagnosis and better outcomes
of both bowel and joint symptoms when this strategy is used.
When deciding on a treatment strategy, it is important to consider
the pattern of arthritis and the type of IBD (UC vs. CD) and its
severity. Some treatments are useful for all of the various manifestations of IBD-associated arthritis, whereas others are effective for
only specific aspects of the disease.
Furthermore, the doses may
be different for IBD as opposed to AxSpA or PsA.
In general, treatment modalities are similar to those used in
SpA (Chapters 76 and 78). We walk through each of the therapies
here and in
Table 79.4
. The use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs (NSAIDs) as a first step in treatment is controversial and is
discussed in more detail in the next section. The introduction of
small molecules/synthetic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs
may be used for peripheral arthritis (and sometimes dactylitis or
enthesitis), whereas axial disease will require a TNF inhibitor if
initial treatment with NSAIDs has been unsuccessful. Physical
therapy is an important part of management and should be recommended to all patients with axial disease
and may also be
beneficial to those with peripheral disease.

### Role of Glucocorticoids

The use and effectiveness of glucocorticoids in IBD-associated
arthritis is similar to that in other forms of SpA, recognizing also

that glucocorticoids may be used for the management of IBD.
The distribution of joint disease should dictate whether or not glucocorticoids are considered. Intra-articular glucocorticoids have a
role in the treatment of oligoarticular peripheral arthritis. As in
other types of SpA, systemic glucocorticoids may have a role in
severe flares of peripheral arthritis. If used for this purpose, they
should be given for the shortest possible course at the lowest possible dose to effectively treat the patient.
Their use should be
avoided for axial disease because of their lack of efficacy and risk
for adverse effects.
Glucocorticoids are often required in the management of bowel
disease in patients with CD and UC. Whenever possible, agents
with limited systemic effects (such as budesonide or topical formulations) are used in mild UC or CD. Compared with oral prednisone,
this formulation of glucocorticoid has less systemic side effects and
less effect on bone mineral density.
The efficacy of budesonide
on inflammatory arthritis is less clear. One study suggested it may
be as effective as prednisolone in RA, and another demonstrated
benefit in treating arthralgias in patients with IBD.
For more severe bowel disease, oral prednisone or intravenous
methylprednisolone is sometimes used for induction of remission.
Patients receiving glucocorticoids for management of bowel disease and peripheral arthritis are at risk for high cumulative doses
over time. In such patients, consider screening for osteoporosis,
and refer to the ACR guidelines for bone health management
in the setting of glucocorticoid use.
In addition to the typical
considerations in using glucocorticoids, particular caution should
be considered in children, as glucocorticoids may lead to growth
retardation in children with IBD.

### Oral Therapies

Traditional oral therapies have an important role in the management of IBD-associated arthritis. With regard to joint disease,
they are most useful for the management of peripheral arthritis.
As in other types of SpA, there is little evidence that they are effective in the management of axial disease.
Methotrexate is an immunosuppressant prescribed as both
mono- and combination-immunosuppression in CD.
It is effective for maintenance therapy in CD.
Conversely, the METEOR
trial found that methotrexate did not improve maintenance of
steroid-free remission in UC, although there was a trend toward
increased levels of remission.
No large RCTs of methotrexate
specifically in patients with IBD and peripheral arthritis have been
conducted. A small observational study found that methotrexate
improved joint symptoms in patients with peripheral arthritis
and UC.
Methotrexate has a long history of use for peripheral
arthritis in patients with SpA. However, there are mixed results for
its efficacy in PsA.
Sulfasalazine is sometimes used for the management of IBD.
There is evidence supporting its use for active bowel disease in
UC.
The evidence is not as strong for its effectiveness in CD, but
there is still occasionally a role for its use, particularly in mild disease.
Sulfasalazine has been shown to be effective in the management of peripheral arthritis in patients with SpA. A study conducted
by the Department of Veterans Affairs of over 600 patients with
SpA found that patients with peripheral articular manifestations
treated with sulfasalazine had a 59% response rate (tender/swollen
joints) compared with 43% in the placebo group.
Importantly,
patients with axial disease treated with sulfasalazine had a similar
response rate to the placebo group, and sulfasalazine is no longer
recommended for treatment of axial SpA.
Azathioprine is used for maintaining remission in patients with
IBD, both as mono- and combination-immunosuppression.
Azathioprine is thought to have some effect on peripheral arthritis
in rheumatic diseases, but it is uncommonly used in SpA and has
not been extensively studied.

### TNF Inhibitors

TNF inhibitors are widely used in the management of patients
with IBD-associated arthritis. There is substantial evidence for
their effectiveness in both IBD and SpA. Multiple large RCTs
have demonstrated benefit in SpA patients with both axial and
peripheral joint disease. Please see Chapters 76 and 78 for a more
detailed discussion about TNF inhibitor use in SpA. Infliximab is
effective in moderate-to-severe CD and UC, promoting fistula closure and mucosal healing and sparing the use of steroids.
Adalimumab is also efficacious in inducing and maintaining remission
in moderate-to-severe CD and UC.
Etanercept, on the other
hand, has been shown to be ineffective for CD
and has been
associated with flares of IBD.
One observational study evaluating etanercept in patients with IBD-associated arthritis found
a positive effect on joint disease but none on bowel disease.
This may be related to its differing structure as a fusion protein.
Therefore given the presence of other TNF inhibitors that work
for both inflammatory arthritis and IBD, etanercept is generally
avoided in patients with IBD.
Other TNF inhibitors have
also been approved for use in both IBD and SpA. Certolizumab
has been approved for the treatment of AS, PsA, and CD, whereas
golimumab has been approved for the treatment of AS, PsA, and
UC.
Note that dosing of TNF inhibitors is frequently different for IBD compared with SpA.
Overall, few studies have evaluated treatment effects in patients
with IBD-arthritis specifically. As such, most of the data informing treatment decisions are from trials in IBD and, separately, trials in PsA, AS, or peripheral SpA. One single-arm trial evaluated
response to infliximab among 30 patients with CD and SpA over
a 12-month period, with periodic evaluation by a rheumatologist
and gastroenterologist.
Joint and bowel disease activity scores
significantly improved and were maintained over 12 months of
follow-up. Patients also reported improvements in health-related
quality of life scores. Another study analyzed pooled results of 11
clinical trials of adalimumab in CD that reported an effect on
extraintestinal manifestations.
Over 1200 patients with arthralgias or arthritis were included. Pooled analysis showed a significant and sustained reduction in arthralgias and arthritis.
Biosimilars are now commonly used in patients with both IBD
and SpA in Europe and will increasingly be used as they have now
become available in the United States. Multiple TNF biosimilars have been approved for use in AS, PsA, and IBD. Their use
specifically in IBD-associated arthritis is extrapolated from these
indications. Because of their similarity with the originator drug
and the use under the same indications as the originator drugs,
the etanercept-biosimilar should likewise be avoided in patients
with IBD.

### Other Biologics and Targeted Therapies

Over the last two decades, an increasing number of targeted therapies have become available for the treatment of immune-mediated
diseases, including SpA and IBD. In this section we review some
of the other non-TNF-targeted therapies relevant for the management of IBD-associated arthritis.

Ustekinumab is a human monoclonal antibody that binds to
the p40 subunit of both IL-12 and IL-23. It has been shown to
be effective for psoriasis and PsA.
It is also effective for CD
that is refractory to TNF therapy.
Trials have also demonstrated
efficacy of ustekinumab in UC.
Extrapolating from the data
in PsA, this is an option for patients with IBD-associated arthritis.
Importantly, ustekinumab was not effective for axial disease, so its
use should be limited to those with peripheral arthritis. Additionally, note that the dose for IBD is higher than the dose used for
PsA. The IL-23 inhibitors (IL-23i) guselkumab and risankizumab
have also been approved for PsA and are being tested in CD
and UC. Similarly, dosing is different for these therapies in IBD
and, like ustekinumab, efficacy has not been demonstrated in axial
SpA.
Because of the relative safety of IL-23i, a UC trial recently
tested the efficacy and safety of golimumab plus guselkumab compared with either therapy alone. While not yet part of standard
care, more combination therapy trials are forthcoming, and this
may be a treatment approach in the future.
The Janus kinase inhibitors, specifically tofacitinib and upadacitinib, also have a role in the management of IBD-associated
arthritis. An additional benefit of these therapies is that they are
also affective for axial SpA.
The dose for both therapies is often
higher in the first 8 to 12 weeks of therapy for IBD. Since their
initial approval, concerns have been raised about the increased
risk for cardiovascular disease and malignancy,
particularly in
patients over the age of 65, and may be avoided or used after other
therapy options have been examined in this population.
Some biologics used in SpA are not effective in patients with
IBD. Secukinumab and ixekizumab are human monoclonal
antibodies that bind to IL-17a. Secukinumab was tested for the
management of CD and was found to be ineffective.
Furthermore, long-term extension studies from RCTs of IL-17 inhibitors,
including the newest IL-17a and IL-17f inhibitor bimekizumab
and IL-17 receptor blocker brodalumab, have identified several cases of new IBD or flares of previously quiescent IBD.
Therefore IL-17 inhibitors should be avoided in patients with
IBD. Abatacept, a CTLA-4 immunoglobulin that inhibits T-cell
costimulation, did not demonstrate efficacy for the management
of IBD.
Tocilizumab, a humanized monoclonal antibody
against IL-6, was not effective in SpA and has been associated with
intestinal ulcers and perforation.
Vedolizumab, a monoclonal antibody against integrin
α
4
β
7, is
unique among biologics used to treat IBD because it specifically
targets the GI tract. It has demonstrated efficacy compared with
placebo in both UC and CD.
It was postulated that vedolizumab could improve articular symptoms by treating GI inflammation that may be driving the joint disease. Post-hoc analysis of
the GEMINI trials, which showed the initial efficacy for UC and
CD, showed mild improvement of joint symptoms.
However,
in clinical practice there is not felt to be any benefit of these drugs
for treating inflammatory arthritis. Additionally, other studies
have suggested the possibility for vedolizumab to trigger new cases
of inflammatory arthritis,
possibly in the setting of switching
from a TNF inhibitor to vedolizumab, where the inflammatory
arthritis was controlled on the TNF inhibitor and switching it off
caused reoccurrence or emergence of the symptoms. Ultimately,
given the limited evidence for its effectiveness and the concerns
over triggering inflammatory arthritis, vedolizumab is not routinely used in the management of patients with IBD-associated
arthritis. Natalizumab, a monoclonal antibody against integrin
α
4
β
1, is approved for use in CD and ozanimod, a sphingosine1-phosphate receptor modulator, is approved for use in CD and
UC. Neither of these therapies has data to support use in inflammatory arthritis.

### Probiotics

Probiotics are widely used among patients with IBD. However,
while there is mild evidence for benefit in UC (particularly in
patients with a J pouch and pouchitis), there is no evidence for
their effectiveness in CD.
Probiotics were not effective for SpA
in one small study.
There is currently no evidence to support
their use in patients with IBD-associated arthritis. Likewise, there
was a recent trial examining fecal transplant in PsA to reset the
microbiome that was not found to be effective.

## Other Enteropathic Arthropathies

### Whipple’s Disease

In 1907, George H. Whipple described a 36-year-old patient with
“gradual loss of weight and strength, stools consisting chiefly of
neutral fat and fatty acids, indefinite abdominal signs, and a peculiar multiple arthritis.”
This constellation of symptoms, now
known as Whipple’s disease, is caused by
Tropheryma whipplei
,
a gram-positive bacillus related to Actinomycetes.
T. whipplei
is
ubiquitous in the environment and is often associated with sewage systems. It is transmitted via oral-oral or oral-fecal route and
is therefore more commonly found in areas with poor hygiene.
The cardinal features of Whipple’s disease include arthralgias,
weight loss, diarrhea, and abdominal pain. GI symptoms are often
associated with malabsorption. Symptoms can present over years,
and arthralgias are often the first to manifest. Patients can also
present with fevers, as well as neurologic, cardiac, dermatologic,
and other organ-specific manifestations. The most common articular symptoms are migratory arthralgias of the large joints. Effusion and joint damage are rare. Sacroiliitis has been described.
Given the somewhat similar picture of joint distribution to SpA,
an association between Whipple’s disease and HLA-B27 positivity
has been hypothesized. However, studies have not confirmed this
relationship.
The disease does seem to have a predilection for
those with European ancestry, and an association between HLA
alleles DRB1*13 and DQB1*06 and Whipple’s disease has been
demonstrated in a cohort of 122 European patients.
Diagnosis
usually involves exclusion of other, more common causes of diarrhea, followed by periodic acid–Schiff staining and polymerase
chain reaction testing for the
T. whipplei
organism from involved
sites, such as the GI tract or synovial fluid. Initial approach to
treatment includes intravenous ceftriaxone followed by maintenance therapy with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. Refractory cases have been treated with combination doxycycline and
hydroxychloroquine.

### Brucellosis

Brucellosis is a zoonotic disease transmitted to humans by contact
with fluids from infected animals or from animal-derived food
products. The most common species in the
Brucella
genus to cause
human infection is
Brucella melitensis
. Brucellosis due to
B. melitensis
is the most common zoonosis worldwide.
Endemic areas
include countries in the Mediterranean, Central Asia, China, SubSaharan Africa, and parts of Central and South America, although
new foci of geographic distribution are constantly emerging.
Acute brucellosis is a systemic febrile illness, and symptoms can

include malaise, arthralgia, myalgia, and headache. Focal infection
occurs in about 30% of patients. Of those with localized infection,
articular disease is the most common, occurring in about twothirds of cases.
Articular disease in brucellosis can present as either peripheral arthritis, sacroiliitis, and spondylitis. Affected children most
commonly present with a monoarticular arthritis of the knee or
hip, and adults have a predilection for both peripheral and axial
disease.
Sacroiliitis can be extremely acute and painful.
Complications of brucellar spondylitis, such as epidural abscess,
can occur.
Diagnosis is made either by culture of the organism
from blood or other involved tissue or by serologic testing. Synovial fluid is not particularly helpful in the diagnosis of brucellosis
and synovial fluid culture is rarely positive.
Synovial fluid analysis may reveal an inflammatory fluid, but white blood cell count
rarely exceeds 15,000.
First-line treatment for noncomplicated
brucellosis is doxycycline for 6 weeks, in combination with either
streptomycin for 2 to 3 weeks or rifampin for 6 weeks.
Patients
with spondylitis require longer treatment and should receive at
least 12 weeks of therapy.
They also appear to respond better to
the combination of doxycycline and streptomycin, compared with
doxycycline and rifampin.

### Celiac Disease

Celiac disease is a chronic gluten-sensitive autoimmune disease
of the small intestine that primarily affects genetically susceptible
individuals. It is estimated that 0.5% to 1% of the world’s population is affected by celiac disease.
The disease is strongly associated with the HLA-DQ2 and DQ8 gene loci, and studies suggest
that DQ2- and DQ8-positive individuals have a rate of celiac
disease of 1:7 compared with 1:2518 in patients without genetic
susceptibility.
Patients most commonly present with diarrhea
and foul-smelling and floating stools related to steatorrhea and
may develop signs of malabsorption.
There may be an association between celiac disease and the
development of arthritis, though this remains unclear. One casecontrol study of 200 patients and 40 controls found that arthritis
was present in 26% of patients compared with 7.5% of controls.
Arthritis was peripheral in 19 patients, axial in 15, and an overlap
of both in 18. There have also been case reports of arthritis as the
presenting symptom of celiac disease.
More studies are needed
to better characterize this association. Importantly, patients with
celiac disease are also more prone to metabolic bone disease than
the general population.
Osteoporosis can also be a manifestation of subclinical celiac disease.
Celiac disease is managed by
adhering to a gluten-free diet.

### Bowel-Associated Dermatosis-Arthritis Syndrome

Bowel-associated dermatosis-arthritis syndrome (BADAS) was
first identified in the 1970s in patients who had undergone jejunoileal bypass surgery. At the time it was reported in up to 20% of
patients who underwent the procedure.
Initially called “bypass
arthritis,” it has since been renamed as it can be seen in patients
who have undergone other types of abdominal surgery and in
patients who have conditions causing abnormal gut function,
such as IBD.
BADAS is a neutrophilic dermatosis characterized
by fever and/or flulike symptoms followed by arthralgias, myalgias, and inflammatory skin eruptions.
The articular manifestations are typically an oligoarticular, asymmetric, episodic, and
nonerosive arthritis of the large and small joints. Tenosynovitis and
enthesitis can also occur. The diagnosis is mainly a clinical one in
patients with the typical cutaneous eruptions. Treatment includes
reanastomosis in patients who have undergone bypass surgery;
antibiotics including minocycline, neomycin, and metronidazole;
and symptomatic relief with local or oral glucocorticoids.

### Other Conditions

There are several other conditions that are relevant to mention
in the context of enteropathic arthritis, but their connection
between gut and joint pathology is not as clear. Microscopic
colitis has been associated with various autoimmune conditions.
Poncet’s disease, an arthritis related to tuberculosis, is thought to
have a possible association with HLA-B27. Enterovirus and the
viral hepatidities affect various portions of the GI tract and can at
times be associated with polyarthritis. Pseudomembranous colitis
and specifically
Clostridioides difficile
have been associated with
ReA.
Various parasites that infect the GI tract can be associated
with arthritis.
