## Introduction

The autoinflammatory diseases, often referred to as
periodic fever
syndromes
, comprise rare, sometimes hereditary diseases characterized by inflammatory symptoms such as fever, abdominal pain,
rash, or arthralgia. The term
autoinflammation
, coined in 1999,
reflects that they are the innate counterpart of autoimmune diseases. Innate immunity is considered a first-line defense mechanism against infection and cellular damage. The diseases can be
classified based on their pathophysiological mechanism and differences in phenotype into four main groups: interleukin (IL)-1
driven autoinflammatory diseases, interferonopathies, defects of
ubiquitination, and nuclear factor kappa B (NFκ
B)–associated
autoinflammatory syndromes.
Of these four, the IL-1-driven
autoinflammatory diseases are the best characterized.

## Inflammasomopathies and IL-1-Driven Autoinflammatory Diseases

The term
inflammasomopathy
is reserved for diseases caused by
defects affecting inflammasomes. Inflammasomes are protein
complexes that are part of innate immunity. They function as a
danger sensor. Upon activation of the central component of the
inflammasome by sensing pathogen- or danger-associated molecular patterns, the formation of a multiprotein complex (the inflammasome) is triggered, leading to production of pro-inflammatory
cytokines such as IL-1
β
. Inflammasomes are named after their
central sensing component. Multiple inflammasomes have been
described, and these are named after their central sensing protein,
including the NLRP3, pyrin, NLRP1, NLRP12, and NLRC4
inflammasome.
Chronic or episodical inflammation with fever (
Fig. 99.1
),
abdominal pain, rash, chest pain, musculoskeletal symptoms, and
a clear acute phase response with elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), and serum amyloid A
(SAA) form the main phenotype in IL-1-driven autoinflammatory
diseases.
These diseases include the four classic monogenic autoinflammatory diseases—cryopyrin-associated periodic syndrome,
familial Mediterranean fever, TNF-receptor-associated periodic
syndrome, and mevalonate kinase deficiency—and diseases without a (clear) genetic background, such as systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis/adult-onset Still’s disease and Schnitzler syndrome.

### Cryopyrin-Associated Periodic Syndrome

In the past, three different autoinflammatory diseases were distinguished that are now known to be part of the cryopyrin-associated
periodic syndrome (CAPS) spectrum: familial cold urticaria syndrome, Muckle-Wells syndrome, and neonatal-onset multisystem
inflammatory disease (also known as the chronic infantile neurologic, cutaneous, articular syndrome). After the finding that all
three of these syndromes are caused by variants in the
NLRP3
gene, the division between the three phenotypes has largely been
abandoned. The exact incidence of CAPS is unknown. Its prevalence is estimated at one to two cases per million.
CAPS is an autosomal dominant disease and is characterized
by fever, urticarial skin rash, abdominal pain, myalgia, arthralgia
or arthritis, and conjunctivitis (
Fig. 99.2
). Sterile meningitis, hepatosplenomegaly, or lymphadenopathy may be present. Possible
neurologic symptoms are intellectual disability and papilledema.

### Familial Mediterranean Fever

Familial Mediterranean fever (FMF) is the most prevalent autoinflammatory disease, with more than 10,000 patients affected
worldwide. It occurs primarily in those originating from around
the Mediterranean basin, including Turks, Arabs, Armenians, and
non-Ashkenazi Jews. FMF is caused by pathogenic variants in the
Mediterranean fever
(MEFV)
gene. FMF is an autosomal recessive
hereditary disease because of pseudodominant inheritance due to
consanguinity and a high carrier frequency of
MEFV
variants. The
high carrier frequency is most likely a result of a survival benefit
•

> cryopyrin-associated periodic syndrome (CAPS) showing greatly enlarged

> epiphyses and patella with punctate increased density. (Courtesy Dr. A.M.

> Prieur, Hôpital Necker-Entfants Maladies, Paris.)

in heterozygous carriers during plague outbreaks because of resistance against
Yersinia pestis
.
In 90% of patients with FMF, symptoms start before the age
of 20 years.
The inflammatory attacks usually last 1 to 3 days.
Attack frequency varies widely; 2 to 4 weeks between attacks is
the most common interval (
Fig. 99.1
). The main symptoms of
FMF are serositis (peritonitis, pleuritis, or synovitis) and fever.
Ninety-five percent of patients suffer from abdominal pain. This
may be so severe that it mimics acute abdominal pathology, such
as acute appendicitis. Arthritis is often confined to a single large
joint (knee, wrist, ankle) and may lead to a more protracted course
of the present episode. In approximately 30% of patients, the skin
is affected, most often as erysipelas-like lesions on the shins or feet
(
Fig. 99.5
).
The main long-term complication of FMF is AA amyloidosis.
In historic cohorts, up to 75% of untreated FMF patients developed AA amyloidosis, which mainly affects the kidneys. Accurate
treatment protects against amyloidosis. Recurrent attacks of peritonitis may lead to intraabdominal or pelvic adhesions, which
may lead to bowel obstruction, or subfertility in women.
FMF remains a mainly clinical diagnosis. The 1997 Livneh criteria are still the most commonly used (
Table 99.3
).
Some patients fulfilling the Livneh criteria do not have genetic
confirmation: in 30% of cases only a single or no pathogenic
variant is detected after exome sequencing.
Even in absence of
genetic confirmation, treatment with colchicine is warranted in
every symptomatic patient with fitting ancestry if the diagnostic
criteria are fulfilled, to prevent against AA amyloidosis.
Etiology and Pathogenesis
The discovery of the genetic background of FMF dates back to
1997, when two groups independently from each other discovered, until then, an unknown gene on the short arm of chromosome 6, consisting of 10 exons.
FMF is predominantly an
autosomal recessive disorder, caused by pathogenic variants in
the
MEFV
gene, which encodes the protein pyrin.
FMF is not
the only disease associated with
MEFV
variants; pyrin-associated
autoinflammation with neutrophilic dermatosis is an autosomal
dominant hereditary autoinflammatory disease, and distinguishes
itself from FMF by the presence of neutrophilic dermatosis.
An autosomal dominant disease with pathogenic
MEFV
variants
has also been described.

### Mevalonate Kinase Deficiency

Mevalonate kinase deficiency (MKD) has a spectrum ranging from
mild to severe disease. On the mild end we find the hyperimmunoglobulin (Ig) D and periodic fever syndrome,
characterized by
early-onset febrile episodes of 5 to 7 days (
Fig. 99.1
), accompanied
by rigors, lymphadenopathy, abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea,
arthralgia, myalgia, and skin rash (
Fig. 99.7
). Hepatosplenomegaly
may be present. Typically, the first episode is triggered by childhood
vaccination. Other triggers are minor trauma, surgery, and physical
or emotional stress.
On the severe end are patients with mevalonic
aciduria. These patients suffer from antenatal or neonatal inflammation, hydrops fetalis, psychomotor retardation, ataxia, failure to thrive,
cataracts, and facial dysmorphia. Although the term hyperIgD syndrome implies elevated Ig D in all patients, IgD may be persistently
normal
and may be elevated in other autoinflammatory diseases,
including FMF. IgA is elevated in 80% of MKD patients.
MKD is confirmed by the finding of pathogenic variants on
both alleles of the mevalonate kinase gene
(MVK)
. In cases with a
fitting phenotype and no or only heterozygous variants, or when
genetic testing is unavailable, urinary mevalonic acid excretion
may aid in the diagnosis. Elevated urinary mevalonic aciduria
excretion during an inflammatory episode has a positive predictive value of 71%, and the negative predictive value is 98%.
Measurement of mevalonate kinase activity requires cell culture
and is only done for research purposes. Variants in
MVK
are not
exclusive to MKD. Pathogenic variants have also been described
in noninflammatory conditions, including retinitis pigmentosa
and certain forms of porokeratosis.
The exact prevalence of MKD is unknown. Over 100 patients
have been reported in the literature. The majority of MKD
patients is of White ancestry, possibly because of a founder effect.
In the Netherlands, the carrier frequency of heterozygous mevalonate kinase mutations is 1 in 153.

In patients with a mild MKD phenotype, prognosis is generally
good. In some patients, fever episodes become less frequent and
less severe after adolescence.
Amyloidosis is rare in MKD.
A
potentially life-threatening complication of MKD is macrophage
activation syndrome (MAS).
Etiology and Pathogenesis
The pathogenic mechanism of MKD has not been fully elucidated.
MKD-associated pathogenic variants in
MVK
lead to constantly
decreased activity of the enzyme mevalonate kinase (MVK). MVK
is part of the sterole and nonsterole isoprenoid pathway. It is located
directly downstream from 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaryl–coenzyme A reductase. Its function is to phosphorylate mevalonic acid.
Mutations in
MKV
lead to a shortage of isoprenoids and defects in
prenylation.
This effect increases upon elevations in core body
temperature, which may explain why many patients with MKD
experience their first attack after childhood vaccination, and (viral)
infections are well-known triggers for MKD attacks.
Defects in prenylation of GTPases, including those of the Rho/Rac/Rap families,
lead to increased IL-1
β
secretion.
In vitro studies of cells from
MKD patients show that these cells are deficient in autophagy and
have mitochondrial dysfunction and a reduced ability to clear antioxidant stress, leading to hypersecretion of IL-1
β
.
Also, mevalonate can train the innate immune system, and cells of MKD patients
showed a constitutive trained immunity phenotype
that could contribute to the attacks of sterile inflammation in these patients.

### Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis and Adult-Onset Still’s Disease

Systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis (sJIA) and adult-onset Still’s disease (AOSD) are relative prevalent, nonhereditary, nonmonogenic,
IL-1-driven autoinflammatory diseases. They are by some considered
two forms of the same disease with the major distinguishing factor
being age of onset: sJIA’s onset is in childhood, and patients with
AOSD are adults. sJIA/AOSD is characterized by chronic or episodic inflammation with fever, skin rash, arthralgia or arthritis, and

hepatosplenomegaly. Other frequent manifestations are pharyngitis,
myalgia, pericarditis, pleuritis, and pulmonary infiltrates. Biochemically, an acute phase response is present, accompanied by increased
serum ferritin. The disease’s course may be fulminant; sJIA/AOSD is
diagnosed on clinical criteria. The Yamaguchi criteria
(
Table 99.4
)
and Fautrel criteria
(
Table 99.5
) are most commonly used.
sJIA/AOSD has three possible disease courses: monocyclic,
polycyclic, or continuous disease. In mono- and polycyclic disease the duration of an episode is highly variable. Inflammatory
episodes may last for weeks to months. It is impossible to predict
what the disease’s course will be in the individual patient and,
because of this, one should always be aware of possible relapse
after an initial sJIA/AOSD episode. As the disease-free interval
may also be long, tapering of immunosuppressive drugs could be
considered when complete resolution of symptoms is achieved.
Prognosis in general is good, except when complications arise.
Possible life-threatening complications of sJIA/AOSD are myocarditis, acute respiratory distress syndrome, fulminant hepatitis,
MAS, diffuse intravascular coagulation, and thrombotic microangiopathy.
Prompt treatment with high-dose corticosteroids or
IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors is warranted in this case.

Etiology and Pathogenesis
Most of the pathogenesis of AOSD still remains elusive. It is
assumed that the multifactorial pathogenesis of sJIA/AOSD is a
combination of infection, genetics, and environmental triggers.
There may be a role for low-density granulocytes (LDGs) in disease activity.
LDGs are a distinct subpopulation of neutrophils
with pro-inflammatory features that co-sediment with the PBMC
fraction after Ficoll gradient centrifugation and was initially only
described in patients with systemic lupus erythematosus.
LDGs in AOSD show increased IL-6 production and increased
neutrophil extra-cellular trap formation,
which induces IL-1
β
secretion.
Serum levels of N-terminal fragments of gasdermin D
(GSDMD-N) have been found to be elevated in AOSD patients
compared with healthy controls.
GSDMD-N are, in an inflammatory setting, released for pore-forming and subsequent cytokine release.
In MAS, an even further elevation of GSDMD-N
was observed.
GSDMD-N was positively correlated with levels
of ferritin and IL-18,
which are elevated in AOSD. Levels of
pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-10 and IL-37, may correlate with disease activity, although it still needs to be elucidated
how they exactly contribute to the pathogenicity.
Disrupted autophagia may also be one of the defects in AOSD,
as it was found that placenta-specific 8, a protein involved in regulating autophagy, correlated with the expression of IL-1
β
, IL-18,
ferritin, and CRP in monocytes of AOSD patients.

## NF- κ B-Related and/or Aberrant TNF Activity Autoinflammatory Syndromes

The NFκ
B complex is an intricately regulated intracytoplasmatic signal complex that conducts signals from cell surface and intra-cellular
danger sensors. After activation of NFκ
B, transcription factors move
from the complex to the nucleus, where expression of pro-inflammatory genes is promoted. Many proteins play a role in NFκ
B-induced
inflammation via sensing, inhibition, and ubiquitination. Prominent
symptoms of NFκ
B dysregulation are fever and systemic inflammation. Granuloma formation may be present, and these diseases may
show signs that may also be seen in Behcet’s disease. NFκ
B and TNF
are closely linked. TNF signaling can activate NFκ
B, and activation
of NFκ
B induces transcription of TNF (
Table 99.9
).

## Diagnostic Approach to Autoinflammatory Diseases

The mainstay of the diagnosis of autoinflammatory diseases is
clinical assessment, including a detailed medical and family history, and preferably at least one observation of the patient during
an inflammatory episode. Especially physical examination (full
body, with specific focus on ocular symptoms, skin rash, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, and arthritis) may show otherwise absent clinical clues during an inflammatory episode. In
most cases, and inevitably in IL-1-driven autoinflammatory diseases, a clear acute phase response including elevated ESR, CRP,
and SAA are present during an inflammatory episode, while these
are normal in between episodes. Leukocytosis with neutrophilia
may be present. Specific laboratory tests—including serum ferritin, IgD and IgA, serum protein electrophoresis, or urine mevalonic acid excretion—may aid in the diagnosis in specific cases.
Upregulation of type-1 IFNs can be measured with a type-1 IFN
signature assay, but this test is nonexclusive and not widely available. It is important to keep in mind that more common causes
of longstanding or recurrent inflammation, such as chronic infections and malignancy, should be excluded, especially in absence of
genetic confirmation of an autoinflammatory disease. Bone marrow biopsy may be helpful when VEXAS is considered. Genetic
testing may aid in the diagnosis. Whole-exome sequencing gene
panels, which include multiple genes associated with autoinflammatory diseases, are becoming the standard for genetic testing,
at least in the Western world. Upon suspicion of an IL-1-driven
autoinflammatory disease, a therapeutic trial with short-acting
specific IL-1 inhibiting drugs can be considered. If a quick and
evident response appears after initiation of these drugs, the pivotal
role of IL-1 in the pathophysiology is proven.

### Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids may be effective in many autoinflammatory
syndromes, but the benefit may be only partial or patients may
relapse during treatment. In most cases, high doses are needed to
maintain a beneficial effect. With the major side effects of longterm high-dose steroid therapy, including infections, weight gain,
Cushing syndrome, diabetes, osteoporosis, and neuropsychiatric
symptoms, and after the introduction of IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors,
corticosteroids are used less and less for autoinflammatory syndromes. For VEXAS and interferonopathies, corticosteroids may
be the initial choice of therapy.

### Colchicine

Colchicine remains the first-choice treatment for patients with
FMF. It prevents inflammatory attacks completely in 60% to
75% of patients, and it significantly reduces the number of
attacks in an additional 20% to 30%.
Doses of colchicine may
range between 0.5 and 3.0 mg per day,
depending on effect and
tolerance. The main side effects of colchicine are gastrointestinal
complaints. Diarrhea and nausea often resolve with dose reduction. Severe side effects, including myopathy, neuropathy, and
leukopenia, are rare and occur primarily in patients with renal or
hepatic impairment or after combination with strong CYP3A4 or
Pgp inhibitors, such as macrolides, azoles, or protease inhibitors.
Long-term use of colchicine may be associated with vitamin B
deficiency. In the past, the use of colchicine has been associated
with infertility. In animal models a teratogenic effect has been
reported. With increasing evidence, it is now known that the use
of colchicine is not associated with a significant adverse effect on
sperm production or function.
Colchicine is considered safe to
use during pregnancy (including the first trimester) and lactation.
As cessation of therapy may lead to increased frequency
of severity of FMF attacks, which may lead to decreased fertility because of intraabdominal adhesions and adverse pregnancy
outcome (e.g., miscarriage or premature birth due to peritonitis), treatment should not be interrupted in fertile women.
When colchicine resistance is present, IL-1 inhibition can be
added to colchicine.
We advise not to stop colchicine, as it still
remains the only drug with proven protection against secondary
AA amyloidosis.

### IL-1 Inhibition

Since the introduction of IL-1 inhibitors, these have become the
mainstay of treatment for many patients with IL-1-driven autoinflammatory syndromes. Three different IL-1 inhibiting drugs
are available: the IL-1 receptor antagonist anakinra, the monoclonal antibody against IL-1
β
canakinumab, and the IL-1 receptor
fusion protein rilonacept. Availability and reimbursement of these
three differ largely between countries. At the moment, there is
evidence of effectivity for IL-1 inhibition in all IL-1-driven autoinflammatory diseases, including CAPS, colchicine-resistant FMF,
TRAPS, MKD, and AOSD/sJIA.
In many of these patients,
IL-1 inhibition will be the treatment of first choice. In other autoinflammatory diseases, a therapeutic trial with IL-1 inhibition
may be considered when there is evidence that the IL-1 pathway is
involved in the pathogenesis based on clinical symptoms and biochemical markers such as elevated CRP or SAA.
For anakinra,
standard dose is 100 mg once daily, and for canakinumab the dose
may range between 150 mg every 8 weeks or less to 300 mg every
4 weeks.

IL-6 inhibition is mostly used in patients with IL-1-driven autoinflammatory diseases that do not sufficiently respond to IL-1
inhibition. Tocilizumab, the IL-6 receptor antagonist, is the most
commonly used IL-6 inhibitor. There is evidence for its effect in
CAPS, FMF, TRAPS, MKD, and AOSD/sJIA, among others.
Tocilizumab is available as subcutaneous injections and intravenous infusions. The main side effect is infection. It is important to
note that IL-6 inhibition blocks the formation of CRP and SAA,

diseases has mainly become one of clinical judgment and pattern
