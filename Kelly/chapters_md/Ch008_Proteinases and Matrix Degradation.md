# Proteinases and Matrix Degradation

YOSHIFUMI ITOH

KEY POINTS

> Proteinases are classified into aspartic, cysteine, serine, and

> metalloproteinases according to their catalytic mechanism.

> Due to the optimal acidic pH and intra-cellular localization within

> lysosomes, most aspartic proteinases and cysteine proteinases are

> involved in the intra-cellular degradation of extra-cellular matrix

> (ECM) components.

> Serine and metalloproteinases function optimally at neutral pH

> and play a central role in the extra-cellular degradation of ECM

> macromolecules.

> ECM-degrading metalloproteinases are mainly the MMP (matrix

> metalloproteinase) and ADAMTS (a disintegrin and metalloproteinase

> with thrombospondin motifs) gene families.

> Most endogenous proteinase inhibitors are proteinase class–specific,

> whereas

> α

> 2 macroglobulin inhibits the activities of all classes of

> proteinase.

> The activities of ECM-degrading proteinases at the local tissues

> are regulated by the balance between the proteinases and their

> inhibitors, which may be determined by the production rates of

> proteinases and inhibitors, their secretion, activation of proenzymes,

> and cell surface anchoring and recycling systems of the activated

> proteinases.

> ProMMPs (precursors of MMPs) are activated via the extra-cellular,

> intra-cellular, and cell surface activation pathways depending on the

> enzymes.

> Aggrecan and type II collagen, two major ECM components, may be

> degraded in articular cartilage by differential or complementary

> actions of the MMP and ADAMTS species in arthritides.

> In rheumatoid arthritis, articular cartilage is destroyed by proteinases

> that accumulate in synovial fluid, direct contact with proteolytic

> pannus tissue, and proteinases derived from chondrocytes. In

> contrast, bone is resorbed by osteoclasts mainly by the action of

> cathepsin K and MMP-9 under acidic and hypercalcemic conditions

> in subosteoclastic compartments.

> In osteoarthritis, chondrocyte-derived metalloproteinases, including the

> MMP and ADAMTS species, contribute primarily to the breakdown of

> articular cartilage.

## Introduction

The extra-cellular matrix (ECM) plays critical roles in the normal
development and function of the organism by interacting with
cells and supporting tissue and organ architectures. The cell–ECM
interaction regulates various fundamental cellular functions,
including growth, differentiation, apoptosis, and migration. The
turnover and remodeling of ECM are transient and under strict
control in physiologic conditions, and excessive degradation of
ECM components by proteinases causes tissue destruction in
many pathologic conditions. Thus understanding the regulatory
mechanisms of ECM degradation is essential to appreciate the
pathogenesis of these diseases. In rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and
osteoarthritis (OA), the activity of ECM-degrading proteinases is
elevated, destroying joint tissue, including cartilage and bone. It
is becoming more apparent that unbalanced ECM metabolism is
due to the upregulation of proteinase genes and additional factors. This chapter provides up-to-date information about ECMdegrading proteinases and their regulation in RA and OA.

## Extra-cellular Matrix–Degrading Proteinases

ECM is a meshwork of large macromolecules, and thus their degradation is caused by endopeptidases or proteinases that cleave
internal peptide bonds of polypeptide chains. The impact of exopeptidases that cleave only a few amino acids either from the N- or
C-terminus in ECM integrity is unlikely to be significant even if
these enzymes exist. Four classes of proteinases have been implicated in ECM degradation, including aspartic proteinases, cysteine proteinases, serine proteinases, and metalloproteinases, which
are classified according to their catalytic machinery.

### Aspartic Proteinases

Most aspartic proteinases have two aspartic acid residues in their
catalytic sites, where the nucleophile that attacks the scissile peptide bond is an activated water molecule. Mammalian aspartic
proteinases include the digestive enzymes (pepsin and chymosin),
intra-cellular cathepsin D and cathepsin E, and rennin. Among
the proteinases in this group, cathepsin D is the major aspartic
proteinase involved in ECM degradation. It exhibits proteolytic
activity against most substrates, such as aggrecan and collagen
telopeptides, with optimal pH between 3.5 and 5. Because of the
optimal acidic pH and intra-cellular localization within lysosomes,
cathepsin D is likely responsible for the intra-cellular degradation of phagocytosed ECM fragments previously degraded in the
extra-cellular spaces. However, a study on cartilage explant cultures using an aspartic proteinase inhibitor suggests that cathepsin
D secreted extra-cellularly contributes to aggrecan degradation in
articular cartilage.

### Cysteine Proteinases

Cysteine proteinases are endopeptidases in which the nucleophile of the catalytic site is the sulfhydryl group of a cysteine
residue. The ECM-degrading cysteine proteinases include lysosomal cathepsins B, L, S, and K and the calpains (see
Table 8.1
).
Cathepsins B and L function at acidic pH to digest the telopeptide regions of fibrillar types I and II collagen, the nonhelical
regions of types IX and XI collagen, and aggrecan. Cathepsin S
has a similar spectrum of substrates within a broad range of pH
values. Cathepsin K, also previously called cathepsin O, O2, or
X, is a collagenolytic cathepsin that cleaves type I collagen at the
triple helical regions at pH values between 4.5 and 6.6.
This
proteinase also degrades gelatin and osteonectin. Cathepsin K is
highly expressed in human osteoclasts,
and inactivating mutations or deletion of the gene result in an osteopetrotic phenotype
in humans and animals indicating that cathepsin K may play a
crucial role in osteoclast-dependent bone resorption (see later
discussion). Because cathepsins B, L, S, and K are expressed in
the synovium or articular cartilage or both in RA and OA, they
may also be involved in cartilage destruction through degradation of the ECM macromolecules when the local environment
shifts to an acidic condition.
Calpains are Ca
-dependent, papain-like cysteine proteinases ubiquitously distributed among mammalian cells. The
best-characterized members of the calpain superfamily are
μ
-calpain and m-calpain, which are also called conventional
(
μ
-calpain) and classic (m-calpain) calpains.
Calpains are
involved in various pathologic conditions, such as muscular
dystrophy, by acting intra-cellularly. They are present in the
extra-cellular spaces and osteoarthritic synovial fluid and can
degrade aggrecan.

### Serine Proteinases

Serine proteinases require the hydroxyl group of a serine residue to act as the nucleophile that attacks the peptide bond. Serine proteinases include the largest number of proteinases, with
approximately 40 family members. Most serine proteinases can
degrade ECM macromolecules. The major ECM-degrading serine
proteinases in joint tissues are described in the next sections (see
Table 8.1
).
Neutrophil Elastase and Cathepsin G
Neutrophil elastase and cathepsin G are serine proteinases synthesized as precursors in promyelocytes in the bone marrow and
subsequently stored in the azurophil granules of polymorphonuclear leukocytes as active enzymes. Mature leukocytes do not
synthesize elastase, but they mobilize azurophil granules to the
cell surface and release the proteinases in response to various
stimuli. Monocytes have low elastase levels but lose enzyme
expression during differentiation into macrophages. Neutrophil
elastase and cathepsin G are basic glycoproteins with isoelectric
points larger than 9 (neutrophil elastase) and about 12 (cathepsin G). They can be readily trapped in a cartilage matrix with a
negative charge.
Neutrophil elastase and cathepsin G cleave elastin; the telopeptide region of fibrillar collagen types I, II, and III; other collagen
types IV, VI, VIII, IX, X, and XI; and other ECM components
such as fibronectin, laminin, and aggrecan at neutral pH. These
serine proteinases can also be indirectly involved in the breakdown
of ECM by activating the zymogen of matrix metalloproteinases

Mast Cell Chymase and Tryptase
Chymase and tryptase are packaged in secretory granules together
with histamine and other mediators in mast cells, which are part
of the cellular infiltrate in rheumatoid synovium. Chymase is a
chymotrypsin-like proteinase with a broad spectrum of activity

against ECM components such as type VI collagen
and aggrecan. It also activates proMMPs such as proMMP-1, 3, and 9.
Although prochymase is activated intra-cellularly and stored in
granules, its activity in granules is limited at low pH. Hence, this
enzyme becomes fully active only when released extra-cellularly.
Tryptase is a trypsin-like proteinase that degrades type VI collagen
and fibronectin; it also activates proMMP-3.
Plasmin and Plasminogen Activators
Plasminogen is synthesized in the liver and secreted into
the plasma. It can bind to fibrin and cells, and, after activation by plasminogen activators, plasmin readily digests fibrin.
Membrane-bound plasmin degrades many ECM components,
including proteoglycan, fibronectin, type IV collagen, and laminin. Other important functions of plasmin are to initiate the
activation of proMMPs, activate latent cell-associated transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
1, and act as proenzyme convertase. Plasmin is generated by the activation of plasminogen via
plasminogen activators, tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA),
and urokinase-type plasminogen activator (uPA). tPA is synthesized as a proenzyme of 70 kilodaltons (kDa) and is secreted into
the circulating blood primarily by endothelial cells, fibroblasts,
chondrocytes, and tumor cells.
uPA was first purified from
urine as a proenzyme of 54 kDa.
It is converted to the active
form of two chains of 30 kDa and 24 kDa linked by a disulfide
bond. Alternatively, the fully active form of 33 kDa can be generated by plasmin. The expression of uPA is found in various
cells, including invasive cancer cells, migrating keratinocytes,
and activated leukocytes in pathologic situations. Pro-uPA and
two-chain uPA bind to a specific uPA receptor, a glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-anchored glycoprotein expressed on the
cell surface of fibroblasts, macrophages, and tumor cells. Receptor-bound uPA preferentially activates cell membrane–bound
plasminogen into plasmin. Cell membrane–bound plasmin can
activate receptor-bound pro-uPA. Despite the highly restricted
substrate specificity of the plasminogen activators, uPA cleaves
other proteins in vitro, including fibronectin, fibrinogen, diphtheria toxin, and possibly uPA itself.
Kallikreins
Two types of kallikreins, plasma and tissue kallikreins, have been
identified. Plasma kallikrein, with two disulfide-linked chains (36
kDa and 52 kDa), is generated from prokallikrein of 88 kDa by
coagulation factor XIIa or by kallikrein itself. It activates kininogens to bradykinin and activates proMMP-1 and 3.
Tissue kallikrein, synthesized in glandular tissues, releases Lys-bradykinin
from kininogen and activates proMMP-8.

### Metalloproteinases

Like aspartic proteinases, metalloproteinases are endopeptidases
in which a water molecule mediates the nucleophilic attack on a
peptide bond. A divalent metal cation, zinc, activates the water
molecule. Among the metalloproteinases, MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases), which are also designated as matrixins (a subfamily of the metzincin superfamily), are key ECM-degrading,
zinc-dependent endopeptidases (
Table 8.2
). In addition, some
members of the ADAMTS (a disintegrin and metalloproteinase
with thrombospondin motifs) family, which is an MMP-related
gene family within the metzincin family, are also responsible for
the degradation of ECM, such as cartilage proteoglycan (
Table
8.3
). Only a few members of the ADAM (a disintegrin and
metalloproteinase) family have limited activity with ECM components (
Table 8.4
).
Matrix Metalloproteinases
The human MMP family comprises 23 members with MMP
designations (numbered according to a sequential numbering system) and common names coined by the authors of the published
reports (see
Table 8.2
). Based on the biochemical properties provided by the domain structures and on their substrate specificity, these family members are classified into two major subgroups:
secreted-type MMPs and membrane-anchored MMPs. MMP-4,
5, and 6 are excluded from the list because they are identical to
other known MMPs (i.e., MMP-3 and 2). MMP-18 and 22 are
also missing in
Table 8.2
because they are assigned to
Xenopus
collagenase-4 and chicken MMP. Many of the secreted-type MMPs
are composed of three primary domains—the prodomain, catalytic domain, and hemopexin-like domain—that are preceded by
hydrophobic signal peptides (
Fig. 8.1
). The N-terminal prodomain has one unpaired cysteine in the conserved sequence of
PRCGXPD. The cysteine residue in the sequence interacts with
the catalytic zinc atom in the catalytic domain to maintain the
proenzyme in an inactive state by preventing zinc from interacting
with water molecules for the catalysis. Zinc is held at the zincbinding motif HEXGHXXGXXH in the catalytic domain, where
three histidines bind to the catalytic zinc atom. The hemopexinlike domain, which is connected to the catalytic domain by the
proline-rich hinge region, is considered a molecular interaction
interface and plays a role in determining the substrate specificity in some MMPs. In addition to these primary domains, gelatinases have additional insertions of three repeats of a fibronectin
type II domain in the catalytic domain (see
Fig. 8.1
), providing
collagen-binding properties. Matrilysins are the smallest MMP
member lacking hemopexin-like domains. Furin-activated MMPs
contain insertions of a basic amino acid motif of Arg-Xxx-Lys-Arg
(RXKR) at the C-terminus of the prodomain that is recognized
and cleaved by proprotein convertases, including furin (see
Fig.
8.1
). These enzymes are thus secreted to extra-cellular milieus as
an active form. MMP-23 is synthesized as a type II transmembrane type MMP having a type II transmembrane domain in the
N-terminus of the prodomain. It contains an RRRR sequence at
the end of the prodomain that can be cleaved by proprotein convertases during secretion, which makes the enzyme soluble. The
domain structure of MMP-23 is also unique among MMP members as it has a cysteine array and an immunoglobulin-like domain
instead of a hemopexin-like domain (see
Fig. 8.1
).
There are two types of membrane-type MMPs: type-I transmembrane type (MMP-14 to 16 and 24/MT1-, MT2-, MT3-,
and MT5-MMPs) and GPI-anchored type (MMP-17 and 25/
MT4- and MT6-MMPs).
Type I transmembrane-type MMPs
have a stalk region, a transmembrane domain, and a short cytoplasmic tail downstream of the hemopexin domain in addition to
the standard domain composition (prodomain, catalytic domain,
hinge, hemopexin-like domain). The GPI-anchored enzymes are
synthesized with a hydrophobic GPI anchoring signal peptide
sequence following the stalk region at their C-terminus. This GPIanchoring signal peptide is cleaved, and the ectodomain is transferred to the de-novo synthesized GPI moiety by a transamidase in
the endoplasmic reticulum (see
Fig. 8.1
).

## Regulation of Proteinase Activity

The activities of ECM-degrading proteinases in tissues are regulated by different means, including their gene expression, activation of their zymogen forms, and inhibition by their endogenous
inhibitors. Some of these enzymes are also regulated by cell surface
binding, endocytosis, and recycling.

### Gene Expression

Matrix Metalloproteinases
Under physiologic conditions, cells express only limited levels of MMPs or TIMPs in tissues. However, under inflammatory conditions, the expression of the genes encoding these
enzymes is stimulated by cytokines and other factors. Neutrophils synthesize MMP-8 and 9 during their differentiation
and store these enzymes within their granules. Macrophages
upon treatment with lipopolysaccharide or phorbol myristate
acetate (PMA; 12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate) express
MMP-1 and 9, MT1-MMP, and TIMP-1. Tumor cells express
many MMPs, such as MMP-1, 7, 9, 10, and 14 (MT1-MMP),
and TIMP-1, driven predominantly by oncogenic stimuli.
Numerous factors, including cytokines, growth factors, and
chemical and physical stimuli, regulate gene expression of
MMPs and TIMPs in tissue cells. Much information is available for regulators of MMP-1 and 3, which are coordinately
expressed in many cell types upon stimulation with cytokines
and growth factors, factors acting at the cell surface, and
chemical agents (
Table 8.5
). Retinoic acid, TGFβ
, and glucocorticoid suppress the upregulated production of MMP-1 and
3. Similar factors regulate gene expression of MMP-7 and 9,
but the regulation is stricter, and fewer factors modulate their
expression (see
Table 8.5
). MMP-14 expression is upregulated
by PMA, concanavalin A, fibrillar collagen, basic fibroblast
growth factor, and TNF, and glucocorticoids downregulate
it in various cells. TNF and IL-1
α
stimulate osteoarthritic
chondrocytes to express the MMP-14 gene. In contrast to these
MMPs, MMP-2 and TIMP-2 are unique in that factors capable of enhancing the production of MMP-1 and 3 and TIMP-1
are inactive. TIMP-1 expression is enhanced or suppressed in
response to many factors, including cytokines, growth factors, and oncogenic transformation (see
Table 8.5
). Effects of
these stimulatory factors are common to the gene expression of
MMPs, but they are regulated independently. TGFβ
, retinoic
acid, progesterone, and estrogen enhance TIMP-1 expression
in fibroblasts, but they suppress the expression of MMP-1 and
3. Although information about stimulatory and suppressive
factors of TIMP-1, 2, and 3 is available (see
Table 8.5
), factors
controlling the gene expression of TIMP-4 are not well known.
Previous studies have identified the elements in the promoters
of MMPs and TIMPs, which are related to or responsible for
the stimulation or suppression of gene expression with various
factors. The structural characteristics of the promoters generally explain the regulation of their gene expression.

μ

### Inhibition of Proteinases by Endogenous Inhibitors

Endogenous proteinase inhibitors control the activities of proteinases in vivo. The inhibitors are derived from plasma or cells
in the local tissues. Plasma contains several proteinase inhibitors,
with about 10% of all plasma proteins being proteinase inhibitors.
Many of these are proteinase class specific, but
α
2 macroglobulin
inhibits the activities of proteinases from all classes. Major endogenous inhibitors of the ECM degrading proteinases are listed in
Table 8.7
.

α
2 Macroglobulin
α
2 macroglobulin (
α
2M) is a large glycoprotein of 725 kDa that
consists of four identical subunits of 185 kDa that are linked
in pairs by disulfide bonds. Regardless of the proteinase classes,
almost all active proteinases cleave a stretch within the molecule,
the so-called bait region, located near the center of the subunits.
Upon cleavage of the bait region, a conformational change in
α
2M occurs and physically traps the proteinase within a molecular cage, resulting in a proteinase–
α
2M complex. Although the
active site of the proteinase in the complex is free and remains
active, the enzyme is trapped by the arms of
α
2M and is prevented from interacting and degrading large macromolecules.
Besides its function as a proteinase inhibitor,
α
2M may also act
as a carrier protein because it binds to numerous growth factors and cytokines such as platelet-derived growth factor, basic
fibroblast growth factor, TGFβ
, insulin, and IL-1
β
.
α
2M is
synthesized mainly in the liver but also locally by macrophages,
fibroblasts, and adrenocortical cells. The concentration of the
inhibitor in plasma is 250 mg/dL. Because of its large molecular weight, it is not present in noninflammatory synovial fluid.
However, during synovial inflammation,
α
2M penetrates the
joint cavity. Rheumatoid synovial fluid has about the same concentration of the inhibitor as plasma.

### Activation Mechanisms of Matrix Metalloproteinase Zymogens

All MMPs are synthesized as inactive zymogens (proMMPs),
which are kept inactive through an interaction between a cysteine-sulfhydryl group in their conserved sequence PRCGXPD and
the zinc ion at their catalytic site, preventing the interaction of a
water molecule with the catalytic zinc that is essential for the enzymatic reaction. Activation occurs by proteolytic removal of the
prodomain that exposes the catalytic site. There are three pathways
of proMMP activation dependent on the enzymes: extra-cellular
activation, intra-cellular activation, and cell surface activation
(
Fig. 8.2
).
Extra-cellular Activation
Many soluble MMPs (e.g., proMMP-1, 3, 7, 8 to 10, 12, and
13) are secreted in a zymogen form and need to be activated
extra-cellularly. In  vitro, this activation can be initiated by
treatment with nonproteolytic agents that disturb the interaction of the Cys within the PRCGVPD motif in the prodomain
with the catalytic zinc.
Such agents include thiol-modifying
reagents, hypochlorous acid, sodium dodecyl sulfate, chaotropic agents, and physical factors (heat and acid exposure).
Among these, 4-amino phenylmercuric acetate (APMA) has
been used for investigations into the activation mechanism of
proMMPs. Upon APMA treatment, APMA disturbs the interaction of Cys in the PRCGVPD motif and causes proMMP
molecules to undergo autolytic processing at the upstream
region of PRCGVPD to remove a portion of the prodomain.
Since the Cys no longer interacts with the catalytic zinc, the
intermediate form possesses partial activity and processes the
rest of the prodomain intramolecularly to generate a fully active
form. This activation mode is designated as the stepwise activation mechanism. In the case of proMMP-9, APMA treatment
only results in the cleavage of the Ala
-Met
bond upstream
of the conserved sequence retaining the PRCGVPD sequence,
but this intermediate form was found to be fully active. Concerning proMMP-9 activation in  vivo during cerebral ischemia, nitric oxide activates proMMP-9 by S-nitrosylation.
A major activation pathway in vivo is proteolytic activation
that utilizes a similar stepwise activation sequence. Different
proteinases initially attack the stretch of the sequence within
the prodomain (the so-called “bait region”), which is upstream
of the PRCGVPD motif of the domain. This partial removal
of the prodomain disturbs the interaction of the Cys in the
PRCGVPD motif with the catalytic zinc, generating a partially
active intermediate form, followed by autolytic removal of the
rest of the prodomain or by other MMPs.

be attacked by different proteinases, which result in the activation of particular MMPs. Potential activators of proMMPs are

### Endocytosis of ECM-Degrading Metalloproteinases

For MT-MMPs, endocytosis is crucial for their regulation. Some
soluble enzymes are also known to be endocytosed via interaction
with membrane proteins. In general, endocytosis is considered a
step of downregulation, but in some cases, it is necessary for the
enzyme to express its biologic function. It has been shown that
MT1-MMP is endocytosed via both clathrin and caveolae-dependent pathways.
The clathrin-dependent pathway is faster than
the caveolae-dependent pathway, but it was also demonstrated
that clathrin-dependent endocytosis is required for MT1-MMP
to promote cellular migration and invasion.
In the cytoplasmic
domain of MT1-MMP, there is an LLY
motif recognized by
adaptor protein 2, a subunit of clathrin, and it is crucial for the
enzyme to be endocytosed through this mechanism.
Just downstream of LLY
, there is a Cys
that is post-translationally palmitoylated.
Interestingly, this palmitoylation is crucial for the
enzyme to be endocytosed via a clathrin-dependent mechanism
and for its cell migration-promoting effect.
MT1-, MT3-, and
MT5-MMP are endocytosed and recycled back to the cell surface,
and three amino acid motifs of DKV
(MT1-MMP), EWV
(MT3-MMP), and EWV
(MT5-MMP) at the C-terminus
were identified as recycling motifs.
Endocytosis is not only the regulatory mechanism of MTMMPs but also for soluble enzymes. It was discovered that several
ECM-degrading metalloproteinases and inhibitors are endocytosed via low-density lipoprotein receptor–related protein 1 (LRP1). LRP-1 is synthesized as a 600 kDa precursor processed by
proprotein convertases during secretion to the cell surface, resulting in a 515 kDa alpha-chain and 85 kDa beta-chain that are
associated noncovalently. The alpha chain contains four extra-cellular binding regions for various molecules. It binds to extra-cellular molecules, rapidly endocytosed through clathrin-dependent
mechanisms, and molecules bound to LRP-1 are degraded in the
lysosome. LRP-1 is widely expressed in different cell types and
controls extra-cellular levels of numerous biologically active molecules to maintain tissue homeostasis.
Currently, more than 50
ligands have been characterized, including lipoproteins, ECM proteins, growth factors, cell surface receptors, proteinases, proteinase inhibitors, and secreted intra-cellular proteins.
In cartilage,
it was shown that LRP-1 controls the Wnt/b-catenin signaling
pathway by interacting with Frizzled-1
and connective tissue
growth factor (CCN2), and both regulate endochondral ossification and articular cartilage regeneration.
It has been shown that
LRP-1 is also responsible for clearing extra-cellular MMP-2, 9,
and 13; ADAMTS5; and TIMP-1 and 3.
LRP-1 also endocytoses tPA, PAI-1, and uPA, and cathepsin D also bound to the
LRP-1 ectodomain.
Endocytic regulation of ECM degrading
enzymes in arthritic tissues is becoming an important area that
directly influences disease progression, and further investigation
is required.

## Joint Tissue Destruction in Arthritis

Articular cartilage is the primary organ that supports joint function by providing smooth joint movement and a shock-absorbing
function. Cartilage is a unique tissue composed of a large volume
of ECM and a small number of chondrocytes. The major cartilage ECM components are type II collagen and a large proteoglycan called aggrecan (about 45% each). These components play
crucial roles in cartilage function: aggrecan provides compressive
resistance, and collagen provides structure for the architecture of
cartilage. Thus the degradation of these components severely compromises joint function. The major aggrecan-degrading enzymes
are thought to be ADAMTSs, but MMPs also serve this function;
collagen degradation is carried out by collagen-degrading MMPs.

In RA and OA, these two subclasses of metalloproteinases are key

synovial fluid, and by chondrocyte-derived enzymes, whereas, in
OA, it is due to the enzymes produced by chondrocytes.

Since MMPs play a central role in the degradation of ECM
molecules in RA and cancers, many MMP inhibitors have been
developed and subjected to clinical trials.
However, all have
failed due to a lack of efficacy. Consequently, MMP inhibitors
for treating patients with RA and OA have been withdrawn from
phase III trials.
Moreover, in clinical trials for patients with cancer, most of the inhibitors had no definite benefit, and prolonged
or high-dose treatment with broad-spectrum MMP inhibitors
caused unexpected symptoms in the musculoskeletal system, such
as inflammatory polyarthritis,
designated the “musculoskeletal
syndrome.” Their failure is thought to be because of the nature of
active site-directed broad-spectrum inhibitors. In humans, there
are 62 metalloproteinases, and many of these enzymes are essential for normal tissue homeostasis. These small molecule inhibitors
broadly inhibit most of these enzymes, causing unexpected side
effects. Going forward, if this becomes a viable therapeutic alternative, it will be crucial to identify the responsible enzymes for
specific diseases and identify highly selective inhibitors.
There are two potential MMPs that can be targeted in a selective manner and have shown efficacy, at least in an animal model
of arthritis. The first is MMP-13, produced by rheumatoid synovial fibroblasts and chondrocytes upon stimulation with various
inflammatory stimuli, including IL-1, TNF, IL-17, and myeloidrelated proteins (S100A8/S100A9) and oncostatin M.
High
levels of MMP-13 are found in the synovial pannus at the cartilage junction.
There is a small molecule inhibitor highly selective
against MMP-13, and administration of this inhibitor blocked the
development of arthritis in several inflammatory arthritis models,
including the SCID mouse co-implantation model and collageninduced arthritis model.
Another target is MT1-MMP (MMP-14). MT1-MMP is
upregulated in rheumatoid synovial tissue
and is highly
expressed in synovial fibroblasts at the pannus–cartilage junction.
Selective MT1-MMP inhibition by expressing a dominant
negative form of MT1-MMP
or knocking down MT1-MMP
inhibited RASF invasion into cartilage in vitro. Also, the highly
selective MT1-MMP inhibitory antibody DX2400 inhibited cartilage erosion in mice with collagen-induced arthritis.
These
findings suggest that MT1-MMP is the enzyme that mediates
synovial pannus invasion and cartilage erosion. As described earlier,
a selective MMP-13 inhibitor also inhibits cartilage erosion in an
animal model of arthritis.
Interestingly, silencing MMP-13 did
not affect RASF invasion into cartilage in vitro.
Therefore both
MMP-13 and MT1-MMP are involved in cartilage invasion, but
their roles may differ; while MT1-MMP directly promotes RASF
invasion by degrading cartilage collagen, the mechanism through
which MMP-13 promotes invasion is unclear. MT1-MMP, as a
cellular collagenase, also plays an essential role in angiogenesis
and monocyte endothelial transmigration
and thus may also
promote disease through other mechanisms.

### Bone Resorption in Rheumatoid Arthritis

Bone composition consists of 40% organic and 60% inorganic
components. Ninety percent of the organic component in mature
bone is an insoluble, highly cross-linked type I collagen, although
type III and V collagens are also present. Other minor components
in the bone matrix are leucine-rich repeat proteoglycans (decorin
and biglycan) and glycoproteins such as osteopontin, osteonectin
(SPARC), osteocalcin (bone Gla-protein), and thrombospondin.
Bone is resorbed by osteoclasts, and this is commonly observed
at the bare zone, where pannus-like granulation tissue invades the
bone marrow and destroys subchondral bone. Activated osteoclasts
attach to only mineralized bone matrix. This cell–matrix contact is
carried out by
α
v
β
3 integrin of osteoclasts binding to the Arg-Gly
Asp (RGD) sequence of osteopontin in the bone matrix. ECM degradation of the mineralized bone can occur only after demineralization of the bone matrix because proteinases cannot permeate the
matrix components in the mineralized tissues. Matrix degradation
by osteoclasts is performed in the subosteoclastic compartments,
which have acidic (pH 4 to 5) and hypercalcemic (40 to 50 mM
Ca2+) conditions.
Under these conditions, a collagenolytic cysteine proteinase, cathepsin K, is thought to be responsible for bone
resorption because of its collagenolytic activity with a broad pH
optimum and selective expression in osteoclasts and giant cells of
giant cell tumors. Mutations of human cathepsin K are responsible
for pyknodysostosis, an autosomal recessive osteochondrodysplasia
characterized by osteopetrosis and short stature.
Cathepsin K–
deficient mice have a similar phenotype. Despite the importance of
cathepsin K in bone resorption, osteoclastic bone resorption cannot
be completely inhibited by cysteine proteinase inhibitors; it is inhibited similarly by MMP inhibitors.
MMP-9 is highly expressed in
osteoclasts in normal and rheumatoid bones
and giant cells of
giant cell tumors. MMP-9 has telopeptidase activity against soluble
and insoluble type I collagen and strong gelatinolytic activity.
ProMMP-9 can be activated by acid exposure and, when activated, it is proteolytically active under acidic and hypercalcemic
conditions.
MMP-9-deficient mice show a transient disturbance of growth plate development. Cathepsin K and MMP-9
may thus be involved in bone resorption in persons with RA.
Although MT1-MMP is also expressed in osteoclasts in RA,
evidence for its direct involvement in osteoclastic bone resorption
is limited. MMP-7 may be involved in the solubilization of receptor activator of nuclear factorκ
B ligand (RANKL) in the murine
prostate cancer–induced osteolysis model,
but no data are available regarding the osteoclastic bone resorption in RA.
For the treatment of osteoporosis and destructive bone diseases,
bisphosphonates that reduce bone turnover by inhibiting the boneresorbing activity of mature osteoclasts are commonly utilized.
Denosumab, a human monoclonal antibody against RANKL, has
been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration and used
to treat osteoporosis in patients with RA. Cathepsin K inhibitors
were considered promising drugs for the treatment of post-menopausal osteoporosis because the reduction of osteoclast-stimulated
bone formation is less compared with anti–bone resorptive drugs
such as bisphosphonates.
These inhibitors were also expected to
be useful for treating osteoporosis in persons with RA. The drug
went to phase III clinical trial and effectively increased bone densities. However, it also increased the risk of stroke, and the development of this inhibitor was discontinued. A detailed mechanism of
increased incidence of stroke upon cathepsin K inhibition is not
clear, but it is likely to be due to other roles of cathepsin K.

## Conclusion

ECM molecules are crucial structural components for all organs.
They fill the gaps between cells, provide the structure, separate
cellular compartments, provide survival signals, act as scaffolding
and cues for migrating and differentiating cells, and act as a pool
of growth factors. Proteinases play crucial roles in the degradation
of ECM components, as described in this chapter. Excess activities of these proteinases cause ECM degradation, leading to tissue destruction and organ malfunction. RA and OA are diseases
with joint tissue malfunction, and thus inhibition of these proteolytic enzymes may prevent joint damage. However, because of
their physiologic roles, careful consideration of the consequence
of proteinase inhibition is essential. In the 1990s, it was presumed
that MMPs would be good therapeutic targets for cancer and
arthritis. Many pharmaceutical companies developed broad-spectrum, active site-targeting small molecular inhibitors. However,
all clinical trials failed due to lack of efficacy and/or significant
side effects, designated “musculoskeletal syndrome.”
These
MMP inhibitors inhibit most of the 62 metalloendopeptidases in
humans, and it is believed that the broad-spectrum nature of the
inhibitors is the culprit of the failure. Thus it appears crucial that
for clinical efficacy and acceptable toxicity, it will be essential to
identify appropriate target enzymes and develop highly selective
inhibitors.
Further understanding of the biochemical properties, regulatory mechanisms, and pathophysiological roles of
these proteolytic enzymes is thus important not only to deepen
our knowledge but also for the development of novel therapeutic
strategies in the future.
