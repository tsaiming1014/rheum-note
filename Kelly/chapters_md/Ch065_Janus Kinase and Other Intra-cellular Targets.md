## Introduction

Advances in treatment for rheumatoid arthritis (RA) during the
past 25 years have resulted in dramatic improvements in quality
of life for these patients. The development of biologic therapies
targeting molecules involved in the pathogenesis of RA has led to
significant improvements in the signs and symptoms of disease in
as many as 60% to 70% of patients and resulted in remission in
10 to 50% of patients, depending on the definition of remission
and disease duration.
Biologic therapies, however, have limitations. More than half
of patients continue to have active disease and biologics require
either subcutaneous or intravenous administration. These therapies are associated with significant expense and can induce immunogenicity. During the last 30 years, the intra-cellular signaling
pathways involved in signal transduction from the cell surface to
the nucleus after ligand-receptor binding to its receptor have been
identified. This improved understanding of these pathways has
provided opportunities for development of small-molecule therapies that could target these pathways. Interruption of the signaling
cascade has been demonstrated in pre-clinical models to reduce
proinflammatory cytokine production, with improvement in animal models of inflammatory arthritis similar to that seen with biologics.
During the last decade, multiple clinical trials in patients
with inflammatory arthritis that evaluated small molecules targeting specific kinases have been conducted. The majority of these
investigational trials failed because of limited efficacy or significant toxicity. In 2012 tofacitinib, a Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitor,
was approved for RA treatment in the United States and Japan,
and other JAK inhibitors were subsequently approved for RA and
other rheumatic diseases, such as psoriatic arthritis and the spondyloarthritis, and are widely utilized in clinic. JAK inhibitors have
been approved or are in development for other immune-mediated
inflammatory diseases (IMIDs) such as juvenile inflammatory
arthritis, ulcerative colitis, Crohn’s disease, psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, and alopecia areata. This chapter focuses on the various
molecules that are approved therapies for rheumatic diseases or are
presently undergoing active investigation.

## Signal Transduction Pathways

Research during the last 30 years has firmly established that
reversible protein phosphorylation is a fundamental mechanism
of cell signaling.
Protein kinases are the enzymes that catalyze
the transfer of the
γ
phosphate of a purine nucleotide triphosphate (i.e., adenosine triphosphate [ATP] and guanosine triphosphate) to the hydroxyl groups of their protein substrates. A total
of 518 kinases have been identified, with the majority selectively
phosphorylating serine or threonine peptides. Ninety kinases that
phosphorylate tyrosine (protein tyrosine kinases [PTKs]) have
been identified that play a major role in signal transduction. These
tyrosine kinases can exist as receptor tyrosine kinases, such as epidermal growth factor and platelet-derived growth factor, but the
majority of kinases are found intra-cellularly and facilitate signal
transduction by interacting with the intracytoplasmic portion of
the cell membrane receptor after ligand binding. Protein phosphorylation ultimately links membrane events to calcium modulation, cytoskeletal rearrangement, gene transcription, and other
canonical features of lymphocyte action.

activation are linked to protein phosphorylation and are physically
associated with protein kinases (

### JAK Selectivity

Tofacitinib has been demonstrated in in-vitro assays to reversibly
inhibit JAK1, JAK2, and JAK3 and, to a lesser extent, TYK2, with
functional selectivity for JAK1/3 and JAK1/2 signaling versus
JAK2/2. Inhibition of JAK1/3 by tofacitinib will inhibit signaling
for cytokines that bind receptors that utilize the common
γ
-chain
receptor, such as IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21.
Reduction in NK cells has been demonstrated with tofacitinib
treatment.
IL-6 inhibition, which signals through JAK1,
may play a role in the efficacy of the molecule and has adverse
events (AEs) such as neutropenia and hyperlipidemia, which have
been seen with monoclonal antibodies to the IL-6 receptor. IL-17
production by T helper 17 cells is also inhibited.
Tofacitinib,
along with all the other JAK inhibitors, do not block IL-1 or TNF
signaling.
Additional JAK inhibitors have demonstrated in pre-clinical
models to be more specific JAK1 inhibitors (upadacitinib/filgotinib).
Peficitinib has been demonstrated in pre-clinical
studies to inhibit JAK1/3 preferentially but regulatory authorities
consider it to be a pan JAK inhibitor (
Table 65.2
).
The assays
evaluating specificity of the JAK inhibitors vary depending on the
methodology, whether it be biochemical or cellular, which makes
the comparative data difficult to interpret. Additionally, depending on the amount of ATP substrate utilized in the assay or degree
of cytokine stimulation, results may vary.
For example, at the
doses used in the clinic, tofacitinib demonstrates substantial nanomolar inhibitory potency in enzymatic assays against all JAK family kinases except TYK2, but in cellular assays it shows functional
greater specificity for JAK1 and JAK1/3 versus JAK2.
Regulatory agencies now consider tofacitinib, baricitinib, and peficitinib
to be pan-JAK inhibitors. Complicating the issue of JAK selectivity is whether the maximal concentration (Cmax) or the average
drug concentration results in the major impact on JAK inhibition.
For example, at Cmax, JAK selectivity may not exist, whereas at
lower concentrations such as the average concentration, preferential JAK inhibition would be possible. Studies have demonstrated
that clinical profiles of cytokine inhibition for a number of JAK
inhibitors in RA are strikingly similar when efficacious doses are
considered, suggesting possible limited potential for differentiation of these therapeutics based on their JAK pharmacology.
One group did demonstrate differences in in-vitro pharmacologic
profiles with tofacitinib, baricitinib, filgotinib, and upadacitinib
in different cell types.
The relevance of specific JAK inhibition in
these assays to their clinical effectiveness remains unknown.

### JAK Inhibitors for Rheumatoid Arthritis

Multiple JAK inhibitors have been approved as treatment for RA
and other IMIDs in patients failing conventional synthetic diseasemodifying anti-rheumatic drugs (csDMARDS) or biologic therapies (
Table 65.3
). Tofacitinib was approved by the U.S. Food and
Drug Administration (FDA) in 2012, followed by baricitinib in
2017 and upadacitinib in 2019. These therapies are now approved
worldwide. Peficitinib was approved in Japan and South Korea in
2019 and is not approved elsewhere. Filgotinib was approved by
the European Medicines Agency (EMA) and outside of the United
States in 2020. All of the approved JAK inhibitors were evaluated
as combination therapy or monotherapy in similar development
programs, including csDMARD incomplete responders, primarily methotrexate; biologic DMARD (bDMARD) incomplete
responders; and in early RA patients compared with methotrexate.

### JAK Efficacy

The approved JAK inhibitors were evaluated in multiple clinical
trials in RA patients, demonstrating statistically significant efficacy
compared with placebo treated patients. Phase II clinical trials
evaluated tofacitinib at doses of 1 to 30 mg bid as monotherapy or
in combination with methotrexate.
Consistent improvement
in clinical outcomes were seen with doses of 5 mg twice daily or
greater, but toxicity was greater at higher doses. Based on the efficacy and safety profile from the phase II clinical trials, tofacitinib
5 mg twice daily and 10 mg twice daily doses were progressed to
phase III trials. Baricitinib was evaluated at doses of 1, 2, 4, and
8 mg once daily in phase II trials.
Efficacy was similar at 2, 4,
and 8 mg daily with a trend for more safety issues at 8 mg, resulting in the 2 and 4 mg doses progressing to phase III clinical trials.
Upadacitinib was evaluated in immediate-release formulations of
6, 12, and 18 mg bid in phase1/2 trials, based on studies demonstrating similar pharmacokinetics to the 12 and 18 mg bid, and a
15 and 30 mg once daily formulation was evaluated in phase III
clinical trials.
Filgotinib was evaluated in phase I/II clinical trials
at 30, 75, 150, 200, and 300 mg daily or 100 mg bid.
Based
on phase I/II efficacy and safety, the 100 and 200 mg once-daily
doses were evaluated in phase III trials. Peficitinib was evaluated
in phase II trials of RA patients at doses of 50, 100, and 150 mg
daily. Based on results from phase II dose-ranging trials, the 100
mg and 150 mg daily doses were evaluated in phase III.
The phase III clinical trials program designs were similar for
all of the approved JAK inhibitors. All of the trials were global
programs, with the majority of patients enrolled outside of the
United States. The development programs were large, with 2500
to 4000 patients enrolled.
The clinical trials evaluated RA patients with active disease despite methotrexate or csDMARDs, patients with active disease despite receiving
TNF inhibitors, and patients naive to methotrexate. All therapies
were also evaluated as monotherapy in patients who had failed
csDMARDS. The ability of these therapies to slow radiographic
progression was evaluated over 12 months in RA patients receiving
background methotrexate compared with an active comparatoradalimumab. Long-term extension studies to evaluate persistence
of response and to monitor long-term safety issues were included
in all of the development programs.
Tofacitinib (5 and 10 mg bid), baricitinib (4 mg daily), upadacitinib (15 and 30 mg daily), and peficitinib (100 and 150 mg)
in combination with methotrexate or csDMARDs all demonstrated statistically significant improvement compared with placebo in signs and symptoms as measured by the American College
of Rheumatology (ACR) response, the Clinical Disease Activity
Index (CDAI), the Simple Disease Activity Index (SDAI) and
change in disease activity as measured by DAS28. In RA patients
on background methotrexate nonresponsive to TNF inhibitors
tofacitinib (5 and 10 mg bid), baricitinib (2 and 4 mg), and
upadacitinib (15 and 30 mg), filgotinib (100 and 200 mg daily)
demonstrated significant improvement compared with placebo
over 6 months.
In RA patients naive to methotrexate,
tofacitinib monotherapy at 5 and 10 mg bid daily was statistically superior to modest dosed oral methotrexate as determined by
ACR70 response.
Baricitinib 4 mg monotherapy and baricitinib
4 mg in combination with methotrexate was statistically superior
to methotrexate in a DMARD-naive population in improvement
in disease activity as determined by ACR response or change in
DAS28.
In a DMARD-naive population, filgotinib 100/200
mg in combination with methotrexate was statistically superior to
methotrexate, but filgotinib 200 mg monotherapy failed to achieve
statistical superiority in ACR20 response.
Upadacitinib 15 and
30 mg monotherapy was demonstrated to be superior to modest
doses of oral methotrexate monotherapy in DMARD-naive RA
patients by ACR50 response and change in DAS28-CRP <2.6.
Data from two 52-week phase III trials of peficitinib 100/150
mg daily in RA patients with active disease despite methotrexate
or csDMARDs demonstrated statistical superiority in improvement as measured by the ACR20/50/70 and change in DAS28
at week 12.
In one study, open-label etanercept was included
as an active comparator with numerically greater ACR responses
than peficitinib and change in DAS28 and low disease activity state (LDAS)/remission, but no statistical comparison was
conducted.

### JAK Inhibitors Versus Adalimumab

In the development program, tofacitinib was compared with
adalimumab in the methotrexate incomplete responder populations with similar clinical benefit, but the study was not powered
to evaluate statistical difference between the groups.
In a phase
IV study, tofacitinib 5 mg bid plus methotrexate was determined
to be noninferior to adalimumab plus methotrexate in ACR50
response at 6 months.
Although there was a good clinical
response with tofacitinib monotherapy, noninferiority to adalimumab/methotrexate was not achieved in this study.
Baricitinib and upadacitinib were both compared with adalimumab in the methotrexate incomplete responder population.

### Patient-Reported Outcomes

Patient-reported outcomes were evaluated in all of the development programs for the JAK inhibitors. These included the Health
Assessment Questionnaire (HAQ), the 36-Item Short Form
Health Survey (SF36), and measures of patient pain and fatigue.
Significant improvements compared with placebo-treated patients

important difference of 0.22. In comparison to the combination

### Post-marketing Clinical Trials

Several clinical trials have been conducted in RA patients after
approval. Tofacitinib 5 mg bid as monotherapy or in combination
with methotrexate was compared with adalimumab plus methotrexate in patients with active disease despite methotrexate in the
Oral Strategy study.
This study was a 12-month noninferiority
trial with the primary endpoint of ACR50 response at month 6.
A total of 1146 patients were enrolled and ACR50 response was
attained in 147 (38%) of 384 patients with tofacitinib monotherapy, 173 (46%) of 376 patients with tofacitinib and methotrexate,
and 169 (44%) of 386 patients with adalimumab and methotrexate. The study demonstrated that tofacitinib plus methotrexate
was noninferior to adalimumab plus methotrexate. Tofacitinib
monotherapy failed to achieve noninferiority to adalimumab plus
methotrexate even though the ACR50 response was robust.
A second study with tofacitinib evaluated the 11 mg XR
formulation and the ability to withdraw methotrexate after
achievement of LDAS at week 24 and maintenance of LDAS as
monotherapy.
The Oral Shift study enrolled RA patients with
active disease despite methotrexate and patients received openlabel tofacitinib 11 mg XR once daily. At week 24 patients in LDA
by CDAI (10 or less) were randomized to blinded withdrawal of
methotrexate. The primary endpoint was the least squares mean
change from Week 24 to 48 (

) in DAS28-4-
erythrocyte sedimentation rate
(ESR) (disease activity) in the double-blind phase full
analysis set. Noninferiority of tofacitinib monotherapy versus
tofacitinib plus methotrexate was declared if the upper bound of
the 95% confidence interval (CI) for the difference in
Δ
DAS28-

The Select Choice study compared upadacitinib 15 mg daily

### Real-World Experience

In the real-world setting, patients initiating tofacitinib tend to
have longer disease duration and have been exposed to more
bDMARDs, in comparison with patients initiating a bDMARD.
A 5-year prospective observational study using the U.S. Corrona
RA registry compared patients initiating tofacitinib with patients
initiating a bDMARD or those initiating a csDMARD (and with
no prior or current tofacitinib exposure).
In this study, patients
initiating tofacitinib had an average duration of disease of 13.7
years, versus 10.5 years for bDMARD initiators and 4.6 years for
csDMARD initiators. The tofacitinib initiators also had exposure
to more bDMARDs (mean 2.7) versus the bDMARD initiators
(1.5) and the csDMARD initiators (0).
In real-world patients, tofacitinib also appears to be effective as
monotherapy, as measured by patient adherence to therapy (i.e.,
filling prescriptions regularly) and persistence with therapy. One
study analyzing tofacitinib use in clinical practice in two U.S.
Health Care Claims Databases assessed a total of 455 patients and
found that slightly over 50% of patients received monotherapy
and over 75% of patients had prior biologic use. There was no
significant difference in adherence to therapy and persistence with
therapy or with all-cause or RA-related costs in monotherapy
versus combination therapy.
Another study assessed real-world
experience with tofacitinib compared with adalimumab, etanercept, and abatacept in RA patients with one previous biologic
DMARD, using data from a U.S. commercial database and a
Medicare supplemental claims database.
Findings showed that
tofacitinib patients were older versus adalimumab patients, the
12-month pre-index use of bDMARDs was greater in tofacitinib
patients (77.6%) versus bDMARD cohorts (47.6%–59.6%),
and tofacitinib patients were more likely to receive monotherapy
(53.1% vs. 41.4%–48.3% of bDMARD patients). Persistence
and adherence to tofacitinib was similar compared with persistence and adherence to the bDMARDs assessed in this study.
The JAK-pot study evaluated the persistence on treatment of
over 30,000 RA patients from 19 international registries in comparison to approved biologics from 2013 to 2021.
Forty-one

percent of the JAK inhibitor treatment courses were baricitinib
and 59% tofacitinib. There were no patients included in this the
study period taking upadacitinib. Patients starting TNF inhibitors were younger, had a shorter disease duration, less previous
b/tsDMARD experience, and were less often on monotherapy.
JAK inhibitors and IL-6 inhibitors were more often given as
monotherapy, and JAK inhibitors were more often prescribed
after several treatment failures. Treatment groups were comparable
for gender, seropositivity, and disease activity.
There were 31,846 treatment courses included in the observational database: 17,522 TNF inhibitors, 2775 abatacept, 3863
IL-6 inhibitors, and 7686 JAK inhibitors. Adjusted analyses of
overall discontinuation were similar across all treatments. The
main single reason of stopping treatment was ineffectiveness.
Compared with TNF inhibitors, JAK inhibitors were less often
discontinued for ineffectiveness (adjusted hazard ratio [aHR]
0.75, 95% CI 0.67 to 0.83), as was IL-6 inhibitors (aHR 0.76,
95% CI 0.67 to 0.85) and more often for AEs (aHR 1.16, 95%
CI 1.03 to 1.33). Adjusted CDAI response rates at 1 year were
similar between TNF inhibitors, JAK inhibitors, and IL-6 inhibitors and slightly lower for abatacept.
There have been no clinical trials evaluating efficacy of initiating a second JAK inhibitor after initial JAK inhibitor failure for
lack of efficacy or intolerability. Data from observational studies
in small numbers of patients have reported that initiating a second
JAK inhibitor after initial failure can be associated with success
regardless of the cause for treatment failure with the initial JAK
inhibitor.
An updated report from the JAK-pot collaborating
registries demonstrated that patients switching from an initial
JAK inhibitor to another JAK inhibitor had similar drug survival
at 24 months to patients switching to a biologic, but an adjusted
analysis showed that cycling to another JAK inhibitor was associated with higher drug retention (
p
= 0.04).
However, when the
initial JAK inhibitor was discontinued due to an AE, it was more
likely that the second one would also be discontinued due to an
AE.

### Safety

The nature of AEs associated with JAK inhibitors have been well
characterized in the clinical trial programs and post-marketing
clinical trials and real-world experience.
The most common
AEs were upper respiratory infections, headache, nasopharyngitis,
and diarrhea, which were seen slightly more often in the patients
on JAK inhibitors than in the placebo group. AEs associated with
JAK inhibitors include serious infectious episodes, opportunistic
infections, herpes zoster, gastrointestinal (GI) perforations, and
possible increased risk of thromboembolic disease (
Box 65.1
).
The type and frequency of AEs are similar to that seen with
bDMARDS except for the increased incidence of herpes zoster.

### Laboratory

Based on their mechanism of action, certain laboratory abnormalities were expected (see
Box 65.2
). JAK1 facilitates signaling
for IL-6, which results in neutrophil homeostasis. IL-6 inhibitors
have been associated with reduction in neutrophil counts, and this
is felt to be part of their mechanism of action in RA, so it would be
expected that inhibiting JAK1, which facilitates signal transduction for IL-6, would be associated with a reduction in neutrophil
counts. A modest reduction in neutrophil counts has been seen
with all the JAK inhibitors, with rare grade 3/4 neutropenia noted.
The reduction in neutrophil counts were infrequently associated
with AEs and not associated with increased infection risk. JAK3
plays an integral role in lymphocyte survival/maturation and all
of the JAK inhibitors have been associated with lowered lymphocyte counts with rare grade 4 lymphopenia. Monitoring complete
blood cell and total lymphocyte counts on a regular basis is recommended by the regulatory agencies, as would be done for routine
care. A boxed warning in the package inserts for both tofacitinib
and baricitinib for lymphopenia and discontinuation of treatment
is recommended for patients with persistent lymphocyte counts

clinical trials. Upadacitinib and filgotinib have also rarely been
associated with lymphopenia and neutropenia.

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Pre-clinical Studies
Tofacitinib.
Pre-clinical data suggest that tofacitinib may be of
benefit in SLE. One study of experimental lupus nephritis assessed
the efficacy of tofacitinib in NZB/NZWF1 mice, a murine model
for lupus nephritis. Animals treated with tofacitinib showed significantly reduced proteinuria and improved renal function and
histologic lesions of the kidney, including diminished C3 and
immunoglobulin G (IgG) deposition in glomeruli.
In another pre-clinical study, NZB/NZWF1 mice were treated
with tofacitinib and found to have reduced levels of anti-doublestranded DNA (dsDNA) antibodies, decreased proteinuria, and
amelioration of nephritis compared with control animals.
A
third study found that in MLR/lpr lupus-prone mice, treatment
with tofacitinib led to improvement in measures of disease activity, including nephritis, skin inflammation, and autoantibody
production. The researchers observed that tofacitinib modulated factors associated with premature vascular damage in SLE,
including endothelium-dependent vasorelaxation and endothelial
differentiation.
There is one published phase I study of tofacitinib in SLE.
This was a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study
primarily designed to assess safety and tolerability in this patient
population. Thirty subjects were randomized to tofacitinib 5 mg
twice daily or placebo in a 2:1 ratio, and no new safety signals
were noted. The secondary endpoints included mechanistic studies assessing cardiometabolic and immunologic parameters felt to
be associated with premature atherosclerosis in SLE. Tofacitinib
was shown to improve a number of these parameters, including
HDL cholesterol levels and arterial stiffness. There are two small
open-label studies currently enrolling patients with SLE and cutaneous lupus, and a third small open-label study in discoid lupus
with or without SLE was terminated.
Baricitinib.
A report of a 24-week phase II, double-blind,
placebo-controlled study of baricitinib demonstrated efficacy in
patients with SLE receiving standard therapy.
Participants were
randomly assigned to receive either placebo or baricitinib 2 mg or
4 mg once daily along with continuation of background therapy,
which is standard for lupus clinical trials. The primary endpoint
was resolution of arthritis or rash, measured by the SLE Disease
Activity Index (SLEDAI-2K) at 24 weeks. Of the 314 participants
who were randomly assigned to treatment, 79, 82, and 83% completed 24 weeks of treatment with placebo, baricitinib 2 mg, and
baricitinib 4 mg, respectively.

4 mg group compared with placebo achieved resolution of SLEDAI-2K arthritis or rash (67% vs. 53%;
p
< 0.05) and had a SLE

p
< 0.05) at 24 weeks
compared with placebo. In addition, the percentage of patients

not the 2 mg dose, significantly improved the signs and symptoms

Bruton’s Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors
Oral BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib) have been approved for treatment of patients with mantle cell lymphoma, Waldenstrom’s
macroglobulinemia, and chronic lymphocytic leukemia who have
received at least one previous therapy.
BTK inhibitors have
been evaluated as potential treatments for RA, SLE, and Sjogren’s
syndrome. BTK plays a prominent role in BCR signal transduction and also has a role in Toll-like receptor and FcR signaling
in myeloid cells.
BTK is a member of the Tec family of cytoplasmic PTKs. In humans, mutations in BTK can result in the
development of Bruton’s agammaglobulinemia (known also as
X-linked agammaglobulinemia), an immunodeficiency characterized by a defect in B cell development and resulting in a significant absence of circulating B cells.
Upon ligand binding to
the BCR, BTK is activated by upstream SRC-family kinases LYN,
FYN, and SYK (
Fig. 65.4
). Activated BTK drives phosphorylation
of PLC
γ
and subsequent PKC activation, which in turn results in
calcium flux and the activation of transcription factors, including
NFκ
B and NFAT, regulating the expression of downstream genes
controlling proliferation, survival, and chemokines and cytokines
(
Fig. 65.6
). B cells play multiple roles in the pathogenesis of RA
and SLE, including antigen presentation, inflammatory cytokine
production and rheumatoid factor, and cyclic citrullinated peptide antibody production. Rituximab, which depletes B cells, has
demonstrated efficacy as treatment for patients with RA, and belimumab for SLE supports efforts to develop a small-molecule BTK
inhibitor that impacts B cell activation.
Animal models of arthritis suggest that BTK inhibition can
result in blockage of B cell receptor–dependent cell proliferation and a reduction of inflammatory cytokine production from
myeloid cells (including TNF, IL-1, and IL-6) by preventing signaling through the FC
γ
RIII receptor.
Additionally, activated BTK has been demonstrated to be increased in peripheral
blood B cells of patients with RA compared with controls.
An
in vitro study demonstrated that BTK inhibition decreased monocyte differentiation into M1 macrophages, which are inflammatory and skewed differentiation more to the M2 phenotype, which
are anti-inflammatory.

evobrutinib were evaluated in two phase II clinical trials in RA

p
= 0.017;
p
= 0.0003). Fenebrutinib 200 mg twice daily

MK2 Inhibitors
The p38
α
mitogen activated protein kinase (MAPK)/MAPK-activated protein kinase 2 (MK2) inflammatory signaling pathway
plays a significant role in the expression of multiple cytokines, chemokines, matrix metalloproteases and other inflammatory signals
including TNF, IL-1
α
and
β
, and IL-6). Although p38 MAPK
inhibition failed in previous clinical trials for RA due to limited
efficacy and safety signals of dermatitis, central nervous system
dysfunction, and transaminitis, there has been a renewed interest
in this pathway. Zunsemetinib is an orally available, small-molecule inhibitor that targets the high-affinity docking interaction
between p38
α
MAPK and MK2. Upon binding to the interface
created upon formation of this bimolecular complex, zunsemetinib blocks MK2 phosphorylation by p38MAPK, locks MK2 in
an inactive conformation, and thereby inhibits the downstream
MK2-mediated inflammatory drive. This inhibitor is being studied in RA, PsA, and hidradenitis suppurativa.
A proof-ofconcept study in 20 RA patients suggested benefit and acceptable
safety and larger trials are ongoing in PsA.
The phase II study
with this compound did not confirm efficacy, although there
might have been a very high placebo effect. Based on these results,
further development of zunsemetinib was discontinued. Other
Mk2 inhibitors are in early clinical trials in RA and axSpA.
Sphingosine 1 Phosphate Modulators
Sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) is an abundant, biologically
active lysophospholipid. In the immune system, changes in local
S1P concentrations and gradients can modify lymphocyte migration patterns, alter inflammatory cell responses, and affect the
barrier function of endothelial cells. Interaction of S1P with its
receptor results in inhibition of lymphocyte egress from primary
and secondary lymphoid tissues, resulting in lymphocyte depletion in the peripheral blood. The major and irreversible route of
S1P degradation is via S1P lyase (S1PL). Fingolimod, a sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor modulator, was approved in 2013 for
relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis.
Pre-clinical studies demonstrated that reduced S1PL activity
has a significant effect in reducing the inflammatory response in
models of RA.
LX3305, a small-molecule inhibitor of S1PL,
was evaluated as a DMARD. Results of a placebo-controlled,
dose-ranging, phase II randomized controlled trial of LX3305 in
patients with RA with active disease despite methotrexate treatment has been reported.
In the study, 208 patients received 70
mg, 110 mg, or 150 mg daily or placebo for 12 weeks, and the
primary endpoint was ACR20 at week 12. Of patients receiving
the 150 mg dose, 60% achieved an ACR20 response compared
with 49% on placebo, which was not statistically different. Various subanalyses were conducted, suggesting potential benefits,
and no significant safety issues were noted. Based on the results of
this proof-of-concept study, development in RA was terminated.
Fingolimod, a S1P receptor modulator, is approved for treatment
of multiple sclerosis but to date has not moved forward in clinical
trials for RA.

Inhibition of type 4 phosphodiesterase (PDE) has been demonstrated to inhibit TNF production and other proinflammatory
cytokines.
Cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) is the
principal second messenger responsible for immune response regulation. PDE4 is the main cAMP-degrading enzyme found in cells
of the immune system and keratinocytes. It is the predominant isoenzyme expressed in macrophages, lymphocytes, and neutrophils.
Elevation of intra-cellular cAMP inhibits TNF production through
the protein kinase A pathway. Three PDE4 inhibitors have been

## Conclusion

The unraveling of the intra-cellular pathways involved in signal
transduction, resulting in T and B cell activation and inflammatory cytokine production, has resulted in the development
of small-molecule inhibitors to these pathways that can abrogate inflammation in inflammatory diseases. Efficacy similar to
bDMARDs and overall acceptable safety profile in clinical trials and real-world data has been demonstrated. Safety issues in
patients over age 65, smokers, and patients with cardiovascular,
venous thromboembolic, or malignancy risk factors have been
reported and risk mitigation strategies for use of JAK inhibitors
should be followed. JAK inhibitors are now utilized widely for
IMIDs in the clinic and evaluation of these therapies and other
kinase inhibitors in diseases such as SLE and dermatomyositis are
ongoing, which may expand the indications for these treatments.
