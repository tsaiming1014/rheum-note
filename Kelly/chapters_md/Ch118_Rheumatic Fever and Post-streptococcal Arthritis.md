## Introduction

Rheumatic fever (RF) and rheumatic heart disease (RHD) occur
in young, susceptible people who have not received treatment
for group A streptococcal (GAS) infection. This chapter briefly
describes the etiologic agent and reviews several genes involved in
developing inflammatory and autoimmune lesions. Of note, the
role of class II human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles is described
in association with the disease and its function in antigen presentation to T cells, which activate the adaptive branch of the immune
response. Mechanisms that underlie organ-specific lesions are
also discussed. Both cellular and humoral immune responses are
involved in the autoimmune reactions, and T helper (Th)1 and
Th17 inflammatory cytokines mediate the development of rheumatic heart lesions. Clinical spectra and treatment are presented,
along with perspectives regarding vaccine development targeting
Streptococcus pyogenes
infection.
Numerous barriers and gaps exist in implementing RHD prevention strategies and effective care of affected patients that fall
short of recommendations. As a result, RHD continues to have a
devastating impact, with estimates of nearly 300,000 deaths globally each year and loss of >10 million disability-adjusted life-years.

## Rheumatic Fever and Rheumatic Heart Disease

### Etiologic Agent (S. pyogenes)

RF is universally preceded by infection with group A streptococcus. Rebecca Lancefield classified the streptococci in 1941 and
identified carbohydrates on the cell walls of the bacteria. She classified them as groups A, B, C, F, and G based on the types of cell
wall polysaccharides present.
Group A is characterized by N-acetylβ
-d-glucosamine and
rhamnose in the cell wall and is responsible for developing RF and
RHD. These bacteria contain M, T, and R surface proteins and
lipoteichoic acid, which are involved in bacterial adherence to epithelial cells of the throat. M protein, the major antigen, comprises
approximately 450 amino acid residues extending from the cell
wall. The N-terminal portion of this protein is highly variable and
defines the emm type that is responsible for the antigenic diversity
of the bacteria. According to the Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention, more than 200 emm genotypes currently exist worldwide, with a variable distribution depending on the region.
The diversity of
S. pyogenes
is also influenced by social and
economic conditions. Diversity is accentuated in low-income
countries, likely because of the lack of treatment, resulting in the
spread of bacteria and favoring genetic mutations. Similarities in
the distribution of emm-type strains have been observed in highincome countries, Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America, in
contrast to those found in Africa and the Pacific region.
Interestingly, most of the streptococcal strains identified and described in
this study were associated with the development of RF and RHD.
The most frequent strain in a majority of countries was emm1,
accounting for approximately 20% of the strains compared with
approximately 5% in Africa and the Pacific.
Interestingly, the
emm1 strain is also frequently associated with post-streptococcal
glomerulonephritis and invasive disease.
In 2014, an analysis of 220 variants of the M protein revealed
48 emm clusters that contained closely related M proteins sharing
binding and structural properties.
Recently, complete genome
sequences of
S. pyogenes
were defined using a PacBio Illumina
sequencing platform that will provide more accurate identification

of the diverse strains of the bacteria, facilitating the diagnostics
for infectious diseases. This advanced classification approach
could further inform the development of new drugs and vaccine
designs for treating and preventing diseases related to streptococcal infection.
The end of the C-terminal half, in contrast to the N-terminal
portion, contains multiple repeat regions, presents a high degree
of conservation of approximately 70%, and serves as the target of
a vaccine development model.

### Epidemiology

The risk of RF after untreated
S. pyogenes
pharyngitis in susceptible people ranges from 0.3% to 3%.
Susceptibility depends on
the individual’s genetic background and is influenced mainly by
low socioeconomic conditions. Crowded households that facilitate the spread of bacteria, lack of access to antibiotics and medical
facilities, and other factors associated with poverty are all wellrecognized risk factors for the development of RF. Likely, the
higher diversity of
S. pyogenes
strains found in low-income countries plays a role in RF susceptibility due to a resulting increase
in the risk of recurrent infections.
A high index of suspicion for acute rheumatic fever (ARF) is
indicated, particularly when caring for patients originating from
geographic settings where ARF is endemic.
RHD affects more
than 40.5 million people worldwide and results in 306,000 deaths
annually.
The incidence is highest among the world’s most disadvantaged populations. Temporal trends in the health burden
posed by RF and RHD, including incidence and related mortality,
have not shown recent signs of improvement.
The incidence of ARF is 8 to 51 per 100,000 people worldwide. It most commonly affects children 5 to 15 years of age after
a group A streptococcal infection.
Since 1990, the prevalence
of RHD has been rising steadily, reaching 40.5 million (95%
confidence interval, 32.1–50.1 million) affected in 2019. The
continuing increase in prevalence reflects increased global awareness, greater availability of echocardiography for case definition,
improved survival in some places, and the chronic nature of
RHD.
In high-income countries, tonsillitis is only a “throat infection.” However, in developing countries, or even those of average income, in the case of Brazil, it may be a sign of RHD,
which kills approximately 233,000 to 500,000 individuals per
year globally.
RF/RHD continues to be a significant cause of morbidity and
mortality in developing countries. This scenario is very different
and diverse in high-income countries. RHD has mostly been eradicated in such countries, but a new disease burden is possible due
to migration flow.
Most ARF and RHD are in low- and middleincome countries where more than 80% of the world’s ARF cases
occur. Global estimates indicate that the burden of RHD is highest among people living in poor-quality housing, followed by rural
areas, and lowest in urban areas.

### Clinical Manifestations

Acute Rheumatic Fever
Manifestations of ARF result in a constellation of symptoms that
may occur in isolation or any combination. In addition, as previously reported, the incidence of ARF manifestations varies in
frequency and intensity among different age groups, individuals,
and localities.
ARF is rare before age 3 and infrequent after age 15, with most
cases occurring between ages 4 and 9. The inflammation tends to
be more intense in younger children, who often exhibit greater
prostration, fever, and carditis, whereas adults tend to show a
greater incidence of arthritis. Symptoms begin approximately 2 to
3 weeks after GAS infection (average, 18.6 days), except for chorea, which usually begins after the fourth to eighth week. However, some patients appear to have a brief latency period due to
clinically unapparent infection some weeks before the observed
respiratory illness.
Pharyngitis
Approximately half of preceding streptococcus pharyngeal infections can be asymptomatic.
The proportion of patients in whom
ARF will develop after a pharyngeal infection largely depends on
the characteristics of the bacteria, population, and individual.
Data obtained from RF outbreaks in U.S. military camps from
1949 to 1950 showed that ARF developed in 2.5% of patients
infected with a rheumatogenic streptococcus strain. In a similar
situation in 1987, the rate of ARF events per case of streptococcal
pharyngitis was 1.6%.

Arthritis
Arthritis is the most common and often the earliest major symptom of ARF, although rarely it may be preceded by carditis. The
relative proportion of patients with ARF who have polyarthritis
and the severity of this manifestation increases with age.
In an
early case series (1962), arthritis was present in nearly 100% of
young adults, 82% of teenagers, and 66% of children.
Tenderness is often disproportionate to the physical findings. A significant proportion of patients exhibit an additive rather than the
classic migratory pattern of involvement, which occurs mainly
in adults. A monoarticular presentation occurred in only 17%
to 25% of the cases in the older case series. Still, it is currently
much more common, attributed to the widespread use of antiinflammatory treatments. Untreated patients typically have 6 to
16 affected joints. Knees (76%), ankles (50%), and elbows and
wrists (12% to 15%) are the most frequently involved sites. The
shoulder (7% to 8%), phalangeal (7% to 8%), lumbosacral (2%),
cervical (1%), sternoclavicular (0.5%), and temporomandibular
(0.5%) joints are less frequently affected.
Articular symptoms
often last only a few days for each joint and uncommonly more
than 1 week for each ARF attack.
Arthritis typically responds
well to nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Other
diagnoses should be considered if this pattern is not followed.
Although arthritis is never radiographically destructive, Jaccoud’s
arthropathy (chronic post-RF arthropathy) has been described in
RF and can occur after several recurrent articular episodes.
Acute Carditis
Carditis occurs in 15% to 91% of the first ARF attacks, depending on the methods of patient selection and whether echocardiography is used for diagnosis.
Carditis affects children more
often and more severely than adults, but the latter are not spared.
Inflammation may involve the pericardium, myocardium, and
endocardium (pan-carditis), and it may be mild and silent (subclinical) or severe and lead to acute heart failure (HF) and death.
In the acute phase, endocarditis is associated with edema of the
valves and possibly vegetations (marantic vegetations) that do not
produce emboli. The mitral valve is affected in almost all cases
of carditis, which is attributed to the predominant migration of
inflammatory cells through the papillary muscles. The tricuspid

valve is also frequently affected, but rarely in a meaningful manner. The aortic valve is involved in approximately 20% to 30%
of cases.
Valvular involvement may lead to a new or changing
murmur used clinically to define carditis. Mitral regurgitation is
the most common acute valvular lesion and may be isolated or
concomitant with aortic regurgitation. Isolated aortic regurgitation is infrequent. Typical acute-phase murmurs consist of the
high-pitched, blowing, holosystolic, apical murmur of mitral
regurgitation; the low-pitched, apical, middiastolic, flow murmur (Carey-Coombs murmur); and a high-pitched, decrescendo,
diastolic murmur of aortic regurgitation heard in the aortic area.
Mitral regurgitation can be caused by valvular inflammation,
deformity, and valve prolapse. The latter is caused by annular dilation and chordal elongation, and it typically involves the anterior
leaflet with minimal leaflet redundancy. Rupture of the chordae
tendineae is possible and often leads to acute HF and the need for
emergent surgery. Aortic or mitral stenosis at presentation is atypical and may indicate previous undiagnosed RF.
HF is the most life-threatening feature of ARF and results
from valvular damage and heart muscle dysfunction due to myocarditis and chamber dilation. HF occurs in 5% to 10% of people
experiencing their first ARF episode and is more frequent during recurrences. Myocarditis in the absence of valvulitis should
not be considered rheumatogenic in origin. Variable degrees of
heart blockage may be present, and a temporary pacemaker may
be required if HF occurs.
In approximately 10% of patients, pericarditis manifests clinically as pleuritic chest discomfort or pain and pericardial friction
rub. Although effusion may be prominent, cardiac tamponade is
rare.

Transition From Acute to Chronic Heart Disease
RHD is a chronic condition resulting from poorly treated betahemolytic streptococci. If not diagnosed and managed appropriately in a timely fashion, RHD commonly leads to HF,
thromboembolic events, and premature death in children, adolescents, and adults.
Inflammatory markers typically normalize within 3 months
after the acute phase, but they may remain high for much longer
in a subset of patients. The causes or prognostic implications of
persistent inflammation are not known. According to the classic view, if the patient received appropriate prophylaxis and did
not experience further GAS infections, then any deterioration
observed after the acute attack would result from mechanical dysfunctions caused by valvular insufficiency (acute injury and scarification) or the slow progression of valve stenosis. However, it is
recognized that local inflammation may persist in the myocardium
and (mainly) the valves of some patients and may, to some extent,
be responsible for observed deterioration over time. Samples from
the valve and papillary muscles of patients with chronic RHD collected during valve replacement procedures often show variable
degrees of mononuclear or lymphocytic infiltrates and sometimes
Aschoff nodules, even in patients considered to be in remission
clinically and based on laboratory results.

In most cases, the fibrotic and neovascularization effects
involved in healing processes do not affect valve function. Nevertheless, scarring may progress over the years and affect cusps,
chordae tendineae, subendocardial tissue, and the annulus fibrosus, leading to valvular thickening, stiffening, incompetence,
or stenosis. The speed of scarification and progression of mitral
stenosis vary between geographic areas. In countries with a low
RF incidence, 20 to 40 years will pass until surgery is required,
whereas in countries with a high incidence of RF, mitral stenosis
is possible in patients younger than 5 years of age. Again, factors
relating to this behavior are unknown but are believed to be linked
to the degree of acute damage, recurrences, and the persistence of
inflammation.
In the classic 1951 series,
clinical signs of rheumatic valvular
disease disappeared in 17% of the patients after 20 years. However, murmurs later developed in another 44% of the patients
who initially did not have clinical carditis. In total, 70% of the
patients eventually evolved to chronic RHD.
More recently, 258
Brazilian children with ARF were followed up prospectively, and
72% and 37% of them eventually experienced chronic valvular
disease based on echocardiography and clinical assessment, respectively. Severe aortic and/or mitral disease occurred in 16% of the
patients. Risk factors for progression to severe chronic valvular
disease were moderate or severe acute carditis, ARF recurrences,
and mother’s low educational level.
Indolent Carditis
Indolent carditis may be defined as carditis of insidious onset
and slow progression (low acute-phase severity and absence of
significant acute progression), especially in people presenting for
a medical diagnosis months after the onset of RF.
Recognition
is usually delayed, and prophylaxis is not introduced; therefore
multiple recurrences are likely to drive progression. Routine consultations typically lead to a diagnosis once symptoms of RHD are
present. It is not rare, especially in the developing world, but the
exact incidence of indolent RHD has not been ascertained.
Subclinical Carditis
Subclinical carditis is defined as pathologic valvular regurgitation
detected by echocardiography that is not clinically evident.
Its
clinical importance is discussed in the section on echocardiography.
Rheumatic Heart Disease
RHD, the most common cause of acquired valvular disease in the
world, is responsible for approximately one in four cases of HF in
endemic countries.
Chronic RHD is typically oligosymptomatic
until HF is established. HF features include a third heart sound,
tachycardia, dyspnea, rales, and edema. If the diagnosis is not
established during the acute phase, RF often remains undetected
for many people until the first symptoms of HF occur. In South
Africa, 82.5% of affected children were previously undiagnosed.
Sixty percent of the patients who are oligosymptomatic at diagnosis will not experience any progression of symptoms in 10 years,
with survival rates exceeding 80%. In contrast, after developing
New York Heart Association functional class III or IV disease, survival is estimated to be 0% to 15% over 10 years if surgery is not
performed. In severe pulmonary hypertension, survival is expected
to be no longer than 3 years without surgery.
Typically, young
patients exhibit pure mitral regurgitation, middle-age adults have
mitral stenosis, and older patients present with mixed mitral valve
disease.
RHD usually remains asymptomatic for an extended
period and frequently is not noticed if ARF does not manifest.

Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders
Associated With Streptococcal Infections
In pediatric autoimmune neuropsychiatric disorders associated
with streptococcal infections (PANDAS), antibodies from the
sera of patients with chorea can bind to neuronal cells and induce
the release of dopamine.
A subset of these patients experience
behavioral disturbances such as tics and obsessive–compulsive
disorder (OCD) years after the initial manifestation of chorea.
Symptoms may abruptly worsen after a new streptococcus infection. These data led to the hypothesis that this spectrum of mental
health disorders results from previous streptococcal infections in
children. A strong association between OCD and the D8/17 antigen on B cells has been described.
This antigen was believed to
differentiate the clinical spectrum of patients who have RF with
high specificity. The PANDAS hypothesis remains controversial,
although it is broadly accepted that streptococcal infection can
worsen symptoms of OCD in some patients.
Fever
Most manifestations of ARF are accompanied by fever, except for
chorea.
Fever is seen in more than 90% of patients; a low-temperature cutoff of 100°F (38°C) has been suggested for diagnosing
fever in endemic regions. In low-resource settings, a clear history
of fever can be more important than the temperature at the time
of examination.
Fever may be absent, subtle, or high. Similar to
subcutaneous nodules, erythema marginatum, and chorea, fever is
extremely rare in adult-onset ARF. Malaise and myalgia are commonly associated with fever and may also be intense.
Erythema Marginatum
While cutaneous manifestations including erythema marginatum
(EM) are uncommon (0%–10%) and sometimes difficult to
detect, these findings are highly specific and considered as major
diagnostic criteria.
EM presents as an evanescent, pink, nonpruritic, nontender rash that involves the trunk and sometimes
the limbs but not the face. It is strongly associated with carditis. The lesion is also known as “erythema annulare” because its
margins often assume the form of a ring. The border is irregular,
serpiginous, and sharp on the outside but diffuses on the inside
edge; it extends centrifugally while the center progressively clears.
Lesions may appear, disappear, and reappear in hours and may be
difficult to visualize on dark skin. A hot bath or shower may make
them more evident. EM usually occurs early during ARF but may
occur at any time, occasionally in the chronic heart disease phase.
Subcutaneous Nodules
Subcutaneous nodules in RF resemble those of rheumatoid arthritis in terms of having a predilection for bony surfaces or areas near
tendons (elbows, knees, ankles, Achilles tendon, and occiput);
however, they are smaller and more fleeting (they rarely persist
for more than a month and usually subside within 1 or 2 weeks).
Lesions range from a few millimeters to 2 cm in size and are firm,
painless, and loose in relation to adjacent surfaces. The skin over
the lesions is not inflamed. The number of nodules present may
vary from one to a few dozen (with an average of four). When
abundant, the lesions tend to occur in crops and have a symmetric
distribution. Subcutaneous nodules are virtually always associated
with significant chronic active carditis and are uncommon in the
early stages of the disease. Subcutaneous nodules were described
in 8.8% of the patients in the series by Bland and Jones
but only
in 1.5% in a more recent series of 786 patients,
representing the
least common RF manifestation.

### Genetic Associations

In autoimmune diseases, the activation of innate and adaptive
immune responses leads to tissue damage, possibly due to a previous infection caused mainly by viruses or bacteria. Controlling
pathogens and triggering autoimmune reactions involve several
genes (
Tables 118.1 and 118.2
).
Predisposition to develop RF and RHD is associated with
some class II HLA alleles, and single-nucleotide polymorphisms
in several genes related to activation of the immune response,
initially driven against
S. pyogenes
and later against self-antigens.
Results from genome-wide association studies from one group
suggested that some amino acid residue variations in
HLA DQA1
and
DQB1
may confer increased risk in indigenous people in Australia. A separate study
showed that the
IGHV4-61*02
allele of
the
IGH
(immunoglobulin heavy chain) gene segment was associated with RHD in the Aboriginal Australian population.
The innate immune system detects pathogen-mediated injury
and other bodily injuries and represents the first line of immune
defense. Both immune and somatic cells present receptors that
can recognize microbial molecules called pathogen-associated
molecular patterns and tissue injury-induced molecules as a sign
of damage-associated molecular patterns.
Most studies involve RF/RHD cohorts from diverse ethnicities
and are representative of the genes involved in the development
of the disease, as summarized in
Table 118.2
. In addition, genes
associated with disease predisposition are described, considering
their role in innate and adaptive immune responses.
Innate Immunity
Toll-like receptors (TLRs) initiate host defense responses in multicellular organisms, acting as sensors of foreign microbial products.
A polymorphism of TLR-2 at codon 753 leads to the replacement
of arginine with glutamine. The genotype 753 Arg/Gln was more
frequent in a Turkish ARF cohort compared with controls. This
mutation likely interferes with the recognition of
S. pyogenes
and
the innate immune response against the pathogen.

surface of pathogens to promote phagocytosis and complement

### Molecular Mimicry and Epitope Spreading

The molecular mimicry mechanism considers the existence of similar or identical antigens in microbes (viruses, bacteria, and other
pathogens) and their hosts and has been implicated in the pathogenesis of RF/RHD. This mechanism enables the microbe to evade
the host immune response and facilitates self-antigen recognition
after infection by cross-reactivity. T and B lymphocytes can recognize pathogens and self-antigens through molecular mimicry by
four different types of mimicry, recognizing (1) identical amino acid
sequences, (2) homologous but nonidentical sequences, (3) standard or similar amino acid sequences of different molecules (proteins and carbohydrates), and (4) structural similarities between the
microbe or environmental agent and its host.
Diversification of the immune response against immunodominant epitopes from a pathogen, human tissue, or soluble protein
leads to its amplification and has been defined as epitope spreading,
a process that favors recognition of several self-antigens
and facilitates tissue damage in different organs. The major manifestations of the disease result from interactions between these
mechanisms.

### Pathogenic Considerations of Specific Manifestations

Arthritis
In the joints, anti-streptococcal antibodies likely against group A
carbohydrates, components of the bacterial cell membrane, or the
M protein precipitate to form immune complexes and activate the
complement cascade to induce transitory arthritis.
Sydenham’s Chorea
The pathogenic mechanism leading to the development of Sydenham’s chorea was clarified by one group.
Briefly, these investigators showed that antibodies from the sera of patients with chorea
could bind to neuronal cells. They also identified gangliosides as
autoantigens that cross-react with N-acetylβ
-d-glucosamine, the
major cell wall streptococcal antigen. They also showed that autoantibodies mediate signal transduction by calcium/calmodulindependent protein kinase II, triggering dopamine release from
neuronal cells. Sydenham’s chorea involves involuntary, purposeless movements of the extremities and trunk; follows the index
episode of acute rheumatic fever by 4 to 8 weeks; and may occur
as an isolated manifestation. It is seen in 10% to 30% of cases of
ARF and is considered a major manifestation for diagnosis.

### Cytokines and the Inflammatory Process

Cytokines are also key players in the inflammatory process during RF episodes and later in the development RHD. Four subsets

differentiation, and survival. Antigen-activated CD4+ T cells
polarize to these subsets, depending on the cytokine secreted.
Th1 cells are involved in the cellular immune response and
produce IL-2, interferon (IFN)-
γ
, and TNF. Th2 cells mediate
humoral and allergic immune responses and produce IL-4, IL-10,
IL-5, and IL-13. Th17 cells are essential for protecting against
bacterial and fungal infections and developing autoimmunity.
TGFβ
, IL-6, and IL-23 induce this lineage and secrete IL-17 to
mediate a pro-inflammatory response.

Throat Cultures
A rayon-tipped throat culture swab obtained by sampling the
tonsils and back of the throat is the current gold standard for
diagnosing GAS pharyngitis. Nevertheless, approximately 75%
of patients have negative throat culture findings when ARF symptoms occur.
A positive throat culture finding may be due to a
new infection or to the convalescent carriage of the rheumatogenic strain.
Rapid Antigen Testing
Rapid streptococcal antigen testing (RSAT) has a high specificity
(
≥
95%) but a moderate sensitivity (65% to 90%).
A positive
RSAT finding helps establish a diagnosis of GAS pharyngitis, but
a negative RSAT finding does not exclude it. In high-risk ARF
locations, where high-stakes decisions are being made, RSATs are
not currently recommended.
If an RSAT is used and is negative,
additional costs are incurred because a follow-up throat swab is
required.
Streptococcal Antibody Tests
Antibody tests are directed against the extra-cellular products of
streptococci and include anti-streptococcal lysine O (ASO), antiDNAse B (ADB), anti-hyaluronidase, anti-nicotinamide adenine
dinucleotidase, and anti-streptokinase antibodies. ASO and ADB
are the most commonly available tests. ASO begins to rise at 1
week and peaks at 3 to 5 weeks after infection. It begins to decline
after approximately 6 to 8 weeks, at a faster pace during the immediate subsequent months, and at a slower rate after 6 months. ADB
begins to rise at 2 weeks and peaks at 6 to 8 weeks after infection.
It begins to decline approximately 3 months after the precipitating infection. Antibody peaks typically coincide with the second
or third week of ARF. Patients will have elevated ASO and ADB
titers 80% and 85% of the time, respectively, when symptoms of
ARF are present. When both ASO and ADB tests are conducted,
92% of patients will have abnormal test results.
The antibody
tests must be interpreted cautiously in areas with high rates of
streptococcal infection and ARF because relatively high titers are
commonly encountered in such populations. A second sample
should be collected 2 weeks later to characterize a recent infection
based on a significant rise in antibody titers. Asymptomatic pharyngeal streptococcus carriers usually have titers just above detectable; therefore a high titer indicates an actual infection. Increases
in ASO occur in response to GAS infections and infections with
groups C and G streptococci and other bacteria such as
Bacillus
,
Clostridium tetani

Listeria monocytogenes
.
Acute-Phase Reactants
Acute-phase reactants, the erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
and C-reactive protein (CRP), rise during an ARF attack, except
in circumstances characterized by chorea or erythema marginatum
as a single manifestation. Acute-phase reactants aid in monitoring the evolution of inflammation and serve as minor manifestations based on the Jones criteria (detailed below). Salicylates or
other NSAIDs may mask the results of these tests. ESR may stay
elevated for up to 2 months after infection, whereas CRP normalizes within days.
Other Laboratory Tests
Transient and asymptomatic abnormalities of liver function
tests, bilirubin, and mild normochromic normocytic anemia are
observed in many patients with ARF and usually normalize after
cessation of inflammation. Blood cultures should be obtained to
help rule out infective endocarditis, bacteremia, and disseminated
gonococci infection.
Depending on the clinical features, additional tests should
be performed to rule out an alternative diagnosis, such as antinuclear antibody and brain magnetic resonance imaging.

Chest Radiography
A chest radiograph may reveal an enlarged heart silhouette if
myocardial dilation and/or pericardial effusion are significant.
In addition, pulmonary edema may be observed in the presence
of HF.
Electrocardiography
Tachycardia and prolongation of the PR interval are the most
common electrocardiographic findings of myocarditis. Rarely,
third-degree heart block may be observed. Pericarditis can induce
a diffuse elevation of the ST segments. Electrocardiographic
abnormalities are present in approximately 21% of patients, 60%
of whom have evidence of heart block.

Echocardiography
Echocardiography is the primary clinical tool for RHD detection and diagnosis.
Echocardiography may be used to estimate
disease prevalence and potentially improve community awareness
to facilitate better RHD control.
With a sensitivity that is 10
times greater than auscultation in identifying valvular lesions,
the use of echocardiography results in a 16% to 47% increase in
the identification of carditis in RHD.
In addition to providing
objective confirmation of valvular lesions, echocardiography provides an accurate means to detect progression during follow-up

### Diagnosis

No single clinical or laboratory finding is diagnostic of ARF,
with the possible exception of Sydenham’s chorea. Diagnostic challenges act as a major barrier to preventing RHD. The
high frequency of patients presenting with mild disease and
the recognized importance of detecting subclinical carditis
prompted the revision of the Jones Criteria (the gold standard
for RF diagnosis) in 2015 to better account for differences in
population risk.
Thus, the diagnosis of ARF remains a clinical decision.
The classic Jones criteria were first published in 1944.
The
latest 2015 revision distinguishes between moderate-to-high and
low-risk populations and includes echocardiography to diagnose

### Treatment

Primary Prophylaxis
The primary prevention or primary prophylaxis of ARF aims
to interrupt the link between Strep A infection and abnormal
immune response to Strep A that causes ARF by early identification and treatment of Strep A infections.
Adequate treatment of streptococcus pharyngitis greatly
reduces the likelihood of the subsequent development of ARF.
Nevertheless, streptococcus cannot be differentiated from other
pharyngeal pathogens on a clinical basis, important as cultures
take days to provide results, and RSAT lacks adequate sensitivity
and adds costs. Most sore throats are viral in origin. Fifteen to
30% of sore throats in children and 10% in adults are estimated
to be due to GAS.
Whether all pharyngeal infections should be
treated a priori with antibiotics strongly depends on the incidence
of ARF in the evaluated population. The case of Costa Rica is
often used by those who advocate the use of antibiotics for all sore
throat cases in locations with a high incidence of ARF. A policy of
early empiric antibiotic use was instituted in Costa Rica in 1970,
and the number of new cases of ARF fell from 94 new cases in that
year to only four in 1991.
A similar decrease in ARF incidence
occurred in Europe and in the United States before the availability
of antibiotics, and critics attribute the decrease observed in Costa
Rica, at least in part, to other socioeconomic improvements,
including housing conditions, education, and health care access.
New Zealand long ago introduced an efficient program of close
surveillance and optimized treatment of pharyngitis for its highrisk populations, but to date these measures have not successfully
reduced the incidence of ARF.
This outcome could be related to
the observation that approximately half of the streptococcus pharyngeal infections can be asymptomatic
and that only a small
portion of patients with a sore throat seek medical attention.
New Zealand and Australian guidelines for sore throat management do not indicate throat swabs or antibiotics for low-risk
people with mild symptoms, provided there is a low occupational
risk of spreading GAS. People with a high risk of developing ARF
should have a throat culture and be treated if positive results are
obtained or should receive empiric antibiotics if follow-up is a
concern.
In a recent study conducted in Ethiopia,
Streptococcus pyogenes
was responsible for 10.7% of acute pharyngitis cases among pediatric patients, and all isolates were sensitive to penicillin. Many
patients showed reduced susceptibility to tetracycline and macrolides. Therefore prior to antibiotic prescription, screening children
with acute pharyngitis for
S. pyogenes
and testing the antibiotic
susceptibility are recommended.

Eradication of
Streptococcus

triggering factor and reduce the spread of rheumatogenic strains.
Household contacts should also have throat cultures, and people
with positive results should be treated even if they are asymptomatic.
Penicillins are preferred, and the long-acting intramuscular

and initiate secondary prophylaxis.

no effective therapies have been demonstrated to alter the natural history of the disease.
One group reviewed 170 articles and

This finding was reiterated by a United Kingdom–United

Guidelines for the management of

In contrast, the guidelines describe little evidence supporting pharmacologic management to alter outcomes. Most of the global
burden of symptomatic RHD occurs in regions where definitive

In that context, pharmacologic management may represent the
only option for symptomatic improvement in such underserved

Arthritis and fever often respond to
NSAIDs within hours and almost always within 3 days. If not,
the diagnosis of ARF should be reconsidered. Aspirin (80–100

inflammatory markers normalize. Most patients will need to take

6 months or more is required.
Alternative NSAIDs can be

alter the course of the articular disease, and since there is no
evidence that the delay in its introduction worsens the prognosis, paracetamol or acetaminophen can be used initially if the

can be treated with another brief course of anti-inflammatory/
full-dose NSAIDs.
Chorea.
Chorea is often self-limited and does not require
specific treatment aside from rest. Treatment decisions in
Sydenham’s chorea are currently based on the treating physician’s clinical experience, the desire to avoid side effects, and
the existence of only limited scientific evidence.
Neuroleptics, diazepam, and anti-convulsant agents are effective in
prolonged or incapacitating cases. Aspirin and glucocorticoid
therapy do not influence this manifestation. A prospective comparison of carbamazepine, valproic acid, and haloperidol in
18 patients with chorea concluded that valproic acid was the
most effective treatment.
A reduction, but not eradication,
of symptoms should be expected after 1 to 2 weeks of therapy.
Medication should be continued for 2 to 4 weeks after the chorea has abated.
Rheumatic Heart Disease Management
In 2022, RHD Australia published an update of previous guidelines focused on the prevention, early diagnosis, and management
of patients with ARF and RHD.
Secondary Prophylaxis.
Secondary prophylaxis involves the
regular administration of antibiotics to people who have had ARF
or RHD to prevent ARF recurrence.
Patients with RF are at
high risk for recurrence and progression of manifestations following a new GAS infection. Because streptococcal pharyngitis is
often asymptomatic, prevention requires continuous anti-microbial prophylaxis rather than treatment of acute GAS pharyngitis
episodes. Continuous administration of benzathine penicillin G
(BPG) reduces the chances of new streptococcal pharyngitis in
71% to 91% of cases and the chances of a new episode of ARF
in 87% to 96% of cases in addition to decreasing the severity of
RHD
and its associated mortality.
Secondary prophylaxis is indicated for all patients with a
documented history of ARF, Sydenham’s chorea, or convincing
evidence of RHD. BPG injections remain the cornerstone of
secondary prevention; challenges with procurement and concern
with adverse reactions remain important issues.
Intramuscular (IM) penicillin is more effective than oral penicillin in preventing recurrences.
Monthly BPG is indicated at
1.2 million units by IM injection for people weighing more than
27 kg (60 lb) and 600,000 units for people weighing less than
27 kg
(20 kg in New Zealand/Australian guidelines).
Serum
penicillin levels may be undetectable or low 28 days after a 1.2
million-unit BPG dose.
IM BPG injections every 3 weeks
should be considered for high-risk patients. Patients treated with
this regimen exhibit fewer streptococcal infections and ARF recurrences
and a greater resolution of mitral regurgitation over
the long term.
Prospective data from New Zealand showed that
recurrences were rare (0.07 per 100 patient-years) among patients
who were entirely adherent to a monthly BPG regimen, which is
the current recommendation in the United States.
If an IM route is not possible or is refused, the second-line
regimen is oral penicillin V, 250 mg twice daily. Patients with a
documented allergy to penicillin should receive oral erythromycin
40 mg/kg (maximum of 1 g/day) daily, divided into two to four
doses for children, and oral erythromycin 400 mg twice daily for
adults and adolescents.
Secondary prophylaxis should extend until the risk of ARF
recurrence is irrelevant; thus the discontinuation time should be
individualized. Guidelines from the WHO, New Zealand, and
Australian guidelines were developed based on their respective data
and differ slightly. In New Zealand, maintenance of prophylaxis
up to 21 years of age is considered safe and effective in cases with
absent or mild heart disease.
In a review of data from the 1993
to 1999 Auckland (New Zealand) Acute Rheumatic Fever Register, only five recurrences occurred after the age of 30 years. Data
from the Northern Aboriginal Territory of Australia showed that
only approximately 1% of 259 ARF episodes recorded between
2005 and 2009 occurred after 40 years of age. Therefore 30 and 40
years of age were considered the most reasonable cutoffs to cease
secondary prophylaxis for patients with severe carditis in New
Zealand and Australia, respectively. In both countries, exceptions
are made for individual circumstances warranting continuing
prophylaxis (e.g., patients wanting to reduce an already remote
chance of a recurrence).
It is unclear whether these prophylaxis regimens should be
followed in sporadic cases in locations with a low incidence of
ARF because these regimens have been developed mainly in
regions where ARF remains a significant burden. In a prospective study in modern Chile, 59 patients with ARF judged to be
at low risk for recurrences were permitted to receive no secondary prophylaxis. During the 3349 patient-months of follow-up,
only two ARF attacks were observed, and the authors suggested
that ARF prophylaxis could be discontinued safely in patients
with a low risk of recurrence who undergo careful prospective
surveillance.
The efficacy of secondary prophylaxis depends entirely on
compliance. In a prospective international study involving 1790
patients, the prevalence of new ARF attacks was 0.45% in compliant patients compared with 11.5% in noncompliant patients.
Only one in five patients with RF from Aborigine communities
of Australia received 80% or more of the scheduled doses.
Reasons for failed compliance were painfulness, a high frequency of
injections, a long treatment period, and the patients’ low socioeconomic and educational levels. In the GOAL trial, secondary
antibiotic prophylaxis reduced the risk of latent RHD progression
in children and adolescents.
Infective Endocarditis Prophylaxis.
Infective endocarditis (IE) is a potentially lethal complication of RHD. Antibiotic
prophylaxis prior to procedures has not been proven effective in
preventing IE, but evidence supporting this practice comes primarily from animal models or empirical observations in addition
to expert opinion.
All patients with RHD or prosthetic valves
should receive IE antibiotic prophylaxis before dental procedures
and other procedures expected to produce bacteremia. People with
a history of ARF but no valvular damage do not require endocarditis prophylaxis.
Transesophageal echocardiogram and esophagus-gastro
duodenoscopy, colonoscopy, and cystoscopy are procedures
that do not require IE prophylaxis in the absence of active
infection. A different antibiotic should be offered for people
receiving secondary prophylaxis for RF. Guidelines aimed at

the optimal diagnosis and management (including prophylaxis) of IE are offered and constantly updated by specialized
organizations.
Vaccine Development
Although RHD control programs have been implemented worldwide, the disease remains a major health problem in several
countries. In addition, the epidemiologic growth of streptococcal diseases in undeveloped and developing countries has encouraged many groups to study vaccine candidates to prevent GAS
infections.
Strep A vaccines have been studied since the 1920s.
As well
as reducing pharyngitis, impetigo, ARF, and RHD, an effective
Strep A vaccine given in early childhood would prevent other
Strep A diseases, including post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis,
cellulitis, and severe and frequently fatal invasive disorders (i.e.,
bacteremia, toxic shock syndrome, necrotizing fasciitis, and infections in pregnant women and newborn babies).
Several vaccine development studies have been proposed
previously without satisfactory results. In the past two decades,
studies on vaccine development have improved with advances
in genomics, proteomics, and immunomics; still, most vaccines
remain in pre-clinical testing phases and few have reached phase
I and II.
To date, the most promising vaccines under development
incorporate C-terminal protective epitopes and differ concerning
their mechanisms of action. One is based on a minimal protective epitope that induces protective antibodies
; the other is
a long peptide (55 mers) identified as StreptInCor.
The advantage of the StreptInCor vaccine model is the possibility of the vaccine epitope undergoing processing by antigen-presenting cells
(monocytes and/or macrophages) to generate several peptides that
induce both B and T immune responses, resulting in a safe and
robust immune response.
Experimental assays have demonstrated that the StreptInCor peptide induces high titers of opsonic
and neutralizing antibodies in outbred immunized mice.
Using class II HLA transgenic mice, it was possible to evaluate the
immunogenicity and safety of the StreptInCor vaccine epitope for
1 year. Specific and nonauto-reactive antibodies were produced.
No autoimmune or other off-target pathologic reactions were
observed in hearts or other organs of the mice.
Research has identified many potential vaccine targets (antigens) on the Strep A bacteria with promising results in laboratory and animal studies. Unfortunately, there have been scientific,
regulatory, and commercial obstacles that have delayed the progress of a vaccine and, almost a century following initial vaccine
development efforts, there is no safe and reliable human Strep A
vaccine available.
The development of a Strep A vaccine is the
most attractive opportunity for a single medical intervention to
meaningfully reduce the global burden of RHD.

## Post-streptococcal Arthritis

Although noncanonical pathogens are increasingly recognized to
play a potential role in reactive arthritis, infections that affect the
gastrointestinal or genitourinary tract are most frequently implicated in this diagnosis (see
Chapter 77
). However, the GAS and
Chlamydia pneumoniae
infections that affect the respiratory tract
have also been implicated in reactive immune-mediated aseptic
arthritis. A disputed question is whether post-streptococcal reactive
arthritis (PSRA) should be considered an atypical and incomplete
form of ARF or a separate disease. The small number of available
publications is predominantly based on small sample sizes and is
hampered by the lack of a generally accepted definition of PSRA.
These case series have suggested significant dissimilarities between
the two conditions. Whereas ARF affects mainly children age 4
to 9 years, PSRA seems to have a bimodal age distribution with
peaks between 8 and 14 years and between 21 and 37 years.
Whereas RF symptoms typically commence 2 to 3 weeks after the
streptococcal infection, the latency period in PSRA is approximately 10 days. Arthritis in PSRA is often additive and symmetric. Knee and ankle joints are usually involved, but small joint
and axial involvement also occur. In PSRA, the mean duration
of arthritis is approximately 2 months, but subsequent arthralgias
may persist for 3 years. In up to 19% of patients, arthritis recurs
after an interval of a few months. The response to salicylates and
other NSAIDs is dramatic in people who have arthritis associated
with ARF but less convincing in patients with PSRA.
A retrospective series involving 26 patients demonstrated that
polytendonitis, tenosynovitis, and enthesitis are common presentations in PSRA and can be the only manifestation.
This finding, and the possibility of axial involvement, raises suspicion that
what is currently known as PSRA is genuinely a heterogeneous
group of disorders, some sharing features of spondyloarthropathies, with others being more similar to ARF. An animal model
of rheumatoid arthritis may facilitate our understanding of this
heterogeneity. In this model, rats are treated via intraperitoneal
injection of streptococcus cell wall fragments, which are highly
resistant to biodegradation.
Within 24 to 48 hours of administration, complement-dependent acute inflammation develops in
the peripheral joints of the animal, together with the dissemination of
cell wall fragments in the blood vessels of the synovia and subchondral bone marrow. In some rats, the initial inflammation subsides;
in others, it persists or subsides and recurs. The chronic, T cell–
dependent erosive polyarthritis that affects some rats is believed
to be initiated by an inflammatory process promoted by macrophages struggling to degrade the cell wall fragments. Chronic
joint inflammation develops only in susceptible rats but is not
dependent on specific streptococcus strains. Remarkably, chronic
arthritis will not develop in rats raised in a germ-free environment,
suggesting that streptococcus cell wall fragments do not elicit a
specific autoimmune reaction but instead function as an adjuvant,
promoting other cross-reactive immune processes involving other
bacteria, perhaps in the gastrointestinal or genitourinary tract. In
PSRA, the streptococcus infection may have a similar adjuvant
rule, promoting only latent autoimmunity (breach of immune tolerance). In such a case, the different clinical pictures of the PSRA
spectrum would follow the different individual susceptibilities.
The most important practical question to be answered is
whether patients with PSRA have an increased risk of RHD
and require secondary prophylaxis. Three echocardiographybased studies in children with PSRA provided evidence of cardiac involvement in 6 of the combined 76 patients. Adults with
PSRA were shown to be free of valvular abnormalities by echocardiographic screening in two case series and in one prospective study involving 75 patients who did not receive antibiotic
prophylaxis and were followed up for almost 9 years.
Because
the present literature on PSRA is mainly based on American and
western European populations, the conclusions may not apply
to people in other parts of the world. The WHO ARF guidelines
state that “patients with arthritis following a streptococcal upper
respiratory infection should be considered to have RF if they fulfill the Jones criteria.” The New Zealand and Australian guidelines state that the diagnosis of PSRA should rarely be made in

high-risk populations and with caution in low-risk populations.
In those countries, patients diagnosed with PSRA at a high risk
of developing ARF should receive secondary prophylaxis for at
least 5 years. Low-risk patients should receive this treatment for
at least 1 year. An absence of valve involvement should again be
confirmed by echocardiography prior to the discontinuation of
antibiotics.

## Conclusion

Acute rheumatic fever is triggered by
S. pyogenes
infection and is
mediated by innate and adaptive immune responses influenced
by environmental and genetic factors. The autoimmune reactivity that follows infection is due to molecular mimicry between
human proteins and bacterial antigens. An imbalance between
inflammatory/anti-inflammatory cytokines produced in response
to the infection leads to acute and chronic inflammation. In addition, the low numbers of Treg cells facilitate increased inflammatory reactivity and chronic disease.
Although rare in most developed countries, rheumatic fever
remains the most common cause of acquired valvular disease
worldwide. Misdiagnosis can lead to severe consequences, so
high levels of suspicion are recommended, especially for high-risk
populations. The diagnosis is based on the 2015 Jones criteria,
which uses clinical, laboratory, and echocardiographic findings
and distinguishes low- and high-risk populations. Although adequate pharyngitis treatment can prevent most ARF cases, primary
prevention policies have never been demonstrated to reduce the
incidence of the disease effectively, and a vaccine against
Streptococcus
is eagerly awaited. Secondary prophylaxis prevents new
attacks and minimizes sequelae in the long term but is dependent
on adequate compliance.
