## Childhood-Onset Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

### Definition and Classification

Childhood-onset systemic lupus erythematosus (cSLE) is a
chronic autoimmune disorder with potential to involve any organ,
most commonly affecting the skin, joints, kidneys, lungs, cardiovascular (CV) system, hematopoietic cells, muscles, and central
and peripheral nervous systems. The clinical presentation of SLE
in both adults and children is heterogeneous, and disease manifestations range from mild to life-threatening. The disease is characterized by periods of disease quiescence and flare. Approximately
20% of SLE patients are diagnosed before age 18 years, and onset
of SLE in childhood is associated with higher mortality and more
widespread and severe organ involvement compared with adult
SLE (aSLE).
Childhood-onset lupus is defined as onset at age younger
than 18 years.
Because no diagnostic criteria specific for cSLE
exist, classification criteria developed for use in adults participating in clinical trials are often used, albeit with limitations, in
clinical practice. The 1997 American College of Rheumatology
the 2012 Systemic Lupus Inter(ACR) Classification Criteria,
national Collaborating Clinics (SLICC) Classification Criteria,
and the 2019 European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(EULAR)/ACR Classification Criteria for SLE
are discussed in
Chapter 81 and have been validated in cSLE populations with varying sensitivities (69% to 98%) and specificities (67% to 100%).

### Epidemiology

Childhood-onset lupus occurs worldwide, and disease prevalence
is lower in children (2 to 34/100,000) compared with adults (20
to 150/100,000).
Reported incidence ranges from 0.2 to
2.5/100,000 per year.
As in adults, cSLE is more common
in Hispanic and non-White populations.
SLE is more likely
to develop in females than males, and the ratio of female to male
involvement varies across age groups. The female to male ratio
is highest between puberty and menopause (ranging from 5:1
to 10:1), whereas before puberty the ratio is lower, estimated at
3:1 to 4:1.
Diagnosis of SLE at an age younger than 5 years is
unusual, whereas diagnosis in adolescence is most common with
peak age of onset at 12.6 years.

### Clinical Features

Childhood-onset SLE manifests with a vast array of clinical symptoms and findings. The most common presenting findings in
cSLE include fever, malar rash, arthritis, glomerulonephritis, neuropsychiatric disease, and hematologic abnormalities.

high-dose corticosteroids (CSs) and cyclophosphamide.
Cutaneous Manifestations
Cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CLE) can occur in isolation or
in the context of SLE. In children presenting with isolated CLE,
evolution to cSLE can occur in up to 26% and is most likely to
occur in the first year after diagnosis.
As discussed more extensively in Chapter 81, subtypes of CLE include acute, subacute and
chronic CLE. Acute CLE manifestations are photo-distributed on
the face (malar rash) or more generally and can rarely include toxic
epidermolysis. Subacute CLE rashes include annular, papulosquamous, and vesiculobullous lesions. Chronic CLE includes discoid
lupus erythematosus (DLE; which can be localized, generalized,
or hypertrophic), lupus panniculitis (also known as lupus profundus), chilblain lupus, hypertrophic lupus, lichenoid CLE, and
tumid lupus. Of these, DLE is the most common, and characteristic histologic features include atrophy follicular plugging. DLE
often leads to abnormal pigmentation and scarring. Individual
patients may experience more than one form of CLE.

Lupus Nephritis
Lupus nephritis (LN) is more common in cSLE compared with
aSLE present in up 40% to 70%.
As in aSLE, LN is suspected
in the presence of unexplained proteinuria, hematuria, urinary
casts, hypertension, abnormal renal function, and edema; renal
biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosis. The distribution of histologic classes of LN is similar to adults (see Chapter 81).
Proliferative LN (International Society of Nephrology/Renal Pathology
Society [ISN/RPS] classes III and IV) often presents with nephritic
urine (proteinuria, hematuria, casts), hypertension, and acute or
chronic kidney injury. Membranous LN (ISN/RPS class V) as in
adults typically presents with proteinuria, nephrotic syndrome
but preserved renal function.
However, LN can be insidious,
sometimes appearing with few or no symptoms, highlighting the
importance of routine monitoring for renal involvement.
Neuropsychiatric Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
Neuropsychiatric systemic lupus erythematosus (NPSLE) is challenging to diagnose, especially in developing children and adolescents who are at risk for mood disorders. The reported prevalence
of NPSLE in children (cNPSLE) and adolescents ranges from
22% to 95% with more frequent occurrence in cSLE, typically
early in the disease course.
The ACR nomenclature and case
definitions for NPSLE (see Chapter 81), primarily derived from
adult data, are used in cSLE. Common presenting symptoms in
cNPSLE include headache, mood disorder, seizures, cerebrovascular disease, and chorea.

Macrophage Activation Syndrome
Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), also known as secondary hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), can complicate
SLE and incurs significant mortality. The underlying pathogenesis
of MAS in SLE may differ from that of systemic onset juvenile
idiopathic arthritis and Still’s disease.
Macrophage activation
syndrome is likely to occur at onset of cSLE and without an infectious trigger.
Typical clinical findings include high fevers,
hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias, liver dysfunction, coagulopathy, hyperferritinemia, and falling erythrocyte sedimentation rate
(ESR) despite evidence of systemic inflammation. Levels of soluble interleukin (IL)-2 receptor are often elevated.

### Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests

Due to the heterogeneous and systemic nature of cSLE, the differential diagnosis is broad and often includes multiple infections
(including Epstein–Barr virus, cytomegalovirus, histoplasmosis,
coccidiomycosis, and tuberculosis), systemic-onset juvenile idiopathic arthritis, vasculitides, MAS, malignancies (especially leukemia and lymphoma), other systemic causes of glomerulonephritis,
neurologic and cardiopulmonary disease, and drug-induced lupus.
The diagnosis of cSLE is clinical and made on the basis of
a detailed history, meticulous physical examination, laboratory studies, and tissue biopsy when required.
The diagnostic
approach mirrors that used in aSLE (see Chapter 81) and includes
assessment of complete blood count (CBC), hepatic and renal
function, complement protein levels, direct Coombs’s assay, and
measurement of autoantibodies including antibodies to nuclear
antibodies (ANA), double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), extractable
nuclear antigens (Ro/SSA, La/SSB, Smith, and ribonucleoprotein
[RNP]), and antiphospholipid antibodies. In cSLE, certain autoantibodies are detected more frequently than in adults, including
anti-dsDNA, anti-cardiolipin, anti-histone, and anti-ribosomal P

While not yet used in clinical practice, several biomarkers show
promise in diagnosing and monitoring cSLE. The IFN signature
has been well described in cSLE but does not always correlate with
disease activity.
The presence of neutrophil extracellular traps

low C3 levels.
Initial studies suggest that elevated levels of cellbound complement activation products, such as C4d deposition

cSLE diagnosis and correlate with disease activity.

As in adults, neuroimaging to detect NPSLE includes standard
and functional MRI and positron-emission tomography (PET)
imaging with technetium. Abnormal findings on neuroimaging include white matter hyperintensities seen on T2-weighted
images, cerebral atrophy, and small cortical infarcts. However,
imaging may be normal in patients with diffuse manifestations
such as headaches, cognitive dysfunction, and psychosis. While
no serum or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) serologic tests are diagnostic of NPSLE, CSF analysis may reveal nonspecific signs of
intrathecal inflammation, including elevated protein levels and
white blood cell counts. The association between anti-ribosomal P
antibodies and psychosis in adult NPSLE has not been confirmed
in pediatric studies. The developmentally appropriate neurocognitive test, pediatric automated neuropsychological assessment
(peds ANAM), is used to detect and follow neuropsychological
abnormalities, although limited by its length and requirement for
special computer software.

### Treatment and Management

Treatment decisions are often based upon adult clinical trial data
as only one drug (belimumab) is approved by the U.S. Food and
Drug Administration for cSLE. Although many aspects of adult
and cSLE pathophysiology are similar, important differences exist
and affect management, including pharmacokinetics, palatability of medications, ability to swallow pills, neurodevelopment,
growth, puberty, and high-risk adolescent behaviors. Similar to
aSLE, treatment of cSLE is tailored to the severity of specific manifestations. Some treatments are recommended for all children and
adolescents with SLE, including hydroxychloroquine and photoprotection. Glucocorticoids are the mainstay for initial treatment
of significant organ involvement; however, the substantial toxicity associated with long-term therapy mandates careful use, and
steroid-sparing approaches are recommended to limit cumulative
exposure.
Hydroxychloroquine
Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ), an anti-malarial drug, is the firstline therapy for cSLE. Because HCQ use is linked to reduced
risk of flare, clotting, CV disease, lupus-related organ damage,
and improved survival, this medication is recommended for all
with cSLE if no contraindications exist. Based on adult studies,
hydroxychloroquine has a favorable safety profile except for its
dose-dependent risk for an irreversible retinopathy. In childhood,
HCQ may be prescribed either in pill or suspension form with
a recommended dose of
≤
5 mg/kg/day; however, weight-based
dosing may not correlate well with drug concentrations.
Dosed
appropriately, the rate of retinal toxicity after 10 years of exposure
is <2%. As in aSLE, routine ophthalmology screening with visual
fields and spectral-domain optical coherence tomography is recommended. Young children may not be able to cooperate with
visual field testing, and involvement of a pediatric ophthalmologist is recommended.
Glucocorticoids
Glucocorticoids prescribed in oral tablet, suspension, or intravenous (IV) preparations remain the mainstay for initial treatment
of significant organ involvement in cSLE. For children, the typical dose of oral prednisone or prednisolone ranges from 0.5 to
2 mg/kg/day, depending on severity of illness. IV methylprednisolone “pulses” of 30 mg/kg/dose (maximum of 1 g/day) for 1
to 5 days are often used to treat severe disease. Although highly
effective in suppressing lupus activity, side effects associated with
glucocorticoid use including weight gain, growth failure, avascular
necrosis, poor bone mineralization, pubertal delay, acne, striae,
and mood fluctuations can be particularly problematic in children and adolescents; thus minimizing glucocorticoid exposure is
critically important. Glucocorticoid tapering is tailored based on
improvements in disease activity as measured by clinical and laboratory indices. If glucocorticoids cannot be reduced or eliminated
due to continued disease activity, then a steroid-sparing, diseasemodifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) or biologic therapy is
recommended.

Other Treatments
Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are used judiciously in cSLE, primarily in treatment of musculoskeletal symptoms and pericarditis.
It is not clear whether the CV toxicity
associated with NSAID use in adults extends to the pediatric
population. Plasma exchange may be used to manage severe and
life-threatening cSLE manifestations.
Nonpharmacologic
interventions including aerobic exercise, dietary supplementation,
psychotherapy, and patient education/support may be promising
adjunctive therapies but require further study.
Approach to Treating Cutaneous Manifestations
Photoprotection is a cornerstone of prevention of cutaneous lupus
lesions. UV radiation exposure can increase the risk of cutaneous
flare and may contribute to systemic flare through delayed clearance of apoptotic keratinocytes.
Routine, daily use of sunscreen
is recommended, as are other photoprotective behaviors, including avoiding midday sun and wearing sun-protective clothing.
When choosing a sunscreen, it is important to look for ingredients
that provide both UVA and UVB protection, including physical
agents such as titanium dioxide, zinc oxide, and chemical filters
such as avobenzone. Sunblock with sun protection factor (SPF) 30
to 50 is recommended and must be applied in sufficient amounts
at least 20 to 30 minutes before sun exposure and reapplied frequently.
Children and adolescents may require school modifications to reduce sun exposure and allow application of sunscreen.
Topical corticosteroids can be effective for localized lesions but
risk cutaneous atrophy with long-term use. Topical calcineurin
therapy can spare topical steroid exposure. Intralesional steroids
can be effective for lesions refractory to topical steroids. Systemic
therapy may be required for cutaneous disease and can include
hydroxychloroquine, systemic glucocorticoids, DMARDs, and
biologic agents. In addition, dapsone, quinacrine, oral retinoids,
intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), thalidomide, and lenalidomide are agents sometimes prescribed in treatment of refractory
cutaneous disease.
Randomized controlled trials for cutaneous
lupus, particularly in the pediatric setting, are lacking.

CTPs for membranous LN have not been developed, and no
pediatric clinical trial data are available to guide treatment decisions. According to a national survey of pediatric rheumatologists,
the most common medications used to manage membranous LN
include glucocorticoids, renin angiotensin blockade, mycophenolate mofetil, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, and azathioprine.
In the setting of end-stage renal disease (ESRD) due to cLN,
hemodialysis is used more often than peritoneal dialysis, and renal
transplant is recommended if feasible.
Approach to Treating Neuropsychiatric Lupus
No randomized controlled trial (RCT) or CTP is available to
guide treatment decisions in cNPSLE, although some recommendations have been published through the Single Hub and Access
Point for Pædiatric Rheumatology in Europe (SHARE) initiative.
Treatment is tailored to the specific NPSLE manifestations
and severity of disease. NPSLE manifestations clearly linked to
ischemic events are treated with anti-coagulation unless contraindicated by comorbidities. For nonischemic, inflammatory NPSLE
manifestations, the treatment approach is similar to that used in
LN treatment. Induction approaches include use of high-dose
glucocorticoids and cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate mofetil,
or azathioprine. Rituximab, IVIG, and plasma exchange are
options. With improved control of disease, maintenance therapy,
often with mycophenolate mofetil or azathioprine, is prescribed.
Adjunctive treatment with anxiolytics, anti-depressants, and antipsychotics may be required. Comanagement with neurology, psychiatry, and psychology is often needed.

Opportunities for Prevention
As survival has improved for cSLE, long-term complications have
emerged as significant challenges, prompting efforts focusing on
reducing the risk of infection, optimizing bone health, and reducing CV risk.
Infection Prevention.
Due to immunosuppression from SLE
and its treatment, children and adolescents are at significant risk
for severe and life-threatening infections.
Vaccinations provide
an important opportunity to prevent infections. Nonlive vaccinations, including those against pneumococcus, meningococcus,
Haemophilus influenzae
, influenza, and human papillomavirus
are considered safe, with variable immunogenicity depending on
immunosuppressive therapy in cSLE.
The ACR developed
recommendations supporting COVID vaccine administration for
adult and pediatric patients with rheumatic disease, and adolescents with lupus and other rheumatic diseases develop immune
responses to mRNA COVID-19 vaccine comparable to healthy
controls.
The safety of live vaccines has not been established in immunocompromised populations. Thus live virus vaccines are withheld
in setting of treatment with most DMARDs or biologics and with
significant prednisone exposure, particularly when dose is
≥
20
mg/day or 2 mg/kg/day for children <10 kg for >14 days.

Fortunately,
Pneumocystis jiroveci
pneumonia occurs infrequently
in lupus, but the infection is associated with significant mortality. As with rheumatologists for adults, pediatric rheumatologists’
thresholds for starting prophylactic trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole, dapsone, or pentamidine in immunosuppressed lupus patients
varies.
Optimizing Bone Health.
Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis and failure to achieve peak bone mass are potential complications of cSLE. In cSLE, bone density loss occurs due to effects of
chronic inflammation, vitamin D deficiency, decreased physical
activity, and glucocorticoid therapy.
Dual-energy x-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) is the preferred approach for assessing changes
in bone mineral density, although CT may be more accurate.
Monitoring with DEXA is recommended in children prescribed
glucocorticoid therapy
≥
3 months.
Lateral thoracolumbar
spine imaging can detect asymptomatic vertebral compression
fractures.
Ensuring adequate vitamin D and calcium intake, counseling regarding weight-bearing exercise, and avoiding risk factors,
including smoking, are universally recommended. Bisphosphonates
are not routinely prescribed for the treatment or prevention of
osteoporosis in cSLE but may be considered in severe cases.
Cardiovascular Risk Reduction.
Because atherosclerosis
begins in childhood and is a major long-term source of morbidity and mortality in SLE, anticipatory counseling concerning
CV risk reduction and screening for modifiable CV risk factors
needs to start at a young age. Control of blood pressure and
lipids with dietary and pharmacologic interventions is indicated.
The Atherosclerosis Prevention in Pediatric Lupus Erythematosus (APPLE) trial results did not suggest that statins should
be routinely offered to all children and adolescents with SLE,
but post hoc analyses suggested that higher-risk subgroups may
benefit.
Reproductive Health.
Most females with SLE have preserved
fertility. While pregnancy can usually be safely accomplished,
adverse outcomes including maternal flare, pre-eclampsia, pregnancy loss, prematurity, and intrauterine growth restriction are
concerning potential complications, particularly in adolescents
and young women.
Pregnancy outcomes are optimal in the
setting of well-controlled disease and when women are taking
medications compatible with pregnancy. Adolescents are at risk
for having unprotected intercourse, and many adolescents with
lupus are taking teratogenic medications. Given the need to avoid
pregnancy during times of active disease or when on teratogenic
medication, assessment of intention to become pregnant and
counseling regarding reproductive health concerns and contraception options are important in postmenarchal (usually ages
≥
12
years) females. Due to estrogen’s potential to incite disease activity and clotting, contraceptive medications containing significant
doses of estrogen are often avoided. The results of the Safety of
Exogenous Estrogens in Lupus Erythematosus National Assessment (SELENA) trial, which included adult women with low
disease activity and excluded those with lupus anti-coagulant
or moderate to high-titer anti-cardiolipin antibodies, suggested
that oral contraceptives with low-dose estrogen do not increase
the risk of clotting or flare in this limited population.
Longer acting contraception such as intrauterine device, implanted
etonogestrel, or intramuscular medroxyprogesterone is often
recommended to adolescents given the risks associated with
estrogen and nonadherence. Consultation with an adolescent
medicine specialist can be very helpful in managing reproductive health concerns.
School Concerns.
Children and adolescents with lupus
have increased risk for lower school educational and vocational
achievement compared with peers, despite similar neurocognitive function and demographic factors.
Children and
adolescents with SLE may require school accommodations. In
certain situations, formal personalized learning plans may be
necessary to assist children with lupus achieve their educational
goals. Examples of these plans in the United States include
504 plans and individualized education plans (IEPs). Neuropsychological testing can help identify deficits and potential
interventions.
Mental Health Concerns.
Mental health disorders are prevalent in cSLE, particularly depression (20% to 59%) and anxiety
(17% to 37%). Remarkably, 14% of patients with cSLE report
history of suicidal ideation.
Untreated mental health disorders affect cSLE disease activity, management, and health care
utilization.
Disparities are evident in the mental health of children and adolescents with lupus. Non-White race is associated
with increased risk of depression and suicidal ideation in cSLE
but with lower likelihood of receiving pharmacologic treatment
for mental health disorders.
Depression severity is associated with poor medication adherence in cSLE, and treatment of
mental health disorders improves adherence.
Thus it is
critically important to identify and treat mental health disorders
in patients with cSLE.
Management of mental health disorders in cSLE is challenging, barriers such as stigma and limited mental health care access
contribute to low utilization of mental health care.
While
not performed universally, routine mental health care screening can improve detection of anxiety, depression, and suicidal
ideation and normalize mental health issues in patients with
cSLE.
Transition to Adult Care.
The transition from child-centered
to adult-centered care is a vulnerable period during which adolescents and young adults with SLE are at risk for gaps in care
and higher morbidity. Transition refers to the gradual process of
preparing the adolescent and young adult for self-management
in an adult care setting; transfer represents the time when care is
handed off to an adult provider. While no SLE-specific guidelines exist for transition to adult care, disease agnostic approaches
and interventions have shown promise.
In general, it is recommended that pediatric rheumatologists develop a transition
policy and share it with patients and families, provide developmentally appropriate self-management recommendations, assess
readiness for transition to adult care beginning at age 14 years,
and track progress and confirm ultimate transfer of care to adult
rheumatologists.

### Outcomes in Childhood-Onset Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Outcomes and Health Disparities
The diagnosis of SLE during childhood increases risk of organ
damage and premature death. Due to longer exposure to disease
and treatment, cSLE is associated with more disease-related damage accrual compared with aSLE.
In particular, development of
cataracts, avascular necrosis, and renal damage are more likely to
occur with cSLE versus aSLE.
The risk of lupus-related damage
is inversely related to age at onset, with prepubertal-onset SLE
patients having higher risk of damage than peri-pubertal or postpubertal.
Premature atherosclerosis is clearly linked to SLE,
and increased carotid intimal medial thickness is evident even in

children and adolescents with SLE.
Malignancies are more frequent in cSLE than in the age-matched population.
Growth
failure and delayed puberty are SLE-related morbidities unique
to childhood and adolescence.
Lower health-related quality of
life in cSLE is associated with female sex, high disease activity, the
presence of damage, non-White ethnicity, and the need for cyclophosphamide or rituximab therapy.
In general, poor prognostic
factors for cSLE include male sex, earlier age at diagnosis, Hispanic ethnicity or non-White race, lower socioeconomic status,
and presence of renal or CNS disease.
Fortunately, advances in understanding of SLE pathogenesis, diagnosis, treatment, and supportive care have improved,
leading to longer survival. The 5- and 10-year survival rates,
which were lower than 40% in the 1960s, have increased to
>90% in the 2000s, with higher survival rates in high- versus
low-income countries.
Although there is no cure for SLE,
most children and adolescents with SLE are now expected to
survive into adulthood. Despite these improvements, mortality rates remain higher in cSLE versus aSLE, and survival rates
have plateaued in recent decades. Mortality rates are higher in
Hispanic and non-White adolescents and young adults.
In the United States SLE is the tenth leading overall cause of
death, the number one cause of death from a chronic inflammatory disease in the 15 to 24 years age group, and the fifth
leading cause of death in Black and Hispanic females aged 15
to 24 years.
Across the age spectrum, early in the course of
SLE, death most often occurs because of infection or severe SLE
organ involvement (particularly renal and CNS disease). In the
longer term, causes of mortality shift to infection, atherosclerosis, or malignancy.
In parallel to overall survival, renal survival rates have
improved; however, complete remission is achieved in only
40% to 60% of cLN despite aggressive therapy. Children who
develop ESRD due to LN have a 5-year mortality of approximately 22%.
Compared with other children on hemodialysis,
those with cLN have double the mortality rate and are less likely
to receive renal transplants despite the fact that, post-transplant, graft survival and infectious complications are comparable.
Black and Hispanic youth with cLN are more
likely to develop ESRD but are less likely than white youth
to receive a renal transplant. Geographic disparities are also a
concern in cLN, with those in the southern United States less
likely to receive renal transplants and more at risk of death during hospitalizations.
Quality of Care and Improvement Initiatives
Because achieving clinical remission in SLE is often unattainable, the concept of a lupus low disease activity state (LLDAS)
has emerged as a goal for SLE treatment.
Attaining
LLDAS reduces the risk of severe flare and accumulating disease
damage in cSLE.
Provider and practice variation contribute
to differences in the ability to attain LLDAS and clinical remission targets in cSLE.
Attempts to reduce variation in care
include international consensus efforts to standardize steroid
dosing cLN,
the SHARE initiative’s evidence-based recommendations for diagnosis and treatment of cSLE,
and a recommended set of 26 quality indicators for the care of patients
with cSLE.
Adherence to quality indicators in cSLE at pediatric rheumatology centers varies.
Approaches successfully used to enhance adherence to quality indicators include
pre-visit planning and development of multispecialty lupus
clinics.

## Drug-Induced Lupus

### Definition and Classification

Drug-induced lupus erythematosus (DILE) is the presence of SLE
manifestations occurring after exposure to specific medications in
individuals with no prior history of SLE. Medications commonly
implicated in DILE are listed in
eTable 110.1
. Specific classification criteria for DILE have not been published, and no published
data address differences between adult and pediatric drug-induced
lupus. Thus DILE is presumed not to differ across the age spectrum (see adult SLE; Chapter 81).

### Genetics and Pathogenesis

The pathogenesis of DILE is not well understood. Apart from an
association with human leukocyte antigen, DR subregion (HLADR4), no specific genetic abnormalities have been associated with
DILE, but acetylator status may be implicated.
Hypotheses
regarding pathogenesis include drugs serving as haptens prompting autoimmune response, direct cytotoxicity of drugs, and consequences of DNA hypomethylation.

### Clinical Manifestations

As in adults, DILE affects males and females equally and is
unlikely to cause significant renal or CNS disease. It can be challenging to distinguish SLE and DILE; however, some clinical and
serologic features and the resolution of symptoms after drug withdrawal can help arrive at the correct diagnosis. In general, DILE
is milder than SLE. Hepatic involvement in drug-induced lupus
is common.
Laboratory abnormalities can include circulating anti-histone
antibodies (present in up to 95% of individuals with DILE),
although these antibodies are not specific and can also be present in SLE unassociated with drug exposure. Anti-dsDNA and
anti-Smith antibodies are unlikely to occur in DILE, and hypocomplementemia is less common in DILE than in SLE. Epigenetic abnormalities are important in drug-induced lupus, and
hydralazine and procainamide, for example, can cause DNA
hypomethylation.

### Treatment

Treatment of drug-induced lupus involves withdrawal of the
offending medication and immunosuppressive therapy if indicated. Resolution of clinical findings may take months to years.

the few rheumatic disorders presenting in the neonatal period or
early infancy. NLE is a passive autoimmune condition caused by
transfer of circulating maternal autoantibodies to the fetal circulation. No diagnostic criteria are available for NLE.

### Treatment

Noncardiac NLE manifestations are reversible and usually do not
require any treatment. Topical CS are sometimes used to treat the
rash, and IVIG has been used for severe cytopenias. However,
congenital heart block and cardiomyopathy are irreversible and
can cause death and morbidity; thus therapies to prevent or delay
the development of cardiac involvement are needed.
Approaches to Detection and Prevention of Cardiac NLE
Given the risk for NLE, it is recommended that pregnant women
with circulating anti-Ro/SSA or anti-La/SSB antibodies undergo
monitoring of fetal PR interval and cardiac function during pregnancy beginning at 16 weeks. Several retrospective studies suggest that maternal HCQ use during pregnancy reduces the risk
of cardiac NLE in offspring, and a prospective open-label, single-arm, two-stage study showed reduction of CHB to 50% of
historical rates. Two prospective observational studies using IVIG
(at replacement doses of 400 mg/kg every 3 weeks during second
trimester) to prevent CHB anti-Ro–positive mothers with previously affected child showed unconvincing results. Treatment with
anti-inflammatory doses of IVIG (1 to 2 g/kg) to prevent NLE has
not been studied.
Approaches to Treating the Fetus Affected With NLE
Evidence is scant and with mixed results for in utero treatment
after cardiac NLE develops. Maternal treatment with fluorinated
corticosteroids has some reported efficacy treating second-degree
heart block.
Treatment with IVIG is sometimes used in the
setting of second-degree heart block; a clinical trial for this indication is underway. Anti-inflammatory therapy does not appear
effective once third-degree heart block has developed.
β
-Agonists
can be used to increase fetal heart rates in the setting of fetal
bradycardia.
Fluorinated CS and/or IVIG pre- and postnatal
treatment improve survival in children with NLE cardiomyopathy/endocardial fibroelastosis.
Cardiac pacing is required for
some children with complete heart block.
Given their family histories, children with NLE may be at risk for autoimmune
disease.

## Conclusion

Although the pathophysiology of cSLE is likely similar to adult
SLE, clinical presentations, prognosis, and some aspects of
management differ. Children and adolescents with SLE have
higher disease activity at presentation; are at substantial risk
for organ damage, including renal and CNS complications;
and have overall higher mortality compared with adult counterparts. Especially because children and adolescents face a
lifetime burden of disease and effects of therapy, early intervention is indicated to reduce the risk of long-term complications,
including CV disease and bone loss. Randomized controlled
clinical trials for pediatric SLE are lacking, and therefore therapeutic approaches are inferred from adult studies. However,
children and adolescents require special attention to uniquely
pediatric concerns, including impact of therapies on growth
and body image, compliance concerns, and psychosocial and
transition needs.
