## Introduction

Over the past few decades, a plethora of work has been done to
develop, evaluate, and validate a number of quantitative health
outcomes in rheumatic diseases. This work has provided the
framework necessary for the field to perform key research trials to
understand disease impact and to change the treatment landscape
in a number of important diseases and inform management strategies. Further, in addition to clear benefits that these outcomes
have provided for research, the formal assessment of health outcomes within clinical care may also promote more precise care and
provide opportunities to monitor the quality of that care.
The aim of this chapter is to summarize and discuss health outcomes in the rheumatic diseases and evaluate their utility in both
research and clinical contexts. Health outcomes may focus on a
single construct such as fatigue, or combine multiple constructs
that are important to patients with a particular disease to evaluate
and quantify the overall impact of the disease (e.g., disease activity). Disease-specific outcomes are often useful for clinical research
because they combine a number of different constructs related to
the disease into a single, quantifiable measure and used to capture
the overall impact of the disease and the effects of interventions to
treat the disease. In other contexts, both clinical and research, it
may be preferable to assess a construct within a single symptom or
health domain in order to better understand the experience of the
individual in a key area. A construct aimed at a single symptom or
health domain (e.g., fatigue) can be assessed across many diseases,

uses in clinical and research settings.

## Types of Outcomes

Health outcomes are often developed with different goals in mind.
Perhaps the most obvious reason that health outcomes are defined
is to be able to test the benefit of therapies or interventions for a
condition. However, health outcomes in rheumatology are used
for many additional purposes including aiding in clinical decision making (e.g., “treat-to-target”), as well as in following and
evaluating the quality of care. Clarity about how one intends to
use the scores from an instrument, or the purpose for measuring,
is important for defining which attributes should be prioritized.
One group describes three purposes: descriptive (measurement of
a domain at a single point in time), predictive (using a current
measure to predict another outcome in the future), and evaluative (helping to determine a change in status, such as the benefit
or harm from a treatment) (
Fig. 33.1
).
Each purpose requires
slightly different evidence to support an argument that an instrument will work well in that setting. Evaluative purposes, for example, place a lot more emphasis on the elements around change,
such as responsiveness. These are not as important for descriptive applications for which change is not the focus. Understanding how and why tools were developed can help understand their
limitations in different applications. For example, an outcome

developed for testing the benefit of a treatment in a trial may not
serve the purpose of a clinician who hopes to use the measure to
inform clinical decision making or to predict who will suffer an
adverse event.

### Validity

Validity is an expression of the extent to which an outcome
assesses what it is intended to measure. The validity of an instrument is assessed in multiple ways. Some may be more appropriate
than others for specific instruments. The method of validation will
vary depending on whether other scales or measures of similar
constructs are already available with which to compare the performance of the proposed instrument.

Content Validity
This concept is often referred to as “face validity” and asks
whether the instrument covers the concepts it is intended to measure. An instrument is built by understanding a concept (e.g.,
fatigue) and then identifying the items, responses, and scoring
systems that seem to capture it. The goal is a score that represents
the domain of interest in the patients and setting within which it
will be used. Content validity can be assessed through a thorough
review of the instrument and information on its administration
by a clinical or research team with patient input. Patient partners

Construct Validity
If a measure is a good representation of the concept it is intended
to cover, then the measure should correlate with the “gold standard” measure of the concept. This is termed
criterion validity
and its assessment requires that a criterion (gold standard) exists
against which a new measure can be compared; e.g., scores on
a measure of depression might be compared with clinical diagnoses of depression. The
sensitivity
of a measure refers to the
proportion of persons who have the condition (e.g., depression)
that are correctly classified as having the condition based on
the new instrument. Alternatively, the
specificity
refers to the
proportion of persons who do not have the condition who are
correctly classified by the measure. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves may be used to illustrate the tradeoff
between sensitivity and specificity and to set ideal thresholds for
the measure.
Where no gold standard criterion exists, other means are necessary to determine the validity of the construct. This can be done
by showing that the measure correlates with other measures of the
same concept/construct (convergent validity) or by showing that
the measure correlates with other variables in a way that is consistent with what would be hypothesized; e.g., a score for depression
correlates with a measure of poor sleep quality. Construct validity
can also be supported by showing discriminant validity. An instrument that is a valid measure of a construct should not correlate
with apparently unrelated constructs; e.g., a measure of depression
should not correlate significantly with a measure of intelligence.
Predictive validity is also a useful concept that asks whether a measure correlates with the event of a criterion measure in the future;
e.g., does a measure of depression performed now predict a subsequent diagnosis of depression?

### Reliability and Reproducibility

In general, assessments of reliability and reproducibility indicate the consistency of scores over time or among different
raters or the extent to which a response of score is free from
random error (precision). Concepts of reliability and reproducibility can be assessed quantitatively using multiple approaches
depending on whether the data are dichotomous or continuous in nature. Kappa coefficients may be used to express agreement between two dichotomous assessments, correcting for the
amount of agreement that would be expected by chance. Kappa
coefficients range from 0 to 1.0, but the ceiling is influenced by
the sensitivity and specificity of the measure. Intraclass correlation (ICC) may be used to express agreement between raters
on continuous measures. The ICC is conceptually similar to a
correlation.

Test/Retest Reliability
This is the concept of assessing the stability of scores from the
measure from one administration to another. Test–retest reliability
is usually measured over a short period of time, during which little
or no change would be expected. If the results are variable between
tests when there is no expectation of change, it suggests that measured changes occurring over time in this measure may suffer from
variability simply due to poor reproducibility of the test.
Intra-/Inter-rater Reliability
A reliable test will result in the same rating or judgment on
repeated administrations on the same subject either by the same
rater or by another rater. A measure with high intra-rater and
inter-rater reliability can be trusted to not vary between administrations or administrators.

### Internal Consistency

,
When a scale is internally consistent, it is expected that the items
that compose that scale measure the same underlying construct
or theme. Internal consistency is usually reported as
Cronbach’s
coefficient alpha (
α
),
which can range from 0 to 1.0. It has been
suggested that an
α
greater than 0.80 should be considered “good”
and sufficient for tests used to compare groups.

### Responsiveness

Responsiveness is good for a measure if scores on the instrument
change to reflect changes in the underlying construct. For example,
a measure has poor responsiveness if it fails to change in a measurable way with effective treatment of the underlying disease. There
are a number of measures of sensitivity or responsiveness. One of
the most commonly used is the standardized response mean, calculated as the mean change in score divided by the standard deviation of the change scores. Standardized effect sizes may also be
used to describe how sensitive instruments are to detecting change.

### Feasibility

Feasibility covers the practical aspects of using the instrument in
its intended setting.
Does it take too much time? Is it expensive
to use? Does it require special equipment? Is it too burdensome for
your patients (e.g., language, literacy, acceptability of questions,
time of procedure, physical discomfort) or observers (training)?
Is the protocol for performing the test clear? Are the results of
the score easily interpretable? Feasibility is often one of the most
important determinants of whether a candidate instrument is
incorporated into a research study or practice.

### Meaningful States or Changes

Thresholds of “States”
Clinicians want to know whether pain is at a tolerable level or
whether a patient is in a low disease state (e.g., disease remission),
which requires us to be able to understand and benchmark scores.
Examples of this type of threshold include patient-acceptable
symptom states (PASSs),
for what patients deem to be tolerable levels of symptoms (e.g., pain level is less than 2 on a scale
of 0 to 10), and estimates of states reflecting low disease activity scores (e.g., disease remission).
These can be determined
by evaluating associations with patient’s own evaluation of their
disease or how the scores affect clinical decision making or predict
long-term outcomes. An important outcome for a clinical study
may be the meeting of one of these thresholds. The achievement
of a low disease activity state may be used to identify patients who
are less likely to suffer from adverse long-term outcomes in clinical
practice (i.e., radiographic damage in RA).
Minimally Important Differences/Changes
Minimally important differences/changes are thresholds that
define a change/difference in a score that represents an important

change/difference as reported by patients. These measures are
particularly important in interpreting whether a change or difference measured in a study is actually important or meaningful. The minimal clinically important difference (MCID) was
originally defined as the smallest difference in score that patients
would perceive as beneficial. Minimal important difference/
change was introduced to remove the emphasis from clinical
interpretations, to include both worsening and improvement,
and to differentiate between cross-sectional and longitudinal
comparisons.
Minimal Detectable Change
Minimal detectable change is a statistical concept and is an estimate of the minimal amount of change needed to differentiate
from random variation or measurement error.

### Floor and Ceiling Effects

Floor or ceiling effects occur when a large number of respondents
have the lowest or highest scores, respectively. Floor/ceiling effects
can reduce the ability of an instrument to detect change at the
ends of the scoring range. This is a problem because it suggests
that the measure does not fully capture the range of experiences
within the population. Some have recommended that floor/ceiling effects are problematic if 15% or more of responses are at
the lowest/highest score; others have suggested more stringent
criteria.

### Outcome Measures in Rheumatology (OMERACT) and Other Organizations

It is unreasonable to expect most clinicians and researchers to
understand the nuances of all outcome measures that are utilized
in the field. Fortunately, there are organizations established with
the aim of bringing information on outcomes and instruments
together for people through the creation of what they consider
suitable sets of outcomes: both what should be measured and
how it should be measured. One such organization is Outcome
Measures in Rheumatology (OMERACT),
an international
consensus-based group established in 1992 that works within the
arthritis communities (i.e., clinicians, researchers, patients, payors,
regulators, industry) to define domains and instruments of importance in different rheumatologic conditions. This organization
examines core outcome sets for research. Another organization,
the International Consortium for Health Outcome Measurement (ICHOM), looks at core outcomes for clinical practice
and quality improvement. Both work on defining a small set of
prioritized outcome domains and matching high-quality instruments that should be used across outcome assessments. There are
also resources compiled specifically for rheumatology that provide
information on specific outcome measures.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis and Peripheral Psoriatic Arthritis

The development of RA-specific outcomes has evolved over
decades and has been discussed in detail in recent reviews and
guidelines.
Outcomes developed for RA have also been extensively used in trials and clinical research studies in other forms of
peripheral inflammatory arthritis such as psoriatic arthritis (PsA).
The discussion provided here is not exhaustive.
American College of Rheumatology Response Criteria
The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) response criteria were developed as clinical trial outcomes and continue to be
widely used. This dichotomous measure of response is defined by
an improvement in swollen and tender joints, as well as a similar
improvement in three of five additional assessments. These include
patient global scores, physician global scores, patient assessments
of pain, results of the Health Assessment Questionnaire (a measure
of function and disability that is described below), and C-reactive
protein (CRP) or erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR). Thus an
improvement of 20% in each of these assessments would be considered an ACR20 response. This measure has shown excellent discrimination and construct validity for clinical trials.
ACR responses
also are associated with a meaningful response for patients. Therefore the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has
described the ACR20 as a well-validated composite end point for
clinical trials and has encouraged its use in guidance to industry on
the conduct of trials in patients with RA.
The complexity of the
measure, the fact that it is relative to some baseline measure (i.e.,
20% improvement), and the lack of ability to define thresholds of
states have made this a poor measure for use in clinical practice and
in observational research studies.

Disease Activity Score in 28 Joints
The Disease Activity Score in 28 Joints (DAS28) is a composite measure of disease activity that has been used widely for
decades. This measure combines a count of tender and swollen
joints, a patient global score, and a measure of systemic inflammation (either CRP or ESR). Each component is transformed
and combined to create a score that ranges from 0 to 9.4. The
DAS28 was developed by identifying factors that were associated with perceived high and low disease activity states and was
a good predictor of physician decision making.
The DAS28
also correlates with other measures of disease activity and of
disability and can predict radiographic damage progression
on x-rays, all of which suggests good construct validity.
The
DAS28 also has high internal consistency, high reliability, and
excellent responsiveness. Thresholds of states have been defined
for the DAS28: high,
≥
5.1; moderate, 3.2 to 5.0; low, 2.6 to
3.1; and remission, <2.6. The DAS28 is somewhat feasible to
perform in clinic and thus has been incorporated in “treat-totarget” studies aimed at goal-oriented therapy to achieve low
disease activity or remission. In addition, MCIDs in the DAS28

Simplified Disease Activity Index
The Simplified Disease Activity Index (SDAI) incorporates some
of the same components as the DAS28, including tender and
swollen joint counts, a patient global score, and a CRP level. Further, the SDAI includes a physician/provider visual analogue scale
assessment of disease activity. The SDAI (range, 0 to 86) has a
simplified approach to calculation compared with the DAS28 (the
individual components are added together without transformation), which improves feasibility for use as a clinical measure. The
SDAI is highly reliable and is strongly correlated with the DAS28,
with similar prediction of radiographic damage progression. It is
also highly responsive to change. Thresholds of states have been
defined, and the minimal clinically important improvement has
been defined.
One study showed that some patients in DAS28
remission had active disease on ultrasound unless they were also
in SDAI remission, suggesting that SDAI remission may be somewhat more strict.
The SDAI is limited in many of the same ways
as the DAS28, including the potential for bias related to comorbid
conditions such as fibromyalgia and obesity.
Clinical Disease Activity Index
The Clinical Disease Activity Index (CDAI) is similar to the
SDAI, with the exception that it does not include an inflammatory marker (ESR or CRP), which means that it can be performed
with clinical assessments only without the need to access laboratory results, improving feasibility. The continuous score represents
the sum of the four component measures (range, 0 to 76). The
components have good internal consistency (
α
= 0.87) and high
reliability. The CDAI converges with other measures such as the
DAS28 and shows moderate agreement to the DAS28 in terms of
categorization of disease states. The CDAI predicts radiographic
damage progression. The CDAI definition of remission is somewhat more strict than the DAS28 and similar to that of the SDAI.
The MCID for the CDAI has been defined.
The CDAI is widely
used in clinical research and practice given its high feasibility and
is preferred when comparing treatments that may have direct
effects on inflammatory markers (e.g., tocilizumab). The CDAI
may be falsely elevated in the context of comorbid fibromyalgia or
other chronic pain conditions.
Routine Assessment of Patient Index Data
The Routine Assessment of Patient Index Data (RAPID3) is
the most widely used of the RAPID measures and includes a
patient global score, a pain assessment, and a functional assessment (Multi-dimensional Health Assessment Questionnaire
[MDHAQ]; range, 0 to 30). The RAPID3 correlates moderately
with DAS28, SDAI, and CDAI, and there is fair agreement in
terms of disease categorization. The MCID for the RAPID3 has
been defined.
One study showed that sustained low RAPID3
scores were associated with reduced radiographic progression.
The high feasibility of this measure in certain circumstances is its
primary strength (e.g., telemedicine), although there may be fees
associated with use of this measure in electronic health systems.
The primary limitation of the RAPID3 is the lower content and
construct validity, with poorer correlation to other RA disease
activity measures and somewhat lower responsiveness compared
with other measures.
The RAPID3 is likely influenced to an
even greater extent by comorbid conditions such as fibromyalgia
and chronic pain syndromes.
Patient Activity Scale Systems
The Patient Activity Scale (PAS, PAS-II) systems are PROs that
include similar elements to the RAPID3 but incorporate the
HAQ or HAQ-II questionnaire, respectively, whereas the RAPID
measures include the MD-HAQ. PAS-II contains fewer questions
than the PAS. Not unexpectedly, the PAS correlates strongly with
the RAPID3, and disease activity categorization agreement with
DAS28 is only fair to moderate. The MCID for the PAS-II has
been defined.
Strengths and weaknesses of the PAS/PAS-II are
similar to those of the RAPID measures, described above.
Boolean Remission
Boolean remission is a strict definition of remission that requires
the presence of all component assessments of disease activity to be
very low (swollen and tender joints
≤
1, patient global score
≤
10
[range, 0 to 100], and CRP
≤
1.0). This definition has excellent
face validity. Patients who achieve this definition of remission have
superior outcomes, suggesting prognostic validity.
As a result,
this definition is often included as an outcome in RA clinical trials. Rates of Boolean remission are lower than for other definitions, and in the real world and for many patients it is not possible
to achieve due to false elevations in inflammatory markers as well
as elevated patient global scores due to other comorbid conditions
(e.g., obesity, fibromyalgia).
Modifications to these criteria
have been proposed but have not been studied in detail.
While
perhaps a useful measure for clinical trials, it is likely that such a
definition would be overly strict for a typical clinical practice.
Disease Activity in Psoriatic Arthritis
The Disease Activity in Psoriatic Arthritis (DAPSA) is meant to
represent peripheral joint disease activity and is the summation
of the tender and swollen joint counts, a patient arthritis global

score, a pain score (0 to 10 cm), and a CRP value. If the CRP
value is not included, the measure is called the cDAPSA.
Strong
correlation with other joint measures suggests good validity,
although the DAPSA does not adequately capture other domains
of interest to patients with psoriasis, such as enthesitis and skin
disease.
Thresholds for disease states have been defined for both
the DAPSA and cDAPSA, but clinically important differences
have not.
The DAPSA is responsive but may be less responsive
than other measures.
While DAPSA may be a valuable measure in clinic, it has not received as much attention as primary
clinical trial outcomes.

### Axial Spondyloarthritis and Enthesitis

Clinical disease assessments developed for RA, a disease that
involves primarily peripheral joints, are not ideally designed for
inflammatory diseases of the axial spine and those that primarily
manifest as enthesitis. As a result, disease activity measures have
been developed for axial spondyloarthritis and for other features
of seronegative spondyloarthritis and have been utilized in clinical trials.
Bath Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease Activity Index
The Bath Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease Activity Index
(BASDAI) is a commonly used patient-reported measure that
aims to capture and quantify symptoms typical of ankylosing
spondylitis. The measure comprises six questions that ask about
back pain, peripheral joint pain, tender areas, fatigue, and morning stiffness, each on a scale of 1 to 10. This measure is easy to
perform, reliable, and responsive.
As a result, this measure
is widely used in clinical trials and research. Active disease has
been defined as a score of 4 or higher, and the minimal clinically
important change and patient acceptable states have also been
defined.
As a result, this measure is widely used in clinical
trials and research. It is important to note, however, that this
measure is subject to the effects of noninflammatory causes of
pain (e.g., fibromyalgia) and psychological comorbidities and
thus should be used with this in mind as it pertains to decision
making in clinical practice.
Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease Activity Score
The Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease Activity Score (ASDAS) is
now the most commonly used outcome in clinical trials for axial
SpA. This composite measure incorporates four patient-reported
outcomes (three of which are taken from the BASDAI) and an
acute phase reactant (ESR or CRP). The lowest possible score
is 0.6, but it has no upper limit. The score has good reliability.
Thresholds have been defined for inactive, low, high, and very
high disease activity, and clinically important changes have been
defined.
The outcome is reliable and correlates with other
assessments of disease, suggesting good construct validity.
A
particular strength of the ASDAS is its responsiveness and ability
to discriminate among therapies in the clinical trial setting.
Bias
from comorbidities such as fibromyalgia are still a concern for use
in clinical care, although this bias may be to a lesser degree compared with the BASDAI.
Leeds Enthesitis Index
Enthesitis is an important and unique feature of PsA and spondyloarthritis that is not captured by other disease activity assessments. For this reason, the Leeds Enthesitis Index (LEI) and other
enthesitis scores have been developed.
The LEI examines tenderness at six sites: the lateral epicondyles of the humerus, the
medial condyles of the femur, and the insertion of the Achilles
tendon. Patients can receive a score between 0 and 6 based on
the number of tender locations. This measure has good reliability
and improves with active therapy in clinical trials.
No important
differences or thresholds of states have been defined. Low content
validity in particular subgroups is a concern, because validation
is difficult with no reference standard and because scores can be
particularly high in noninflammatory conditions such as fibromyalgia.
Thus caution in overinterpreting tenderness in this and
other contexts is needed.

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a notoriously protean disease, and the development of a single quantity that encompasses
disease activity has been a long-term challenge. Several indices
have been developed to measure both disease activity and organ
damage, but there is no universal consensus about which to use.
These measures are used primarily in clinical trials and less frequently in clinical settings.
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Disease Activity Index
The Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Disease Activity Index
(SLEDAI-2K)
is completed by the physician, who assesses
for the presence of disease manifestations in nine core domains
including rash, alopecia, oral ulcers, and proteinuria. The score is
then weighted to give a global score ranging from 0 to 105. Categories of disease states and MCID has been defined. The score
is relatively easy to complete, although it does require review of
medical records including laboratory assessments and physical
exam findings. While widely used, a limitation of the SLEDAI is
the inability of the score to capture gradation or severity within
individual domains, thus limiting responsiveness. Modifications
of the SLEDAI-2K include the Safety of Estrogens in Lupus
National Assessment (SELENA)-SLEDAI, which permits assessment on ongoing disease activity,
and the SLEDAI-2KG, which
accounts for glucocorticoid use (SLEDAI-2KG).
British Isles Lupus Assessment Group Index
The British Isles Lupus Assessment Group (BILAG) 2004 index
is a disease activity index that assesses disease manifestations. It
consists of 97 items across 9 organs/systems plus an additional 4

items to calculate the glomerular filtration rate. Each item is scored
as not done or 0 to 4. Scores are converted to alphabetic scores for
each system: A indicates severe disease activity, B indicates moderate disease activity, C indicates mild disease, D indicates inactive
disease but previously affected, and E indicates that the system was
never involved. Reliability, validity, and responsiveness have been
demonstrated, and MCID has been defined.
Scoring requires
either a computerized system or training, so the administrative
time burden is a consideration.
However, the BILAG does capture partial improvements.
Cutaneous Lupus Erythematosus Disease Area and
Severity Index
The Cutaneous Lupus Erythematosus Disease Area and Severity
Index (CLASI) focuses specifically on cutaneous manifestations
of both SLE and cutaneous lupus.
The CLASI yields scores
of both disease activity and damage. Definitions of mild, moderate, and severe disease have been established. The performance
of the CLASI requires training in the cutaneous manifestations
of SLE.

Safety of Estrogens in Lupus National Assessment–
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Disease Activity Index
Flare Index
The Safety of Estrogens in Lupus National Assessment (SELENA)–
SLEDAI Flare Index was developed to distinguish severe disease
flares from moderate/mild flares.
It includes changes in the
SELENA–SLEDAI score, a physician global assessment, and
treatment modifications. A modified version does not require
completion of the SLEDAI. Both the original and modified versions have demonstrated acceptable reliability and validity.
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Responder Index
The Systemic Lupus Erythematosus Responder Index (SRI) and
its modification, the SRI-4, are composite indices developed to be
used in trials to assess response to treatment.
The SRI requires
scoring of the SLEDAI-2K, the BILAG, and a physician global
assessment (PGA).
British Isles Lupus Assessment Group–Based Composite
Lupus Assessment
The BILAG-based Composite Lupus Assessment (BICLA) is
another measure used to assess response to treatment. It also
includes the BILAG, SLEDAI-2K, and a PGA, but scoring is
driven primarily by the BILAG. Both the SRI and the BICLA
provide comprehensive view of disease, but the time to complete
the measures is a major caveat that may limit feasibility in some
settings.
Systemic Lupus International Collaborating Clinics/
American College of Rheumatology Damage Index
The Systemic Lupus International Collaborating Clinics/American College of Rheumatology Damage Index (SDI) is the most
widely used measure of disease damage accrual in SLE.
It contains 41 items covering 12 systems. SDI scores range from 0 to 47,
but scores greater than 10 are rare. A complete history and physical examination are required for completion. Reliability and validity have been demonstrated. By definition, scores do not decrease
but tend to increase over time. The SDI is rarely used in clinical
trials, but scores are associated with adverse outcomes including
mortality. The need for a complete chart review may limit feasibility for patients with long-standing disease.
Patient-reported measures of both disease activity and damage
have been developed. The Systemic Lupus Activity Questionnaire
(SLAQ)
was developed to correspond to the physician-completed Systemic Lupus Activity Measures (SLAM), which is now
rarely used. The SLAQ consists of 24 items, rated as no problem,
mild, moderate, or severe. Total scores range from 0 to 44. Also
included are a flare report and a 0 to 10 global assessment of disease activity. While the SLAQ was validated against the SLAM,
it demonstrates low correlations with other physician-completed
measures of disease activity. The Brief Index of Lupus Damage
(BILD) was developed as patient-completed proxy for the SLICC
Damage Index.
The BILD consists of 26 items and is scored
from 0 to 31. Reliability and validity have been established. The
BILD has a moderately high correlation with the SDI.

### Systemic Sclerosis

A number of outcomes have been developed to capture the manifestations of systemic sclerosis (SSc) and to facilitate the study
of novel interventions. These measures range from assessing skin
only to capturing overall disease activity and damage that includes
other organ involvement. As with other disease states, the list and
discussion here are not exhaustive. Further, a number of measures
have aimed to address specific symptoms related to the disease
and are too numerous to cover here but have been discussed
elsewhere.

European Scleroderma Study Group Activity Index
The European Scleroderma Study Group Activity Index (EScSGAI) aims to capture disease activity in SSc by evaluating 10 variables including those that assess skin changes, the presence of
arthritis, levels of inflammatory markers, and patient-reported
assessments of cardiopulmonary function.
The weighted score
ranges from 0 to 10. The validity of this measure has been assessed
through the demonstration of associations with physician assessments of disease activity and through the prediction of progression of visceral disease and functional disability.
A small study
suggested good responsiveness to change.
Categories of disease
have been defined, with a score greater than 3 indicating active
disease. Revised indices have also been derived and validated.
Medsger Disease Severity Scale
The Medsger Disease Severity Scale aims to capture the severity
of SSc, with inclusion of both reversible and irreversible components and includes components from nine health domains that
cover multiple organ systems affected by SSc.
Each system is
graded from 0 to 4 by an evaluator and summed to create a score
that ranges from 0 to 36. Convergent validity has been established
showing that the score correlates with disability and quality of
life.
Thresholds of disease states have not been defined. Further,
partly because the score is meant to capture overall damage from
the disease, the responsiveness to change is unclear, and thus the
use of the score as an outcome in clinical trials has been limited.
Scleroderma Clinical Trials Consortium Damage Index
The Scleroderma Clinical Trials Consortium Damage Index
(SCTC-DI) is a physician-assessed measure that covers 23 items
over 6 domains related to SSc and aims to quantify organ damage
from the disease.
The weighted summed score ranges from 0 to
55. Criterion and construct validity have been supported by associations with disease and with the prediction of worsening morbidity.
The observation of accrual of items over time supports

responsiveness in this context; however, the short-term responsiveness and utility in the context of clinical trials have not been
established.
Scleroderma Health Assessment Questionnaire
The Scleroderma Health Assessment Questionnaire (SHAQ) is a
combination of the HAQ and six visual analogue scales specific
to SSc. This is a patient-reported measure that has excellent reliability and internal consistency. Convergent validity has also been
supported by strong associations with other measures of quality of
life and disease severity.
The SHAQ is responsive to change, and
the MCIDs for improvement and worsening have been defined,
although the thresholds of change have not.
The SHAQ overlaps
with other domains and is likely to be influenced by comorbid
conditions that can influence physical function.
Modified Rodnan Skin Score
The Modified Rodnan skin score is the most commonly used measure over many decades of quantifying of skin disease in SSc.
This approach has been used in numerous clinical trials and clinical studies to quantify skin disease and follow changes over time.
The measure is performed by a trained evaluator and is focused on
capturing the severity of skin involvement by grading skin disease
(normal to severe thickness) in multiple areas. The method has
good reliability and reproducibility
and has been validated by
longitudinal correlations with other manifestation of the disease
as well as skin biopsies.
This measure is responsive to change
and has been used in many clinical trials to evaluate the effects of
treatment. This measure is limited in that it does not address other
manifestations of the disease (which may be more important to
measure over the long term) and does not include any assessment
of the patient experience of their condition, which may not always
correlate with the objective skin findings.
Scleroderma Skin PRO
The Scleroderma Skin PRO (SSPRO) is an 18-item patientreported outcome that asks patients to report on four domains
related to quality of life related to skin involvement. These domains
cover physical, emotional, and social aspects of the impact of the
skin disease.
The reliability and internal consistency of the measure is very good, and the validity is supported by correlations
with other patient-reported outcome and associations with physician-reported assessments of skin involvement. Responsiveness to
change has not been well defined. This patient-reported outcome
is infrequently used in clinical studies compared with the Rodnan
skin score.

### Vasculitis

A number of outcomes have been defined in different types of vasculitis in order to study the impact of the disease and the effect of
interventions. As with outcome measures in other settings, some
measures aim to quantify disease activity, and others aim to quantify the damage that has occurred from the disease.
Birmingham Vasculitis Activity Score
The Birmingham Vasculitis Activity Score (BVAS) is the most
widely used measure of disease activity in vasculitis. This physician-derived measure consists of 56 weighted items addressing
symptoms in 9 organ systems. The measure is reliable, and the
validity of the BVAS is supported by evidence that it identifies
patients with physician-assessed active disease (versus remission),
as well as through correlations with quality of life, biomarkers,
and other measures of disease activity.
Categories of disease
states have not been defined other than “active” (score > 0) versus
“inactive” (score = 0). The score is sensitive to change; however,
clinically important changes have not been defined.
The limitations of the BVAS are that it was developed to be used in multiple
different forms of vasculitis and that it relies on some judgment on
the part of a physician evaluator as to what symptoms are related
to damage versus active disease. Further, whether the measure can
be used as a continuous measure to capture subtle differences in
disease activity among those with active disease is unclear. The
BVAS may not perform well among patients with large vessel vasculitis conditions, such as giant cell arteritis.
Vasculitis Damage Index
The Vasculitis Damage Index (VDI) consists of 64 items and is
commonly used to assess for irreversible damage related to the
disease. The validity of the VDI has largely been based on the
observation of correlation with damage scores for SLE and with
preceding vasculitis disease activity.
The score is also predictive of
future mortality.
The score is relatively easy to perform, although
there is concern that it may miss important impacts of vasculitis.
and additional questions have been proposed.
Further, the measure is likely most useful in long-term studies in which morbidity
from the disease is the primary interest.

### Gout

Formal and well-defined health outcomes are rarely utilized in
clinic to assess gout. However, several have been recently developed primarily with the goal of assessing the impact or burden of
gout over time in the clinical research or trial setting.
Gout Assessment Questionnaire
The Gout Assessment Questionnaire (GAQ) is a PRO measure
that was developed for use in clinical trials. This is a 31-item
questionnaire that collects information about gout impact and
assesses pain, well-being, productivity, and treatment satisfaction
on a 5-point Likert scale.
While this measure has good reliability, the internal consistency and construct validity have been
questioned.
The responsiveness is also low, overall, particularly
among those without a high disease burden,
although the measure was able to discriminate those that had gout flares from those
who did not. There are no defined thresholds or clinically important differences. While this measure attempts to fill an important
gap in terms of patient-reported gout outcomes, it is unclear what
its role should be in clinical research given these limitations, and
there is currently no evidence to support its use in routine clinical
practice.
Gout Activity Score
The Gout Activity Score (GAS) collects information on gout
flares, serum urate levels, overall well-being, and tophi.
These
measures are combined and generate a weighted score through a
freely available formula. While the reliability has not been well
defined, the score demonstrated reasonable construct validity by
discriminating those who were more likely to be recommended to
escalate urate-lowering therapy, as well as the patients’ perception
that they were in remission. Thresholds correlating with remission have been defined.
While this measure has not been widely
used in clinical research to date, it may have value in this setting.
Its role in clinical care remains unclear.

Gout Flare
Gout is, by nature, a disease that presents with episodic symptoms, or flares. Thus work has been done to define and validate an
outcome for acute episodes or flares of gout.
This definition
was highly correlated with the physician reference standard in one
study (73% sensitive and 96% specific). This outcome is likely to
be incorporated in clinical trials for gout, and it may also be helpful for clinical care since this definition may guide clinicians in
terms of the appropriate questions to ask when a patient reports a
gout flare over the phone.

### Knee and Hip Osteoarthritis

Knee and hip osteoarthritis (OA) are some of the most common
joint conditions seen by rheumatologists. Several patient-reported
outcomes have been developed, primarily for research purposes.
Knee Osteoarthritis Outcome Score
The Knee Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS) is a PRO that is
highly similar to the Western Ontario and McMaster Universities
Arthritis Index (WOMAC) but is free to use. The KOOS covers five domains related to symptoms of knee OA including pain,
other symptoms, function, participation in sports and recreation,
and quality of life. The KOOS has demonstrated good reliability
and construct validity and has excellent responsiveness in patients
with OA as well as knee injury populations.
Clinically
meaningful changes have been defined,
although thresholds
of disease states have not. The KOOS is long (42 items) but is feasible to perform in most settings. The KOOS and WOMAC have
been utilized in many clinical trials and other research studies.
The main limitation to more widespread incorporation in clinical
practices is the large number of questions, which can limit feasibility in a busy practice.
Hip Osteoarthritis Outcome Score
The Hip Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (HOOS)
is highly
similar to the KOOS and incorporates 40 items over 5 subscales. The measure has good reliability, internal consistency, and
construct validity.
Like the KOOS, the HOOS is somewhat burdensome in terms of time (10 to 15 minutes). Minimally important differences and thresholds of states have been
defined.
Short forms of both the KOOS and HOOS are also
available.

## Measures That Address Symptoms and Individual Health Domains

Many symptoms are shared by patients who have different rheumatic diseases. Of these, perhaps the most relevant are pain,
limitations in physical function, and fatigue. Mental health challenges, such as depression and anxiety, are also relatively common
in people with rheumatic diseases. Measures commonly used to
assess these symptoms and domains may be free-standing or may
be part of the PROMIS suite of measures.

### PROMIS (Patient-Reported Outcome Measurement Information System)

The National Institutes of Health Patient-Reported Outcomes
Measurement Information System (PROMIS) initiative was
undertaken to improve and standardize measurement of PROs.
The PROMIS measures cover physical, mental, and social health
domains, including domains of particular importance in rheumatic conditions, such as pain, fatigue, and function. PROMIS
measures may be administered as traditional static questionnaires
(known as short forms) or through computer adaptive testing.
All PROMIS scale scores can be transformed to T scores, with
a mean of 50 and standard deviation of 10, allowing comparisons to the general population and across conditions. Both adult
and pediatric measures are available (
https://www.healthmeasures.
net/search-view-measures
); only the adult measures are addressed
here. Short forms are available in multiple languages. PROMIS
measures have been integrated into some electronic health record
systems.

### Pain

The most commonly used measure of pain in rheumatic conditions is a simple rating of pain severity. This may be done as a
visual analogue scale or as a numeric rating scale.
In a visual
analogue scale, a horizontal line is presented, usually 100 cm long,
with verbal descriptors at each end (e.g., no pain and severe pain).
Respondents are asked to mark the line at a point representing
their severity of pain. In a numeric rating scale, respondents would
be asked to rate their pain on a scale, typically from 0 to 10 or 0 to
100. Both forms have reasonable reliability, validity, and responsiveness; however, the VAS may be more difficult to implement
in clinical settings.
PROMIS includes several pain measures
that have been used in rheumatic conditions. The PROMIS Pain
Interference scale assesses the impact of pain on daily activities.
It has been used in multiple rheumatic conditions and is reliable,
valid, and responsive.

### Function

The Health Assessment Questionnaire (HAQ), also referred to as
the HAQ-Disability Index (HAQ-DI), was the first widely used
questionnaire to measure physical function in rheumatology.
Published in 1980 for use in RA, its use has since expanded to
other rheumatic conditions, either in the original form or slightly
modified. Scoring of the HAQ is relatively complex as both ratings
of difficulty on 20 items plus answers to an additional 21 items
regarding use of assistive devices or help are required. HAQ scores
range from 0 to 3, with higher scores indicating more functional
impairment. While the HAQ is reliable, valid, and responsive,
more recent use shows a proportion of 0 scores (floor effect),
which limits responsiveness. Multiple variations of the HAQ have
since been developed, with the Multidimensional Health Assessment Questionnaire (MDHAQ), currently in greatest use, likely
due to its inclusion in the RAPID3.
The MDHAQ consists
of items rating difficulty in 10 daily activities. Scores range from
0 to 3. Despite improvements intended to improve assessment
at the lower end of the scale, the MDHAQ has a skewed distribution that may affect responsiveness, specifically the ability to
detect improvement among people with low scores (i.e., better
function).
The HAQ-II was developed to improve the psychometric qualities of the HAQ, specifically to correct floor effects.
It includes 10 items with scores ranging from 0 to 3. While demonstrating adequate reliability and validity, the HAQ-II also shows
floor effects that may affect its ability to detect improvement at the
lower end of the scale.
PROMIS includes multiple short forms
assessing physical function. The most commonly used in rheumatology are the 4- and 10-item versions. The 10-item PROMIS

Physical Function 10a, along with the MDHAQ and the HAQ-II,
have been recommended by the American College of Rheumatology as functional status assessments for RA.
Function may also be assessed through performance measures,
mostly commonly in rehabilitation settings. The Timed Up and
Go (TUG) test measures the ability to rise from a chair, walk 3
meters, turn around, walk back, and sit down.
A time longer than 14 seconds is associated with poor health outcomes.
Timed walking tests, based either on the time to walk a specified
distance (e.g., 400 meters) or the distance covered in a specified
time (e.g., 6 minutes), are used primarily in studies of OA or joint
replacement.
Both measures appear to be reliable and valid in
OA populations; use in other conditions is less common. Scores
predictive of poor health outcomes (e.g., fast-paced walking test
time slower than 1.0 meter/second
) have been established for
many timed walking tests. The Osteoarthritis Research Society
International (OARSI) has endorsed the TUG and a fast-paced
walk (40 meters) as core outcome measures for adults with hip or
knee OA.
Both are often used to assess function after surgery.
Space constraints may limit the use in clinical settings.
The Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB) was originally
developed for use as a measure of frailty in older populations.
It consists of a test of balance, timed chair stands, and a 4-meter
walk test. Scores range from 0 to 12. The SPPB is predictive of
falls and other poor health outcomes; scores lower than 10 are predictive of all-cause mortality.
Because of the potential impact
of RA on hand function, grip strength, measured either with a
full-grip dynamometer or as a “pinch grip” between thumb and
forefinger, may be measured in either clinical trial or rehabilitation settings.
Reference values are not agreed upon, so the best
use of these measures would be to follow individuals over time for
deterioration or improvement with treatment.

### Fatigue

While fatigue is a concern for many rheumatic conditions, there
are no agreed-upon measures for assessing it. A 2020 review of
fatigue outcome measures for rheumatology included almost 20
individual questionnaires.
Some were designed for RA, the condition in which the most research on fatigue has been conducted
(e.g., Bristol Rheumatoid Arthritis Fatigue scales
); others are
generic (e.g., SF-36 Vitality
) or were developed for other conditions and are now used in rheumatology (e.g., the FACIT-F, which
was developed for oncology
). Some provide a single score for
fatigue, while others yield scores for different domains of fatigue
(e.g., physical fatigue, cognitive fatigue, emotional fatigue). More
recently, the PROMIS Fatigue measures are gaining popularity.
There are multiple fatigue short forms, ranging from four to eight
items, as well as the CAT version. The psychometric properties of
the PROMIS Fatigue measures have been examined in multiple
rheumatic conditions and demonstrated to be adequate.

### Depression and Anxiety

A variety of measures of depression or depressive symptoms have
been used in rheumatic conditions. Historically, probably the most
common was the Center for Epidemiological Studies-Depression
scale (CESD).
The CESD contains 20 items, with scores ranging from 0 to 60. Scores of 16 or higher are generally used as an
indication for possible depression. Alternative (higher) cut points
have been proposed for specific rheumatic conditions.
More
recently, the various forms of the Patient Health Questionnaire
(PHQ) are used. The longest version, the PHQ-9, was developed
to correspond with the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM)
criteria for major depression.
Scores range from 0 to 27. PHQ-9
scores of 5, 10, 15, and 20 represent thresholds for mild, moderate, moderately severe, and severe depression, respectively. The
PHQ-9 includes a question on suicidality, so if it is used, a realtime plan to follow-up on endorsement of suicidality is needed.
The PHQ-8, which excludes the suicidality question, is often used
in research settings where real-time follow-up is not feasible.
A cut point of 10 or higher is used for defining depression. The
psychometric properties of both instruments are strong, including good correspondence with clinical assessments of depression
(criterion validity). The PHQ-2 is often used in clinical settings as
a screening measure.
Scores of 3 or higher yield acceptable correspondence with clinical diagnoses.
PROMIS includes both
short form and CAT measures for depression. These have been less
commonly used but do appear to have acceptable psychometric
properties.
PROMIS Depression scores of 55, 60, 65, and 70
appear to be reasonable thresholds for defining mild, moderate,
moderately severe, and severe depression.
Anxiety has been less frequently studied in rheumatic conditions. The Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) is a measure
developed to correspond to clinical diagnostic criteria for GAD.
Scores range from 0 to 21. The seven-item scale has good reliability and validity, and a cut point of 10 has high correspondence
with clinical GAD diagnosis. The two-item GAD-2 can be used
as a screening measure.
Scores of 3 or higher have acceptable
correspondence with clinical diagnoses.
The PROMIS Anxiety
measures have acceptable psychometric properties.
T scores of
60 to 62 appear to correspond to the GAD-7 cut score of 10.

## Imaging and Biomarker Outcomes

As technology has advanced and there has been improvement
in molecular and imaging tests in many areas, these tools have
often been used as biomarkers and outcomes in both interventional and observational studies. For example, the assessment and
quantification of structural damage over the long term has been
an important outcome for decades. Many studies have evaluated
scoring systems using radiography, magnetic resonance imaging,
computed tomography, and ultrasound to assess the activity and
impact of a range of diseases.

some examples of the most common imaging outcomes that have
been utilized in clinical research studies.
Although none of

that each of these should undergo a similar process of validation as
other clinical outcome measures.
Imaging and biomarker studies have primarily been performed
as part of research studies. There is limited evidence at this time
to suggest that these biomarkers are useful in routine clinical practice. There have been several examples of studies demonstrating
no clear benefit of incorporating imaging outcomes into clinical
care compared with using clinical assessments alone in the management of RA.
Thus future study is needed to determine
what value these outcomes will play in the care of patients with
rheumatic diseases.

## Conclusion

There has been an explosion of health outcomes in rheumatology
over the past several decades. The utility of each of these health
outcomes depends on the purpose for which it each is being used
as well as its validity, reliability, feasibility, and responsiveness to
change, among other qualities. It is important for clinicians and
researchers to understand the strengths and weaknesses of health
outcomes in order to more appropriately interpret research studies, as well as interpret clinical assessments performed with the
goal of managing patients with disease.
