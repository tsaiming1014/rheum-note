# Treatment of Rheumatoid Arthritis

BRYANT R. ENGLAND AND JAMES R. O’DELL

KEY POINTS

> Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) can, and should, be diagnosed early, and

> disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) therapy should be

> initiated at the time of diagnosis.

> Treatment and escalation of treatment for all RA patients should be

> based on a disease activity target—either remission or low disease

> activity.

> Over 20 DMARDs are available to treat RA, including biosimilar biologics.

> Patients benefit when skilled rheumatology professionals use all of

> these tools in a highly individualized approach.

> The type of DMARD (conventional or biologic) therapy the patient

> receives is not as important as ensuring that each patient achieves

> the disease activity target.

> The initial DMARD, and the cornerstone of therapy, for most patients is

> methotrexate (MTX). If needed, MTX can be titrated to 25 mg weekly

> and administered subcutaneously.

> Many patients will require combinations of DMARDs, with or without

> biologic agents, to achieve the disease activity target.

> Many very effective biologic DMARDs are available for use in RA;

> essentially all DMARDs, especially biologics, are more effective

> when administered with MTX.

> Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs may provide useful symptom

> control but are almost never indicated without concomitant use of

> DMARDs and should be discontinued after disease status has been

> improved with DMARDs.

> Glucocorticoids are rapidly effective, powerful DMARDs but have several

> short- and long-term adverse effects. They should be used only with

> other DMARDs and as a bridge to effective DMARD therapy. The best

> dose of glucocorticoids for chronic use is zero.

> Tapering DMARDs should only be considered in patients who have

> reached and achieved stability at their treatment target or remission.

> Often, at least one DMARD must be continued.

> The ubiquitous comorbidities of RA—particularly cardiovascular

> disease—must always be considered and addressed aggressively

> by interprofessional teams.

## Introduction

The treatment of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) has evolved dramatically during the past 30 years, perhaps more so than for any of the
other rheumatic diseases.
The majority of patients newly diagnosed with RA who have access to health care and medications
can expect to experience remission or low disease activity if
they are treated early by a skilled rheumatologist.
This expected
outcome is truly remarkable, and it is appropriate to take a step
back and examine the reasons behind this sea change. Biologic
agents, which became available in 1998, are very effective, and
importantly, they have helped invigorate clinicians and patients
alike, making both parties believe that higher expectations regarding control of RA are justified. However, it is critical to acknowledge all the other changes that occurred in roughly that same
period and that have been universally embraced by rheumatologists (
Table 73.1
). It is difficult, if not impossible, to know which
of these changes are most important. Early diagnosis aided by use
of anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPAs) and the recent
American College of Rheumatology (ACR)/European Alliance of
Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR) RA classification criteria are critical.
The universal acknowledgment that all patients
with RA should be treated with a disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD)
and the effective use of methotrexate
(MTX) with folic acid have also played huge roles. In the mid1990s, the use of combination DMARDs became an accepted
strategy.
Together, the early diagnosis, effective use of MTX and
combination DMARDs, and biologic agents
are the tools that
allow rheumatologists to successfully manage RA
(
Table 73.2
).
The development and periodic updating of evidence-based clinical practice guidelines by the ACR and EULAR over the past
three decades has provided clinicians guidance on the use of these
tools.
Treat to target (T2T)
defines the scope of work and job of
rheumatologists.
T2T is arguably one of the most important
factors in improved outcomes in RA. The Tight Intensive Control of RA (TICORA) trial (see later) was the seminal study that
put T2T on the map because it dramatically illustrated the benefits of this approach.
The TICORA trial demonstrated that if
patients are treated aggressively to achieve remission or low disease activity, the outcomes improve significantly both clinically
and radiographically, with or without the use of biologic agents.
Similarly, in the Dutch Behandel-Strategieen (BeSt) study, the
aggressive use of therapeutic adjustment (i.e., T2T) led to significant clinical improvement regardless of the initial choice of
DMARDs, which included both biologic agents and conventional regimens.
Therefore
the most important tenet of successful RA therapy today is not which of the many DMARDs,
conventional or biologic, are used (see
Table 73.2
), but that
all patients are treated to meet the disease activity target. In
short, this treatment strategy is more important than individual medication choices.

## Goal of Rheumatoid Arthritis Treatment

It is truly remarkable that rheumatologists now have more than 20
approved conventional or biologic DMARDs in their toolbox (see
Table 73.2
). Despite all these terrific DMARD options,
the most
important paradigm shift for the treatment of RA has been the
realization that patients should be treated early and to a target
of low disease activity or remission.
For people outside the
rheumatologic community, this seems like stating the obvious—if
patients have hypertension, hyperlipidemia, or diabetes, they are
of course treated so that their blood pressure, low-density lipoprotein, or Hb A
levels, respectively, are reduced to a defined and
easily measured goal. Unfortunately, no single examination finding or laboratory test satisfactorily measures disease activity in RA.
Many RA disease activity measures have been proposed,
all
of which are composite measures that include information derived
from some combination of joint examinations, patient and physician assessment of disease activity, patient function, and/or laboratory measures of inflammation (erythrocyte sedimentation rate
[ESR] or C-reactive protein [CRP] levels). The ACR has endorsed
a list of disease activity measures for regular use that possess the
best psychometric properties and feasibility (see
Table 73.3
).
Each of these measures has strengths and weaknesses; some rely
only on data from the patient, some require complete joint counts
by clinicians, and some require laboratory tests to measure inflammation. Busy clinicians rarely have time to document disease
activity in more than 60 tender and swollen joints or wait for laboratory test results to make treatment decisions during a patient’s
visit. Therefore measures that simplify this process as much as
possible are being embraced, including measures that limit the
joints that are examined to 28, do not require laboratory tests, or
are entirely dependent on patient-reported data. These measures
frequently correlate, and treatment guided by different measures
has produced largely comparable results.
Thus, currently in the
clinic
it is very important that disease activity be regularly measured and far less important which of these measures is used.
This sentiment is shared by health care payers, with regular RA
disease activity assessment being a quality measure in the meritbased incentive payment system in the United States.
Because none of our therapies cure RA, it would seem obvious
that the next best goal should be remission. However, the concept
of remission as a goal for patients with RA is problematic. First,
a definition of remission that is both relevant and practical has
been elusive. To be relevant, remission should be highly predictive of the absence of disease progression over time, not just in
the joints but systemically, including, but not limited to, vascular
damage. To be practical for clinicians, a definition of remission
should be easy to apply in real time to patients seen in the clinic.
A provisional definition of remission for use in clinical trials was
developed by a joint ACR/EULAR effort, rigorously tested against
short-term radiologic outcomes in 1- to 2-year randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
This definition, termed Boolean 1.0, and
proposed index-based definitions standardized remission and were
a huge step forward for reporting and comparing results across
clinical trials. However, the Boolean 1.0 definition was designed
for clinical trials and not for clinical care, where rigorous counts
of joints with disease activity are not always performed, the need
to have CRP results in real time becomes a major problem for
clinical workflow, and comorbid conditions such as osteoarthritis,
fibromyalgia, and mental health disorders may influence components of these definitions (e.g., patient global assessment). Recognizing the latter, ACR/EULAR recently revised the definition by

Perhaps more problematic is that many people who believe
that remission is defined by clinical data alone will always underestimate the amount of low-level disease activity that could be
found if synovial biopsies or advanced imaging techniques such
as ultrasound or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) were used.
Significant data indicate that many, perhaps most patients with
RA who meet the definitions of “remission” are found to have
active disease when they are assessed by ultrasound or MRI.
For example, in a substudy of the Treatment of Early Aggressive Rheumatoid (TEAR) trial (discussed later), it was reported
that all patients believed to be in clinical remission had evidence
of joint inflammation on MRI scans of the wrist.
Indeed, the
ACR/EULAR definition allows for a swollen joint, which many
people would argue is not really full clinical remission. Another
major problem with the term
remission
is that, according to currently available data,
it is not clear that remission, regardless of
how it is defined, should be the treatment goal for all patients
with RA.
Many patients do very well despite having low levels
of disease activity.
The following important question remains
unanswered in RA: Where is the tipping point between pushing the disease activity ever lower and the toxicity and cost of
doing so?
To further complicate matters, it is almost guaranteed
that the tipping point will be different for each patient. The following issues need to be considered:
• When do the risks and considerable expense of some of our RA
therapies outweigh the benefits of escalating therapy?
• Which patients who have improved dramatically but still have
two tender or swollen joints need to switch to another medication? How should this decision differ when patients have been
treated with one or two conventional DMARDs compared
with multiple biologic agents?
• Which patients with RA are most at risk for toxicities if we
push too hard for remission?

## Classes of Medications

### Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs

DMARDs have the ability to change the course of RA for the
better. The most rigorous application of this definition requires
RCTs to show that a DMARD has the ability not only to change
the clinical course of the disease but also to decrease or halt
radiographic progression. By this definition, all of the nine conventional DMARDs, at least two JAK inhibitors approved for
use, and the 10 biologic DMARDs listed in
Table 73.2
qualify,
with the possible exception of minocycline and hydroxychloroquine (HCQ), for which the few small studies that address this
can only suggest radiographic benefits. When the DMARDs
listed in
Table 73.2
are used individually or in combinations of
two, three, or four, as is often the case, 10,641 possible combinations exist for each individual patient, assuming that biologic
and JAK agents are not used in combination. Obviously, this
huge number of choices is both good and bad news; it is great
to have all the options but impossible to keep them all straight
or try them in individual patients. Therefore to use all these

medications effectively, the clinician must have goals, strategies,
and an up-to-date knowledge of the medications and their interactions, toxicities, and unique niches.
Conventional Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs
The most widely used conventional DMARDs—MTX, sulfasalazine (SSZ), HCQ, and leflunomide (LEF)—are discussed in detail
in
Chapter 61
. Together, these four DMARDs, along with glucocorticoids (see
Chapter 60
), currently account for the vast majority of conventional DMARD use. New medications in this arena
are tofacitinib, baricitinib, and upadacitinib, with several more on
the way (see
Chapter 65
); their role in the treatment of RA is discussed later. Although less commonly used, azathioprine (AZA),
cyclosporine (CSA), and the tetracyclines (minocycline and doxycycline) remain important as niche therapies. They are not covered
elsewhere in this book and thus are discussed later in this chapter.
Biologic Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs
The biologic DMARDs are discussed in detail in
Chapters 63 and
64
. Within this class of agents we now have the ability to inhibit
multiple inflammatory cytokines, including TNF, which is inhibited with three monoclonal antibodies, infliximab, adalimumab,
and golimumab; the TNF receptor protein, etanercept (ETAN);
the pegylated Fab fragment certolizumab, which also binds TNF;
interleukin (IL)-1, which is bound with the IL-1 receptor antagonist anakinra; and IL-6, which is inhibited by the monoclonal
antibodies to the IL-6 receptor, tocilizumab or sarilumab. We also
have the ability to kill or inhibit cell lines important in inflammation, including B cells (with rituximab) and T cells (with
abatacept).
Biologic agents are key advances that changed the
landscape of RA therapy
in terms of therapeutic expectations
and the understanding of RA pathogenesis. Because of their often
rapid onset of action, particularly with the TNF and IL-6 inhibitors, and their ability to retard radiographic progression of disease,
they are increasingly used earlier and more often in people with
RA. Arguably, all the biologic agents work better with concomitant use of MTX, and this phenomenon is particularly true of
the TNF inhibitors. One reason for the increased efficacy of TNF
inhibitors with concomitant use of MTX is the ability of MTX
to prevent the formation of antidrug antibodies. The challenge
for clinicians is to appropriately utilize conventional and biologic
therapies—escalating to biologic agents when necessary but ensuring that much less expensive but very effective conventional therapies are first optimized.
Glucocorticoids
Glucocorticoids, discussed in detail in
Chapter 60
, have a long
and storied history in the treatment of RA. Several landmark
studies have proven not only clinical efficacy
but also the significant radiographic efficacy of glucocorticoids.
The Combinatietherapie Bij Reumatoide Artritis (COBRA) trial
and the
COBRA arm of the BeSt study (discussed later
) again demonstrated the significant clinical and radiographic benefit that glucocorticoids can provide. However, despite their rapid onset and
other positive benefits of glucocorticoids in RA, their toxicities are
also abundant.
Currently glucocorticoids are most often used along with
DMARDs as part of initial “induction” therapy to gain rapid control of RA, and then they are aggressively tapered as the sloweracting DMARD begins to take effect. While glucocorticoid-aided
induction clearly hastens improvement, it may complicate the
assessment of therapeutic response to other agents and whether
better disease-related outcomes such as disease activity and radiographic progression result over longer periods of follow-up (e.g.,
>12 months) remains unsettled.
Historically, the belief has
been that once a patient with RA began taking glucocorticoids,
the patient would never be able to stop using them. This scenario
is clearly no longer true with the effective DMARDs that are
now available; successful tapering of glucocorticoids is now the
rule.
The inability to taper glucocorticoids successfully is a
strong indication that the current DMARD regimen is not working and should be modified. Unfortunately, the optimal glucocorticoid tapering strategy is unknown and, in real-world settings,
tapering off them may take 2 to 5 years.
Long-term use of glucocorticoids at doses of greater than 5 to 7.5 mg per day (prednisone
equivalent) is a clear indication that DMARD therapy needs to
be escalated, but emerging evidence suggests glucocorticoid risks
also occur at prednisone doses <5 mg per day and with short-term
use.
Importantly,
glucocorticoids should almost never be
used to treat RA without concomitant use of DMARDs, with
the ultimate goal of maintaining patients in remission or low
disease activity without glucocorticoids.

Other Conventional Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic
Drugs
Azathioprine.
AZA, 50 to 200 mg/day, has been used to treat
RA for more than 50 years. Although clearly it is not a first-line
DMARD in contemporary RA treatment, AZA is most commonly
used as a substitute for MTX when contraindications or intolerance
to it exist. This situation most commonly arises in patients with the
so-called “MTX flu,” but other situations—including pregnancy,
MTX nodulosis, and liver, pulmonary, or renal disease—may be
indications for use of AZA in people with RA. A particularly problematic patient population is patients with RA, frequently male,
who have RA-associated interstitial lung disease (ILD). Clinicians
may be reluctant to use MTX and/or LEF, so AZA more frequently
becomes the conventional DMARD of choice.
Neutropenia is the most common complication of AZA treatment. Neutropenia can be predicted with a genetic test for polymorphisms of the enzyme thiopurine methyltransferase. Patients
who are homozygous for the mutant polymorphism that is nonfunctional (1 in 300 or 0.3% of patients) are very sensitive to
bone marrow and other toxicities of AZA. Patients who are heterozygotes (
≈
10% of the population) may have milder neutropenia.
Unfortunately, this test is expensive; in some centers it may
cost more than $1000, and reimbursement is not always assured.
Some clinicians elect to start with low doses of 50 mg/day, check
complete blood cell counts at 2 weeks, and then increase the dose
as needed if the white blood cell count is acceptable. It has been
speculated that the subset of patients with the rare nonfunctional
polymorphisms could be the patients who, when AZA was added
to a stable MTX regimen, experienced an acute febrile toxic reaction characterized by fever, leukocytosis, and a cutaneous leukocytoclastic vasculitis.
Cyclosporine.
In the 1990s, CSA, mostly used in transplantation to prevent allograft rejection, gained a foothold in the treatment of RA.
Interest in CSA peaked when studies showed that
the addition of CSA (2.5 to 5 mg/kg/day) to a stable dose of MTX
provided substantial additive benefit compared with MTX alone
(
Fig. 73.2
).
Moreover, this therapy seemed to slow radiographic
progression.
Unfortunately, follow-up reports revealed that only
22% of patients continued taking this combination at 18 months,
with the most common reason for discontinuation being hypertension or increased creatinine levels.

### Minocycline and Doxycycline

Tetracycline and its derivatives have a long and somewhat
checkered history with regard to the treatment of RA and other
arthritides.
They are not mainstream therapy for RA but
have a niche in patients for whom laboratory monitoring is
not feasible, in those with frequent infections, or in patients
who have concomitant periodontal disease. The mechanism
of action (MOA) of tetracyclines in RA is poorly understood.
Tetracyclines are, of course, antibiotics, but they also inhibit
metalloproteinases, modulate immune responses, and have
anti-inflammatory effects. Given in a dose of 100 mg twice
daily, moderate, statistically significant improvement in clinical
parameters of disease activity was found in patients with established RA who were treated with minocycline compared with
placebo.
Findings in the treatment of early-stage, seropositive RA have similarly demonstrated improvement in clinical
measures and the ability to taper glucocorticoids.
Although
doxycycline is less frequently studied, a trial of patients with
early RA found doxycycline plus MTX was superior to MTX
alone.
Potential adverse effects of tetracyclines include lightheadedness, vertigo, rare liver toxicity, lupus-like syndromes,
and, with longer-term use, cutaneous hyperpigmentation.
Hyperpigmentation may, but does not always, resolve with discontinuation of minocycline and occurs much less commonly, if
at all, with doxycycline.

### Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs

NSAIDs, including salicylates, which are covered in detail in
Chapter 59
, have been a ubiquitous part of RA treatment for
more than a century. During the past several decades, as toxicities (particularly gastrointestinal [GI],
cardiovascular,
and
renal
) have become apparent and our DMARDs have become
more effective and abundant, the use of NSAIDs, fortunately,
has been declining. The cardiovascular toxicities, which are now
known to be strongly associated not only with the cyclooxygenase-2-specific NSAIDs but essentially with all NSAIDs, have been
particularly concerning in RA patient populations in which the
excess mortality is largely due to accelerated cardiovascular disease.
As with glucocorticoids,
NSAIDs should rarely be used without concomitant use of DMARDs,
and the goal should be to
taper their use as soon as possible to avoid GI and cardiovascular
toxicities.

### Historical Treatments

Gold salts, used in the treatment of RA for more than a century,
are an effective therapy, particularly if used intramuscularly,
but it is cumbersome to administer, has less efficacy than many
other available agents, and has a narrow therapeutic window. Similarly, penicillamine is of historical interest but is rarely used.
These therapies are not discussed further in this chapter.

## Treatment Approaches and Strategies

As previously detailed, the goal of treatment for all patients with
RA is remission or at least low disease activity. Other than toxicity concerns and expense,
clinicians should not focus on which
DMARD or combination of DMARDs is used in an individual patient but should focus on getting patients to the disease activity target with whatever DMARDs achieve that goal.
There is no evidence-based correct DMARD or combination of
DMARDs that is right for all patients. Each patient presents a
unique challenge and comes with unique expectations, biases, disease activity level, damage burden, comorbidities, and, in many
countries, health care coverage issues.
One of the most important areas of investigation in RA is to identify parameters that
will predict which patient will respond to which therapy.
Several studies have suggested that factors such as serologic status,
human leukocyte antigen–DRB1 haplotypes,
and a variety of
findings in synovial tissue
are statistically associated with
responses to therapy. This is a highly active area of investigation,
but no clear-cut answers that are applicable to routine clinical care
have emerged yet.
Some investigators have suggested that treatment should be different for patients with RA who have a good prognosis versus poor
prognosis. This concept is problematic because separating patients
into those who have a good versus a poor prognosis is difficult.
Although some data suggest that certain features are associated
with worse prognosis (
Table 73.6
), unfortunately, no data exist
that suggest that stratifying our therapies based on prognosis at
the individual patient level yields better outcomes. These prognostic factors are primarily disease-centric, identifying patients most
likely to have treatment nonresponse or radiographic progression.
However, equally as important when selecting therapies are the
therapy-related toxicity prognostic factors, such as the potentially
disease-unrelated risk factors for infections (e.g., age, comorbidities, and vaccination status, among others). Most patients who
meet the criteria for classification of RA in the clinic, and essentially all those included in clinical trials, have poor-prognosis
RA, with several of the factors listed in
Table 73.6
. Regardless
of the prognostic factors, the goal for each individual patient is
to achieve at least a low level of disease activity. Until, or unless,
predictive parameters are identified, rheumatologists will out of
necessity continue to use their clinical judgment at the individual
patient level.

differentially

Initiating Treatment With a Single Disease-Modifying
Anti-rheumatic Drug Versus Combinations of
Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs
Although most clinicians still favor starting treatment with
monotherapy, combination DMARD therapy has clearly
changed the treatment of RA forever. In the early 1990s, the
treatment model called for individual DMARDs and then,
when necessary, switching to a different DMARD. At that time,
combinations of DMARDs were not used. Studies published in
the mid-1990s showing the impressive efficacy and tolerability
of various combinations of DMARDs dramatically changed
the treatment paradigm.
Now, the majority of patients
with RA are treated with combinations of two, three, or more
DMARDs. The study that put combination DMARD therapy
on the map was the so-called
triple therapy study
by the RAIN
group of investigators.
This study clearly demonstrated that the
triple combination of MTX, HCQ, and SSZ was significantly
more effective than MTX alone or the combination of HCQ
and SSZ. Importantly, this combination of DMARDs did not
lead to an increase in toxicity. Multiple publications on this and
other successful combinations of conventional DMARDs soon
followed.
Today the important question of whether treatment of all
patients should start with combinations of DMARDs that are
then stepped down (i.e., tapered) or whether monotherapy
should be used initially and then stepped up only if patients are
not at target is an ongoing debate. Both approaches have their
supporters, and data can be presented to support both sides.
On the one hand, there is no doubt that if short-term responses
are examined in groups of patients, combinations outperform
monotherapy. This is true for combinations of conventional
DMARDs
and combinations of conventional DMARDs
with a biologic agent.
On the other hand, the strategy of initial
monotherapy with step-ups only in the patients who need it was
clearly effective in the TICORA study (as previously discussed
and, importantly, in the BeSt
and TEAR
trials; although
initial responses were better in both of the latter trials in the
clinical parameters of patients initially treated with combination
therapy, all groups in both the BeSt and TEAR trials had identical DAS or DAS28 scores at the end of 2 years. Clinicians do not
treat groups of patients but individual patients, and if the results
are similar at 2 years or beyond,
effective treatment approaches
that minimize the number of DMARDs and therefore their
potential toxicities and expenses are desirable for patients
and health care systems alike.
Like other clinical situations in
RA treatment, we need the precision medicine tools that can
guide patient selection for initiating monotherapy versus combination therapy.

### The BeSt Study

The BeSt study, now nearly two decades old, continues to be an
important multicenter trial of 508 patients with early RA randomized to receive one of four treatment “strategies” in an openlabel study.
The four arms of the study were as follows: group
1, sequential DMARD monotherapy; group 2, step-up combination therapy; group 3, initial combination therapy; and group 4,
initial MTX and infliximab. Treatment adjustments were made
every 3 months with the target of a DAS less than or equal to
2.4 (corresponding to low disease activity). The major results at
1 and 2 years are shown in
Fig. 73.5
; both combination groups
(groups 3 and 4) improved more quickly than did groups 1 and
2, which was expected, with high-dose prednisone in group 3 and
a high initial dose of MTX and also infliximab in group 4. At 1
year, DAS and other clinical outcomes were similar in all four
groups and, importantly, at 2 years they were identical. Health
Assessment Questionnaire (HAQ) scores improved more in the
early combination groups (groups 3 and 4) at 1 year but were
not different at 2 years, and radiographic progression at 2 years
was greater in group 1 than in group 2 and greater in groups 1
and 2 than in the combination groups. Progression of the joint
space narrowing score (the importance of which is discussed in a
subsequent section), although numerically higher in group 1, was
not statistically different among the four groups. Interpretation
of these differences is complicated by the fact that despite having
disease for only 6 months, group 2 had more radiographic progression at baseline.
The main conclusion of the BeSt study should be that all
patients treated with a disease activity target will do well, even
if they are receiving very different therapies. The type of therapy
patients receive is not as important; it is only important that they
are at low disease activity or in remission. Several important caveats must be noted:
• The strategy of adding DMARDs, as in group 2, was more
effective than the strategy of switching from one DMARD to
another, at least for the DMARDs and their order of use in the
BeSt trial. Although the clinical outcomes were similar at 2
years for these groups, radiographic progression was greater in
group 1, more group 1 patients required infliximab, and many
group 1 patients ended up taking combinations of medications
anyway.
• Initial therapy with combinations of conventional DMARDs
(group 3) or combinations including biologic agents (group 4)
works more quickly than step-up therapy (group 2). However,
at 2 years, the clinical results were identical. There was a statistical advantage favoring the combination groups in terms of
)
total radiographic progression (not reaching a threshold of clinical significance unless it continues at a similar rate for years),
but no difference was noted in terms of joint space narrowing.
When relevant, further treatment changes need to be made
among the small group of patients (perhaps 10%) receiving
only conventional therapy who may experience radiographic
progression.

### Treatment of Patients With Active Disease Despite Treatment With Methotrexate

As previously discussed, excellent data from clinical trials in which
patients in general have high disease activity and poor prognostic
features show that approximately 30% of patients will achieve a
state of low disease activity or better if treated with modest doses
of oral MTX. This figure is consistent across three major trials that
documented this response.
If MTX therapy is optimized
by switching to SC delivery and pushing doses to at least 25 mg/
week, additional patients will experience disease control. Those
who have still not achieved this level of control are candidates
for treatment escalation, though hesitancy to make changes may
occur among patients who experienced significant improvement
with MTX. The first question to ask is: Should patients switch to
another DMARD, or should other DMARDs be added to MTX?
Data from multiple studies, including the previously discussed
BeSt trial, strongly support adding conventional DMARDs
or biologic agents to MTX rather than switching to another
DMARD.
A trial of 151 patients has addressed the question
of stopping MTX versus continuing MTX in patients with active
disease who start taking ETAN. The group randomized to continue to take MTX fared much better, with an ACR20 response
rate of 86% versus 64%, and they experienced significantly less
radiographic progression.
Excellent data now exist that support the efficacy of at least
16 different clinically available DMARDs or DMARD combinations when added to MTX in patients who have active disease
despite treatment with MTX. This is true for all of the commonly
used DMARDs, conventional, JAK inhibitors, and biologic
agents alike.

### Treatment of Patients With Active Disease Despite TNF Inhibition

Despite the effectiveness of conventional DMARD combinations
and the TNF inhibitors, a subset of patients will continue to have
unacceptable levels of disease activity. Estimates of the size of this
subset range from 20% to 40% of patients with RA. The good
news is that we still have multiple options with data to support
them, which include switching to another TNF agent,
starting rituximab,
starting abatacept,
inhibiting IL-6,
or
adding a JAK inhibitor.
Currently, data elucidating this
choice are limited, as discussed next. Thus until markers or parameters are available that may help clinicians select a therapy potentially reflecting a patient’s unique disease mechanisms, we will
again be making this decision largely empirically (see

in patients who cannot take MTX. Increases in low-density lipoprotein cholesterol were observed with tocilizumab treatment in

to biologic agents after MTX or TNFi failure and is discussed in
a later section.

### The Role for JAK Inhibitors

The therapeutic role for tofacitinib, baricitinib, and upadacitinib is being elucidated (see
Chapter 65
for a detailed discussion on JAK inhibitors). All three JAK inhibitors work well as
monotherapy in early-stage disease,
when added to MTX in
patients who have active disease despite use of MTX,
and
in patients who have failed to respond to a TNF inhibitor.
Unlike all other DMARDs, the efficacy of these JAK inhibitors is
not increased substantially by MTX. The considerable efficacy of
these medications is not in question, but their place in the treatment algorithm is largely being determined by their safety profile
compared with biologics.
Unlike biologic versus biologic trials restricted to MTX-inadequate responders, comparative trials of JAK inhibitors versus
biologic agents exist for both MTX- and biologic-inadequate
responders. Among MTX-inadequate responders, several trials
suggested that their efficacy is similar to, or marginally exceeds,
that of adalimumab.
In a trial of RA patients with
an inadequate response to, or intolerance of, a biologic agent,
treatment with upadacitinib was reported as superior to abatacept. However, these findings appear to be driven by the unique
influence of JAK inhibitors on CRP and slightly early responses
observed at 12 weeks.
Together, convincing evidence that JAK
inhibitors are meaningfully superior to biologic agents is currently
lacking.
Initial safety concerns emerged regarding thrombosis and
the increased frequency of herpes zoster infections with JAK
inhibitors. Furthermore, a mandatory post-marketing study was
completed to evaluate the safety of tofacitinib with regard to cardiovascular and malignancy events. ORAL Surveillance was a randomized, open-label, noninferiority trial of patients with active
RA despite MTX who were over 50 years of age and had at least
one additional cardiovascular risk factor.
The co-primary endpoints were MACE and malignancies, excluding nonmelanoma
skin cancer. Patients were randomized to add tofacitinib at doses
of 5 mg twice daily, 10 mg twice daily, or TNF inhibition (adalimumab or ETAN based on geographic region) to background
MTX. During the trial, a higher risk of death and pulmonary
embolism prompted switching all patients on the tofacitinib 10
mg dose to 5 mg. Among 4362 patients and over nearly 15,000
patient-years of follow-up, the incidence of MACE and malignancies in the tofacitinib-treated arms exceeded the noninferiority
margin (HR of 1.8) for comparisons to TNF inhibitors. Additionally, the pooled tofacitinib-treated arms had a 48% increased risk
of malignancy, which was statistically significant. Post hoc analyses
demonstrated the MACE risk related to tofacitinib was primarily
observed among the patients with a history of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease.
Further illustrating the importance of considering the population under treatment, pharmacoepidemiologic
studies using real-world evidence replicated trial findings among
a population mirroring ORAL Surveillance eligibility criteria,
but these findings did not generalize to the broader routine-care
RA patients initiating these therapies.
These concerning safety
results have brought further uncertainty to the decision of when
to utilize a JAK inhibitor in the treatment of RA, mandated rheumatologists to engage in discussions with patients about their risk
profile, and raised important questions such as the generalizability
of these findings to other JAK inhibitors.

### Treatment of Patients With “Refractory” Disease

As our expectations for our patients increase and the availability of different DMARDs expands, we push to control disease
sooner and use biologic agents or JAK inhibitors earlier. As a
result, RA is being labeled “refractory” by clinicians much earlier
than ever before.
When faced with “refractory” disease, a close
inspection of previous treatments that “failed” is in order.
It
is common for patients to be treated initially with low doses of
MTX (
≤
15 mg by mouth) for 3 months and then be started on
a TNF inhibitor with or without MTX. When the patients still
have active disease after a few months, they are inappropriately
labeled as
refractory
. It is critical to assess whether MTX therapy
has been optimized (see the previous discussion and
Chapter
61
); unless contraindications are present, most patients should
have their dose of MTX pushed to 25 mg per week, and SC
administration should be used. As previously discussed, data
show that triple therapy is as effective as MTX-ETAN
and
can provide benefit in patients who failed to respond to MTX
and ETAN.
Thus, this combination should be considered
before progressing too far down the biologic road. Some people
have raised concern about treatment with a subsequent biologic
agent after treatment with rituximab, which depresses B cell
counts significantly for at least 6 to 12 months. Limited data
from observational studies provide some reassurance because a
significant increase in toxicities has not been reported.
The lack of a universal definition of “refractory” RA and of
“unacceptable” levels of disease activity in this population has
hampered this discussion. With regard to levels of disease activity,
it is clear and probably appropriate that clinicians are willing to
tolerate more active disease in this “refractory” group before changing therapy, compared with patients who are naive to treatment or
those treated with active disease despite use of MTX only. EULAR
recently convened a task force to derive an initial definition for
“refractory” RA, selecting the term “difficult-to-treat RA.”
This
definition requires the following: (1) failure of
≥
2 biologic agents
or JAK inhibitors after conventional DMARD failure, (2) signs of
active or progressive disease, and (3) management of signs and/or
symptoms is perceived as problematic by rheumatologist and/or
patient. Once fulfilling this definition, additional strategies proposed by the EULAR task force that may assist in management
include
:
• Reassessing the RA diagnosis
• Assessing for comorbidities that mimic RA signs/symptoms or
interfere with RA assessment

• Assessing RA activity, including the use of advanced imaging if
uncertain
• Evaluating and optimizing treatment adherence
• Increasing focus on nonpharmacologic treatments (e.g., education, exercise, self-management)

### Biosimilars: Why Have We Been Slow to Adopt?

Many have voiced large and mostly unfounded concerns for the
introduction of biosimilars into the RA marketplace. We have of
course been using biosimilars for years; every batch of any biologic
is only biosimilar and never identical to the one before it. Simply
put, biosimilars should be interchangeable
and, with the advent
of new and better production processes that decrease impurities,
the challenge for the biosimilar developer is to not make them
bio-better. Almost all approved biosimilars have quicker onset
of action and lower injection site reactions than the innovator.
Therefore the real question in the United States, where biosimilars
have been approved for four bio-originators, is not should we be
using them but how to overcome incentives that drive physicians
to use the branded product. Biosimilars have been widely successful in Europe, with huge cost savings and no loss of efficacy.
Whether similar savings can be achieved in the United States given
the differences in health care systems remains to be determined.

### Use of Combination Biologic Agents (and JAK Inhibitors)

Combinations of conventional DMARDs and combinations of
conventional DMARDs, especially MTX, with biologic agents
have played a critical role in the vast improvement in responses to
treatments that we currently see. However, thus far efforts to combine biologic products have not met with success. To date, studies
to combine ETAN with anakinra have shown no improvement in
disease control, with an ACR50 score of 41% in the ETAN-only
arm compared with 31% in the combination arm, and they have
shown an increase in serious infections in the combination arm
(7.4% vs. 0%).
Similarly, in patients with active disease despite
use of ETAN, patients were randomized to add abatacept or placebo; efficacy showed a nonstatistically significant trend toward
improvement (ACR20 for combination, 48% vs. 31%;
p
= 0.07),
but again, with an increased risk of serious adverse events in the
combination group (17% vs. 3%) and serious infections (4% vs.
0%).
Additionally, a small trial in which patients who had active
disease despite use of MTX and either ETAN or adalimumab were
treated with the addition of rituximab or placebo showed very modest improvement in efficacy (an ACR20 score of 30% vs. 17%).
Again, serious adverse events including infections were numerically
greater in the group treated with combination therapy.
Despite these less-than-encouraging results from biologic combination trials, it is likely that ultimately patients will be treated
with combinations of biologic agents. What is critically needed are
good ways to measure the degree of immune suppression we create with our interventions—a type of thermostat for TNF, IL-1, or
IL-6.
Currently, it is likely that suppression is inadequate in some
patients, therefore not achieving optimal disease control, and that
suppression in other patients is occurring at dangerous levels, leading to increased toxicity. When better immune system monitoring
techniques are developed, not only will we be able to use the biologic agents we currently have in a more effective manner but it is
likely that we will be able to safely and effectively combine biologic
agents to improve outcomes in terms of both efficacy and safety.

## Managing Patients “At Target” Who Are Taking Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs

It is a powerful testament of how far therapy for patients with RA has
come that this section has now become a regular part of this chapter.
It is common practice for clinicians to ask whether and how to taper
DMARDs in patients who are “at target.” Unfortunately, few definitive data exist regarding what level of disease control is necessary
before considering tapering, how to identify the optimal patients for
tapering, and the specific DMARDs (and their order) to taper.
Because of their well-chronicled toxicities, the highest priority should be discontinuation of glucocorticoids and NSAIDs for
all patients. A number of trials
have shown that with effective DMARD therapy, glucocorticoid tapering not only is possible but should be expected. An excellent example of this is from
the BeSt trial, in which 92% of group 3 (the group that received
high-dose prednisone initially) had stopped taking all prednisone
at 2 years.
In this trial, prednisone was tapered only if the DAS
was at or below 2.4 (a low level of disease activity). More recently
in the SEMIRA trial, 65% of RA patients who achieved stable
low disease activity with tocilizumab and prednisone were able to
taper off prednisone over 16 weeks (5 mg to 0 mg by 1 mg every
4 weeks) and remain in low disease activity.
After a patient is off glucocorticoids and is still at target, it is
more difficult to select which patients are candidates for tapering
their DMARDs and a tapering approach. For many patients who
cannot reach remission, low disease activity represents their ideal
treatment “target.” For these individuals, maintaining low disease
activity is a reasonable goal when considering tapering,
although
their risk of flare is higher. A greater depth of disease control, not
only remission but “deep” (i.e., Boolean) remission, is associated
with lower risk of flares when tapering.
Regardless of which
disease activity state is chosen, a key determinant of tapering success is how long the patient has been at that disease activity state.
While the exact amount of time needed at the disease activity target is uncertain, patients should generally be stable for at least 6
to 12 months, reflecting eligibility criteria of many tapering trials.
Unfortunately, few additional predictors of tapering success have
been elucidated, namely seronegativity
(or attenuated autoantibody responses
) and the absence of synovitis and Doppler
signal on ultrasound (
Table 73.8
).
We do not currently have
laboratory tests or clinical parameters that can accurately predict
which patients can safely undergo tapering; their identification
and validation is a high priority.

trials have consistently studied TNFi, similar findings have been
observed when tapering rituximab doses.
A common DMARD tapering situation is the patient who is
receiving combination therapy with MTX and a biologic agent.
Because of the expense and concerns for long-term toxicities, the
approach tested in most tapering trials is to taper the biologic to
the lowest dose possible by decreasing the dose or lengthening the
interval between administrations.
Many of these patients
in practice may have started taking this combination initially or
had the biologic added after receiving only a very short course of
MTX (often with suboptimal use). We also know from early trials
that biologic doses lower than those usually clinically prescribed
can be very effective. For example, the ERA trial compared doses
of ETAN of 10 mg and 25 mg given twice weekly
; although the
data were slightly better for the higher dose, the low dose clearly
had substantial efficacy nearly equal to “full-dose” ETAN, both
as measured by clinical (ACR20) parameters and lack of radiographic progression. Therefore a significant percentage of current
patients in remission may be overdosed.
Two trials have directly compared tapering or discontinuing
conventional DMARDs versus TNFi. In the TARA trial, 57%
patients who tapered TNFi maintained disease control compared
with 67% tapering conventional DMARDs (
p
= 0.17).
Tapering was highly successful in both arms, with 82% of those tapering TNFi being on half or less of the original dose at the end
of the study. Importantly, there were no significant differences
in DAS, functional status, quality of life, and radiographic progression between the arms. Substantially higher health care costs
were incurred for those who continued TNFi (tapering conventional DMARDs) related to medication expenses.
Productivity
costs seem to offset this difference, though these estimates were
based on costs in the Netherlands and are expected to differ in
other countries and health care systems. In the SEAM-RA trial,
RA patients in SDAI remission on MTX and ETAN were randomized to MTX and ETAN continuation, MTX discontinuation, or ETAN discontinuation.
The proportion who remained
in SDAI remission at 48 weeks without disease worsening was
52.9%, 49.5%, and 28.7% (p = 0.004 between discontinuation
arms), respectively. What may initially seem a meaningful difference between discontinuation arms must be interpreted in the
context of three key points: (1) ETAN was utilized at full dose (50
mg/week) compared with suboptimal use of MTX (mean dose of
16 mg/week), (2) >90% of patients were in low disease activity at
week 48 with the use of strategies to recapture disease control, and
(3) prior tapering studies have established that abrupt discontinuation is not an appropriate strategy for de-escalating DMARDs.
Clearly, for those on conventional DMARDs and biologic agents,
tapering of a therapy should be chosen over discontinuation; a
substantial proportion of patients can maintain disease control
without, or with a lower dose of, a biologic agent; and there do
not appear to be significant consequences to attempting to taper
biologic agents.

## Prevention Trials: A Step in the Right Direction

Many observations clearly show that the immune response that
becomes RA starts years before most patients present with the classic symptom complex.
Studies to elucidate this transition
are ongoing,
as are initial studies aiming to intervene with
medications during this pre-RA period. These RA prevention trials have recruited individuals at risk of developing RA based on
autoantibody positivity, arthralgias, genetic risk, elevated hsCRP,
and/or subclinical joint inflammation on MRI or ultrasound.
Among these at-risk populations, atorvastatin, dexamethasone,
MTX, HCQ, abatacept, and rituximab have been compared with
placebo.
While none of these trials has demonstrated the
ability to prevent RA, some have shown the potential to delay its
onset and/or the benefit of early, or very early, treatment. In the
PRAIRI trial, a single 1000 mg dose of rituximab delayed the onset
of clinical arthritis by 12 months.
In the TREAT EARLIER
trial, 1 year of MTX treatment produced sustained improvements
after treatment cessation in physical function, work outcomes,
and MRI findings.
Preliminary results from the ARIAA trial
have similarly reported the ability to delay RA onset and improve
subclinical joint inflammation with 6 months of weekly SC abatacept.
Two intramuscular doses of dexamethasone reduced autoantibody concentrations up to 6 months later.
In contrast to
these trials, neither HCQ nor atorvastatin prevented or delayed
RA onset in their respective trials, both of which were terminated
early.
Designing, recruiting for, and conducting these prevention studies has been challenging, but these founding prevention trials will pave the way for future prevention efforts.

Finally, what is the place, if any, of “advanced” imaging techniques in evaluating patients with RA? Clearly, both ultrasound
and MRI are powerful techniques to evaluate early erosions and to
detect synovitis. One potential use of these obviously sensitive techniques, as previously suggested, may be to verify the true absence
of synovitis in patients who are believed to be in remission on clinical grounds.
However, trials have not found treatment guided
by these advanced imaging modalities to outperform conventional
T2T approaches. In a 24-month, open-label, randomized trial of
238 DMARD-naive patients with active RA, ultrasound-guided
tight control did not improve clinical outcomes compared with
conventional (DAS-guided) tight control.
Similarly, a 2-year
randomized trial of 200 RA patients in clinical remission found
equivalent remission and radiographic progression rates between
MRI-guided and conventional T2T strategies.

are shown by ultrasound to have synovitis are more likely to have
flares, the difference is of questionable clinical significance.
More importantly, these advanced modalities and expertise to
appropriately interpret these modalities is not widely available,
which significantly limits their practical application.

## Adjuncts to Medications

### Patient Education

RA is a lifelong disease, so it not only makes sense that patients
who are educated about their disease might do better, but good
data exist to show that education is associated with better outcomes.
Use of the Arthritis Self-Management Program leads
to less pain, decreased visits to physicians, and economic savings.
Chronic diseases such as RA affect the entire family,
and participation of spouses in educational group sessions leads to
additional beneficial effects.
Training in stress management is
particularly effective in improving measures of helplessness, coping, pain, self-efficacy, and health status.
Unfortunately, formal
education level, a factor that we cannot readily modify, is associated with morbidity and mortality rates. Formal educational level
is inversely proportional to these outcomes, and this finding cannot be explained by age, duration of disease, joint count, functional measures, or medications.
It is clearly important for patients to take an active role in the
management of their chronic disease. The more patients understand their disease and medications, the more control they feel
they have over this chronic condition. The bond that develops
between patients and clinicians as they work together to control
this lifelong disease is an important factor in outcome and the satisfaction level of both parties. The stronger the bond, the less likely
patients will become frustrated and turn to alternative therapies
that are not only expensive but may do more harm than good.

### Pain Control

If patients with RA are treated early and effectively with DMARDs
and therapy is escalated to achieve excellent control of the active
components of disease, the need for specific pain medications,
particularly narcotics, can be minimized. If pain is a major problem, the first thing to do is review the DMARD regimen and
modify it to achieve maximum control of any active synovitis.
Unfortunately, some patients present later in the course of their
disease after substantial joint damage has already occurred, and
their pain needs to be addressed. Pain can be the factor that limits
effective physical and occupational therapy. In addition to inhibiting function, pain is a major cause of depression in patients with
polyarthritis. To maximize therapy in patients with early-stage RA
or undifferentiated polyarthritis, pain must be controlled without
altering consciousness or generating addiction. Treatment strategies of education, rest, exercise, and disease-modifying therapies
are generally favored as an approach to pain control in people with
arthritis, and strategies that rely on narcotic derivatives do not
address control of active RA. In most medical centers, experts in

pain management are available for consultation by rheumatologists and primary care physicians (PCPs).
Chapter 68
provides an
excellent discussion of analgesics in rheumatic disease.

### Rest and/or Exercise and Activities of Daily Living

Education and supervision of a patient by trained professionals
regarding the importance of finding the best balance of rest and
exercise for inflamed joints are essential. This component of therapy can be started well before a definitive diagnosis is made. No
matter the cause, finding this balance should ensure that a patient
develops or retains sufficient strength to support joint function
without exacerbating inflammation.
Details of physical and occupational therapy are outlined in
Chapter 38
. While rest and splints may be needed to immobilize
the acutely and severely inflamed joints, even the most painful
joints must be moved passively through a full range of motion
each day to prevent flexion contractures. For moderately inflamed
joints, isometric exercises with muscles contracted in a fixed position provide adequate muscle tone without exacerbating joint
inflammation and pain. Patients with well-controlled arthritis
will benefit from variable-resistance programs, high-intensity
strength training, and aerobic exercise, which provide significant improvements in functional status, pain, and fatigue levels.
Aquatic therapy offers benefits of both aerobic and resistance exercise while simultaneously avoiding excess mechanical loading on
joints. Older patients with RA benefit from progressive resistance
exercises, similar to younger patients. In a study of older patients
given closely regulated workouts, maximal strength of all major
exercised muscle groups was increased 75% without exacerbation
of disease activity.
Not only does prescribed sustained exercise
increase muscle strength but also it helps the ability of patients to
perform daily routines, improves global assessments and moods,
and can decrease pain.
Most patients with RA should have one or more sessions with
a licensed occupational therapist (OT) to learn how to preserve
joint function and alignment while carrying out the necessary and
enjoyable activities of daily living and to be introduced to available assistive devices. Ideally, this initially should occur early in
the disease course and be utilized again, as needed, throughout the
disease course. The basic concept is to avoid excessive force applied
across non–weight-bearing joints and to avoid unnecessary impact
loading on weight-bearing joints. A prospective and controlled
Canadian trial demonstrated that home therapy by OTs produced
a statistically significant and clinically important improvement in
function in patients with RA.

thankfully decreasing, few interventions have been as successful in
improving patient mobility and quality of life as having properly
timed hip and knee replacements.

## Perioperative Management of Rheumatoid Arthritis Medications

Not uncommonly, the question of what to do with RA medications around the time of surgery comes up. The ACR has worked
with orthopedic surgeons to put together excellent guidelines,
which were recently updated.
Highlights of these guidelines
include continuing all conventional DMARDs through the surgical period, holding all biologics prior to surgery for a length
of time consistent with their individual functional half-lives, and
holding JAK inhibitors for 3 days prior to surgery. These therapies
can be resumed after the wound shows evidence of healing, typically around 14 days post-operatively.

## Patients With Rheumatoid Arthritis Are Doing Better

The prognosis for patients with RA has improved dramatically.
Every rheumatologist fortunate enough to see the changes during
the past few decades is only too happy to witness the improvements in care. The clinic days when wheelchairs were commonplace and symptomatic C1 cervical subluxations, chronic
leg ulcers, constrictive pericarditis, and corneal melts were not
uncommon are, we hope, gone forever. Despite this firmly held
perception by clinicians, strong data to support this change have
been slow to accumulate.
It may take up to 20 years for the results of a change in therapy or treatment approach to fully translate into improvement
in long-term outcomes, such as joint replacements and mortality. However, emerging evidence suggests the therapeutic principles utilized in the past two decades are beginning to impact
these long-term outcomes. Cohort studies from the Veterans
Health Administration and Olmstead County have demonstrated
improved longevity in RA over more recent time periods.
In the same cohort of patients in Olmstead County, knee surgeries and hand surgeries substantially decreased.
A 30% reduction in severe extra-articular manifestations of RA has occurred
since the year 2000 among more than 35,000 U.S. veterans with
RA.
Data from California for the years 1983 to 2001 showed
that hospitalizations for RA vasculitis or splenectomy for Felty’s
syndrome decreased by 33% and 71%, respectively.
Data from
Sweden and Spain
both show that DASs and health assessment
scores have significantly improved during the past decade. Further
evidence of improved long-term outcomes in RA is expected to
follow these early reports.

## Conclusion

Despite the progress made, no cure exists for RA, and the vast
majority of our patients require lifelong DMARD therapy. Thus
plenty of opportunity remains for further progress. By the time
the 13th edition of this book is published, we will most likely
have new biologic agents and small-molecule DMARDs available
for use in treatment regimens. Regardless of new therapies that
expand our portfolio of choices, the two areas where we still need
to see the most progress are profiling patients based on parameters that predict differential responses to different therapies and
monitoring the types and intensity of immunologic modification
that we are producing. When substantial progress is made in these
areas, even without new therapies, we will be able to obtain vastly
superior and more expeditious disease control.
