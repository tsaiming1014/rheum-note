PHILIPPE GASQUE AND XAVIER GUILLOT

## Introduction

Many viruses are associated with arthritis that can be reminiscent
of rheumatoid arthritis (RA): Old-World alphaviruses such as
Chikungunya virus (CHIKV) and Ross River virus (RRV)
and,
more rarely, parvovirus B19, Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), rubella,
and hepatitis B and C virus (HBV/HCV).
Other viruses
such as adenoviruses, coxsackie viruses, HIV, influenza, measles,
mumps, and varicella zoster are more likely to be linked to autoimmune responses such as type 1 diabetes and may also affect the
joints, leading to reactive arthritis.
Also associated with arthritis in some cases, Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) is discussed elsewhere in this text (see
Ch. 119
).
The phenotypes of these diseases may be distinct in terms of
the primary cells and tissues initially targeted, the specific humoral
and cellular immune responses elaborated, and the inflammatory
effector pathways that predominate. Despite the different nature
of each viral disease, however, they clearly share some common
pathogenic mechanisms involved in arthralgias including acute
nocioception at nerve endings, macrophage activation and polarization, and fibroblast hyperplasia and invasion sustained by
major angiogenesis in the synovial tissues as described in RA (
Fig.
117.1
).
Early and robust systemic innate and adaptive immune antiviral responses are important events to protect the host, including
the contribution of the epithelia, a major portal of entry for many
of these viruses at the lung and skin levels.
However, in the context of a major infection associated with high viremia and a failure
to mount a robust anti-viral response, it has been proposed that
the virus would escape from immune surveillance and find protective niches in tissues such as synovial tissues.
Indeed, the acute
viral infection might, in a genetically predisposed or otherwise
susceptible host (e.g., association with human leukocyte antigen
[HLA] or immune senescence in elderly patients), be able to recapitulate a polarized inflammatory response as described in RA and
other autoimmune diseases.
Viruses could also polarize host-cell
defense mechanisms such as interferon (IFN) type I signaling pathways, apoptosis, and autophagy to its own advantage.
As a Trojan horse scenario, it has been shown that viruses (e.g., CHIKV)
could hide in apoptotic cell remnants only to then be phagocytosed
by tissue-resident macrophages and lead to viral persistence.
Synovial tissue is a sanctuary for many arthritogenic viruses,
which can recruit reactive T cells and more rarely B cells to the
joints to drive chronic inflammation and, ultimately, contribute
to hyperplastic synovial pannus tissue formation and, finally, to
the destruction of cartilage and bone as found in RA.
Viruses
could be brought into the synovial tissue by infected monocytes,
fibrocytes, or lymphocytes before being transferred to resident
cells such as synovial fibroblasts.
Autoimmunity against self-antigens prominently expressed
by infected synovial fibroblasts may be exceptionally engaged
through bystander activation and the expression of cryptic antigens and more rarely through well-described molecular mimicry
mechanisms.
Surprisingly, many of the self-antigens are nuclear or mitochondrial proteins released by dying cells as a consequence of the
infection but are also critically involved as natural adjuvants (also
called alarmins) to alert innate and adaptive immune cells.
Chronic viral infection in the joint may contribute to the continuous release of several of these alarmins (e.g., high-mobility group
box 1 [HMGB1], cardiolipin, adenosine triphosphate [ATP]),
which could signal through pattern recognition receptors (PRRs)
such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs) to activate nuclear factor kappa
B (NFκ
B) signaling and the synthesis of pro-inflammatory
cytokines.

Paradoxically, the events mobilized by the innate immune system and involved in the clearance of viruses could also contribute to sustained chronic inflammation leading to arthralgia and
ultimately the development of destructive arthritis. Regulatory
mechanisms to control chronic inflammation have been recently
emphasized, but little is known about their contributions in synovial tissues and whether or not they could favor viral persistence
and chronic arthritis.
Preventive measures should include vaccinations whenever
possible in endemic regions and particularly for vulnerable individuals such as older adults. Other treatment regimens include
analgesics and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
for pain relief, and methotrexate (MTX) to control actual arthritis, although this may compromise patients’ immune surveillance
to prevent viral reactivation.
This chapter describes key examples of some of the major
DNA and RNA viruses associated with arthritis and proposes
common pathogenic mechanisms that may be the driving force
of these chronic viral arthritic diseases. It should be emphasized
that the mechanisms may involve exceptionally autoreactive T and
B cells but more commonly involves a crosstalk between persistently infected synovial fibroblasts and macrophages, contributing
to chronic inflammation as described in several chronic fibrotic
disorders.

## Examples of Arthritogenic Viruses

### Parvovirus B19

Parvovirus B19 is well known for causing infection that has been
linked to a number of conditions, such as fifth disease in children
and arthralgias in adults (
Table 117.1
).
This virus can mutate
at high frequencies and as much as those typical of RNA viruses.
It has long been known to be also associated with arthritis.
B19
is the smallest single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) virus of 6 kb and is
20 to 25 nanometers (nm) in diameter. The genome encodes for
two structural proteins, VP1 (nucleotides 2444–4786) and VP2
(nucleotides 3125–4786), and for the major nonstructural protein
NS1 (nucleotides 436–2451).
Parvovirus B19 infection may be particularly deleterious during pregnancy because of the consequences on the fetus. The
critical time of infection is during the first 20 weeks of gestation,
when the fetal immune system is thought to be immature and red
cell mass increases more rapidly, resulting in inhibition of erythropoiesis. B19 is the cause of erythema infectiosum (also known
as fifth disease), a common childhood rash, and of various joint
complications especially in adult women, although many B19
infections remain subclinical. The seroprevalence increases with
age and more than 70% of adults are seropositive.
Parvovirus
B19 is mainly transmitted by respiratory aerosol spread from individuals with acute infection.
Parvovirus B19 replicates in human red blood cell precursors
of the bone marrow.
This leads to transient arrest of red cell formation. Hence, B19 infection can lead to transient aplastic crisis
manifesting as severe anemia. B19 infection has, however, been
shown to affect other hematopoietic cell lineages, causing various
cytopenias. B19 DNA (DNA remnants) or antigen has furthermore been found in the fetal heart, liver, spleen, kidney, lung,
muscle, and brain and in the heart, lung, liver, kidney, testes, cerebrospinal fluid, and synovium of children or adults.
The main
cellular receptor of B19 has been identified as the P-antigen of the
blood group P system.
P antigen, also known as globoside, is
present on erythrocytes, erythroblast, megakaryocytes, endothelial
cells, fetal liver, and heart skeletal muscle cells. B19 also has two
co-receptors:
α
5
β
1 integrin and KU80 nuclear autoantigen.
Immunity after infection with B19 is conferred by a B cell
immune response with development of neutralizing antibodies.
T cell–mediated responses are also involved and directed essentially against the capsid proteins VP1 and VP2.
B19 has been the object of intense investigation to clarify
whether it is able to trigger autoimmune diseases.
It has been
suggested that it might be a major contributing factor or the precipitating agent of several autoimmune disorders including RA,
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), antiphospholipid syndrome,
systemic sclerosis, and vasculitides. The joint manifestations
sometimes mimic those of RA, and up to about 50% of patients
with chronic B19 arthropathy meet the criteria of the American
College of Rheumatology (ACR) for RA, suggesting that the persistence of the virus may be involved in the initiation and perpetuation of the disease.
The involved joints—mainly the wrists,
knees, and small joints of the hands—are painful, swollen, and
often symmetrically affected.
The onset of arthralgias coincides with the detection of circulating immunoglobulin (Ig) M and IgG antibodies against the viral
structural proteins, suggesting that the deposition of immune complexes in the synovial membranes may be responsible for the onset
of the symptoms, as it happens for erythema infectiosum. Parvovirus B19 DNA can be detected in synovial fluid and cells, and
synovial biopsy specimens of affected joints revealed infection of
CD20-positive B cells or CD3-positive T cells by double immunostaining.
In a subset of RA patients, parvovirus B19 DNA was
detected in peripheral blood cells and synovial tissues, and VP1/
VP2 proteins were detected in peripheral T cells, macrophages, and
synovial neutrophils.
In a more recent study, the levels of circulating cytokines were measured in B19 DNA–positive or –negative
RA patients. Plasma levels of interleukin (IL)-4 were higher in the
former group while the level of pro-inflammatory cytokines was
either not significantly different (e.g., for IL-12, IL-2, and tumor
necrosis factor [TNF]) or was reduced (IFNγ
).
A reduction in
the level of key pro-inflammatory cytokines may contribute to an
immunosuppressive environment and favoring viral persistence.
Molecular mimicry between host and viral proteins may contribute to the induction of autoimmunity.
B19 infection has been
associated with production of antibodies directed against a plethora of autoantigens, including nuclear antigens, rheumatoid factor (RF), neutrophils, cytoplasmic antigens (CAs), mitochondrial
antigens (MAs; e.g., cardiolipin), smooth muscle actin (SMA),
gastric parietal antigens, and phospholipid.
For instance, one
group reported that antibodies against B19 viral proteins reacted
specifically with human keratin, collagen type II, ssDNA, and
cardiolipin.
More importantly, levels of reactivity against keratin and collagen type II were correlated with clinical hallmarks.
Specifically, immunoglobulins from patients with arthritis reacted
preferentially with collagen type II, whereas immunoglobulins
affinity purified from patients with skin rashes reacted preferentially with keratin. To confirm a role of the virus in inducing an
autoimmune response, BALB/c mice were immunized with the
viral proteins and autoantibodies against keratin, collagen type II,
cardiolipin, and ssDNA were subsequently detected in the majority of the mice that developed a strong anti-virus response.

In patients with systemic sclerosis, the
presence of parvovirus B19 in bone marrow and/or skin biopsy
specimens has been reported from a significant number of subjects.
Interestingly, the same authors found high levels of B19
DNA and of TNF expression in endothelium and fibroblasts of
scleroderma patients.
The degree of viral transcript expression
correlated with active endothelial cell injury and perivascular
inflammation—features considered to be important in the initial
phases of the disease. In SLE patients, the presence of circulating
B19 DNA is uncommon (less than 5%), but an association was
found between a former B19 infection (as confirmed by high IgG
and IgM levels to the virus) and the prevalence of autoantibodies
against phospholipids.
It has been shown that B19 can induce in vitro an invasive phenotype in normal human synovial fibroblasts, given their robust
ability to degrade reconstituted cartilage matrix, but the signaling
mechanisms involved in cell infection and cell polarization remain
largely ill-characterized.
More recently, it has been shown that
the viral NS1 protein of B19 can activate the NLRP3 inflammasome pathway involved in robust cytokine production by leukocytes.
Additional experiments are warranted to better establish
biologic links between the capacities of viruses to polarize resident
joint cells into pro-inflammatory and aggressive phenotypes.

### Rubella Virus

Rubella virus is the sole member of the genus
Rubivirus
and
belongs to the
Togaviridae
family together with alphaviruses (see
next section). Rubella is an enveloped ssRNA virus composed of
9757 nucleotides and containing two long open reading frames,
the 5
′
proximal (which encodes nonstructural proteins) and the 3
′
proximal (which encodes the structural proteins, Capsid, E1, and
E2). The virion is spherical and measures 50 to 70 nm in diameter,
with a 30 nm dense core.
Transmission is by nasopharyngeal secretions. Most cases of
infection lead to a mild, self-limiting measles-like disease but
major concerns arise when rubella infects the fetus, which can lead
to miscarriage or congenital rubella syndrome (CRS). In CRS,
rubella virus is able to infect the placenta and spread to the fetus,
causing systemic inflammation and altering the function of many
organs.
Rubella E1 protein binds to myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein (MOG) and ectopic expression of MOG on nonpermissive
cells can contribute to infection.
Moreover, there is a high level
of homology between rubella E2 protein and MOG, which could
explain the ability of antibodies against rubella to cause demyelination at least in vitro.
Joint symptoms are a complication of naturally acquired
rubella and occur in more than 50% of adult females with rubella
but are rare among children and adult males with rubella.
Symmetric or migratory arthralgias are more common than synovitis.
Morning stiffness is prominent and symptoms usually resolve over
a few days to 2 weeks. Proximal interphalangeal, metacarpophalangeal, wrist, elbow, ankle, and knee joints are most frequently
affected. Periarthritis, tenosynovitis, and carpal tunnel syndrome
may be seen. In some patients, symptoms may persist for months
to years. Following vaccination, joint symptoms occur less frequently and are usually less severe and of shorter duration than
those that occur following naturally acquired rubella.
Live attenuated rubella vaccines have also caused a high frequency of postvaccination myalgia and paresthesia—symptoms similar to those
in natural infection—beginning 2 weeks after inoculation and
lasting less than a week. However, in some patients, symptoms
may persist for more than a year. RA27/3, the vaccine strain in
current use, may cause post-vaccination joint symptoms in 15%
or more of recipients.
Persistent rubella infection may be established in synovial cell
cultures and as described in tissues.
Rubella-specific antibody
titers in the synovial fluid of some patients with chronic arthritis
are higher than those in serum, suggesting local synthesis of specific antibody. Persistence of rubella virus is also suggested by our
previous studies, which demonstrated that virus-specific IgM may
persist for up to 4 years following both natural infection and vaccination.
The presence of rubella RNA in synovial tissues from
patients may be due to reactivation and as a result of immunodeficiency or declining immunity in old age.
Rubella arthritis needs to be differentiated from other viral
arthritides and from inflammatory arthritides, including RA. It
may be clinically confused with parvovirus B19 infection, but
serologies differentiate the two infections. NSAIDs are useful to
control symptoms.

### Chikungunya Alphavirus

CHIKV is an enveloped RNA positive-strand alphavirus of 12kb
belonging to the
Togaviridae
family, transmitted by
Aedes
mosquitoes (
Table 117.1
).
Aedes albopictus
, the main competent vector
of CHIKV, has become indigenous to many regions throughout
the world. The
Alphavirus
group comprises 29 viruses, six of which
can cause human joint disorders (arthralgias evolving into arthritis), namely CHIKV, O’Nyong-Nyong virus, Semliki forest virus
(SFV) (in Africa, Asia, and Europe), RRV (in Australia and the
islands of the Pacific), Sindbis virus (cosmopolitan), and Mayaro
virus (in South America and French Guyana).
Several million CHIKV cases in humans have now been
described in Africa, Southeast Asia, islands of the western and
eastern Pacific and Indian oceans, and the Middle East (for a
review, see ref.
69
). Recent CHIKV outbreaks have been reported
since November 2013 and have spread to several parts of the
Americas.
CHIKV infection in humans results in a brief, usually 5- to
7-day viremia, which is in principle controlled primarily by IFN
type I and antibodies.
However, CHIKV disease is often
debilitating and associated with persistent arthralgias and arthritis
that can last from weeks to months.
Many patients developing chronic symptoms do not respond well to the usual analgesics, suggesting that the nature of chronic pain may be not only
nociceptive but also neuropathic. Persistent symptoms, mainly
joint and muscular pain, have been reported several months after
CHIKV infection.
Long-term CHIKV rheumatic manifestations are frequent underlying post-epidemic conditions. Predictors
of nonrecovery include age >45 years, severe initial joint pain, and
the presence of underlying osteoarthritis (OA).
Rheumatic
manifestations occur in up to 50% of adult patients and typically consist of a febrile arthritis mainly affecting the extremities
(ankles, wrists, phalanges).
Typically, joint symptoms occur
in a fluctuating manner (often perceived as infectious relapses by
the patients) but do not change anatomic location. Pain within or
around tendons is also a common trait and may evolve into tenosynovitis. One group
reported high levels of CHIKV IgM antibodies in a cohort of Indian patients with post-CHIKV RA-like
illnesses. These patients were naive for musculoskeletal disorders
prior to CHIKV infection. Surprisingly, few were positive for RF
or anti–cyclic citrullinated peptide (CCP) antibodies, and these

findings were confirmed in several surveys of CHIKV patients
with chronic arthralgias months post-infection.
Tenosynovitis/enthesopathy rather than synovitis was evidenced by ultrasonography in several cases and these unique observations are in
agreement with our x-ray imaging of enthesitis in post-CHIK RA
patients in la Réunion.
In one study, patients with a diagnosis of RA following CHIKV
infection were given classic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic
drug therapy with MTX, salazopyrine, leﬂunomide, or hydroxychloroquine. In cases of inadequate response to MTX, treatment
was successful with TNF inhibitors (e.g., etanercept, adalimumab).
As the chronic disease progresses, more severe arthritis
was reported 1 to 2 years post-CHIKV infection. Some patients
with chronic rheumatic symptoms developed progressive erosive
arthritis.
In Manimunda’s study, it was confirmed that the
levels of RF and anti-CCP antibodies were not elevated in contrast
to what is known in traditional RA.
In the same study, one-third
of the patients who had joint pain met the ACR classification criteria for RA. Magnetic resonance imaging findings included joint
effusion, bony erosion, marrow edema, synovial thickening, tendonitis, and tenosynovitis.
CHIKV infection has been reported in multiple organs of the
body and particularly the synovial tissues in humans and animal
models.
In vitro studies have been instrumental for the
comprehension of CHIKV tissue tropisms and to provide insights
into the causal pathogenic mechanisms. The main target cells for
CHIKV infection and replication are fibroblasts.
Recent studies corroborate this finding in addition to demonstrating that
CHIKV readily infects human fetal lung fibroblasts (MRC-5 cells)
and human synovial and skin fibroblasts.
Not all fibroblasts
are equally susceptible to CHIKV infection. It has been shown
that resting fibroblasts and a subpopulation of synovial fibroblasts
(CD141+/CD90+) manifest some resistance through mechanisms
still largely unknown.
CHIKV can also infect epithelial cells
(e.g., HeLa, HEK293, but not A549), endothelial cells, hepatocytes (carcinoma HuH7), neurons, astrocytes, neuroblastoma
cell lines (e.g., SH-SY5Y), and muscle satellite cells.
The cellular receptor that mediates virus entry has recently been
characterized and identified as matrix remodeling associated 8
antigen (mxra8, also known as limitrin).
Mutagenesis experiments have shown that mxra8 binds to a surface-exposed domain
of CHIKV E2 protein. CD147 may also contribute to the binding to E2 and facilitate viral entry into host cells.
Of note, the
receptor to mediate SFV entry has recently been identified as the
very low-density lipoprotein receptor.
Evidence from biopsies
of infected patients showed that CHIKV antigens were detected
mostly in cells located in the skeletal muscle epimysium, of the
joint capsule, and of the dermis.
In contrast, immune cells, such
as monocytes, lymphocytes, dendritic cells, natural killer (NK)
cells, and their cell line counterparts (THP1, U937, H9) are not
permissive to CHIKV infection.
However, human monocyte
and macrophage infection remains controversial and it is likely
that they could be infected but with a poor capacity to replicate
CHIKV.
There are very few studies to support the idea that alphaviruses
will contribute to chronic RA-like diseases through the production of autoantibodies against self-antigens as it has been described
for B19, EBV, or HCV. One study reported that Finnish patients
with Pogosta disease following Sindbis infection had autoantibodies to NA, MA, and RF at a higher prevalence compared with
healthy Finnish subjects at 3 years post-infection.
Interestingly,
anti-CCP antibodies were detected only in one patient out of 49,
who was also positive for RF. It remains to be determined whether
this autoimmune profile is more prominent in recovered Pogosta
patients versus those with long-term arthritis.

### Epstein-Barr Virus

EBV has been found in association with several common autoimmune diseases including SLE and multiple sclerosis (MS).
The epidemiologic association with RA is more controversial.
EBV belongs to the genus
Lymphocryptovirus
within the subfamily
Gammaherpesvirus
of the
Herpesvirus
family. It is the fourth
member of this family. Herpesviruses are large DNA viruses with
a linear double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) genome inside a membrane-coated icosaedrical capsid. The EBV genome has a length
of 172 kilobases (kb). EBV has strong tissue tropism for B lymphocytes. The virus is associated with a wide variety of neoplasms
including lymphoid tumors like Burkitt lymphoma, Hodgkin’s
disease, NK T cell lymphoma, epithelial malignancies, especially
undifferentiated nasopharyngeal carcinoma, gastric carcinoma,
and carcinomas of the salivary glands. The virus is shed into the
saliva of persistently infected individuals who spread the virus to
uninfected individuals. When transmitted orally, EBV initially
infects either epithelial cells or resting tonsillar B lymphocytes.
EBV binds through its viral envelope glycoprotein (gp)350 to
the complement receptor type 2 (CR2/CD21) that is primarily
expressed on B cells.
This receptor can also interact with IFNα
, but the role of such molecular interactions in the IFN-mediated anti-viral response is unknown.
B cell–bound virions can
also efficiently transfer to CD21-negative epithelial cells, which
enhances the transfer of virus to others cells (possibly to fibroblasts) and establishes latency in B cells and other cells.
Both the latent and lytic EBV proteins are potent immunogens and elicit vigorous B and T cell responses. However, patients
with RA have impaired control of EBV infection given that their
peripheral blood T lymphocytes are less efficient at controlling
the outgrowth of EBV-infected B cells.
In immunosuppressed
and immunodeficient patients, or in individuals with a functional
defect of EBV-specific T cells, lytic EBV replication is regularly
activated and an increased viral load can be detected in the blood.
Enhanced lytic replication results in new infection events and
EBV-associated transformation events and seems to be a risk factor both for malignant transformation and the development of
autoimmune diseases. It is conceivable that an increased load or
altered presentation of a limited set of lytic or latent EBV proteins
that cross-react with cellular self-antigens triggers and perpetuates
the pathogenic processes that result in arthritis, MS, and SLE.
Of critical note, patients with RA develop EBV-positive B cell
lymphomas when MTX is used for immunosuppression, which is
not the case for other immune-modulating drugs.
It has been
proposed that MTX could induce lytic EBV replication in latently
infected EBV-positive cell lines in vitro. Thus MTX may promote
the generation of lymphomas in RA and polymyositis patients by
its immunosuppressive properties, combined with a specific reactivation of latent EBV genomes.
While a causal role of EBV in the generation of malignancies
is established, a causal role in the generation of autoimmune diseases is circumstantial. However, cross-reactivity between viral
antigens and self-antigens and the immortalization of pre-existing
autoreactive B cells through infection with EBV with the consequent production of autoantibodies may be plausible viral mechanisms for triggering autoimmunity. Indeed, one group recently
demonstrated high-affinity molecular mimicry between the EBV

transcription factor EBV nuclear antigen 1 and the central nervous system protein glial cell adhesion molecule (glial CAM on
astrocytes and oligodendrocytes). Post-translational modification
of glial-CAM may facilitate the molecular mimicry. A resulting
abundance of cross-reactive antibodies may contribute to demyelination in MS patients. Strong genetic risk factors for developing RA are the alleles that contain peptide sequence homologies
to EBV gp110 (BALF4).
BALF4 is normally one of the most
abundant EBV proteins in late lytic infection and also serves as
a target of antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity against
EBV-infected cells. The BALF4 open reading frame contains the
sequence QKRAAQRAA, which is highly homologous to the
RA susceptibility determinant (i.e., QKRAA found in certain
HLA class II DR
β
chains, such as DR4). Carriers of this shared
epitope have a significantly higher risk of developing anti-CCPpositive RA, particularly among those with a history of cigarette
smoking.

### Hepatitis C Virus and Other Flaviviruses

HCV is a positive-strand RNA virus of the family
Flaviviridae
.
Studies of this virus have made huge leaps forward since the
implementation of efficient cell culture systems. Worldwide, over
130 million people are chronically infected with HCV. The viral
genome consists of a single open reading frame that is less than 10
kb long. The proteins of this virus are arranged along the genome
in the following order: N terminal-core-envelope (E1)–E2–
p7-nonstructural protein 2 (NS2)–NS3–NS4A–NS4B–NS5A–
NS5B–C terminal.
Acute infection goes along with mild and generalized symptoms, and therefore mostly remains undiagnosed. In more than
half of the patients, however, the infection persists and, over the
years, can cause liver damage such as fibrosis, cirrhosis, or hepatocellular carcinoma. Viral antigen is detectable within the first
weeks of exposure, accompanied by a rise in serum liver transaminases. Antibodies develop and clear HCV in some patients;
however, in chronic HCV infection, viral anti-genemia and HCV
RNA persist despite humoral and cellular immune responses.
There is also evidence to suggest a possible pathogenic role for
HCV-associated immunity in a broad spectrum of autoimmune
diseases, including RA.
Although this association may be sporadic or indicate an increased incidence of HCV infection in
patients with autoimmune disease, case reports documenting the
occurrence of acute and chronic HCV infection preceding autoimmune disease suggests a causal role in individuals who may be
predisposed to the development of autoimmunity. Liver biopsy in
HCV-infected patients is useful to establish disease severity and to
exclude other causes of hepatitis such as alcoholism or hemochromatosis. A probable role for HCV infection in autoimmune hepatitis is emerging with the concurrent detection of autoantibodies
such as anti-nuclear antibody (ANA) and anti-SMA.
Polyarthralgias and polyarthritis have been associated with
HCV infection, and HCV RNA has been isolated from synovial
fluid.
Patients with chronic HCV associated with polyarthralgias/myalgias often have antibodies including RF and ANA without anti-dsDNA or anti-CCP antibodies.
In one study, the
prevalence of arthritis in chronic HCV patients was estimated to
be 6.8%, and ANAs were more frequently positive in the subset of
arthritic HCV patients.
The optimal treatment for HCV-associated polyarthritis has not been determined, but MTX has been
used with some success and minimal complications, although
this agent should be avoided in those with cirrhosis.
Discussed
further in
Chapter 92
of this textbook, arthralgias/arthritis may
also act as a manifestation of HCV-mediated mixed (type II)
cryoglobulinemia.
Diverse receptors or factors are involved in HCV infection and
include CD81, scavenger receptor class B type I and claudin-1.
Occludin is also an essential HCV cell entry factor.
It has been
demonstrated that synovial fibroblasts from RA or OA patients
were not able to support HCV replication despite the fact that
they express CD81.
Arthralgia post-dengue infection has been reported but without translating into arthritis as described for the other arboviruses
such as CHIKV. However, in the context of acute infection, articular inflammation may be more pronounced in patients already
affected by RA. In a Brazilian retrospective cohort study including 69 SLE and 301 RA patients suffering from dengue infection, the morbidity and hospitalization rate were significantly
increased compared with noninfected SLE and RA patients.
Zika is another emerging RNA flavivirus. It has been detected in
the synovial fluid of a RA patient and has been linked to disease
exacerbation.
The precise prevalence of arthralgia in Zika cases
remains poorly defined, although a recent meta-analysis revealed
no evidence of chronic rheumatic manifestations following Zika
infection.

## Arthritogenic Viruses Hijack the Innate Interferon Type I Response to Favor Persistence

A major role of the immune system (innate and adaptive) is to rapidly control infection by viruses, for instance, at the major portals
of entry (skin, nasopharynx) and before dissemination to other
organs.
To initiate an effective anti-viral immune response, all
cells should be capable of sensing and responding appropriately
to pathogens. This coordinated response should in principle limit
viral spreading and persistence through the mobilization of adaptive immune T and B cells.
A growing number of germ-line pattern recognition receptors
(PRRs) have been characterized and are expressed on immune
and nonimmune structural cells. These receptors are capable of
recognizing a plethora of conserved pathogen-associated molecular patterns that do not occur under normal circumstances in the
host.
The PRRs sense specific viral structures and induce the
production of type I IFN. Two types of PRRs exist: ubiquitously
expressed cytoplasmic RNA sensing molecules and membranebound TLRs. The expression of TLR is more limited than that of
the cytoplasmic receptors.
The retinoic-acid-inducible gene I (RIG-I)–like RNA helicase
receptors (RLRs), which include RIG-I and melanoma differentiation associated gene 5 (MDA-5), are a ubiquitously expressed
group of cytosolic receptors that detect viral RNA structures and
induce type I IFNα
and -
β
production.
Binding of viral RNA
enables interaction between the RLRs and downstream signaling molecules such as IPS-1 (IFNβ
promoter stimulator-1; also
known as MAVS, CARDIF, and VISA), which activate transcription factors (e.g., interferon regulatory factor [IRF] 3) and
regulate the production of type I IFN. The expression of RLRs
is low in the steady state but is highly induced by type I IFN,
which enhances their capacity to recognize viruses and to propagate the anti-viral response. At least 10 TLRs have been identified
in humans, of which three (TLR3, TRL7 and TLR9) recognize

nucleic acid viral components and stimulate type I IFN production.
Interestingly, TLR expression is also regulated by IFN.
TLR3 is triggered by dsRNA, whereas TLR7 recognizes guanosine-rich and uridine-rich ssRNA, and TLR9 senses unmethylated
cytosine–phosphate–guanine motifs. TLR4, which is expressed on
the cell surface, recognizes lipopolysaccharide from bacteria but
also viral proteins and can stimulate type I IFN production.
Release of type I IFN from infected cells results in autocrine
and paracrine stimulation of the IFNα
/
β
receptor, which through
associated tyrosine and Janus (JAK) kinases leads to the phosphorylation of STAT (signal transducers and activators of transcription)
1 and 2. STAT1/2 heterodimers associate with IFN regulatory factor 9 and bind to IFN-stimulated response elements present in the
promoter region of the IFN-stimulated genes (ISGs).
ISG-encoded proteins represent the anti-viral effector molecules that directly inhibit molecular and biochemical activities
required for virus replication. Expectedly, type I IFN was shown to
be essential for the anti-viral activities against many arthritogenic
viruses as demonstrated in vitro and in vivo.
However,
many viruses (including CHIKV) block nearly all aspects of the
IFN regulatory pathway. This includes the disruption of dsRNA
and IFN receptor/JAK–STAT signaling events, the inhibition of
IRF and NFκ
B functions, and other mechanisms that target the
anti-viral actions of specific ISG products.
The anti-viral molecules induced by IFN include members of
the apolipoprotein B mRNA-editing enzyme catalytic polypeptide (APOBEC) family and can mutate viral genomes, the Mx
GTPase, an inhibitor of viral polymerases, and members of the
tripartite motif family including the autoantigen Ro/SSA 52 kDa
(Ro52, also known as TRIM21), which can inhibit viral replication at multiple stages in the life cycle. RNA-activated protein
kinase (PKR) is yet another example of an anti-viral molecule.
Immune recognition is enhanced in cells exposed to type I IFN
and controlling major histocompatibility complex class I expression and resulting in enhanced presentation of both viral and selfantigens for recognition by the immune system. Other changes
include the induction of molecules (such as p53) that increase
susceptibility to cell death–inducing stimuli, thus enhancing the
killing of virally infected cells by the immune system. Type I IFN
regulates pathways in target cells, antigen-presenting cells (such as
dendritic cells [DCs] and macrophages), and effector cells (which
mediate anti-viral effects and tissue damage).
Plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs) have the unique capacity
to rapidly secrete large quantities of type I IFN upon recognition of nucleic acid structures by TLR7 or TLR9.
These cells
are ideally suited to respond rapidly to viruses in the absence of
prior IFN exposure. pDCs are, therefore, thought to have a central
role in the self-amplifying nature of autoimmune diseases through
the production of large quantities of type I IFN, which mediates
numerous downstream functional effects on target cells and cells
of the immune system.
Surprisingly, it was found that some parvoviruses do not
appear to induce an IFN response in normal human fibroblasts,
glia cells, or melanocytes.
Furthermore, the authors found that
parvovirus infectivity was unaffected by IFN treatment of human
normal or tumor cells. The IFN-resistant phenotype of parvoviruses may give them an advantage, including the capacity to persist in tissue sanctuaries.
In contrast, a great deal of information
has been gathered over recent years about the innate and adaptive
immune responses to clear CHIKV. Very high levels of circulating CHIKV can be reached in acute settings, ranging from 10
to
10
virus RNA particles per milliliter of blood during the first
days of infection.
CHIKV is efficiently cleared within a matter of days and the symptoms usually subside within a couple of
weeks, suggesting a robust and efficient innate immune response
that limits systemic viral replication. CHIKV is a ssRNA virus;
therefore it may be detected by TLR7 and/or TLR8 (in mice).
The helicase RIG-I and MDA-5 may also be involved in the recognition of CHIKV. Moreover, CHIKV can have a dsRNA intermediate during the replication cycle, which may be detected by
TLR3. Recently one group showed that CHIKV infection leads
to activation of the transcription factor IRF3 and subsequent transcription of IRF3-dependent anti-viral genes, including IFN
β
.
IRF3 activation occurred via the engagement of the adaptor
molecule IFN promoter stimulator 1 (IPS-1).
Recently investigators showed that CHIKV does not directly stimulate type I
IFN production in immune cells such as DCs. Instead, infected
nonhematopoietic cells sense viral RNA in an IPS-1-dependent
manner and participate in the control of infection through their
production of type I IFN.
Although the IPS-1 signaling pathway contributes to the immune response, there is also evidence
for a MyD88-dependent PRR that may be critical for preventing viral dissemination.
CHIKV-induced type I IFN response
seems to be protective in vitro and possibly induces the expression
of restriction factors such as ISGs and oligoadenylate synthetase
(OAS).
OAS has been shown to be active against CHIKV
replication in HeLa cells, and its role in other cell types needs to
be further established.
In patients and in the nonhuman primate model, the secreted
IFNα
response was transient and dropped to basal levels within
24 hours, while the peak of viremia persisted for several days.
These data suggest that CHIKV may be capable of controlling
the IFN response. Infection and replication of alphavirus usually cause a general host translational shutoff, leading to severe
cytopathic activities in mammalian cells. One group confirmed
this paradigm, demonstrating that translation of cellular (but not
viral) genes is blocked during CHIKV infection.
CHIKV was
found to trigger inactivation of the translational molecule eukaryotic initiation factor subunit 2
α
(eiF2
α
) through a PKR-dependent pathway. Moreover, another group showed that CHIKV
replication is resistant to inhibition by IFN once RNA replication
has been initiated and that CHIKV actively suppresses the antiviral IFN response by preventing IFN-induced gene expression.
Indeed, CHIKV infection efficiently blocked STAT1 phosphorylation and/or nuclear translocation in mammalian cells induced
by either type I or type II IFN. Nonstructural protein 2 was a
potent inhibitor of IFN-induced JAK-STAT signaling.
Hence,
CHIKV, like many viruses, has evolved to evade or counteract the
anti-viral responses and, hence, favoring viral persistence.
An additional anti-viral molecular response is reported during
CHIKV infection. Macroautophagy, referred herein as autophagy,
is a fundamental homeostatic process that leads to the degradation and recycling of long-lived proteins and organelles.
Autophagy is first a fundamental cell-surviving process during
starvation conditions but it also plays a role in both innate and
adaptive immunity in response to pathogens.
We have previously explored the role of the autophagy machinery in CHIKV
infection and showed that cellular autophagy was promoted
within CHIKV-infected cells and, furthermore, contributed to
enhanced CHIKV replication.
A robust protective adaptive immune response develops after
CHIKV infection,
and this is also the case for all arthritogenic
viruses listed previously. IgM antibodies against CHIKV can be
detected in the sera of infected patients during the acute phase of

the infection and anti-CHIKV IgG antibodies, especially neutralizing, may prevent virus reinfection.
Kinetics involved in the
appearance of IgG, sometimes within the first days’ post-infection,
is in favor of a rapid development of a protective adaptive immune
response. At the cellular level, a major immune response in the
acute stage is characterized by a rapid mobilization of CD4+ and
CD8+ T cells. Surprisingly, the level of TNF and IL-1
β
was rather
weak during the acute phase and not significantly elevated compared with control groups.
Other Th1 cytokines were elevated in acute settings (IFNγ
and IL-12), although the response
seemed to be skewed toward a Th2/Th17 response as indicated
with high levels of IL-4, IL-13, and IL-17.
Another study demonstrated a production of IL-4, IL-10, and IFNγ
in the acute
phase of CHIKV associated with a robust CD8+ T lymphocyte
response in the early stages of the disease, and a CD4+ T lymphocyte–mediated response in the later stages.
Of note, a specific CHIKV IgM response was shown to persist several months
after infection
in patients suffering from chronic arthralgias/
arthritis. This observation may be related to viral persistence.

## Arthritogenic Viruses Engage Trojan Horse Scenario to Avoid Adaptive Immunity

Many studies have searched for DNA or RNA of viruses in synovial tissues or fluid samples mostly of arthralgic patients. Many of
the arthritogenic viruses aforementioned can be found in synovial tissue or fluid. The diagnostic significance of these findings
depends both on the particular microbe and on the clinical context, whereby the findings should be interpreted with caution.
Furthermore, for establishing the etiology of chronic arthropathy induced by latent viral infection, the concentration and the
location of the pathogen—that is, in the synovial membrane, in
synovial fluid, or in the cells of the latter—might be of importance. Even if the pathogen is found by polymerase chain reaction
(PCR), or it can be cultured from the arthritic joint, its causal role
is not easy to prove.
One of the emerging paradigms is that synovial tissue cells
are infected and display an altered phenotype involved in tissue
injuries.
Focusing on alphaviruses, it was found that persistent signs
and symptoms such as joint pain (arthralgia) and arthritis (not
necessarily associated with bone erosions) were reported several
months after CHIKV or RRV infection.
Indeed, rheumatic manifestations affect up to 50% of the adult patients 6 to
15 months post-CHIKV infection.
There is a strong body of
evidence that persistent joint damage is linked with the persistence of arthritogenic alphaviruses by mechanisms still poorly
characterized.
This hypothesis is now thoroughly tested for
CHIKV; it is plausible that CHIKV hides in immune privileged
niches and that those sanctuaries contribute directly to synovial
tissue damage.
The study of one group showed the presence
of viral antigens and RNA 3 months post-infection in joints,
muscles, lymphoid organs, and the liver.
It is noteworthy that
they found high levels of macrophage/mononuclear cell infiltration in lymphoid organs and large amounts of CHIKV antigen
and viral RNA in spleen macrophages for prolonged periods (up
to 3 months), demonstrating that macrophages act as the cellular reservoirs for CHIKV persistence.
The same conclusions
were drawn from a study in humans. Indeed, using a longitudinal study of hospitalized patients in la Réunion, we showed that
CHIKV (RNA and protein) was found in perivascular synovial
macrophages in one chronic patient 18 months post-infection.
One group confirmed this in a mouse model of acute infection.
Indeed, rheumatic disease was associated with a prolific infiltrate
of monocytes, macrophages, and NK cells and the production of
MCP-1/CCL2, TNF, and IFNγ
.
As one plausible cytopathic mechanism, we found that
CHIKV can induce apoptosis in vitro and in synovial tissues and
that completion of the apoptotic process was important for viral
propagation. Furthermore, CHIKV is able to hide in apoptotic
blebs to enhance viral spreading to neighboring cells, especially
macrophages that engulf the bleb.
This “Trojan horse” scenario may take place in tissues, allowing CHIKV to escape the
immune response and cause chronic pathologies, such as arthritis.
These infected macrophages may recapitulate humoral and cellular inflammatory processes to drive arthritis (angiogenesis, tissue
remodeling, and injuries).
This Trojan horse scenario is a possible immune evasion strategy reported for many viruses (B19,
HBV, Poxvirus, HIV, vaccinia).

## Other Mechanisms Favoring Viral Persistence Inside Synovial Tissues

Historically it has been thought that many viruses including B19
are, by the actions of the host immune system, fully removed from
the body within a few weeks or months of infection. With highly
sensitive detection tools, such as PCR and in situ hybridization,
it is becoming increasingly obvious that eradication of a virus (if
not all pathogens) from the human body is seldom complete. It
is, however, critically important to also study healthy controls in
order to avoid misinterpretations of disease causality.
Defective interfering (DI) particles may play a role in establishing or maintaining persistent infections.
DI particles are replication-deficient deletion mutants that interfere with the growth of
the homologous wild-type virus.
Another possible reason for viral persistence is that the synovial tissue cells constitute a good hiding place from the host’s
immune defense. The dominant local cell populations in joints
are synovial and cartilage cells. Synovial cells can be divided into
fibroblast-like and macrophage-like synoviocytes. These cells pre
sent several distinct phenotypical characteristics compared with
classical fibroblasts and tissue macrophages.
It may be that
these cells have major immunoregulatory mechanisms to control
tissue injuries but may also be mobilized during viral infection
to favor viral persistence.
A positive correlation was detected
between CD4+CD25+ T regulatory (Treg) cell frequency and
HCV chronic infection as detected by high levels of RNA titers
in patient-derived liver tissues.
It has been proposed that Treg
cells may play a role in viral persistence by suppressing HCV-specific T cell responses in a cell–cell contact manner. Chronic HCV
infection may also be favored given the concomitant presence of
virus-specific CCR7− CD8+ T cells.
These CD8+ Treg cells are
antigen specific, as they can be stimulated by HCV epitopes and
suppress T cell responses through the production of considerable
amounts of IL-10. It remains to be addressed whether these Tregs
are mobilized by other arthritogenic viruses.

## Mechanisms Involved in Chronic Arthralgias

As a testament to the severity of joint pain encountered in many
alphaviral arthritides in humans, the native African name for

CHIKV means “that which bends up.” These arthralgias can be
observed through the acute phase of the infection and persist over
months to years in chronic patients.
Joint pains are also clinical
hallmarks associated with chronic B19 or HCV infections.
The joint is densely innervated, and its sensory innervation is
geared predominantly toward nociception, indicating how vital
positioning sense and awareness of potentially injurious movement are to proper joint function.
Chronic pain pathways are
subject to complex levels of control and modulation, both in the
periphery and in the central nervous system. In humans, articular
branches of the tibial nerve that innervate the posterior knee joint
capsule contain 70% to 80% unmyelinated C-fibers and sympathetic nerves and have also been associated with pain.
Nociceptors (nerve endings involved in pain) are abundant in the joint
capsule, ligaments, periosteum, menisci, subchondral bone, and
synovium. As cartilage is aneural, it is not involved in nociception
of the joint.
Nociceptors express a broad range of receptors for ligands that
will induce action potentials when the receptors are engaged and
transmitting nociception to the brain.
These ligands include
cytokines, chemokines, neuropeptides, and prostaglandins, which
can all form part of the biochemical milieu in the joint persistently
infected by arthritogenic viruses.
As a result of this peripheral
sensitization, joint movement within the normal range becomes
painful.
TNF, IL-17, and IL-6 are inflammatory cytokines abundantly
expressed in response to infection by many of the arthritogenic
viruses.
Pro-inflammatory factors such as IFNα
, IL-6, and
IP-10 were also found at higher levels in the serum of patients
with CHIKV-induced polyarthritis than in noninfected healthy
individuals, indicating their potential causative role in chronic
joint and muscle pain.
These molecules can cause prolonged mechanical hypersensitivity and sensitizing joint nociceptors to mechanical stimuli.
The pro-inflammatory cytokines
IL-1
β
and TNF can directly affect sensory neurons and can also
trigger pain via downstream mediators such as other cytokines,
chemokines, prostaglandins (PG), neurotrophins, nitric oxide,
kinins, lipids, ATP, and complement anaphylatoxins.
Interestingly, it has been reported that IFN produced by CHIKV-infected
fibroblasts induced elevated expression of prostaglandins.
HCV, and particularly the NS3 viral protein, is also known to
modulate cyclooxygenase 2 activity and hence prostaglandin
synthesis.
All in all, PG may contribute to mechanisms of
nociceptor activation and sensitization. In addition, the activation of complement system and the production of complement
anaphylatoxins (C3a, C5a) in RRV-infected joints may contribute to arthralgia.
Inflammatory cytokines can also enhance local levels of nerve
growth factor (NGF), a major contributor to peripheral hypersensitivity.
The binding of NGF to its high-affinity receptor, TrkA,
on peripheral nociceptors causes rapid potentiation of the thermosensitive ion channel TRPV1 (transient receptor potential cation
channel, subfamily V, member 1), thus lowering the pain threshold and contributing to pain hypersensitivity. It remains unknown
whether arthritogenic viruses directly affect NGF biology in the
joint, although it has been shown that alphaviruses such as Sindbis
can increase NGF production by glial cells.
Nociceptors express TLRs, which recognize a variety of alarmins released during tissue injury and contribute to pain generation. The primary function of these alarmins is to boost innate
and adaptive immune cell anti-viral activities, but they could also
contribute to chronic inflammation and joint pain.

## Mechanisms Involved in Chronic Inflammatory Viral Arthritis Resembling Rheumatoid Arthritis

RA is a heterogeneous systemic autoimmune disorder that is
characterized by an antigen-driven immune response against selfantigens with the destruction of host tissue with a highly disabling
impact that affects up to 1% of the world population.
A hallmark of RA is its self-sustaining and auto-amplifying nature, in
which immune-mediated tissue damage and tissue repair collaborate in a feed-forward loop to provide the destructive power and
the fuel that sustain the inflammatory process.
In RA, CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, NK cells, and macrophages
contribute to the local and chronic inflammation of the joints.
Secretion of inflammatory cytokines like IL-1
β
, IL-6, and TNF
play a pathogenic role in the disease process. Overproduction of
pro-inflammatory cytokines is believed to be orchestrated predominantly by macrophage-like synoviocytes. Fibroblast-like synoviocytes invade the cartilage and contribute to joint destruction.
The activation and differentiation of resident macrophages into
osteoclasts is a key process leading to bone erosion.
While the pathogenesis of virally induced arthritis might be
mediated through different routes, outcomes are remarkably similar with chronic forms of inflammatory arthritis such as RA. Available studies also suggest several overlapping features of disease
pathogenesis. Similar to observations in RA, several studies have
suggested that macrophage recruitment and subsequent secretion
of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines play a major role
in the development of arthritis in alphavirus infection.
Other cellular injuries occur that can mimic features in RA that
include direct cellular lysis of virally infected cells, indirect effects
mediated by the immune response, and/or enhancement of the
invasiveness of synovial fibroblasts (acute or chronic arthritis) and
production of immune complexes.
It has been demonstrated that osteoblasts and synovial fibroblasts infected by CHIKV drive osteoclastogenesis in vitro and in
essence may contribute to joint damage.
Osteoclasts are specialized cells derived from the monocyte/macrophage hematopoietic lineage that develop and adhere to bone matrix, then secrete
acid and lytic enzymes that degrade it in a specialized, extra-cellular compartment.
Osteoprotegerin (OPG), receptor activator of NFκ
B (RANK), and RANK ligand (RANKL) together
regulate osteoclast function. Similar again to observations in RA,
CHIKV and RRV patients presented high levels of RANKL and
low levels of OPG, which would favor the appearance of macrophage-derived osteoclasts in the joints.
Using a mouse model
of CHIKV infection, one group demonstrated that blood monocytes were recruited to sites of infection through the action of chemokines such as CCL2 and differentiate into osteoclast-like cells
leading to bone erosion. Furthermore, they observed that treatment with an IL-6 neutralizing antibody reduced bone loss and
reduced RANKL/OPG ratios in sera of mice.
Bindarit (a CCL2
inhibitor) was also able to control for the osteoclastogenic activity
of CHIKV.

## Treatment and Prevention

Arthralgias and arthritis postviral infection are mainly treated with
analgesics and NSAIDs, which may provide symptomatic relief
but do not significantly modify the underlying disease process.
For chronic arthritis lasting more than 3 months post-CHIKV

infection, treatment with MTX has yielded clinical benefit, possibly related to anti-proliferative effects on invasive fibroblasts
or for its capacity to control the pro-inflammatory activities of
alarmins such as HMGB1.
Whether chloroquine may
be useful as a prophylactic treatment or in the case of chronic
infection needs further investigation.
Several molecules are in
development and have been tested in animal models. IFNα
treatment was able to prevent arthritis in mice but only if given before
infection, suggesting that IFNα
may have a limited therapeutic
role.
High levels of MCP1/CCL2 are produced following alphaviral infection in humans
and in mice suffering from polyarthralgia and polyarthritis post-alphavirus infection.
Bindarit
may hold promise by reducing the influx of inflammatory cells
that mediate tissue damage post-infection.
CTLA4-Ig
(abatacept) showed promising results in CHIKV-infected mice,
particularly when co-administered with a human monoclonal
antibody directed against CHIKV. In treated mice, the authors
observed significant decreases in joint swelling, synovial virus
levels, inflammatory cell infiltrates, and chemokine/cytokine
levels.
In CHIKV-infected mice, intraperitoneal injections of
suramin reduced viral loads, synovial inflammation, and cartilage
lesions.
Mefenamic acid associated with ribavirin,
phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligomers,
pentose polysulfate,
and
favipiravir
also improved arthritis and reduced the viral load in
CHIKV-infected mice.
Vaccination remains the best strategy to prevent most vector-borne diseases. Ideally, vaccines should combine low cost,
single-dose efficacy, and rapid and long-lived immunity with
negligible risk of serious adverse reactions. For example, recombinant CHIKV vaccines are under development and have been
tested in mouse or macaque models, using different technologies.
Some vaccines efficient against both CHIKV and Zika
are also under evaluation in mouse models.
CHIKV vaccines
are currently being evaluated in phase II and III clinical trials and
with promising outcomes. The most advanced CHIKV vaccine
is a live-attenuated East/Central/South African strain (LR 2006-
OPY1) isolated during the outbreak in la Réunion in 2006 and
administered as a single-dose vaccine, VLA1553.
VLA1553
has been granted PRIME (PRIority MEdicine) designation by the
European Medicines Agency in 2020, and regulatory submissions
are planned in Europe in 2023. In addition to live-attenuated
viruses and virus-like particle vaccines, recombinant live-attenuated vaccines such as measles-CHIKV vaccine (also known as
MV-CHIKV) have been developed and are under phase II clinical
trial.

## Conclusion

Our understanding of the pathologic mechanisms involved in
chronic arthralgias and arthritis developing postviral infection is
still in its infancy given that several intricate humoral and cellular pathways are involved. Causal links will need to be verified in
animal models and may be controlled for immune status, immune
senescence, or any other comorbidities, such as chronic systemic
inflammation. The development of animal models of chronic
and flaring viral arthritis will be instrumental for screening new
drug treatments and vaccines and to evaluate the short- and longterm effects of immunomodulation therapies of these arthritides.
Addressing these knowledge gaps is critical and will require intensive investigations to improve animal models currently reported
in the literature, which do not appear to develop chronic joint
inflammation. Gene and protein profiling studies will be required
to address the phenotypic polarization of the synovial cells (fibroblasts and macrophages), which are chronically infected and contribute to progressive joint destruction. It is increasingly possible
that viruses may control many of the inflammatory pathways,
which may be of further interest to the pathogenesis of similar
conditions such as RA and in the development of novel therapeutic avenues.
