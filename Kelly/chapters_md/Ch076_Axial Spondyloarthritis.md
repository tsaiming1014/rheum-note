> radiographic axial SpA (r-axSpA), also known as ankylosing

> spondylitis (AS), and nonradiographic axial SpA (nr-axSpA).

> Classification criteria for axial SpA with the ability to use magnetic

> resonance imaging (MRI) have expanded the spectrum of axial SpA;

> however, using classification criteria as a diagnostic tool leads to

> overdiagnosis and potentially inappropriate management.

> Axial SpA occurs about two to four times as often as by the modified

> New York criteria and may show comparable disease activity.

> nr-axSpA is more heterogeneous than r-axSpA/AS; in nr-axSpA, human

> leukocyte antigen

> (HLA)-B27

> prevalence varies widely, there is no

> male preponderance, and patients may or may not progress to

> radiographic sacroiliitis.

> MRI of the sacroiliac joints may show inflammatory and structural

> lesions years before structural changes appear on conventional

> radiographs.

> In many populations, r-axSpA occurs in 1% to 3% of

> HLA-B27

> –positive

> individuals. The disease is more common in families of affected

> probands; r-axSpA occurs in about 10% of first-degree relatives of

> HLA-B27

> –positive AS patients.

> patient education represent first-line therapy.

> TNF inhibitors and interleukin (IL)-17 inhibitors effectively treat the signs

## Introduction

Axial spondyloarthritis (axSpA) is a term introduced in 2004
to
help describe patients with inflammatory arthritis of the sacroiliac
(SI) joints and spine who did not have radiographic sacroiliitis.

they may be considered forms of axial SpA and be classified as

aspects also apply to patients with axial SpA.
Inflammatory sacroiliitis is considered a hallmark of axial SpA
and may be identified pathologically by various imaging techniques. Although inflammation of the SI joints and spine may
eventually lead to bony ankylosis, spinal ankylosis tends to appear
in late stages of the disease and does not occur in many patients
with mild disease.
Many patients with AS have onset of back pain during the
third decade of life. It takes, on average, 6 to 8 years from the
onset of back pain until a clinician establishes a definite diagnosis of AS. This diagnostic delay in the majority of patients results
mainly from the relatively late appearance of definite radiographic
sacroiliitis on conventional plain radiographs.
Active sacroiliitis
on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) predicts the later appearance of sacroiliitis on radiographs. Thus many patients at an early
stage of AS typically are seen with characteristic clinical features
of AS, but definite sacroiliitis may not yet be detected on radiographs. Therefore they may not be classified with AS according
to the modified New York criteria (see also
Chapter 75
).
In such
patients with suspected early stage AS, sacroiliitis may best be
detected on MRI.
Radiographic sacroiliitis (i.e., structural damage of SI joints observable on plain radiographs) may develop in a
minority of such patients over time (approximately 20% to 25%
over a period of 15 years, 5% to 10% over 5 years).
In addition, modern treatment may have the potential for preventing

structural damage. All of this implies that a significant proportion
of patients will never show radiographic changes, despite harboring inflammation of SI joints at least at some point in time but
without incurring radiographically detectable damage during the
subsequent years. Therefore to describe these patients correctly,
the term
early AS
has been dropped in favor of the term
nonradiographic axial spondyloarthritis
(nr-axSpA).
The term
axial
spondyloarthritis
comprises both nr-axSpA and classic AS (patients
who generally fulfill the modified New York criteria). The total
prevalence of axial SpA has been estimated at two to four times
that of AS.
Contrary to the population of patients classified
by the modified New York criteria, there is no male preponderance among patients with nr-axSpA.
In population-based studies on axial SpA, human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-B27 prevalence
varies widely (from 20% to 97%) depending on how patients
were included.
At the group level, nr-axSpA patients tend to
respond less favorably to treatment with TNF inhibitors than AS
patients defined by modified New York criteria.
Patients classified as nr-axSpA who lack objective signs of SI or spinal inflammation, despite apparently active disease, do not typically respond
to TNF inhibition.
Such an observation raises questions about
the accuracy of the diagnosis of such patients.

## Classification Criteria for Ankylosing Spondylitis and Axial Spondyloarthritis

Classification criteria are intended for clinical and epidemiologic
studies and also for research on the etiopathogenesis of diseases.
Such criteria should create homogeneous groups of patients who
have already been diagnosed clinically. Inappropriate use of classification criteria as diagnostic criteria increases the likelihood of
overdiagnosis due to inclusion of patients with false-positive diagnoses, especially if the criteria lack specificity and the prior probability of disease is low. Note that only about 5% of people with
chronic back pain have axial SpA.
The diagnosis of axial SpA is based on the integration of clinical, laboratory, and imaging features in a specific context. In daily
practice, a presumptive clinical diagnosis of axial SpA is usually
supported by evidence of sacroiliitis on imaging. Although radiographic sacroiliitis is frequent in AS, it is by no means an early or
obligate manifestation of the disease.
Contrary to the New York
criteria, radiographic evidence of sacroiliitis is not obligatory to fulfill the Rome criteria (
Table 76.1
). Both the Rome and New York
criteria were primarily intended for use in epidemiologic studies.
Lack of either sensitivity or specificity led to a modification of the
New York criteria for AS
(
Table 76.1
). Two criteria, limitation of
lumbar spine motion and limitation of chest expansion, appear to
reflect disease duration since they are usually not present in early
disease.
Therefore the modified New York criteria are not useful for inclusion of early cases of AS. MRI is useful to detect axial
manifestations before the presence of radiographic sacroiliitis.
To encompass both nonradiographic AS and AS (according to the
modified New York criteria), classification criteria (the Assessment
of SpondyloArthritis [ASAS] criteria) have been proposed for the
full spectrum of AS (see
Chapter 75
,
Fig. 75.2
).
Full spectrum of
AS

axial spondyloarthritis
are essentially synonyms. These terms
provide an umbrella for patients with predominantly axial involvement, regardless of the presence of structural damage on radiographs. According to the ASAS classification criteria,
a patient

be classified with axial SpA if sacroiliitis on imaging (radiographs
or MRI) is present, plus at least one other SpA feature, or, in the

## Epidemiology

### Prevalence

Prevalence estimates of axial SpA vary among populations and
are associated with the occurrence of the
HLA-B27
allele in the

population but are also dependent on the disease (sub)category
(SpA, axial SpA, AS, or nr-axSpA). The prevalence of AS closely
parallels the frequency of
HLA-B27.
This holds true for those
HLA-B27
subtypes that are associated with the disease, but it
is not true for populations in which the
HLA-B27*06
subtype,
which lacks a strong association with AS, occurs frequently.
In the general population, AS is likely to develop in approximately 1% to 2% of
HLA-B27
–positive adults who have a diseaseassociated
HLA-B27
subtype, although there may be regional or
geographic differences.
For example, in northern Norway,
radiographic AS has been estimated to develop in 6.7% of
HLAB27
–positive people.
The estimated prevalence of AS, as defined by the modified
New York criteria, ranges from 0.07% in a population older than
20 years in the Netherlands, to 0.2% in the United States
and from 0.02% (95% confidence interval [CI], 0.00% to 0.21%)
in sub-Saharan Africa to 0.35% (95% CI, 0.24% to 0.48%) in
Northern Arctic communities.
The prevalence of SpA ranged
from 0.20% (95% CI, 0.00% to 0.66%) in Southeast Asia to
1.61% (95% CI, 1.27% to 2.00%) in Northern Arctic communities.
Although the population prevalence of axial SpA and especially nr-axSpA is not clearly defined,
there is evidence to suggest
that the prevalence of axial SpA could be two to four times the
prevalence of radiographic AS.
The risk is considerably increased for first-degree relatives of
patients with AS compared with individuals in the general population. This applies in particular to
HLA-B27
–positive relatives. Sex
also affects the risk as axial SpA occurs more often among
HLAB27
–positive children of mothers with AS (risk up to 41.4%)
compared with
HLA-B27
–positive children of fathers with AS
(risk up to 12.6%).
Axial SpA is much more common among
HLA-B27
–positive first-degree relatives of
HLA-B27
–positive AS
patients; up to 30% of them may have signs or symptoms of axial
SpA.
On the other hand, the risk for
HLA-B27
–negative relatives of AS probands to develop axial SpA is low (about 1%).

### Incidence

AS and axial SpA prevalence and incidence may have increased in
the United States between 2006 and 2014. Reasons are unknown
but may include increased disease awareness leading to increased
detection.
Clinical features, age of onset, and survival time have remained
stable over time.
A Scandinavian study revealed an overall ageand sex-adjusted incidence of 7.3 per 100,000 person-years,
whereas an overall incidence of 3.1 per 100,000 person-years was
observed in the United States.

### Sex Distribution

Clinically, AS is more common in males, with a reported male to
female ratio of approximately 2:1 to 3:1.
In contrast, studies of patients with nr-axSpA usually show female preponderance,
but these women are more often negative for
HLA-B27.
One possible explanation for the male preponderance of AS could
be that structural changes are less likely to develop in women. It
has been shown that females with nr-axSpA demonstrate increased
heterogeneity in disease manifestations and have lower polygenetic risk scores (less genetic predisposition) than males with nraxSpA or males and females with AS.
Disease expression of AS is thought to be different in males and
females. A case-control study comparing 35 female patients with
70 male patients with AS showed no differences in spinal symptoms, chest expansion, peripheral arthritis, extra-articular manifestations, or functional outcome. However, males with AS more
often had radiographic spinal changes and hip joint involvement
than their female counterparts. There is still some controversy, but
on average, the disease seems to be more severe in men.

### Burden of Disease

Axial SpA is associated with a considerable burden to the patient
and society. Apart from axial and peripheral articular manifestations, extra-articular manifestations, such as enthesitis and acute
anterior uveitis (AAU), and associated comorbidities, such as
inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and psoriasis, contribute to the
burden of disease. In addition, a large proportion of patients have
spinal osteoporosis, leading to vertebral fractures and thoracic
kyphosis. All of these features result in a decreased quality of life
(QoL). Impaired physical functioning and disease activity correlate clearly with psychological scores for anxiety and depression.
The prevalence of comorbid depression in axial SpA is high.
The
impact of AS can also be seen in various aspects of employment,
ranging from required assistance at work to increased sick leave
and withdrawal from the workforce.
Socioeconomic factors
including higher education levels are associated with a higher
likelihood of patients maintaining employment.
Uveitis, older
age, reduced physical function, and higher disease activity predict
long-term adverse work outcomes.
Patients with AS render an
important impact on health care and non–health care resource
utilization, resulting in mean total costs (direct and productivity)
of approximately $6700 to $9500 per year per patient.
The burden of illness increases with disease duration. Because
the burden caused by AS reduces QoL, and because all types of costs
associated with AS result from loss of function and disease activity,
early diagnosis and treatment are necessary to prevent or reduce
functional decline and improve long-term patient outcomes.
Although nonradiographic axial SpA may be associated with
fewer inflammatory features and less spinal stiffness, the burden
of disease appears to be similar to that of AS by modified New
York criteria.
Comorbidities in SpA adversely influence physical function, work ability, and QoL.
The increased prevalence of
cardiovascular disease among patients with AS contributes to the
burden of disease.

## Genetics and Pathogenesis

### Genetics

Twin and family studies have shown that the risk for AS runs
strongly in families and is largely genetically determined and that
genetic factors also play major roles in determining disease activity and severity.
Major progress has been made in recent years
in defining the alleles (genetic variants) that underlie this genetic
predisposition, with at least 116 known loci (genetic regions) associated with the disease, including different loci within the major
histocompatibility complex (MHC) and in non-MHC regions.
Major Histocompatibility Complex Associations With
Ankylosing Spondylitis
The association of
HLA-B27
with AS was first reported in the early
1970s, and since then increasing numbers of
HLA-B27
subtypes
have been identified, with nearly 400 subtypes defined by DNA
sequence now known. The majority of these subtypes are too rare

for us to know whether they are disease associated. The ancestral
HLA-B27
subtype is
HLA-B*2705,
from which the other subtypes
have arisen largely by point mutation. AS has occurred in individuals with the following
HLA-B27
subtypes:
B*2702, B*2703,
B*2704, B*2705, B*2706, B*2707, B*2708, B*2710, B*2714,
B*2715,
and
B*2719.
But two subtypes appear to show reduced
or possibly absent association with AS:
HLA-B*2706,
which is
found predominantly in Southeast Asia, and
HLA-B*2709,
which
is found in Sardinia. The impact of subtype variation on AS risk
has been a cornerstone of research into the mechanism by which
HLA-B27
induces AS.
HLA-B27
is found in 85% to 95% of cases
of primary AS compared with a prevalence in most populations of
European descent of approximately 8%.
A lower prevalence of
HLA-B27
(
∼
60%) is found in AS associated with psoriasis or IBD.
HLA-B27 homozygotes have roughly double the risk of AS,
compared with B27 heterozygotes. The odds ratio (OR) for AS for
HLA-B27 (as defined by the tag single nucleotide polymorphism
[SNP] rs116488202) heterozygotes is 62 and for homozygotes
105.
HLA-B27 is widely used in clinical practice as a diagnostic
aid in patients with chronic back pain, whereas the clinical arm
of the ASAS axial SpA classification criteria requires the presence
of HLA-B27 to classify the condition (see
Chapter 75
,
Fig. 75.2
).
HLA-B27 typing is also of use in determining the risk of occurrence of AS in families, with the risk of occurrence extremely low
in the absence of HLA-B27 (<1% chance). Because the prevalence of AS is quite low in the community, and because only
approximately 5% of HLA-B27 positive individuals develop AS,
population screening that uses HLA-B27 is not recommended.
Disease develops in HLA-B27–negative individuals on average a
decade later than HLA-B27–positive individuals, but their disease
activity and severity of ankylosis is not different. There is some
evidence to suggest that HLA-B27–negative individuals are less
responsive to TNF inhibition; nonetheless, these agents are clearly
effective overall in treating HLA-B27–negative AS. Whether other
genetic loci influence responses to TNF inhibitors is unknown in
AS, but given that large studies in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) have
failed to show any convincing evidence of an association between
individual genetic variants with response to TNF inhibition, it
would appear unlikely that such an effect will be seen in AS. There
are no data at this point regarding potential genetic determinants
of response to interleukin (IL)-17A, or IL-17A/IL-17F inhibition.
The prevalence of AS worldwide roughly parallels the prevalence
of HLA-B27. Regions with high HLA-B27 prevalence include
Scandinavia and in some North American indigenous populations,
and low prevalence is found through most of Africa and in Australian Aboriginals. This variation may be the result of population
bottlenecks in human history or natural selection for HLA-B27
in some populations. It should be noted that HLA-B27 is protective for HIV infection in the general population.
It has recently
been suggested that HLA-B27 may influence survival, potentially
through an effect on cardiovascular risk. This finding suggests
another potential selective pressure on HLA-B27 prevalence.
HLA-B27 is not the only HLA-B allele associated with AS.
Multiple studies have confirmed a role for HLA-B60 (also known
as HLA-B40), which further increases AS risk by approximately
1.5-fold,
with many other HLA-B, HLA-A, and HLA Class II
alleles yielding modest effects on AS susceptibility.
Non–major Histocompatibility Complex Associations of
Ankylosing Spondylitis
Despite well-characterized associations between
HLA-B27
with
disease susceptibility, the majority of the genetic risk for AS is
thought to be determined by loci outside the MHC. To date over
110 non-MHC loci have been convincingly demonstrated to be
associated with the disease, and these have cast new light on the
disease pathogenesis (summarized in
Table 76.2
).
Aminopeptidase Genes and Ankylosing Spondylitis.
Three
genes in the aminopeptidase pathway are robustly associated
with AS:
ERAP1
and
ERAP2
encoded at chromosome 5q15 and
NPEPPS
(puromycin-sensitive aminopeptidase) at chromosome
17q21. At least two associated haplotypes are present at
ERAP1,
with one driven by the noncoding SNP rs30187 and the second
haplotype where the AS-protective SNP leads to splice-site variation, transcription of a different isoform, and ultimately to far
less protein production and so reduced functional ERAP1.
At
ERAP2
the genetic association is very broad, but on the key disease-associated haplotype, one protective variant (rs2248374[G])
leads to a transcribed RNA product that undergoes nonsensemediated decay and thus no translated protein product.
This
variant is thus the major functional variant at
ERAP2.
This variant has recently been demonstrated to protect from
Yersinia pestis
infection (the “Black Death”), which is thought to have driven its
high current prevalence.
Gene–gene interaction between
ERAP1
and
HLA-B27
is the
first example of replicated, statistically significant gene–gene interaction in any human disease and indicates that the mechanism
by which
HLA-B27
induces AS likely involves aberrant peptide
processing.
ERAP1
also interacts with
HLA-B40
in causing AS,
and it is now also known that the same variants in
ERAP1
are
associated with psoriasis, and interaction is observed with
HLACw6,
the major psoriasis class I HLA allele.
Similarly, in Behçet’s
disease, there is
ERAP1
association restricted to
HLA-B51
cases,
the major Behçet’s disease class I HLA risk allele.
This suggests
that
HLA-B27, HLA-B40, HLA-Cw6,
and
HLA-B51
operate in
these diseases through similar mechanisms.
Interleukin-23 Pathway Genes and Ankylosing Spondylitis.
The genetic discovery of the association of
IL23R
with AS was the
first demonstration of the involvement of this pathway in AS and
has driven the repositioning of inhibitors of the IL-23 pathway
for clinical use in AS. There are now multiple
IL23R
polymorphisms identified that are associated with AS, IBD, psoriasis, and
other associated diseases. Several other IL-23 pathway genes have
also now been associated with AS, including
IL12B, TYK2,
and
JAK2,
as well as other genes that interact with the IL-23 pathway
such as
IL1RL1, IL1RL2, IL6R, PTGER4,
and
CARD9
(
Table
76.2
). The molecular mechanisms underlying these associations
largely await dissection, but these findings clearly demonstrate
that this is a major pathway in AS pathogenesis. Consequently,
it is a major target for development of therapies for AS. The
association of
IL23R
is found in both
HLA-B27
–positive and
HLA-B27
–negative disease, and thus, the pathway is relevant to
both AS subsets.
Whether these genes play any role in controlling disease activity or severity of ankylosis is the subject of
ongoing research. Association of
IL23R
variants with anterior
uveitis, however, has been demonstrated, along with association
with other AS-associated variants including
HLA-B27
and SNPs
in
ERAP1
and chromosome 2p15, at genome-wide significant
thresholds.

cell types in AS pathogenesis. Associated genes include
EOMES,
RUNX3, TBX21, ZMIZ1, IL7,
and

Polygenic Risk Scores
There is a clear need for improved approaches to enable earlier
diagnosis of axial SpA and for better screening tools to identify
early cases or people at high risk of the disease. Given the high heritability and low prevalence of axial SpA, genetic tests are an obvious candidate. Polygenic risk scores (PRS) are a method in which
weighted scores of genetic variants carried by patients provide
a measure of genetic load for particular diseases that are carried
by individuals. PRS have been developed for both AS
and the
AS-related disease AAU
and shown in different studies to have
high discriminatory capacity in both patients of European and
East Asian descent. A replication study performed in a 35-year
follow-up study in Swiss AS patients, their family members, and
unrelated healthy controls demonstrated the PRS had an area
under the curve (AUC) in receiver operator characteristic analysis
of 0.96, compared with an AUC of 0.91 for
HLA-B27
.
This
exceeds the discriminatory capacity for both MRI and C-reactive
protein (CRP).

Analysis of the overlap between AS-associated SNPs and epigenetic marks of gene activation in cell and
tissue types has implicated, not surprisingly, the involvement of
cells of the immune system (cluster of differentiation [CD]4+ and
CD8+ T cells, T-regulatory cells) but also of the gastrointestinal
mucosa, implicating a role of disordered gut immunity in disease
pathogenesis.
HLA-B27
Despite more than 40 years of research, the precise role of
HLAB27
in AS remains unclear. Current models for how
HLA-B27
operates in AS can be divided into classic models of how class I
alleles operate in antigen presentation versus nonclassic models
associated with irregular properties of
HLA-B27,
including a slowfolding rate and cell-surface homodimer formation.
The dominant role of class I HLA molecules, such as
HLAB27,
is in the presentation of antigens to CD8+ T lymphocytes. In
the case of infected or transformed cells, presentation of foreign or
tumor antigens provide danger signals to CD8+ T lymphocytes.
The “arthritogenic peptide” model of AS pathogenesis suggests
that in genetically predisposed individuals, aberrant presentation of self-peptides by HLA molecules results in recognition of
innocuous self-antigens as harmful, inducing autoreactivity from
CD8+ T cell responses. Self-reactive T cells have been isolated
from synovium of AS patients, but these studies have not been
convincingly replicated.
This model is not well supported
by studies in B27-transgenic rats, in which disease pathogenesis
is independent of CD8+ T cells.
One caveat to this model is
that B27-transgenic rats express approximately 40 to 50 copies of
HLA-B27
per cell compared with, at most, two copies in humans.

The effects of such overexpression of class I alleles in this animal
model require cautious interpretation of data from such experiments because it is possible that an AS-like condition may arise
from pathogenic mechanisms that may not be relevant to human
disease.
During antigen processing and presentation, the highly polymorphic heavy chain of
HLA-B27
noncovalently associates with
β
2-microglobulin, and that complex is loaded with a peptide
before transport of this complex to the cell surface. The highly
ordered process of HLA:peptide assembly, which occurs in the
endoplasmic reticulum (ER), happens more slowly for
HLA-B27
than any other HLA molecule.
The unfolded protein response
(UPR) theory suggests that slow assembly of
HLA-B27
complexes
initiates ER stress pathways that lead to IL-23 secretion from
macrophages. Support for the UPR theory is, again, strongest in
B27-transgenic rats but has also been reported in the synovium
of AS patients.
However, studies of circulating cells from AS
patients have found little evidence for ER stress.
Following transport to the cell surface,
β
2-microglobulin can
dissociate from the heavy chain, leaving a free heavy chain. Free
heavy chains can then dimerize to form cell surface heavy-chain
homodimers. Recent reports have shown that subsets of T cells
and natural killer (NK) cells recognize B27 homodimers through
specific killer immunoglobulin receptors (KIRs). Recognition of
cell surface B27 heavy-chain homodimers by KIR3DL2
CD4+ T
cells leads to IL-17 secretion by those T cells.
However, studies
of genetic variants of KIR that determine the circulating KIR profile in individuals have shown little evidence that these associate
with disease.
Further, the ability of
HLA-B27
subtypes to form
homodimers has been shown not to correlate with their association with AS, making this theory unlikely to be relevant in the
mechanism by which
HLA-B27
induces the disease.
The development of sequencing-based methods to profile T
cell receptor (TCR) usage has provided strong evidence that the
arthritogenic peptide hypothesis is involved in the mechanism by
which
HLA-B27
drives AS pathogenesis.
CD4 and CD8 lymphocytes carry a TCR, which is a heterodimer made up of a TCRa
and TCRb chain. The TCR recognizes antigenic peptide presented
by HLA proteins on antigen presenting cells like dendritic cells.
CD4 T cells recognize antigen presented by HLA Class II proteins
like HLA-DRB1, and CD8 T cells typically recognize internally
processed antigens presented by HLA Class I proteins, such as
HLA-B27.
The two TCR chains are highly variable between cells,
with up to 10
different TCRs theoretically possible in any individual. Expansion of a clone of T cells carrying the same TCR is
strongly indicative of a common antigenic driver. Multiple studies
have now demonstrated that particularly among CD8 T-lymphocytes from AS patients, there is expansion of T cell clones bearing a characteristic motif in the hypervariable CDR3 region of
TCRb.
This motif was found in 37 of 37 AS cases but in
only 4 of 19
HLA-B27
–positive healthy controls in one study
and is overrepresented in cases of both European and East Asian
ancestry.
Collectively, these data implicate this TCRb motif in
the pathogenesis of AS in different environments and likely in
patients carrying different
HLA-B27
subtypes. Notably, this motif
has also been identified in patients with bacterial induced reactive arthritis,
further supporting the theory that AS is driven
by interactions between the gut microbiome and a genetically
primed host immune system. This discovery raises the potential
that, using the AS-associated T cells, the antigenic triggers for AS
could be defined and that targeting this subset of CD8 T cells
could be therapeutic and potentially preventive for AS.
ERAP1 and Other Aminopeptidases
Outside the
HLA
locus,
ERAP1
has the strongest association with
AS susceptibility. ERAP1 is an aminopeptidase involved in trimming of peptides that have been processed through the proteasome from approximately 13 to 15 amino acids in length down to
typically 9 amino acid peptides. ERAP1 is therefore considered to
be a “molecular ruler,” shaping peptides before loading onto class I
MHC molecules. The association with AS has been described and
validated in several studies.
At least two AS-associated
ERAP1
haplotypes have been described, tagged by the nonsynonymous
SNPs rs30187 and rs10050860.
These SNPs only show associations in
HLA-B27
–positive patients, thus demonstrating gene–
gene interaction between
ERAP1
and
HLA-B27.
Despite this
knowledge, our understanding of the precise role of
ERAP1
in AS
pathogenesis remains limited.
ERAP1
exists in either a closed (enzymatically active) or open
(enzymatically inactive) state. Radiograph crystallography revealed
that the main AS-associated SNP rs30187 (K528R) in
ERAP1
is
located at the hinge region of the enzyme that controls the switch
between open and closed conformations.

comparing the
HLA-B27

Interleukin-23 Signaling Pathway
IL-23 signals through a cell surface receptor composed of an
IL-23–specific subunit (IL-23R) and a subunit shared with IL12R (IL-12RB1). IL-23 signaling is necessary for the development of IL-17– and IL-22–secreting cells. As described previously,
polymorphisms in
IL23R
and, more recently, downstream components of the IL-23 signaling pathway,
JAK2
and
TYK2,
and the
IL-23 subunit
IL12B,
have been associated with AS.
Under
normal physiologic conditions, IL-23, IL-17, and IL-22 regulate
gut homeostasis. IL-23 expression is increased in spondyloarthritis synovial fluid,
and serum IL-17 levels and the number
of IL-23R expressing CDR T-lymphocytes are increased in AS
patients.
Improved understanding of the cells involved in IL-23 signaling in AS has shone light on new immune cell subsets involved
in pathogenesis. Until recently, CD4+ T cells were thought to
be the dominant cell type involved in IL-23–mediated immune
responses. However, studies in IL-23R–green fluorescent protein
(GFP) reporter mice revealed that CD4+ T cells are only a small
component of the IL-23 signaling family. Several other cell types
in these mice express high levels of IL-23R, including
γδ
T cells,
macrophages, dendritic cells, and lymphoid tissue inducer-like
(LTi) cells. In AS patients, several “noncanonical” IL-23–responsive cells have been described, including
γδ
T cells, KIR3DL2
CD4+ T cells, mast cells, and neutrophils.
The precise
hierarchy of these cell types remains unclear, but it is conceivable that each cell type may play tissue-specific roles in regulating
IL-23 responses in AS. The prominent source of IL-23 in AS also
remains unclear.
Whatever the source of IL-23 and IL-17, clinical trials of biologics that block these cytokines have shown divergent results.
Phase III clinical trials of IL-17 blockade with the fully humanized monoclonal anti-IL-17, secukinumab, demonstrated clinical
benefit,
leading to this agent and other agents targeting
IL-17 now being widely used in clinical practice for patients with
AS. In contrast, inhibition of IL-12/IL-23 signaling, through
blockade of the IL-12/23 shared subunit by the fully humanized monoclonal antibody ustekinumab, failed to reach primary
endpoint measures in AS, although it did show beneficial effects
in subsets of patients.
This conclusion is supported by the suggestive evidence that ustekinumab-treated
HLA-B27
–positive
psoriatic arthritis patients had greater reductions in neck/back/
hip pain than
HLA-B27
–negative patients.
Thus, this agent
is likely at least partially effective in AS. In contrast, inhibition
of IL-23p19, the IL-23–specific subunit, showed no efficacy
in well-designed studies.
These unexpected findings remain
unexplained.
Mucosal Immunity and the Microbiome
Clinical overlap between AS and gut inflammation has been recognized for many years with 5% to 10% of AS patients developing
IBD and a further 70% presenting with subclinical gut inflammation.
Furthermore, patients with AS display increased intestinal
permeability.
Early genetic studies provided evidence of shared
genetic susceptibilities between AS and IBD, but cross-disease
genetic comparisons facilitated by the Immunochip project has
demonstrated the extent of that genetic overlap.
Of the overlapping genes identified, several lie in the IL-23 signaling pathway,
including
IL23R, IL12B, TYK2, JAK2,
and
IL27.
Several other
genes are implicated in mucosal immunity, including
TBX21,
RUNX3, EOMES, NKX2-3, ZMIZ1,
and
ICOSLG,
while others
play roles in microbial sensing (
GPR35, GPR37,
and
GPR65
).
IL-23 is an important regulator of mucosal health, mediating
anti-microbial responses and protection from extra-cellular bacteria. IL-23 is markedly increased in the terminal ileum of AS
patients.
It is hypothesized that IL-23 responses in AS are exaggerated as a result of altered intestinal microbiome. Examination
of the microbiome in both mucosal biopsies and fecal samples
from AS patients demonstrates unique compositions of the intestinal microflora.
These findings have been replicated widely,
including in patients of European and East Asian ancestry.
Histopathology studies have also shown increases in bacteria in
the gut wall of AS patients compared with healthy controls and
correlated this with evidence of inflammasome activation.
Alterations to the microbiome, however, do not necessarily indicate a pathogenic mechanism since an altered microbiome may
be a consequence, rather than a driver, of disease. Nonetheless,
in animal studies there is strong evidence for a causal role for
the microbiome in disease pathogenesis.
HLA-B27
–transgenic
rats express multiple copies of human
HLA-B27
along with an
invariant MHC light chain,
β
2-microglobulin. These animals
develop SpA-like disease, but both intestinal inflammation and
joint inflammation are reduced in a germ-free environment.
Carriage of
HLA-B27
has been demonstrated to influence the gut
microbiota in
HLA-B27
–transgenic rats and AS patients.
Additionally, studying healthy subjects, a significant effect of
HLA-B27
was seen on the gut microbiome,
indicating that
the major risk factor gene for AS influences the microbiome in
the absence of disease, favoring models in which the microbiome
drives disease rather than the converse. However, the precise interplay between host genetics, immune responses, and microbiota is
yet to be fully mapped in humans, and their collective influence
on health and disease remains unclear. Trials of therapies targeting
the microbiome such as fecal microbiome transplant therapy, or
broad-spectrum antibiotic therapies, which are effective in IBD,
are awaited in AS.
Structural Remodeling and Ankylosis
A major distinguishing feature of seronegative from seropositive
arthropathies is that although inflammation typically causes erosion during early stages of both groups of diseases, osteoproliferation occurs in later stages of seronegative disease. There is little
direct evidence in humans about the processes involved in osteoproliferative disease because of the difficulty in obtaining suitable
specimens for histopathologic examination. Therefore most of the
information that is available comes from imaging studies or from
studies of murine models of disease.
Inflammation is clearly a critical early step in the osteoproliferative process. Baseline CRP is the only robust predictor of future
radiographic change that has been reported to date (reviewed in
ref. 127), and there is increasing evidence that although TNF inhibition does not altogether prevent bone formation, it does significantly slow ankylosis.
This is particularly evident in patients
achieving clinical remission of disease activity. It had been postulated that TNF inhibition releases Wnt pathway–driven bone
formation as it reduces expression of the Wnt inhibitor DKK1.
However, this would be expected to lead to accelerated ankylosis in TNF inhibitor–treated patients, when in fact the converse
appears to be the case. This may reflect the suppressive effect of
TNF inhibition on disease activity in early stages of inflammation
prior to activation of bone-forming pathways.

IL-17A and IL-12/23p40 levels are elevated in sera and synovial
fluid of patients with AS. IL-17A regulates expression of JAK2,
which is highly expressed in bone tissue from AS patients, and this
may promote osteoblastic activity.
IL-17A expressing
γδ
T cells
may promote osteoblast differentiation from mesenchymal stem
cells.
IL-17A enhances expression of proinflammatory cytokines, such as RANKL, and metalloproteinases by a variety of cell
types.
This dual role of IL-17A on bone formation and resorptive pathways may depend on the extra-cellular cytokine milieu
and stage of differentiation of different cell types but suggests the
possibility that IL-17 may be a target cytokine linking inflammation with new bone formation. IL-17 family contains several
different cytokines (IL-17A to IL-17F), and it has been proposed
that IL-17F has a greater role than IL-17A in osteoproliferative
diseases.
The development of IL-17F–specific inhibitors raises
the potential to test this hypothesis.
Chronic nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) use,
particularly in patients with elevated acute phase reactants, retarded
new bone formation.
This was thought possible because of the
known association of AS with genetic variants in
PTGER4,
which encodes a receptor for prostaglandin E
(PGE
). PGE
is
produced at entheses in response to physical stress, driving IL-23
production and bone formation. However, a subsequent study of
chronic NSAID use compared with on-demand treatment and
using a very similar study design failed to demonstrate any benefit
in retarding new bone formation, even in patients with elevated
acute phase reactants.
MRI studies of the spine and SI joints suggest that bone marrow
fat metaplasia is an intermediate step prior to new bone formation
and that once fat metaplasia has occurred, new bone formation
may develop.
The cellular and molecular processes that are
involved are not well understood, but animal model studies suggest involvement of the Wnt and bone morphogenetic protein
(BMP) pathways, which are the major bone anabolic pathways
in animals.
These may prove useful targets for reducing the
rate of ankylosis in AS, but medications that specifically target
inflammation-induced bone formation may be required to avoid
generalized osteoporosis as a therapeutic side effect.

## Clinical Manifestations

### Skeletal Manifestations

Low Back Pain and Stiffness
Back pain is an extremely common symptom, occurring at some
point in up to 80% of the general population. Therefore it is important to note that back pain in axial SpA may have inflammatory
features that help differentiate it from mechanical back pain,
although these traditional clues to inflammatory disease have
limited diagnostic performance with sensitives 74% to 81% and
specificity 25% to 44% for axial SpA
(

). Back pain is
the most common symptom of AS (

However, at 10 years from the onset of IBP, the probability of having
spondyloarthritis for patients with IBP was only 30%, while the
resolution of IBP occurred in 43%. Those who progress to clinical
axial SpA are predominantly
HLA-B27
–positive.
Among relatives of AS patients, a clinical history of IBP at an early age failed
to reliably predict axial SpA as long-term outcome.
The most
important predictors for progression of IBP to spondyloarthritis
were uveitis, male sex, and family history of SpA.

Typically, the low back pain of axial SpA is initially felt primarily deep in the gluteal region, is dull in character, difficult to
localize, and insidious in onset. Pain can be severe at this early
phase of the disease; it localizes in the SI joints but is occasionally referred toward the iliac crest or greater trochanteric region
or down the dorsal thigh. Radiation of buttock pain may suggest root compression of the sciatic nerve. Buttock pain in axial
SpA typically alternates from side to side. Coughing, sneezing, or
other maneuvers that cause a sudden twist of the back may accentuate pain. Although the pain is often unilateral or intermittent

at first, within a few months, it usually becomes persistent and
bilateral, and the lower lumbar area becomes stiff and painful.
The pain is associated with a feeling of low back stiffness that is
worse in the morning and may awaken the patient from sleep,
particularly during the second half of the night. Many patients
do not differentiate between low back pain and stiffness. The
morning stiffness may last up to 3 hours or longer. Both the stiffness and the pain tend to be eased by a hot shower, an exercise
program, or physical activity; they do not improve with rest.
Fatigue as a result of chronic back pain and stiffness may be an
important problem and can be accentuated by sleep disturbances
due to these symptoms.
Chest Pain
With subsequent involvement of the thoracic spine (including
costovertebral and costotransverse joints) and the occurrence of
enthesitis at the costosternal and manubriosternal joints, patients
may experience chest pain accentuated by coughing or sneezing,
which is sometimes characterized as “pleuritic.” Chest pain at the
sternal region may also be an early manifestation of the disease
and has important diagnostic value (sensitivity 72%; specificity
83%).
Likewise, mild to moderate reductions of chest expansion may be detectable in an early stage of axial SpA. Chest pain
occurs relatively often in
HLA-B27
–positive relatives, even in the
absence of radiographic evidence of sacroiliitis.
Tenderness Due to Enthesitis
Extra-articular tenderness due to enthesitis is a prominent
complaint in some patients. Common tender sites include the
costosternal junctions, spinous processes, iliac crests, greater trochanters, ischial tuberosities, tibial tubercles, and heels (Achilles
tendonitis or plantar fasciitis). Radiographically, bone spurs may
develop at these sites.
Extra-axial Joints
The girdle or “root” joints (hips and shoulders) are the most frequently involved extra-axial joints in AS, and pain in these areas
is the presenting symptom in as many as 15% of patients. Shoulder involvement,
but especially hip involvement, may cause
considerable physical disability. Co-existing disease in the lumbar spine often contributes significantly to disability of the lower
extremities. Hips and shoulders are involved at some stage of disease in as many as 35% of patients with AS. Hip disease is relatively more common as a presenting manifestation if the disease
starts in childhood (juvenile AS). In boys 8 to 10 years of age, hip
disease as a manifestation of juvenile AS is the most frequent type
of chronic arthritis. Children with hip disease in AS are mostly
HLA-B27
positive and anti-nuclear antibody (ANA) negative.
The knee joint may also be affected in AS, often presenting as an
intermittent effusion. The temporomandibular joint is involved in
approximately 10% of patients.
Osteoporosis
Decreased bone mineral density (BMD) can already be seen in
early stages of AS. It is reported in more than 50% of patients
with less than 10 years disease duration. The overall prevalence
of osteoporosis approaches 15%.
Osteoporotic deformities
of the thoracic spine contribute significantly to abnormal posture, particularly fixed hyperkyphosis.
Disease-related damage
to the cervical and lumbar spine, thoracic wedging, and disease
activity are determinants of hyperkyphosis.
An increased
occiput-to-wall distance is associated with vertebral fractures.
Symptomatic osteoporotic spinal fractures are increased in
AS.
Fused osteoporotic segments may be particularly susceptible to fracture. Neurologic complications occur rather frequently, even after minor trauma.
Proper assessment of bone
density in the spine is difficult in the presence of syndesmophytes
because they may give rise to falsely high BMD values on dual
x-ray absorptiometry (DEXA).

### Extramusculoskeletal Manifestations

Constitutional symptoms such as fatigue, weight loss, and lowgrade fever occur frequently. Other extramusculoskeletal manifestations are more localized.
Eye Disease
AAU or iridocyclitis is the most common extra-articular manifestation of AS, occurring in 25% to 30% of patients at some time
during the course of the disease. Even rates of greater than 50%
have been observed with very long disease duration.
There is
no clear relationship between activity of the articular disease and
this extra-articular manifestation. The onset of eye inflammation is usually acute and typically unilateral but the attacks may
alternate. With AAU, the eye is typically red and painful, often
accompanied by visual impairment. Photophobia and increased
lacrimation may be present. If the eye remains untreated or if
treatment is delayed, posterior synechiae and glaucoma may
develop. Most attacks subside in 4 to 8 weeks without sequelae
if early treatment is provided. AAU is more common in
HLAB27
-positive than
HLA-B27
-negative patients with AS.
Relatives who have AAU are at higher risk for AS. The calculated
incidence of AAU in a Swiss family study was 89 attacks per
1000 patient-years for AS patients, but only 8 attacks per 1000
person-years among healthy
HLA-B27
–positive relatives.
Up
to 70% of patients with
HLA-B27
–positive AAU have axial SpA
by ASAS criteria.
Cardiovascular Disease
Cardiac involvement may be clinically silent or may cause considerable problems. Manifestations of cardiac involvement include
ascending aortitis, aortic valve incompetence, conduction abnormalities, cardiomegaly, and pericarditis. In rare situations, aortitis may precede other features of AS. Aortic incompetence was
noted in 3.5% of patients who had the disease for 15 years and
in 10% after 30 years.
Inflammation and dilation of the aorta
are the main causes of aortic valve incompetence. Cardiac conduction disturbances are seen with increasing frequency with the
passage of time, occurring in 2.7% of those with disease of 15
years duration and in 8.5% after 30 years.
Both aortic incompetence and cardiac conduction defects occur twice as often in
patients with peripheral joint involvement. In one study, a history
of prior myocardial infarction was significantly more common in
patients with AS (4.4%) than compared with the general population (1.2%).
Cases of AS with concurrent Takayasu arteritis have
been reported.
Pulmonary Disease
Lung involvement is a rare and typically late manifestation of
AS. It is characterized by slowly progressive fibrosis of the upper
lobes of the lungs, appearing, on average, two decades after the
onset of AS. Patients may complain of cough, dyspnea, and

sometimes hemoptysis.
High-resolution computed tomography (CT) may be helpful in detecting interstitial lung disease in
patients with respiratory symptoms whose chest radiographs are
normal.
In appropriate clinical circumstances, especially
where TNF inhibitors (TNFi) are being used, pulmonary tuberculosis must also be considered as a potential cause of apical lung
disease.
Pulmonary ventilation is usually well maintained; an increased
diaphragmatic contribution helps compensate for chest wall
rigidity, which is due to involvement of the thoracic joints in the
inflammatory process. Vital capacity and total lung capacity may
be moderately reduced as a consequence of the restricted chest
wall movement, whereas residual volume and functional residual
capacity are usually increased.
Neurologic Involvement
Neurologic complications of AS can be caused by vertebral fracture, instability, compression, or inflammation. Traffic accidents
or minor trauma can cause fractures of a vulnerable osteoporotic
spine.
The C5–C6 or C6–C7 level is the most commonly
involved site.
As in rheumatoid arthritis, atlantoaxial joint subluxation,
atlanto-occipital subluxation, and upward subluxation of the axis
may occur in AS as a consequence of instability resulting from
the inflammatory process. Spontaneous anterior atlantoaxial subluxation is a well-recognized complication occurring in approximately 2% of patients and manifests with or without signs of
spinal cord compression.
Nontraumatic causes of neurologic
complications caused by compression include ossification of the
posterior longitudinal ligament (which may lead to compressive
myelopathy), destructive intervertebral disk lesions, and spinal
stenosis.
Cauda equina syndrome, affecting lumbosacral nerve roots,
is a rare but serious complication of long-term AS. Most often
characterized by pain and sensory loss, this complication is accompanied by gradual onset of urinary and fecal incontinence, impotence, saddle anesthesia, and occasionally the loss of ankle jerk
reflexes. Motor symptoms, if present, are usually mild. CT and
MRI allow the accurate noninvasive diagnosis of this complication.
Arachnoiditis and arachnoid adhesions may be important
in the pathogenesis.
Renal Involvement
Renal complications, including immunoglobulin (Ig)A nephropathy, can be observed in AS.
IgA nephropathy complicating
AS may be accompanied an elevated IgA serum level (93%) and
renal impairment (27%).
Microscopic hematuria and proteinuria appear in as many as 35% of AS patients. The significance
of these findings in terms of subsequent deterioration of renal
function is unclear.
Amyloidosis (secondary type) is a rare
complication.

## Imaging

### Conventional Radiography

Characteristic radiographic damage is an obligatory criterion in
the definition of AS and r-axSpA.
Patients with these conditions must, according to the modified New York criteria, have at
least grade 2 radiographic sacroiliitis bilaterally (small localized
areas with erosion or sclerosis, with no alteration in joint width)
or at least grade 3 radiographic sacroiliitis unilaterally (moderate or advanced sacroiliitis with erosions, evidence of sclerosis,
widening, narrowing, or partial ankylosis).
The entire modified
New York grading system and examples of the grades are given
in
Fig. 76.1
.
Conventional radiography visualizes skeletal structures with
a high resolution and shows cumulative joint damage.
The
advantages of radiography are that it is fast, accessible, relatively
inexpensive, and reliable. It has the limitation of being a twodimensional (2D) method, which may lead to projectional superimposition when visualizing three-dimensional (3D) structures.
Radiography remains the most commonly used imaging modality
in SpA.
In patients with AS/r-axSpA, radiographic changes are primarily seen in the axial skeleton, most often with the earliest changes
in the SI joints, characteristically bilaterally and symmetrically.
The initial findings consist of blurring of the articular surface, followed by erosions and sclerosis of adjacent bone. Progression of
subchondral bone erosions can lead to radiographic pseudowidening of the joint space (
Fig. 76.1D
). With time, the definition of
the joint may gradually be lost with radiographic superimposition
of erosion, sclerosis, and new bone formation, which fills in the
erosions and the original cartilaginous “joint space,” with ankylosis as the end stage (
Fig. 76.1E
).
In the axial SpA spine, radiographic manifestations may be seen
in discovertebral, apophyseal, costovertebral, and costotransverse
joints. The earliest radiographic manifestations are often vertebral
corner focal sclerosis (the “shiny corner”) or erosion (the “Romanus lesion”) at the sites of attachment of the annulus fibrosis to
the vertebral end plate caused by previous inflammation at the
enthesis.
Furthermore, periosteal proliferation of new bone
may fill in the normal concavity of the vertebral bodies, causing
the anterior borders of vertebrae to appear straight (“squaring”)
and also often contributed to by erosion at the anterosuperior and
anteroinferior vertebral margins.
Furthermore, gradual ossification of the outer fibers of the annulus fibrosus may occur, starting
as thin vertical bone proliferations before forming “mature” syndesmophytes. Progressive growth of these syndesmophytes may
lead to complete bone “bridging” between vertebrae (bridging
syndesmophytes, ankylosis).
Concomitant ankylosis often occurs in the apophyseal joints,
with ossification of the adjacent ligaments. In a number of
patients, this may result in complete fusion of the spine (“bamboo spine”). The thin vertical syndesmophytes should be distinguished from other (more voluminous) types of bone formation
occurring in other inflammatory and degenerative conditions,
such as psoriatic arthritis, diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH), and degenerative disk disease/osteoarthritis.
The key radiographic feature of peripheral manifestations of
SpA is the combination of osteodestructive and osteoproliferative changes.
Bone erosions and radiographic osteitis (“whiskering”) at sites of osseous attachment of tendons and ligaments
are frequently seen, e.g., at the calcaneus, ischial tuberosities, iliac
crest, femoral trochanters, and vertebral spinous processes. In axial
SpA, the hips are frequently involved. Hip involvement may lead
to concentric joint space narrowing, irregularity of the subchondral bone with subchondral sclerosis, osteophyte formation at the
outer margin of the articular surface, and, ultimately, ankylosis.
Proliferative changes in psoriatic arthritis are seen as ill-defined
ossifications around the joint or “juxta-articular new bone formation.” Other radiographic features include joint space narrowing

### Ultrasound

## Diagnosis

There are no validated diagnostic criteria for AS or axSpA. Clinical
manifestations of AS usually appear in patients in early adulthood;
only rarely do they first appear in adults older than 40 years.
This
presumably must also be the case for axial SpA, and age constitutes an important part of the ASAS Classification Criteria for
axial SpA. However, the diagnosis of axial SpA remains challenging. The critical consideration when assigning a diagnosis of axial
SpA is the belief that there is an inflammatory disease of the axial
spine or SI joints or both. This can be pathologically confirmed
by a positive plain radiograph or MRI. A raised CRP level is also
supportive of an inflammatory process, although is not specific
to inflammatory SI or spinal disease. Absence of these features is
associated with nonresponse to TNF inhibition, suggesting that
such patients may not have axial SpA.
A careful history combined with examination and appropriate
imaging is also required. Back pain is a common symptom in the
community.
Those with back pain for longer than 3 months
of insidious onset, without a precipitating event, especially those
with associated features such as psoriasis or anterior uveitis, should
be further evaluated for the possibility of axial SpA. A primary
care study is illustrative of a possible approach: people with back
pain for greater than 3 months, with an age of onset of less than
45 years, who did not have a current confirmed diagnosis of axial
SpA, who then had at least one of (1) IBP, (2)
HLA-B27,
or (3)
sacroiliitis on any modality of imaging were evaluated for axial
SpA by rheumatologists. In this group 42% were ultimately given
a diagnosis of axial SpA.

### History

The history should focus initially on back pain. While back pain
was traditionally considered to be predominately of an inflammatory nature (
Table 76.3
), a large proportion of patients with
axial SpA do not have IBP. The specificity of IBP in axial SpA is
25% to 44% with a sensitivity of 74% to 81%.
Family history is relevant as a positive family history increases the pretest
probability of r-axSpA from 0.1% for a person belonging to the
general population to approximately 10% for any first-degree relative of an
HLA-B27
–positive radiographic axial SpA proband.
The probability that a first-degree relative with a positive family
history of r-axSpA will themselves have r-axSpA increases from
10% to nearly 50% if this relative also has IBP. In contrast, the
likelihood for a person who has IBP (without any other inflammatory feature) and negative family history for r-axSpA, is increased
from 0.1% to only 1%. The other important features to note are
the presence of peripheral inflammatory arthritis, especially lower
limb inflammatory arthritis and/or hip joint involvement in addition to anterior uveitis, skin psoriasis, IBD, and enthesitis. Less
common features include dactylitis (which is usually seen in those
with psoriasis or reactive arthritis), aortitis, and IgA nephropathy.

### Examination

The examination should focus on the spine and SI joints and associated features such as peripheral arthritis, psoriasis, and enthesitis.
Early in disease and in those in remission, the physical examination will often be normal.
Spinal Mobility
To arrive at an early diagnosis of AS, the physician must perform
a thorough physical examination, noting that patients with early
axial SpA often have minimal or no abnormalities on exam. On
examination of the spine, there may be some limitation of motion
of the lumbar spine as elicited by forward flexion, extension, or
lateral flexion. Early loss of the normal lumbar lordosis is often the
first sign and is easily assessed on inspection.
The Schober test (or its modifications) is useful to detect any
limitation of forward flexion of the lumbar spine, although it is
typically normal in early disease. While the patient stands erect,
one mark is placed with a pen on the skin overlying the fifth lumbar spinous process (usually at the level of the posterosuperior iliac
spine or the “dimple of Venus”), and another mark is placed 10 cm
above in the midline. The patient is then asked to bend forward
maximally, without bending the knees. If the distance between
both marks is less than 15 cm, this indicates reduced lumbar spine
mobility. Lateral flexion may also be diminished, and spinal rotation may cause pain. Normal values for forward and lateral spinal
flexion should be corrected for age and sex.

Chest Expansion
Mild to moderate reduction of chest expansion may be detectable
in early stages of AS. Normal values are age- and sex-dependent,
and there is considerable overlap between normal values and those
obtained from AS patients.
Values less than 5 cm in young
people with an insidious onset of chronic, inflammatory low back
pain strongly suggests AS. Chest expansion should be measured
on maximal inspiration after forced maximal expiration at the
level of the fourth intercostal space in males and just below the
breasts at the xiphisternal level in females.
Enthesitis
Examination of the ischial tuberosities, greater trochanters, spinous processes, costochondral and manubriosternal junctions,
supraspinatus insertion, and iliac crests can determine the presence of enthesitis. Heel pain, especially when getting out of bed,
is a characteristic manifestation of Achilles tendon enthesitis and
plantar fasciitis.
Sacroiliitis
Direct pressure over the SI joints may elicit pain, as may special
testing maneuvers (for example pelvis rock test; FABERE—Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation, Extension; and Gaenslen
test), although these latter maneuvers lack specificity and sensitivity. These signs may also be absent in early disease or may become
negative in late stages as inflammation is replaced by fibrosis or
bony ankylosis.
Posture
Over the course of the disease, the patient may lose normal posture. Involvement of the cervical spine is manifested by pain and
limitation of neck movement. The physician can detect a forward
slope of the neck by having the patient stand against a wall and try
to position his or her occiput against it.
After many years of progression in patients with severe disease,
the entire spine may become increasingly stiff, with loss of normal
posture from gradual loss of lumbar lordosis and the development
of thoracic kyphosis.
The abdomen becomes protuberant;

breathing is primarily by diaphragmatic action. These typical
deformities usually evolve after a disease duration of 10 years or
more (see supplemental
eFig. 76.10
on Elsevier eBooks+).

### Laboratory Tests

Axial SpA can be diagnosed on clinical and imaging grounds with
a negative
HLA-B27
test. This test often has no additional value
in diagnosis but can be useful in screening procedures.
Acute
phase reactants such as CRP can be raised in axial SpA patients,
but a substantial proportion of patients with early disease or nraxSpA have normal or near normal CRP. A substantial minority of
patients with radiographic axial SpA (AS) also have a normal CRP.
The clinician should not be discouraged from further evaluation by
a normal CRP or a negative
HLA-B27
in the right clinical context.
Generally, blood tests are not helpful for the diagnosis of AS.
Elevated levels of fecal calprotectin may suggest associated IBD in
the appropriate clinical context
and have specificity and sensitivity exceeding 90%.
A normal erythrocyte sedimentation
rate (ESR) or normal CRP level does not exclude active disease,
although CRP does correlate with patient reported disease activity,
and elevated CRP is associated with better treatment response.
Neither ESR nor CRP is superior in assessing disease activity.
A mild normochromic anemia may be present in up to 15% of
patients. Elevation of serum alkaline phosphatase (derived primarily from bone) is seen in some patients but is unrelated to disease
activity or duration. Elevations of serum IgA are frequent in AS
and correlate with acute phase responses.

### Imaging

Imaging is often initiated with a plain radiograph of the pelvis,
but this is commonly normal early in disease or in those with
nr-axSpA. The complex anatomy of the SI joint and the fact that
a radiograph is a 2D representation of a 3D anatomy and frequent overlying bowel gas and soft tissues make reliable detection
of radiographic sacroiliitis difficult, even for experienced readers
and after extensive training and calibration.
Diagnosis was
previously dependent on presence of bilateral moderate or unilateral severe radiographic sacroiliitis, using the modified New York
classification criteria incorrectly for diagnosing AS.
This delayed
the diagnosis by an average of 7 to 10 years.
MRI of the SI joints is a more useful option than radiography,
particularly in early disease. The use of MRI has been endorsed by
the European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR)
recommendations for the use of imaging in the diagnosis and management of SpA in clinical practice and by the European Society of
Musculoskeletal Radiology (ESSR) Arthritis Subcommittee.
The introduction of MRI has resulted in a major improvements in
the diagnosis and evaluation of patients with SpA. Now, through
the ASAS (ASsessment of SpondyloArthitis) classification criteria
for axial SpA, MRI has a key role in detection of axial SpA, as
patients with “active sacroiliitis on MRI” plus one clinical feature
(e.g., psoriasis, enthesitis, or uveitis) should be classified as axial
SpA.
A consensus-based definition of active sacroiliitis on MRI,
i.e., to fulfill the MRI criterion of the ASAS criteria (“ASAS-positive MRI”), includes both that SI BME should be “highly suggestive of axial SpA” and that BME should be present in at least two
sites and/or in at least two consecutive slices.
Subsequent
data have demonstrated that incorporating structural damage
lesions (erosions) into the criteria could further improve the diagnostic utility of MRI.
In contrast, SI joint fat lesions and/or
spine lesions have not been documented to improve the diagnostic
utility of MRI.
An update from the ASAS MRI working
group emphasized that subtle BME changes should not be overinterpreted, that determining their importance should never be
made in isolation of the clinical context, and that the presence
or absence of structural changes, particularly erosions, should be
taken into account in case of subtle BME.
In accordance with
this, separate reports have shown that BME that resembles milder
degrees of inflammatory sacroiliitis can be observed in athletes
and postpartum women.
It is characteristic that such falsepositive MRI findings are located in the anterior upper part of
the joint and/or the posterior lower part, while the midportion
is spared.
In axial SpA, inflammation can also be observed
in these same areas but is then generally also located in the midportion of the joint.
Furthermore, patients with axial SpA
will often also have other manifestations, such as fat metaplasia in
the bone marrow (homogenous, intensely bright, sharply marginated), and bone erosion is common, often in close relation to areas
of BME, which will markedly increase the likelihood of it being
axial SpA.
Simultaneous contextual interpretation of MRI
that allows for visualizing features of inflammation as well as of
structural damage is crucial for both diagnostic evaluation and
application of the ASAS definition of a positive SI joint MRI for
the purpose of disease classification.
It must be remembered
that the ASAS criteria are classification criteria, not diagnostic criteria, and that diagnosis should not be made on MRI alone but be
made based on the combined information of the clinical context
(signs and symptoms), laboratory tests (
HLA-B27
and CRP), and
imaging (MRI and potentially radiography).
An important differential diagnosis is osteitis condensans ilii,
which is most frequently, but not solely, seen in postpartum women;
this is characterized by anterior iliac sclerosis and frequently anterior BME (
Fig. 76.4
).
For juvenile inflammatory arthritis,
MRI is much more reliable for diagnosis of sacroiliitis than radiography.
Pediatric-specific definitions for sacroiliitis in children
are needed, since normal variation frequently mimics BME
with
more than half of normal healthy children harboring findings that
satisfy the criteria for an adult definition of erosion.
Spinal MRI is not recommended for routine diagnostic evaluation of axial SpA, unless the SI joint MRI is equivocal and the
clinical suspicion of axial SpA is high.
Vertebral corner lesions
demonstrating BME and/or fat metaplasia may be observed at up
to two to three spinal levels in healthy individuals, especially in
patients with mechanical disorders.
To assess all components of
spinal involvement in axial SpA, it is essential that the sagittal images
extend sufficiently laterally to cover all lateral spinal structures that
could be inflamed, including the transverse processes.
In 2012, ASAS proposed a definition of a positive MRI of
the spine for classification of axial SpA, requiring the presence
at least three inflammatory lesions in the vertebrae, and each of
these lesions should be present on two or more consecutive sagittal
slices.
However, the addition of MRI of the spine as an imaging
criterion to the ASAS axial SpA classification criteria only led to
an increase of 1% to 2% in the percentage of patients classified
with AS/axial SpA; it is not sufficiently specific and is therefore
not recommended.
Conditions such as neoplasia, fracture, and infectious arthritis are also differential diagnoses but are rarely a major diagnostic
challenge, although more chronic infections, such as tuberculosis
or brucellosis, may pose challenges. MRI characteristics of infection are unilaterality, high volume and intensity of BME, and
spread into surrounding soft tissues.

## Prognosis and Outcomes

More is known about outcomes of patients with AS than of
patients with nr-axSpA. One study indicates that the burden of
patient-reported outcomes, peripheral arthritis, and extraskeletal
manifestations over time is similar between AS and nr-axSpA.
The course of AS is highly variable, characterized by spontaneous
remissions and exacerbations. The disease may run a relatively mild
or self-limited course or lead to severe spinal deformity, disability,
and extra-articular disease, including aortitis. Men with AS may
have greater progression to radiographic damage.
Life expectancy
is reduced, particularly after 10 years of disease.
Three studies
report higher standardized mortality rate (SMR) for females than for
males.
In a Swiss study, the mean number of life-years lost for
female AS patients was 15.6 years compared with 4.7 years for male
patients.
A study from Finland indicates that the risk of dying
for patients with AS is increased by 50% compared with controls
matched for age and sex. Causes of death include complications of
the disease such as amyloidosis and spinal fractures, as well as cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, and renal disease.
No differences exist
between familial and sporadic AS in terms of age at onset, age at
diagnosis, or prevalence of peripheral arthritis and AAU.
Functional limitations increase with disease duration. Although
structural damage seen on radiographs is clearly associated with
impaired physical function and spinal mobility at the group level,
individual patients with normal radiographs might exhibit a major
reduction in spinal mobility, whereas those with severe radiographic
abnormalities might function quite well in everyday tasks.
In at least one study, sick leave was comparable in AS and
nr-axSpA.
Withdrawal from work in those with paid jobs varies from 10% after 20 years of disease duration to 30% after 10
years, depending on the characteristics of the patients included
and the social security system considered.
The ageand sex-adjusted withdrawal rate from labor-force participation
was 3.1 times higher among Dutch patients compared with the
general population.
Older age at disease onset, manual work,
lower educational level, and coping strategies characterized by the
limitation and pacing of activities were associated with a higher
risk for work disability.
Vocational counseling, job
training, easy access to the workplace, and support of colleagues
and management may reduce the probability of withdrawal from
work.
Sick leave in those with paid jobs has been linked to
higher disease activity and presence of extraspinal disease manifestations.
Patients with peripheral joint involvement
are more likely to take sick leave than are AS patients with axial
manifestations only. Generally, poor QoL in patients with axial
C

SpA is associated with high disease activity, poor function, sleep
disturbance, mood, and widespread pain.
Overall, the first 10 years of disease are particularly important
with respect to subsequent outcome. Most of the loss of function
among patients with AS occurs within this period and is associated with the presence of peripheral arthritis, spinal radiographic
changes, and development of a so-called
bamboo spine.
Although radiographic progression is highly variable among
patients, radiographic evidence of spinal involvement, especially
the presence of syndesmophytes, appears to be the primary factor
that independently predicts further radiographic progression.
Increasing evidence supports the importance of inflammation
in predisposition to radiographic progression (i.e., the progression of structural damage and inflammation are coupled in axial
SpA). Clinical parameters of inflammation, notably the Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease Activity Score (ASDAS) and CRP levels,
are associated with prognostic risk, especially early in the disease
course, which is consistent with MRI data that evaluate prognostic risk of inflammatory lesions in the spine.
This
is also consistent with data from an observational retrospective
cohort study showing that the introduction of anti-TNF therapy
early in the disease course may be associated with a lower risk
for radiographic progression by reducing inflammatory activity.
Smoking (increases radiographic progression) and NSAID utilization (reduces radiographic progression) influence prognostic
risk, although it is unclear whether this is related to inflammation.
Biomarkers reflecting connective tissue remodeling
(e.g., metalloproteinase 3 and citrullinated vimentin) and formation of bone (e.g., sclerostin and dickkopf-1) have been associated
with radiographic progression.
Involvement of the hip joint may cause serious disability. After
30 years of disease duration, 12% to 25% of patients have at least
one hip replaced.
It is well known that only a minority of people with nr-axSpA eventually develop radiographic sacroiliitis after
a mean follow up period of 10.6 years, although the longer the
observation period, the higher the transition rate.

physical functioning, depression, higher disease activity, unemployment, and poor self-efficacy.

### Pharmacologic Therapy

The following section will review the evidence and recommendations for the use of pharmacologic therapy in axial SpA. For
drug-specific mechanisms of action and adverse events, please also

refer to other excellent chapters in this book for information on
NSAIDs (
Chapter 59
), glucocorticoids (
Chapter 60
), biologic
agents (
Chapters 63 and 64
), and JAK inhibitors (
Chapter 65
).
Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs
In patients with active axial SpA, first-line therapy is typically a
full dose of an NSAID used continuously, although consideration
of adverse effects is important, especially in patients with coexistent IBD, comorbid hypertension, or cardiovascular or renal
disease. There is no particular NSAID that is preferred, and indirect evidence suggests that different NSAIDs yield similar efficacy.
However, this evidence is based only on those NSAIDs
that have been evaluated. In the authors’ opinion, using an
extended-release formulation is more helpful in patients with
active disease as it allows a basal drug level through the night without the need for multiple-dosing per day. If a first NSAID does
not result in adequate efficacy, a second NSAID should be tried.
Typically, response to treatment is rapid, and a reassessment can
be performed at 2 to 4 weeks after initiation.
In patients who attain a low disease activity state or inactive
disease, should they maintain continuous or daily high-dose
NSAID therapy? There are no withdrawal studies to address this
question directly. The current recommendation in patients with
stable disease is that they use on-demand dosing of NSAIDs.
The caveat to this situation is that they may require daily or highdose NSAIDs to maintain this state. There is a single study using
celecoxib in AS continuously versus on demand that suggested
continuous use celecoxib resulted in less spinal radiographic progression at 2 years compared with on-demand use.
However,
more recently diclofenac was studied in the Effects of Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs on RAdiographic Damage in Ankylosing Spondylitis (ENRADAS) trial with a primary endpoint
of radiographic progression, and no benefit was observed in the
continuous-use group compared with the on-demand group.
Glucocorticoids
There is no evidence that systemic glucocorticoids (at low to moderate doses) work in axial SpA, and current guidelines strongly recommend against their use.
Systemic oral glucocorticoids have
been assessed in a 2-week placebo-controlled trial of daily 50 mg
prednisolone, 20 mg prednisolone, or placebo. The study found no
difference in primary endpoint (50% improvement in BASDAI)
based on treatment group, although mean reductions in BASDAI
were greater for those in the high-dose prednisolone group versus
placebo (2.39 versus 0.66, respectively).
Intra-articular injections for both the peripheral joint and SI joints can be considered.
The authors’ opinion is that SI joint injections work only temporarily and are associated with additional unnecessary radiation
with fluoroscopic or CT-guided injection, although American College of Rheumatology (ACR)/Spondylitis Association of America
(SAA)/Spondyloarthritis Research and Treatment Network (SPARTAN) guidance provides a conditional recommendation for intraarticular administration of glucocorticoids for isolated sacroiliitis.
However, because axial SpA often affects the bilateral SI joints, a
unilateral injection may not fully benefit the patient. Additionally,
in the presence of unilateral sacroiliitis, infection should be considered as one contemplates injection or other therapies.
Conventional Synthetic Disease-Modifying
Anti-rheumatic Drugs
Traditional csDMARDs have been studied without demonstration of benefit in axial disease.
In a subgroup of patients with
peripheral synovitis, there may be benefit on the peripheral arthritis, although if active axial disease exists, consideration should be
made for the most effective and safest therapeutic options to cover
both manifestations.
Tumor Necrosis Factor Inhibitors
TNF inhibitors have revolutionized the treatment of axial SpA.
Their introduction was based on the finding of TNF expression
in SI joint biopsies of AS patients, the observation that overexpression of TNF leads to sacroiliitis in animal models, and earlier
clinical trial data demonstrating the efficacy of one TNF inhibitor,
infliximab, in Crohn’s disease. Five TNF inhibitors are of proven
benefit in AS according to pivotal phase III trials: infliximab, etanercept, adalimumab, certolizumab, and golimumab (subcutaneous and intravenous). In nr-axSpA, adalimumab, certolizumab,
etanercept, and golimumab have been studied. Infliximab is an
IgG
chimeric monoclonal antibody, with the Fab portion derived
from the mouse. It is given in a dose of 5 mg/kg every 6 to 8
weeks after loading at 0, 2, and 6 weeks. Etanercept is a recombinant TNF receptor IgG
fusion protein that is self-administered
by subcutaneous injection once weekly (50 mg). Adalimumab and
golimumab are human monoclonal antibodies that are self-administered by subcutaneous injection on alternate weeks (40 mg) or
every 4 weeks (50 mg), respectively. Certolizumab is a pegylated
Fab fragment of a humanized anti-TNF monoclonal antibody
that is given as a subcutaneous injection either as 200 mg every 2
weeks or 400 mg every 4 weeks after loading. None of these agents
require concomitant therapy with MTX. The guidelines recommend these agents be used as monotherapy as there is not enough
evidence that MTX is necessary for primary efficacy or prevention
of anti-drug antibody formation. Infliximab is the most immunogenic of the TNF inhibitors, and consideration for low-dose MTX
is reasonable, despite the absence of strong evidence.
When patients have inadequate response or are intolerant of
NSAIDs, TNFi are recommended for the treatment of axial SpA,
although the option to use an IL-17 inhibitor can also be considered, especially if there is a high burden of psoriasis. Countries
have different requirements for therapeutic eligibility, with much
of the world requiring a Bath Ankylosing Spondylitis Disease
Activity Index (BASDAI) of
≥
4 (at least moderate disease activity), although there is a shift to consider the ASDAS (
≥
2.1) as the
threshold to initiate a biologic based on the 2022 updated ASAS–
EULAR guidelines. The United States requires only the rheumatologist opinion for indication and an International Classification
of Disease (ICD) diagnosis code. For the indication of nonradiographic disease specifically, the EMA has granted approval in the
setting of objective signs of inflammation (MRI BME and/or an
elevated CRP level) in addition to subjective disease activity. Similarly, the ASAS–EULAR guidelines recommend this as a requirement for nonradiographic disease. The United States currently
does not require objective inflammation for initiation of immunomodulatory therapy. The FDA granted approval for the first
indication in nonradiographic disease in 2019 for certolizumab
pegol based on a Phase III, 52-week double-blind randomized
controlled trial (RCT).
Consideration of Extramusculoskeletal Manifestations.
There are special situations to consider when initiating treatment
with a TNF inhibitor. If a patient also has IBD, then prescribing
a TNF inhibitor that is a monoclonal antibody is recommended.
Adalimumab, golimumab, and infliximab are approved for ulcerative colitis, while adalimumab, certolizumab pegol, and infliximab are approved for Crohn’s disease. In patients with frequently

recurrent anterior uveitis, consideration for the use of a monoclonal antibody is also recommended. In this case the best evidence
exists for adalimumab and infliximab. Golimumab and certolizumab have been studied in an uncontrolled manner, with a suggestion that their use might associate with less flares.
Stable Disease on TNF Inhibitor Therapy.
When a patient
with axial SpA responds to TNF inhibition, should the NSAID be
discontinued? The Study to Evaluate the NSAIDS Sparing Effect
of Etanercept in Subjects with Axial Spondyloarthritis (SPARSE)
trial addressed the NSAID-sparing effect of TNF inhibition in
axial SpA patients, finding that 41% of patients exposed to etanercept were able to become NSAID-free compared with 14% of
the placebo group (
P
= 0.013).
When patients reach inactive
disease, should the TNF inhibitor be withdrawn or dose–deescalated? Several trials have now been completed randomizing
patients in remission on TNF inhibition to either remain on drug
or withdraw. One study randomized patients with early axial SpA
on certolizumab pegol to stay on drug, dose reduce or discontinue.
This study showed patients on any dose of certolizumab
did similarly well, compared with the placebo group, who almost
all flared, suggesting we may be able to safely taper but not stop
TNF inhibition when patients reach remission, especially when
therapy is started at an earlier stage. Tapering can be considered
per the ASAS–EULAR guidelines but is conditionally recommended against in the 2019 ACR/SAA/SPARTAN guideline.
In nr-axSpA, withdrawal studies have also been completed. In
ABILITY 3,
patients with active nr-axSpA were initiated on
adalimumab in an open-label fashion for 28 weeks. If inactive
disease was reached, patients were randomized to maintain adalimumab versus discontinuing the agent, and all patients were
followed for 40 weeks. As expected, more patients randomized
to placebo flared, but 47% patients randomized to placebo did
not flare through 40 weeks of follow-up. During the etanercept
withdrawal trial with the same patient population, patients were
treated with etanercept for 24 weeks and if attaining inactive disease (ASDAS <1.3), they were randomized to stay on drug or
discontinue with up to 40 weeks of follow-up. Approximately
25% remained flare-free for the 40 weeks of follow-up, while
the rest flared at various timepoints. Absence of MRI sacroiliitis
and CRP concentrations of
≤
3 mg/L at randomization predicted
remaining in a flare-free state following treatment withdrawal.
Unlike RA and psoriatic arthritis, for which RCT data show
TNF inhibitors reduce radiographic progression, high-quality
RCT data for inhibition of radiographic progression do not exist
in axial SpA.
Interleukin-17 Inhibitors
There are two IL-17A inhibitors approved in axial SpA, in both
radiographic and nonradiographic disease based on phase III clinical trials.
Additionally, bimekizumab, a dual IL-17A and IL17F inhibiting humanized monoclonal antibody is effective in both
radiographic and nonradiographic axial SpA in Phase III trials.
Bimekizumab is now approved for use in psoriatic arthritis and in
axial spondyloarthritis. Secukinumab is given as a 150-mg subcutaneous injection every 4 weeks with or without a loading regimen
of 150 mg weekly for 4 weeks. Ixekinumab is given as an 80-mg
subcutaneous injection every 4 weeks. North American treatment
recommendations recommend secukinumab or ixekinumab after
inadequate response to TNF inhibition or where TNF inhibitors are contraindicated; however, the most recent 2019 guideline
was published soon after the approval of only one IL-17A inhibitor, and the evidence base was small. The 2022 ASAS–EULAR
recommendations suggest IL-17A inhibitors be considered in a lateral position to TNF inhibition, with the exception of the presence
of extramusculoskeletal manifestations, which might favor a specific
targeted biologic (e.g., TNF inhibition in context of IBD).
Janus Kinase (JAK) Inhibitors
Two different JAK inhibitors have Phase III data in AS, and one
of them, upadacitinib (JAK1 inhibitor), has Phase III data in nraxSpA and an FDA indication for both radiographic and nonradiographic disease in the setting of inadequate response to TNF
inhibition. Tofacitinib, a JAK 1,3 inhibitor, was studied in a Phase
III 16-week trial, with significant improvement in the symptoms
and signs.
The tofacitinib Phase II trial additionally showed
improvement in MRI inflammation.
Upadacitinib was studied
in three Phase III trials: two in radiographic disease (biologic naïve
and TNF inhibitor inadequate responder) and one in nr-axSpA.
All trials met their primary endpoints with a difference in ASAS40
at 14 weeks.
What is not clear is whether the risk of cancer
and major adverse cardiovascular events noted in patients with RA
enrolled in the tofacitnib ORAL Surveillance study
will translate to patients with axial SpA. However, until there is more direct
evidence in this group to suggest otherwise, we should assume
similar risk as observed in RA. As such the 2022 ASAS–EULAR
guidelines recommend against JAK inhibition in patients with
cardiovascular disease or risk or in patients 65 or older.
Other Agents
A controlled dose-response (60 mg vs. 10 mg) evaluation of a
bisphosphonate, pamidronate, given intravenously on a monthly
basis for 6 months, showed evidence of symptomatic efficacy,
primarily in patients with only axial disease.
In a 48-week
open-label RCT, axial SpA patients assigned to pamidronate versus golimumab had no difference in pain or QoL, but the golimumab arm had significant suppression of inflammation detected
both by CRP and MRI compared with the pamidronate-exposed
patients.
In general, side effects are significant and benefit is
limited, such that these agents are not recommended for the primary treatment of AS.
Thalidomide has been used in three open-label studies in AS
because of its known anti-TNF effect, through enhanced degradation of the TNF mRNA.
In one study, improvement was
reported in 80% of patients, with loss of efficacy 3 months after
treatment discontinuation.
Frequent side effects are drowsiness,
constipation, and dizziness. Particular caution is required because
of other major thalidomide-associated side effects, such as teratogenicity and peripheral neuropathy. Thalidomide is not recommended as a treatment for axial SpA.
Failed Biologic Trials
Several biologic agents have been studied in up to Phase III trials
with failed primary endpoints. Rituximab was studied in a phase
II study with suggested efficacy in some patients but not in TNFiinadequate responders.
Abatacept did not demonstrate
efficacy in AS.
Tocilizumab, an IL-6 inhibitor, and sarilumab,
an IL-6 receptor inhibitor, did not demonstrate efficacy, including no improvement in MRI BME.
Despite ustekinumab’s
efficacy and approval for use in Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis,
psoriasis, and psoriatic arthritis, the phase III program was closed
early, when the trial in biologic-naïve AS patients did not meet
the primary endpoint.
The IL-23 inhibitor, risankizumab, did
not meet its primary endpoint in AS, despite suggested efficacy in
Crohn’s disease, psoriasis, and psoriatic arthritis.

Treat-to-Target
A treat-to-target pragmatic cluster-randomized controlled trial
(TICOSPA) was performed in axial SpA.
Centers were randomized to usual care (patients assessed every 12 weeks) or tight
control (patients assessed every 4 weeks) with a treatment target
of ASDAS <2.1 (low disease activity or less). Although a difference in the primary endpoint was not reached (the proportion
of patients achieving a 30% improvement in the ASAS Health
Index), the 2022 ASAS–EULAR guidelines recommend following the ASDAS for at least a clinically important improvement
(change in ASDAS
≥
1.1) to continue therapy.

### Nonpharmacologic Therapy

Physical Therapy
This therapeutic approach is important for patients with axial
SpA. Patients feel better with exercise; improvement with exercise is one of the IBP criteria. The ASAS–EULAR guideline
for axial SpA recommends exercise for all patients.
Physical
therapy improves outcomes in axial SpA patients.
In addition to formal physical therapy, patients should perform back
exercises and stretching.
In an RCT, a program of supervised physiotherapy in groups was superior to individualized
programs in improving thoracolumbar mobility and fitness.
The program, which consisted of hydrotherapy, exercises, and
sporting activities twice weekly for 3 hours per session, resulted
in improved overall health and less stiffness, as reported by the
patient.
An intensive 3-week spa-exercise therapy program
resulted in marked improvement in both subjective and objective assessments that lasted for as long as 9 months. Health
resource use, in particular NSAID use and sick leave, was significantly reduced during this 9-month follow-up period. The
clinical benefits of such treatments can be achieved at acceptable
costs.
A Cochrane review concluded that a home exercise
program is better than no intervention, supervised group physiotherapy is better than home exercises, and combined inpatient
spa-exercise therapy followed by supervised outpatient weekly
group physiotherapy is better than weekly group physiotherapy
alone (level A evidence).
The tendency toward positive effects
of physiotherapy in the management of axial SpA calls for further research in this field. Moreover, the impact on physical
functioning of physiotherapy in patients who respond well to
biologics needs to be assessed.
Lying prone for 15 to 30 minutes once or several times a day
is useful to reverse the tendency toward kyphosis, which is aggravated by pain and fatigue, as well as flexion contractures of the hip
joints. Patients should sleep fully supine on a firm mattress with
only a small neck-support pillow. In addition to formal physical
therapy, patients should perform back exercises and stretching.
There is no evidence for an anti-inflammatory or gluten-free diet
in axial SpA, although, in the authors’ opinion, a diet free of processed food is generally a good principle for all patients, regardless
of disease state.
Surgery
The long-term results of total hip replacement in AS are satisfactory. The outcome of 138 total hip replacements and 12 revisions
was good or very good in 86% of patients, and 63% of patients
had no pain. Mobility was good or very good in 44% of patients.
The mean follow-up was 7.5 years (range, 1 to 34 years). Altogether, 69% of the male total hip recipients younger than 60 years
were at work at the time of the survey.
Ectopic bone formation
may occur, with the rate of heterotopic ossification being 30%.
One large study of cemented implants with more than 10
years of follow-up showed 96% had low pain scores, and implant
survival was excellent.
In the United States, the majority of
patients undergo cementless stem total hip replacement.
Vertebral osteotomy may be required in selected cases to correct marked flexion deformity when forward vision is severely
impaired but note that the 2019 ACR/SAA/SPARTAN guideline conditionally recommends against vertebral osteotomy for
severe kyphosis. Diaphragmatic herniation may result from the
procedure.
In patients with r-axSpA, careful consideration of neck involvement is required pre-operatively, including imaging. General anesthesia needs to be planned well because it requires intubation that
may involve neck manipulation. These movements may increase
the risk of fracture through syndesmophytes or osteoporotic vertebrae. If there is extensive cervical spine disease, special positioning
may be required because of kyphosis. Respiratory assessment is
also important in established disease because lung function may
be adversely affected.

### Treatment Summary

The therapeutic options for axial SpA remain limited after
NSAIDs, with only two biologic classes (TNF and IL-17 inhibitors) and two JAK inhibitors approved in axial SpA. The advent
of biologics and targeted small molecules has nevertheless revolutionized the therapeutic landscape of the disease. Despite the
dramatic improvements, most patients do not reach an inactive
disease state, possibly mediated by a still unacceptable delay to
diagnosis and significant burden of damage at first recognition
of disease. The treatment options are even more limited based on
the differential treatment effects across the extramusculoskeletal
manifestations. Treat-to-target trials have failed to show benefit,
although treatment targets may still be important for the individual patient.

## Conclusion

The term
axial spondyloarthritis
has now largely replaced the traditional name of the disease,
ankylosing spondylitis
. nr-axSpA is
more heterogeneous than radiographic AS. Uniform nomenclature will facilitate communication and reporting of study results.
Therefore it has been suggested that the term
axial SpA
should
be the preferred nomenclature. Inappropriate use of classification criteria for diagnostic purposes increases the risk of falsepositive labeling of individuals as having nr-axSpA. NSAIDs,
physiotherapy, and patient education remain first-line therapy,
but three new classes of drug treatment are available for NSAID
nonresponders. Current evidence suggests that early suppression
of inflammation with biologic treatment may decrease or retard
the risk of new bone formation of the axial skeleton. Research is
needed to more accurately identify those patients who will pro
gress from nr-axSpA to r-axSpA and on how progression can be
prevented or delayed by early treatment. Better understanding of
gut mucosal immunology interactions may lead to novel treatment approaches.
