# Relapsing Polychondritis

GAYE CUNNANE

KEY POINTS

> Relapsing polychondritis (RPC) is a rare, systemic autoimmune disorder

> characterized by episodic inflammation of cartilaginous structures.

> Classic clinical findings include “cauliflower ear” and “saddle nose”

> deformities.

> RPC may cause significant pathology of the upper airways, eyes, inner

> ears, kidneys, and blood vessels, with potentially life-threatening

> consequences.

> The disease may be primary or it may develop in association with other

> diseases, such as myelodysplasia, vasculitis, or other autoimmune/

> connective tissue disorders.

> Although there are no specific laboratory markers for RPC, the diagnosis

> is made by observing the classic clinical features and may be

> confirmed by the characteristic histologic findings of affected tissues.

> Because of the multisystem nature of the disease, a broad approach to

> diagnosis and management is essential.

> Treatment is frequently empiric because of the wide variety of

> disease manifestations and lack of controlled trials. However,

> immunosuppression is the mainstay of therapy.

## Introduction

Relapsing polychondritis (RPC) is a rare autoimmune disease
of unknown etiology, characterized by episodic inflammation of
cartilaginous structures throughout the body (e.g., the ears, nose,
upper respiratory tract, chest wall, and joints). Noncartilaginous
tissues may become involved, particularly those with an abundance
of proteoglycans, such as the eye, inner ear, heart, blood vessels,
and kidney.
Associated diseases such as vasculitis or myelodysplastic syndromes are present in as many as one-third of cases. The
severity of the inflammatory process frequently requires the use
of immunosuppressive agents. As its name suggests, RPC has an
episodic pattern with periods of intense inflammation. Prognosis
is variable, with a 5-year survival rate of 45% to 95%, depending
on organ involvement and disease or treatment complications.
This disease was first described in 1923 by Rudolf Jaksch von
Wartenhorst, an internal medicine physician, when he published
the case of a young male with characteristic clinical features and
named the disease
polychondropathia
.
In 1960 investigators provided a more detailed description and renamed the disease
relapsing polychondritis
.

## Epidemiology

The worldwide incidence of RPC is unknown, but researchers
from Rochester, Minnesota, suggest an annual incidence of 3.5
per million in that community.
RPC occurs in all racial-ethnic
groups and with similar frequency in men and women. Peak age of
onset is 40 to 50 years, but it has been described in children
and
in people older than 80 years.
No familial pattern of inheritance
has been shown.

## Pathology

### Normal Cartilage

There are three types of cartilage tissue (hyaline, elastic, and
fibrocartilage), which differ in the composition of the extracellular matrix. The cellular component of cartilage consists of
chondrocytes, whereas the extra-cellular matrix is composed of
interlinking fibrils of type II collagen, other collagens, hydrophilic proteoglycan aggregates, and a variety of matrix proteins.
One such protein, matrilin-1, is exclusive to the respiratory
tract, ears, and xiphisternum in adults and is only found in
articular cartilage before skeletal maturity.
Cartilage is an avascular structure that derives essential nutrients from adjacent
tissue. The composition of cartilage confers resilience to external compressive forces. In healthy adults, turnover of cartilage
components is slow, with incomplete repair processes.
Degradation of collagen fibrils occurs with age, resulting from imbalances between naturally occurring proteolytic enzymes and their
inhibitors.

### Relapsing Polychondritis Cartilage

There are limited data on the microscopic changes in tissue
affected by RPC, partly because of the low disease incidence
and also because the diagnosis is usually made on clinical
grounds. In the initial stages, inflammatory cells infiltrate the
perichondrium, with evidence of lymphocytes (CD4
T cells),
macrophages, neutrophils, eosinophils, and plasma cells on
hematoxylin-eosin staining, whereas the structure of the cartilage
appears unaffected.
Immunofluorescence may reveal deposits
of immunoglobulin and cartilage components at the cartilage–
perichondrium junction.
Later, the inflammatory cells invade
the cartilage, releasing proteolytic enzymes such as matrix metalloproteinases and cathepsins.
As the cartilage is destroyed,
it loses its basophilic staining, and elastic and collagen fibers
become disrupted.
Necrosis and a reduction in cartilage components may be
observed. The cartilage is subsequently replaced by fibrotic tissue,
with areas of calcification. Granulation tissue and gelatinous cysts
may be observed within involved areas.

## Pathogenesis

Although the pathogenesis of RPC is unknown, evidence supports the concept of an autoimmune process. The presence of
inflammatory cells and immune complexes in involved cartilage,
the detection of autoantibodies in cartilage components, the association with human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-DR, the frequent
concurrence with other autoimmune diseases, and the response
to immunosuppressive treatment all support this hypothesis.
Researchers have observed autoantibodies to types II, IX, and XI
collagen in patients with this disease,
and autoantibodies to
matrilin-1 and cartilage oligomeric protein (COMP) have been
detected in those with respiratory tract involvement.
Type II
collagen neoepitope can be found in the urine of patients with
active disease.
More recently, the serum level of soluble triggering receptor expressed on myeloid cells-1 has been cited as a
potential biomarker of disease activity in RPC.
Pro-inflammatory chemokines involved in the recruitment of monocytes and
modulation of macrophage function, such as monocyte chemoattractant protein-1, macrophage inflammatory protein 1
β
, and
interleukin (IL)-8, are significantly elevated in active RPC compared with controls.
TNF expression is also higher in RPC.
Furthermore, there is a class II HLA association with RPC. A
two-fold increase in HLA-DR4 has been found in RPC, although
a specific subtype has not been identified.
There is a negative
association between the extent of organ involvement in RPC and
HLA-DR6.
Several animal models have helped to elucidate some of the
pathogenetic mechanisms underlying RPC. Chondritis can be
induced in certain rat or mice species following injection of type
II collagen.
In addition, mice that express HLA-DQ6alphabeta/8alpha-beta transgenes develop a spontaneous form of
polychondritis with auricular, nasal, and joint involvement.
Experiments that used the matrilin-1-induced rodent model of
RPC have demonstrated the importance of T cells, B cells, and
complement components in the pathogenesis of this disease.
Deletion of IL-10 in this model resulted in increased disease severity, suggesting a role for this endogenous cytokine in suppressing
episodic inflammation.
The processes involved in the initiation
of disease, escalation of the inflammatory response, and subsequent cartilage destruction are poorly understood. In a healthy
individual, cartilage is an immunologically privileged site.
However, in RPC, cartilage components are exposed and vulnerable
to immunologic attack, resulting in a perpetuating process of
systemic inflammation and local tissue damage. In genetically
susceptible individuals, cartilage microtrauma or molecular mimicry between an infectious/inciting agent and cartilage structures
might instigate a cycle of inflammation similar to other autoimmune disorders.

### Respiratory Disease

Involvement of the upper respiratory tract may be life-threatening
and should be investigated at an early stage in the disease course.
Although present in just 10% of patients at presentation, approximately 50% of those with RPC will ultimately develop pulmonary
symptoms that are associated with a worse prognosis.
Hoarseness,
dysphonia, a persistent dry cough, or anterior neck stiffness could
indicate upper-airway disease. Acute inflammation of the tracheobronchial tree may lead to tracheomalacia, dynamic obstruction,
and acute respiratory failure, whereas repeated inflammatory episodes can cause subglottic stenosis, chronic dyspnea, and increased
susceptibility to infection.
In some patients, respiratory
C

symptoms can occur in the absence of the more typical clinical features of RPC, resulting in diagnoses of asthma or obstructive sleep
apnea prior to confirmation of the RPC syndrome.
Collapse of the upper airways may be precipitated by attempted
intubation or bronchoscopy. Therefore a cautious approach to invasive investigations should be undertaken in patients with suspected
RPC.

### Cardiovascular Disease

RPC is most commonly associated with valvular heart disease
and large vessel vasculitis and, more rarely with atherosclerosis,
pericarditis, and myocarditis.
Although more likely to occur
in patients with long-standing disease, involvement of the heart
and/or blood vessels may develop acutely with few prior symptoms.
A review of 440 patients with RPC found a prevalence
of aortic regurgitation in 7.7%, mitral regurgitation in 1.8%, and
combined valvular disease in 1.6%, all more prevalent in males.
Aortic valve disease is thought to be the result of progressive dilatation of the aortic ring rather than active local inflammation.

Mitral valve incompetence may result from valvitis or papillary
muscle involvement.
Myocarditis can lead to cardiac failure and
conduction system abnormalities.
Vascular involvement in RPC ranges from 5% to 25% and affects
blood vessels of any size.
Aortic disease, including aortitis, aneurysm formation, and dissection, occurs in up to 10% of patients.
RPC-associated vasculitis has been observed in a wide variety of locations, including the subclavian, carotid, iliac, renal, vertebral, axillary, femoral, inferior mesenteric, and brachiocephalic vessels, and in
one small study was noted to be more common in young females.
Small-vessel vasculitis has also been reported in RPC and may cause
end organ damage to the skin, kidneys, testes, sclera, cochleovestibular system, or other areas.
Arterial and venous thromboses have
been described in patients with RPC.
There are also case reports of
clinical findings similar to Takayasu’s arteritis and the development
of aneurysms in vessels smaller than the aorta.

### Eye Disease

As many as 65% of patients with RPC will develop ocular disease,
most commonly episcleritis and scleritis.
Recurrent inflammation
can lead to thinning of the sclera, giving a bluish appearance to the
eye.
Scleritis associated with RPC is more likely to be bilateral,
necrotizing, recurrent, and linked to some visual loss compared
with other autoimmune disorders.
Keratoconjunctivitis sicca
may occur with associated Sjögren’s syndrome. Uveitis, retinal
vasculitis, and optic neuritis are rare complications. Extraocular
muscle palsy, periorbital edema, and proptosis may also occur as
part of the RPC symptom complex.

### Renal Disease

Proteinuria and/or hematuria may be observed on urinalysis in
approximately 10% of patients with RPC.
Renal pathology
shows mesangial proliferation and segmental or pauci-immune
necrotizing crescentic glomerulonephritis.
Electron microscopy may demonstrate immunoglobulin and complement deposits. Renal pathology may also occur in the context of co-existing
diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus or other rheumatic
diseases. Patients with RPC and renal involvement have a greater
incidence of associated vasculitis and a resultant worse prognosis.

### Neurologic Disease

Central and peripheral nervous system involvement is rare, affecting up to 10% of patients.
Cranial neuropathy, particularly of
the fifth and seventh cranial nerves, is the most common manifestation, but a wide variety of problems including headache, ataxia,
hemiplegia, transverse myelitis, mononeuritis multiplex, aseptic
meningitis, encephalopathy, and dementia have been described.
Such lesions may be vasculitic or autoimmune in nature.

### Mucocutaneous Disease

Skin and mucous membrane changes develop in approximately
50% of patients with RPC. Rashes are present in as many as 35%
of patients with primary RPC, whereas 90% of patients with associated myelodysplasia have mucocutaneous involvement.
Oral
aphthous ulcers are the most common symptom. Peripheral nodules, similar to erythema nodosum, can be noted in approximately
15% of patients.
Rare skin lesions include peripheral limb ulcers,
livedo reticularis, panniculitis, superficial thrombophlebitis, urticaria, and angioedema. Leukocytoclastic vasculitis, dermal vessel
thrombosis, or septal panniculitis may be evident on biopsy.

### Joint Disease

Musculoskeletal manifestations are common and develop in 75%
of patients during the course of the disease.
Involvement of the
joints may be oligoarticular or polyarticular, most commonly
affecting the ankles, wrists, hands, and feet. Recurring pain in the
sternoclavicular, costochondral, and manubriosternal joints has
been described.
However, other joints, including the sacroiliac
joints, may also show evidence of inflammation, although it is
possible that the presence of sacroiliitis relates to the co-existence
of a spondyloarthropathy.
The arthritis associated with RPC is
typically asymmetric, episodic, and nonerosive, and it does not
correlate with RPC activity. Nevertheless, joint destruction in
RPC has been described.

## Differential Diagnosis

Auricular chondritis with sparing of the earlobe is characteristic of
RPC. Redness and swelling of the whole ear, including the earlobe,
may be the result of trauma or exposure to temperature extremes
and should be distinguished from RPC.
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
or
Staphylococcus aureus
are potential causes of unilateral otitis
externa, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
Other
infections associated with chondritis include leprosy and syphilis. Damage to the nose cartilage may be the result of trauma,
local infections, or granulomatous lesions such as anti-neutrophil
cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–associated granulomatosis vasculitis or lethal midline granuloma. Intranasal cocaine use should
also be considered. A wide variety of inflammatory, rheumatic,
and infectious conditions may mimic some features of RPC, particularly when multiple organs are involved. Cogan syndrome is

## Diagnostic Tests

### Laboratory Tests

There are no specific blood tests for the diagnosis of RPC. Routine
laboratory investigations may demonstrate nonspecific findings
such as anemia or mild thrombocytosis. The acute phase response
is typically elevated during periods of inflammation. Urinalysis
should always be performed to determine the presence of subclinical renal disease or infection. Autoimmune tests are nonspecific,
except when associated diseases are present concurrently.

### Tissue Sampling and Histopathology

In typical presentations of RPC, a biopsy is not necessary for diagnosis. However, when the diagnosis is unclear, a biopsy of inflamed
cartilage may show evidence of perichondritis, as described earlier
(
Fig. 106.3
).
Areas of skin rash may demonstrate leukocytoclastic vasculitis; other frequent findings include panniculitis,
neutrophilic dermatoses, or dermal vessel occlusion.

### Pulmonary Studies

Clinicians should conduct pulmonary function tests in all suspected cases of RPC, even in patients who are asymptomatic at
presentation. These tests (including spirometry, lung volumes,
and inspiratory/expiratory flow-volume loops) should be repeated
in the event of new respiratory symptoms. If the test results are
abnormal, CT of the chest is recommended to detect the presence of tracheal or bronchial stenoses or dynamic airway collapse,
which may be evident only during the expiratory phase of the
respiratory cycle.
Extrapulmonary disease from costochondritis may cause a restrictive pattern on pulmonary function testing.
Positron emission tomography/CT (PET/CT) scanning may have
additional value because it can identify the presence of inflammation in areas where it is otherwise undetectable. PET/CT can also
demonstrate treatment response with a reduction in inflammation
after therapeutic intervention
(
Fig. 106.4
).
Bronchoscopy is warranted only if specifically indicated
because the procedure may cause inadvertent damage to the upper
airways, with the risk of precipitating respiratory failure. However,
indirect laryngoscopy is helpful to monitor airway involvement.
Bronchoscopic ultrasound can also be used to monitor disease.

### Imaging

Plain radiographs of involved joints may show joint space narrowing or periarticular osteopenia. Erosions are not usually observed
unless there is an associated rheumatic disorder. Dual radiograph
absorptiometry may demonstrate osteopenia as a consequence of
the underlying inflammation, prolonged corticosteroid use, or
general debility.

## Treatment

Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs and low-dose corticosteroids may successfully control minor inflammation of the nose,
ear, or chest wall (
Table 106.5
). Dapsone can also be used for noncritical cartilaginous inflammation,
and colchicine is of reported
benefit in auricular chondritis.
However, the presence of potentially life-threatening disease or severe chondritis requires highdose prednisone, typically at a dose of 1 mg/kg, reducing slowly as
the inflammation recedes.
Although the RPC is usually steroid
responsive, it can be difficult to withdraw these drugs completely
without risking a flare in disease activity. Many patients require
maintenance doses of prednisone and consideration of alternative immunosuppression. Disease-modifying anti-rheumatic
drug (DMARD) treatment is indicated in patients who require a
steroid-sparing agent and/or in whom life-threatening manifestations have developed.
Because of the rarity of this disease, there are no controlled trials of therapeutic interventions in RPC. However, methotrexate,
cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate mofetil, azathioprine, chlorambucil, and cyclosporine have had successful results in some
patients.
Varied outcomes have been reported with leflunomide.
Plasmapheresis has been used in resistant cases.
Furthermore, intravenous immunoglobulin has been used successfully in recalcitrant RPC.
There are several case reports and small case series detailing the
use of biologic agents in this disease.
Such drugs are typically
initiated in order to reduce corticosteroid use, particularly if oral
disease-modifying agents, such as methotrexate, have not been
successful in controlling inflammation. TNF inhibitors (TNFi)
have been the most frequently studied, with further data available

## Outcome

RPC is an episodic, progressive disease that results in tissue
destruction of target organs. Some patients have a mild course,
complicated only by recurrent chondritis, whereas others develop
potentially life-threatening problems. The most common cause
of death is pulmonary infection, arising as a result of the disease
or its immunosuppressive treatment.
In young patients, adverse
prognostic factors at presentation include anemia, hematuria,
upper-airway disease, arthritis, and saddle nose deformity, whereas
in older patients (more than 51 years of age), only anemia at presentation and/or myelodysplasia predicts increased mortality.
The
presence of vasculitis worsens prognosis, with a 5-year survival rate
of 45%.
In 1986, investigators reported the 10-year survival rate
to be 55%, whereas in 1998, perhaps because of improvements in
disease monitoring and treatment, another study described a 94%
survival rate.

## Conclusion

RPC is a systemic autoimmune disorder, characterized by episodic
inflammation of cartilaginous structures (e.g., the upper aspect of
the ears, proximal airways, eyes, kidneys, heart valves, and blood
vessels). It may occur in isolation or in association with other
inflammatory diseases. Involvement of the trachea or cardiovascular system may have life-threatening consequences. Immunosuppressive treatment, including the use of biologic agents, abrogates
disease features. A holistic approach is recommended to improve
the morbidity and mortality of these patients. Above all, international collaboration to support clinical trials of therapeutic options
in this rare condition is a process likely to yield much-needed data
and more focused treatment approaches for the different phenotypes that constitute RPC.
