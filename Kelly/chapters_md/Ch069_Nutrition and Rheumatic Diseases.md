## Introduction

Inflammatory arthritis, which includes rheumatoid arthritis (RA),
ankylosing spondylitis (AS), and psoriatic arthritis (PsA), has
undergone a therapeutic revolution over two decades with the use
of targeted therapies that have profoundly changed their prognosis. However, the management of these patients should not be
restricted to pharmacologic drugs and to the treat-to-target strategy, as nonpharmacologic treatments also play an important role.
Moreover, the holistic patient-centered care approach, beyond
articular outcomes, is now emphasized, giving an important place
to nondrug therapy. Along this line, changes in lifestyle behaviors
in inflammatory arthritis have a central position, especially with
the wish to incorporate wellness practices to improve quality of life
and health status.
Among the lifestyle behaviors, improving dietary habits has
a special place because patients may consider that it is a natural
way to manage their diseases themselves. Health-care professionals (HCPs) are also increasingly interested by this aspect
of care.
Such growing interest on both sides is well illustrated
by recent dedicated recommendations from scientific societies
(French Society of Rheumatology [SFR] and European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology [EULAR]) (
Table 69.1
,

).
Dietary behavior improvement may represent a way to target
joint symptoms, which can be especially relevant for subjective
persistent symptoms (e.g., residual joint pain or stiffness), without the need for medication changes. Dietary changes can also be
critical for managing associated comorbidities, such as depression,
cardiovascular diseases, and osteoporosis, and the risk of cancer.
From the patient education perspective, change in dietary
behavior can allow patients to be actively involved in their own
care beyond medication adherence. Such patient involvement
can enhance empowerment, self-efficacy, and coping ability.
However, nutritional advice in inflammatory arthritis is
not a substitute for disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs
(DMARDs).
It is essential that patients understand that
dietary approaches are adjunctive to disease-modifying therapies. Indeed, nutritional intervention studies often have a low
level of evidence due to methodological drawbacks. Further,
efficacy of nutrition interventions remains modest compared
with those related to DMARD, and they tend to focus only
on symptoms, with no demonstrated effect on the structural
damage noted in RA. They are therefore primarily additional
adjunctive measures to pharmacologic treatments that must
not interfere with DMARDs.

## Rationale for the Use of Diet to Modulate the Immune System

A large majority of our immune system resides in the digestive
tract and is therefore in close contact with food antigens and
metabolites. Furthermore, immune cells from the digestive tract
can migrate to the periphery and notably to the joints.
Thus a
digestive “priming” could have quite direct consequences on articular immune cells. Diet affects the immune system in several ways,
outlined below.

### Direct Effects

Various nutrients are required for proper functioning of the
immune system, including vitamins A, C, and E, zinc, and
selenium.
Deficiencies in these nutrients can impair immune
function, while adequate intake can support optimal immune
function. For example, vitamin D decreases Toll-like receptors
(TLRs), TNF, and interleukin-6 (IL-6), while increasing tolerogenic dendritic cells and regulatory T cells. Vitamin D prevents
arthritis in mouse models.
Salt promotes T helper cell (Th)17
differentiation,
and salt restriction prevents arthritis development in mice.
Furthermore, the nutritional landscape affects
immunometabolism and immune cell polarization.
Antioxidants, such as vitamin C, vitamin E, and
β
-carotene, can also
help neutralize reactive oxygen species and modulate immune
function.

### Gut Microbiota

The gut microbiota, which consists of trillions of microorganisms
that reside in the intestinal tract, play a critical role in immune
system function. Certain dietary components, such as fiber, can
influence the composition of the gut microbiota. Commensal
bacteria, recognized by TLRs, mediates development of the host
immune system and plays a crucial role in the maintenance of
intestinal epithelial homeostasis.
Gut microbiota modulates the
immune system:
•
By releasing food metabolites:
digestion of fiber releases shortchain fatty acids (SCFAs) that induce regulatory T cells in
the intestinal epithelium, while digestion on L-carnitine
releases trimethylamine
N
-oxide (TMAO) that promotes
inflammation and tryptophan metabolites regulation gut
barrier.
•
By releasing extra-cellular vesicles (EVs):
EVs derive from
either plasma membrane or intra-cellular membranes and
carry proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids. Pathogenic bacteria–derived EVs have proinflammatory effects of host
immune cells, while probiotic-derived EVs mostly shape
the immune response toward tolerance.
•
Through membrane contacts:
Polysaccharide A (PSA) derived
from
Bacteroides fragilis
membranes induces regulatory T
cells in the intestinal epithelium.
The gut microbiota shapes the intestinal barrier. Patients with
inflammatory arthritis have an increased gut permeability that
may precede disease symptoms.
Leaky gut allows the passage of bacterial antigens. Those antigens might promote Th17
cells that can migrate to the joints.
The Th17 environment
will also favor alteration of anti-citrullinated protein antibodies
(ACPA) glycosylation patterns that precede RA symptoms.
In
addition to its impact on immune system, the gut microbiota
may also influence pain through vagal tone and the gut–brain
axis (
Fig. 69.1
).

### Patient Belief and Experience About Diet and Inflammatory Arthritis

Different dietary patterns occur in the general population from
“prudent” to “processed” patterns.
Exclusion diets or consumption of organic food are increasingly practiced with the aim to
improve health state and also for other nonmedical purposes (e.g.,
ecology).
Of note, the World Health Organization (WHO)
states that all adults should aim for a healthy, balanced diet
containing fruits, vegetables, nuts, and whole grains and limited
free sugar, fat, and salt.
In the case of inflammatory arthritis, diet behaviors represent
for the patient a natural way to influence their disease symptoms.
Practices can be diverse and varied, ranging from avoidance of
specific foods to more comprehensive exclusion diets. For example, in a recent U.S. study of 217 patients with RA,
≈
25% of
patients reported an effect (beneficial or detrimental) of foods
on their symptoms, and
≈
25% avoided certain foods to control
their symptoms.
In another study investigating several inflammatory diseases (inflammatory arthritis, psoriasis, and inflammatory bowel diseases), 28% to 42% of patients (depending on the
disease) had changed their dietary habits since their diagnosis.
Of note, the impact of dietary changes was sometimes negative
(e.g., more fatigue), while others did not experience any particular effect, suggesting that a response to dietary changes does not
occur in everyone and may differ across populations according to
sociocultural specificities.
One critical point is that those dietary experiences of patients
with inflammatory arthritis have long been underestimated by
rheumatologists since the majority of patients engage in dietary
modifications on their own without having sought the advice of
HCPs. Patients use multiple sources of information easily accessible (social networks, websites, and health books), but the content is not always valid. Patients can thus be exposed to fake news,
which can lead to false beliefs and erroneous practices, especially
restrictive or popular fad diets. HCPs should therefore warn
patients about promotion of diets based either on no data, on
scientific empiricism, or on experimental pre-clinical or in vitro
data that have not been translated in humans.

## Challenges in Studying Dietary Effects

### Dietary Assessment

It is challenging to accurately measure dietary intake in clinical
trials or observational cohort studies, particularly over extended
periods of time. Different methods of dietary assessment, such as
food frequency questionnaires, 24-hour dietary recalls, and diet
records, can be used. However, the reliability is often limited by
recall bias or important variations that can exist between different
preparations of a dish, for example. eHealth solutions might solve
some limitations related to questionnaires in the near future.

### Confounding Factors

It is impossible to evaluate a nutrient or food item individually. In
fact, eating behaviors are often global. As an example, it was shown
that people avoiding gluten are 14 times more likely to have an
overall healthy dietary pattern.
Moreover, diet is often just one
of many lifestyle factors that can influence health outcomes. Other
factors, such as physical activity, smoking, and alcohol consumption, are important confounding factors that need to be considered.

### Adherence in Interventional Studies

In interventional studies, adherence with dietary interventions is
critical. However, it can be challenging to ensure that participants
follow the prescribed diet consistently. Adherence can be affected
by various factors, such as taste preferences, cultural habits, and
social support. Moreover, the accurate nutritional supervision
implemented in clinical studies is not always easily applicable to
routine care.

### Study Design and Placebo Effect

While double-blind randomized controlled trials are the gold
standard for evaluating the efficacy of interventions, it is not
always possible in dietary intervention clinical trials. In some
supplementation studies (e.g., omega-3), a double-blind design
with a placebo arm can be readily achieved, whereas in some
dietary intervention trials (mainly exclusion diets), it is not
feasible to use a placebo control group, and the design is most
often open-label, raising the problem of the placebo effect. In
the context of dietary intervention trials, the placebo effect can
be a concern because participants may experience changes in
health outcomes simply because they believe they are receiving
a beneficial treatment, even if the treatment has no actual effect
on their health.

### Effect of Diet on Disease Risk Must Be Distinguished From Effect on Disease Activity

It is very important to distinguish studies investigating association between diet or nutrients and the risk of inflammatory
arthritis from those investigating the effect of diet on disease
activity and symptoms in established inflammatory arthritis.
There are numerous case-control and cohort studies reporting
an association between diet or nutrients or food and the risk
of developing inflammatory arthritis.

### Absence or Paucity of Scientific Data Does Not Mean Absence of Effect of Diet

Patients are interested mostly in exclusion diets such as intermittent fasting or ketogenic diet despite absence of clinical data in the
context of inflammatory arthritis. It is important to consider that
giving nutritional advice based on results from basic research studies (e.g., experimental murine models or cell culture experiments)
do not necessarily translate to clinical effects. Another challenge
occurs in the case of available clinical data that is very low quality, as frequently occurs for dietary factors. In conclusion, dietary
interventions in clinical trials face several methodological and scientific challenges that often limit the quality and interpretability
of dietary studies, which complicates the explanations given to the
patient in practice.

## Weight Loss

Weight management is an important focus since obesity is a frequent comorbidity in inflammatory arthritis.
Further, obesity is a
risk factor of RA (especially seronegative) and of PsA, may worsen
disease outcomes, and makes clinical assessment difficult, leading to
erroneous clinical evaluation. Interestingly, in RA, not only are systemic inflammatory features (e.g., C-reactive protein [CRP] or number of synovitis) worse with obesity but so are subjective symptoms
such as pain.
This may lead to excessive DMARD prescription,
which will be less effective because obese patients also respond less
well to DMARDs, particularly anti-TNF drugs.
Weight gain can
also occur with use of anti-TNF drugs.
Moreover, overweight and
obesity are known to increase the risk of cardiovascular diseases and
depression. Surprisingly, obesity is associated with less radiographic
structural progression in RA.
Encouraging weight loss is part of the holistic management
of the patients with inflammatory arthritis.
Weight loss provides symptom alleviation and further decreases disease activity
when combined with TNF inhibitor initiation: in a study with
126 patients with PsA, following a hypocaloric diet versus a freely
managed diet, minimal disease activity (MDA) was more often
achieved with the hypocaloric than the free managed diet (hazard
ratio [HR] = 1.85; 95% confidence interval [CI], 1.02 to 3.35)
with a dose effect noted between the importance of weight loss
and clinical response.
Psoriasis may also improve with weight
loss.
In RA, a proof-of-concept, 12-week, single-blind, randomized controlled trial of 40 obese RA patients (body mass index
[BMI]
≥
30) with the disease activity score on 28 joints (DAS28)
≥
3.2 indicated that a diet intervention (hypocaloric diet of 1000
to 1500 kcal/day and high-protein meal replacements) induced
an average loss of 9.5 kg and provided significant improvements
for Routine Assessment of Patient Index Data 3 (RAPID3) and
positive trends for DAS28 and health-assessment questionnaire
(HAQ)-Disability Index compared with control (0.5 kg loss).
Weight loss can also be achieved by bariatric surgery, which
leads to substantial weight loss. According to a retrospective
cohort study, bariatric surgery decreased disease activity in RA and
may be associated with less RA-related medication use.
In practice, weight loss is encouraged possibly with the supervision by a dietitian and/or nutritionist, who will also explore the
potential complications of obesity and who will avoid any nutritional deficiency during the weight loss program.
Of course, the
effect of weight loss goes beyond articular outcomes since it has a
favorable cardiovascular, metabolic, and psychological effect. There
is no specific predefined weight loss goal in inflammatory arthritis.
The role of the rheumatologist is to document patient weight, calculate body mass index, and initiate a discussion about obesity and
the need for weight loss: such a discussion is an important first step
to induce dietary change behaviors.
Some simple messages may be
given to patients (e.g., intake of fruit, vegetables, and water; reducing
unhealthy food/drinks; reducing portion sizes; and switching to lowcalorie food/drinks).
Some points of vigilance regarding weight loss in patients with
inflammatory arthritis are (1) to explore unexplained weight loss or
weight loss insufficiently explained by diet alone, as it may be a sign of
another progressive condition; (2) weight loss must not be accompanied by muscle wasting and sarcopenia, especially in elderly patients
with frailty; and (3) a long-term improvement in eating habits to
maintain weight loss over time should be preferred rather than a strict
hypocaloric diet that will not last over time. The pharmacologic management of obesity is currently evolving with the use of glucagon-like
peptide 1 (GLP-1) inhibitor, and thus weight loss management in the
context of inflammatory arthritis will change soon.

## What Do We Know About Exclusion Diet in Inflammatory Arthritis?

In this section, we will present data exploring the effect of different
exclusion diets on the risk of inflammatory arthritis development
and on disease activity in established inflammatory arthritis. We
will also discuss potential risk or disadvantages of each exclusion
diet. The most popular diets will be addressed, that is the glutenfree and dairy-free diets, the vegetarian/vegan diet, fasting, and
the ketogenic diet. Other diets, such as the paleo/Seignalet diet,
the Kousmine diet, or a diet rich/poor/supplemented in starchy
foods (bread, cereals, potatoes, pulses) or “acidic” foods (tomatoes,
etc.), are practiced by some patients, but there are no data in the
literature to be discussed.

### Gluten-Free Diet

A gluten-free diet is becoming very popular in the general population for its supposed beneficial effects on health, with around 10%
of people without celiac disease avoiding gluten.
This is also the
case in patients affected by inflammatory arthritis. Among the
patients with inflammatory arthritis who declared to have change
their dietary habits, about 25% followed a gluten-free diet.
Are Inflammatory Arthritis and Celiac Disease Linked?
Contrary to their specific HLA pre-disposition, RA and celiac disease share multiple non-HLA loci. A recent mendelian randomization study suggested that genetically predisposed RA increased the
risk of celiac disease in individuals of European descent by 46%,
while genetic predisposition to celiac disease was not associated with
an increased risk of RA.
In a small cohort of 45 patients with
celiac disease, ACPA-specific auto-antibodies seemed to be overrepresented, as they were found in 35% of the patients.
However,
an association between RA and celiac disease remains very rare.
Moreover, perceived gluten intolerance is reported with a prevalence
similar in RA patients as in the general population.
Mucosal reactivity to gluten is seen in a minor fraction of RA patients and is not
related to the frequently perceived intolerance to these proteins.
Does Gluten Increase the Risk of Inflammatory Arthritis?
No study supports the link between gluten consumption and the
risk of developing inflammatory arthritis.
Is a Gluten-Free Diet Effective in Decreasing Joint
Symptoms?
Some patients report symptom improvement after eliminating
gluten from their diets.
No randomized study has evaluated the
effects of a gluten-free diet alone. Three randomized controlled studies were performed involving a combined a gluten-free diet with a
vegan diet,
with or without prior fasting,
or with meat and
dairy product exclusion.
The diet was followed for 1 year and for
3.5 (preceded by a 10-day fast) and 3 months, respectively. These
small studies suffer from important methodological issues. While
the outcomes were evaluated by an independent observer in the
first study, the evaluation was open in the others. Moreover, a large
proportion of the patients in the “intervention” group dropped out
from the study before it ended (42% [16 of 38], 37% [10 of 27],
and 30% [12 of 40], respectively), and in the first and last studies,

the effectiveness was only determined in the patients who completed the study (per protocol analysis), which greatly limits ability
to make valid conclusions. In the subgroup of patients who finished
the first study, DAS28 decreased significantly in the intervention
group (from 5.3 to 4.3; P < .001) but not in the control group (from
5.3 to 5.0; P = .19).
In the third study, DAS28 did not improve
significantly; visual activity score decreased significantly after the
exclusion diet (from 50 to 40; P = .003), while the decrease was not
significant in the control group.
No intergroup comparison was
performed. In the second study, where an intention-to-treat analysis
was done, the number of tender and swollen joints was less during the follow-up period in the intervention group. Nevertheless,
it is important to note that patients in the intervention group still
had on average 18 tender joints and 8 swollen joints, which is not
anywhere near the current treatment target for RA. Also, structural
progression was observed in both groups. In the first two studies,
the patients in the intervention groups lost a significant amount of
weight (mean
≈
4.2 and 6 kg, respectively), contrary to patients in
the control groups (mean
≈
0.7 and 0 kg, respectively). This weight
loss could at least partly explain the observed effect.

What Are the Potential Disadvantages of a Gluten-Free
Diet?
First, the cost of gluten-free products is two to three times that of
the standard corresponding foods.
Then, while gluten-free products
are perceived as healthier than conventional products, they contain
fewer proteins and more monounsaturated fatty acids, carbohydrates,
and sugar.
In addition, following a strict gluten-free diet may have
impact on patients’ lives socially.
Moreover, two American cohorts
suggest that, in the general population, the consumption of gluten
may have cardiometabolism benefits. Using two U.S. longitudinal
cohorts with 26 years follow-up, over 2.27 million patient-years, and
6529 coronary events, gluten consumption was associated with 15%
decreased coronary risk after adjustments for cardiovascular risk factors and cereal consumption.
Gluten consumption also decreased
the risk of diabetes by 20%.
Given that cardiovascular risk is
increased in patients with inflammatory arthritis, this is important
data to consider when assessing the benefit/risk balance of the glutenfree diet in inflammatory arthritis. Thus the French national rheumatology recommendation proposed that “A gluten-free diet should not
be proposed as a means to control chronic inflammatory rheumatic
disease activity, in the absence of confirmed celiac disease.”

### Dairy-Free Diet

Do Dairy Products Increase the Risk of Inflammatory
Arthritis?
As with gluten, the belief that dairy products have deleterious
effects is widespread. Nevertheless, there are no studies supporting
this hypothesis in inflammatory arthritis. Dairy consumption is
not associated with the risk of RA.
Is a Dairy-Free Diet Effective in Decreasing Joint
Symptoms?
In a pre-clinical study, bovine milk–derived extra-cellular vesicles were
beneficial, attenuating arthritis scores.
As with gluten, the intestinal
mucosa of RA patients is not hyper-reactive to cow’s milk proteins,
and there is no correlation between the intolerance experienced by
the patient and the reactivity of the intestinal mucosa.
In patients,
the only data in the literature comes from a study in which the reintroduction of dairy products after the vegan period did not lead to a
change in joint parameters.
To our knowledge, no other controlled
human or animal studies have been conducted on the effect of dairy
products, or their avoidance, in inflammatory arthritis.
What Are the Potential Disadvantages of a Dairy-Free Diet?
Dairy products are the main dietary source of calcium. Patients with
inflammatory arthritis are at increased risk of osteoporosis due to their
disease and frequent and prolonged exposure to corticosteroids. A
recent meta-analysis confirms the beneficial effect of calcium supplementation for 12 to 24 months on bone mineral density, especially
in countries with low dietary calcium intake.
Another cohort metaanalysis reported a protective effect of high dairy consumption on hip
fracture risk, with a 13% risk reduction.
This fracture risk reduction
was observed with fermented dairy products (i.e., yogurts, cheese,
and fermented milk) but not with milk. However, this work has been
criticized because of the great variability from one study to another of
the definitions of the consumption categories, leading to a possible
classification bias, as well as the many possible confounding factors.
Indeed, patients at higher risk of fracture may voluntarily consume
more dairy products.
In addition, fermented dairy products also appear to decrease
cardiovascular events (stroke, myocardial infarction, ischemic heart
disease, and cardiovascular mortality) by 4%, the risk of type 2 diabetes by 27%, and the risk of metabolic syndrome by 20%.
Whole
milk consumption was also associated with a lower coronary calcification score at inclusion and less progression during follow-up after
multiple adjustments.
Thus the working group proposed following
the recommendations issued for the general population, which are an
intake of two dairy products per day for adults. Because fermented
dairy products are more digestible and could have more marked extraarticular beneficial effects, they could potentially be a favored form of
dairy intake.

### Fasting

Three controlled studies have been conducted on fasting in RA: one
with a short-term evaluation of a 7-day fast involving 13 patients
(cross-over study)
and two with an evaluation of a 7- to 10-day fast
followed by a lacto-vegetarian diet for 9 weeks
(n = 26) or a glutenfree vegan diet for 3.5 months followed by a lacto-vegetarian diet for
9 months (study described in the previous paragraph, n = 53).
The
first study showed a short-term beneficial effect of fasting but did not
provide any information on the maintenance of joint effects after
return to eating.
The second study reported a beneficial effect of
fasting in 5 of the 15 patients, but this was not maintained after the
lacto-vegetarian diet period. In the third study, the beneficial effect on
joints was obtained at the end of the fasting period, and the vegetarian diet maintained this effect without providing any additional benHowever, the analysis was conducted on a per-protocol basis,
efit.
without considering the numerous patients who discontinued the
intervention (10 of 27 in the intervention group and 9 of 26 in the
control group), which makes the results difficult to interpret. In conclusion, although a prolonged 7-day fast might have beneficial effects,
these do not seem to be maintained after refeeding, which considerably limits its value. It is important to remember that prolonged fasting must be strictly medically supervised. No interventional study has
explored alternative fasting of shorter duration.

### Vegan/Vegetarian

In addition to the studies mentioned above, two other studies
in RA patients combined a vegetarian diet with either omega-3–
rich fish oil
or lactobacillus-rich drinks.
In the groups without omega-3 supplementation in the first study, only pain was
improved by the vegetarian diet compared with the Western diet

(no effect on the number of painful or swollen joints or on the
HAQ).
The other study found no significant effect of vegetarian
diet on RA activity parameters.
Thus the data on the vegetarian
or vegan diet do not support joint efficacy. In addition, meat consumption is not associated with the risk of RA.

## What Do We Know About Mediterranean Dietary Pattern and Omega-3 Fatty Acids?

### What Is the Mediterranean Dietary Pattern?

The Mediterranean dietary pattern is based on the traditional
foods that people used to eat in countries bordering the Mediterranean Sea. It corresponds to a various and balanced diet and is
characterized by high consumption of unprocessed cereals, fruits,
legumes, vegetables, and olive oils and a low intake of animal
products. The features of this diet are described in the
Box 69.2
.
A benefit of this diet has been shown mainly on cardiovascular
events and neurologic diseases.

### Do Mediterranean Diet and Omega-3 Fatty Acids Decrease the Risk of Inflammatory Arthritis?

In the American cohorts of nurses, NHS-I and NHS-II (3,511,050
person-years of follow-up and 913 incident cases of RA), a Mediterranean dietary pattern did not decrease the risk of RA in women.
However, in the same cohorts, a healthy lifestyle, assessed on five
modifiable components, i.e., smoking, alcohol consumption, body
mass index, physical activity, and diet, was associated with a decreased
risk of RA.
In a French cohort of 62,629 women with 480 incident cases of RA, the Mediterranean diet adherence score was also not
significantly associated with RA risk (HR = 0.86 [95% CI, 0.67 to
1.09] for high score versus low score; P = .09). However, among eversmokers, a higher score was associated with a decreased risk of RA
(HR = 0.91 [95% CI, 0.84 to 0.99] for one-point increase in score;
P = .03).
Fish is probably the food most often considered protective. Fish consumption is associated with a decreased risk of RA.
A
100-g/day increment in fish intake is associated with a 15% decreased
risk of RA. Dose–response analysis shows a modest U-shaped association between fish consumption and incidence of RA, with the lowest
risk at a fish intake of 20 to 30 g/day.

### Is a Mediterranean-Type Diet Effective in Decreasing Joint Symptoms?

Five randomized controlled studies on the joint effect of a Mediterranean diet in inflammatory arthritis have been conducted,
including three in Scandinavian countries, one in England, and
one in Mexico.
Three were open-label studies, and two were
studies with independent assessors. The quality of the studies was
poor (Jadad score between 0 and 3). In two articles, no details were
available on the proposed diet. For the others, regular consumption of fish (more than two times/week or three to four times/
week depending on the study), whole grains (several servings/day),
fruits and vegetables (at least five servings/day), legumes (one to
two or more than two/week depending on the study), nuts and
oilseeds (daily), and low-fat dairy (limit other dairy) were recommended with limited meat consumption (red meat fewer than two
times/week or any meat three times/week or fewer) and the use
of vegetable fat (margarine, olive oil, or grape seed oil). No study
encouraged alcohol consumption, but one study suggested regular

### Are Omega-3 Fatty Acids Effective in Decreasing Joint Symptoms?

Contrary to the other interventions, polyunsaturated fatty acid
supplementation was evaluated in 43 randomized controlled trials
against placebo, and several meta-analyses were performed.
The
vast majority of the studies involved patients with RA. Supplementation with polyunsaturated fatty acids significantly reduced all
the clinical parameters studied, with a significant effect on activity
visual analog scale (VAS); a moderate effect on pain VAS, DAS28,
and HAQ; and a weak effect on morning stiffness. It also significantly reduced the sedimentation rate but not C-reactive protein.

Most of the studies involved supplementation for 3 to 6 months,
a supplementation of at least 2 g/day of omega-3 of animal origin, with mainly fish oil and eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA)/docosahexaenoic acid (DHA). Analyses after 1 month did not show a
significant effect, suggesting that supplementation should last at
least 3 months. Most studies involved omega-3 supplementation
and, less frequently, other polyunsaturated fatty acids (omega-6,
omega-9). There are no structural data, and this supplementation
can only be considered as a complement to medical treatment for
inflammatory arthritis. Although the data are based on few studies
and not definitive, a recent meta-analysis suggested a protective
effect on the risk of cardiovascular events of polyunsaturated fatty
acid supplementation only at doses of 2 g/day and greater.
Conversely to the EULAR recommendations, the French guidelines about diet in inflammatory arthritis advocated the possibility of
a supplementation by capsule, with a preference for fish oils (essentially EPA and DHA) at dosages of 2 to 3 g/day for a period of 3
to 6 months, which should be re-evaluated according to the effects
observed by the patient (
Table 69.1
,
Box 69.2
).
It should be noted
that supplementation at high doses or for very long periods of time
could be associated with an increased risk of bleeding, and it is therefore preferable to respect these dosages and prescription periods. To
date, there are no data on the prescription of repeated courses of treatment, and it is therefore difficult to propose a frequency or number of
courses of treatment to be offered if the patient experiences beneficial
effects. Importantly, no structural protection has been shown with
omega-3 supplements, which may have only a symptomatic effect.
Patients can also be encouraged to increase their dietary intake of
essential polyunsaturated fatty acids, mainly omega-3, in particular
by following a Mediterranean diet.

## Specific Dietary Supplements

Many dietary supplements have been proposed to control inflammatory arthritis activity. The most studied supplements are vitamins (A, B, C, D, E), probiotics, or trace elements (selenium or
zinc).
Overall, none of them have displayed relevant clinical
efficacy and thus cannot be recommended to specifically alleviate joint symptoms. However, the usefulness of vitamin B
(i.e.,
folic acid) and of vitamin D for methotrexate tolerance and bone
health, respectively, is not questioned.
Concerning probiotics, such a strategy seems very attractive considering the role of gut dysbiosis in immune response.
However,
studies investigating probiotics have reported heterogenous results,
and their interpretation is limited by the different strains of probiotics,
heterogeneity across studies, and few or no replication studies. Overall, it is impossible to specially recommend one strain of probiotic
to alleviate joint symptoms without eliminating a potential benefit
from other strains. Some other supplements presented as encapsulated spices concentrates such as saffron, ginger, garlic, or sesamin
have been sparsely evaluated with interesting results, but these results
need replication in larger trials.
The 2022 ACR guideline regarding integrative interventions for rheumatoid arthritis conditionally
recommends following established dietary recommendations without
dietary supplements over adding dietary supplements.

## How to Deal With Diet in Patients With Inflammatory Arthritis: Practical Aspects

In practice, we face two types of situations: patients who want
or have started an exclusion diet and patients who want general
recommendations. In the first situation, it is essential to listen and
remain pragmatic. Large-scale interventional studies are lacking,
and small studies might suffer from interpatient variability. However, it is important to stay open-minded and listen to patients’
feelings and experiences and to keep in mind that absence of data
on one specific diet does not mean that it is not effective. We propose explaining possible deleterious effects of a gluten-free diet on
cardiovascular risk and lack of evidence for efficacy on joints, as
well as beneficial effects of fermented milk (on bone, the cardiovascular system, and metabolic syndrome) and lack of evidence of
diary-free diet efficacy on inflammatory arthritis. If patients still
wish to practice exclusion diets, we recommend proposing the
advice of a dietician to avoid deficiencies with alternative source
of calcium if dairy-free, of fiber if gluten-free, and of iron, B
,
and zinc if vegan. We propose to insist on the “diagnostic” interest
of the approach with no need to prolong the diet if it is not effective on symptoms and the importance of gradually reintroducing
foods to achieve a balanced diet as much as possible.
In the second situation, we support overweight and obese individuals toward weight loss by referring patients to a nutritionist and
encouraging a Mediterranean diet and a diet rich in omega-3 and
olive oil. Omega-3 EPA/DHA supplementation of >2 g/day as complements may also be proposed. Addressing the problem of obesity as
a comorbidity aggravating inflammatory arthritis outcome is critical.
More generally, beyond these two situations, the rheumatologist should inquire about patients’ dietary habits and to ask
whether they have tried some specific diet for their arthritis. This
will be the first step to initiate discussion about dietary issues.
Finally, the rheumatologist has to not forget the usefulness of
some specific diets in specific medical settings such as diabetes,
osteoporosis, gout, and sarcopenia.

## Diet and Other Types of Arthritis

### Osteoarthritis

In osteoarthritis (OA), the main issue is weight management in
case of overweight or obesity since they are main risk factors for
OA (especially on knee), and higher body weight is associated
with greater pain.
Many studies have shown clinical efficacy
in knee OA of various dietary interventions leading to weight
loss.
Among all interventions, bariatric surgery induces the most
important weight loss in OA patients.
Addressing weight loss
is central in the management of patients with knee OA and may
involve dietitian or nutritionist for supervision, follow-up, medical
management, screening for obesity complications, and bariatric
surgery discussion. Beyond obesity and overweight management,
patients should be educated that there are no specific diets shown
in trials to be effective for OA. Cohort studies have shown that
oral dietary fiber intake could decrease the risk of symptomatic
knee OA and pain worsening.
In a more global approach, analyses from the Osteoarthritis Initiative cohort indicated that higher
adherence to Mediterranean diet is associated with a lower risk of
knee OA incidence, less knee pain worsening, better knee function, and better quality of life.
Two studies on probiotics (
Lactobacillus casei
or
Streptococcus thermophilus
) have not shown any
relevant efficacy in knee OA.
Many oral supplements have been investigated in OA without identifying one of them unanimously as providing relevant
clinical efficacy on OA-related symptoms. There is a particular
interest in vitamin D, curcuma, and omega-3 fatty acids. Several
well-conducted clinical trials indicated that vitamin D does not

provide any efficacy.
Curcuma supplements have given interesting results, but the studies are very heterogeneous, especially in
term of the nature of the curcuma formulation.
In a randomized
double-blinded placebo-controlled trial including 235 patients
with knee OA, supplementation with high-dose omega-3 (4 g
krill oil/day 4 g containing 0.60 g EPA/d, 0.28 g DHA/day, and
0.45 g astaxanthin/day) provided a statistically significant effect
on pain over placebo (mixed vegetable oil), but with debatable
clinical relevance.
Finally, a well-balanced healthy diet in association with physical activity will be helpful for general health and
certainly on osteoarthritis symptoms, especially in case of weight
loss if appropriate.

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

In systemic lupus erythematosus, higher weight is associated
with worse function, comorbidities, and mental health, justifying the inclusion of weight loss in the global management of the
patients.
Healthy lifestyle including physical activity and healthy
diet is again advocated.
A diet that addresses the adverse effects
of glucocorticoids should be considered. There is no supplement
that can be recommended, although omega-3 could improve some
parameters.
Again, a healthy diet is not viewed as an alternative
to drug therapy but as a complementary strategy.

### Gout

Dietary factors in gout are reviewed in
Chapter 97
. Most studies to date have focused on the risk of developing gout. Overall,
dietary factors have small effects on serum urate levels in people
in the general population and in people with gout.
Thus, uratelowering therapy is the foundational cornerstone to gout management. Nonetheless, avoidance of certain foods, such as animal
sources of purines and alcohol of all types, is conditionally recommended by the 2020 ACR Guideline for the Management of
Gout due to their associations with triggering gout flares, as well
as avoidance of high-fructose corn syrup.
However, the impact
of diet on the long-term clinical course in gout, such as on tophi
or radiographic damage, is uncertain. Weight loss is also conditionally recommended by the 2020 ACR Gout Guideline.

## Conclusion

Diet is an important factor for health in general since it interacts
with gut microbiota and innate and adaptive immune response
and subsequently modulates inflammation. Such interactions
explain the close relationship with auto-immune process and
influence on inflammatory arthritis.
The interest of rheumatologists for diet management in patients
with inflammatory arthritis is recent and addresses patient expectations. Diet behavior improvement is a key objective of patient education and should be thus fully integrated in the holistic management
of the patients for inflammatory arthritis outcomes, for comorbidities
and for health status and disease copying. Such an approach must be
combined with physical activity promotion but should not interfere
with DMARD usage. It is important to acknowledge that the benefit
of dietary changes remains modest and is primarily of low-quality evidence, especially considering the efficacy of DMARD.
In case of obesity, weight loss is recommended in all types of
arthritis. A healthy balanced diet or the Mediterranean diet are generally advisable, and in fact, both are recommended by the WHO
for the general population.
There is no clear demonstration of
the usefulness of restrictive diets, but more high-quality trials are
needed. Finally, it is essential that rheumatologists and nutritionists
take up this subject in order to bring scientifically proven information to patients, who can be exposed to abundant fake news.
