## Introduction

Sarcoidosis is an orphan, systemic, and clinically heterogeneous
disorder. Its cause has yet to be identified, but environmental,
genetic, and infectious causes have been suggested. The hallmark
of sarcoidosis is the development and accumulation of noncaseating granulomas in any organ system. Organ system involvement,
which is unpredictable and varies between patients, is the primary
determinant of morbidity and mortality in sarcoidosis. Any organ
system can be involved, with the lungs affected in more than 90%
of cases. Lymphatic, skin, and ocular findings are also common.
Given the variability of sarcoidosis manifestations, diagnosing
this disorder is often difficult. Patients may be asymptomatic or
may present with a range of nonspecific symptoms. Occasionally,
specific symptoms such as cough, dyspnea, burning of eyes, or a
rash may suggest the diagnosis.
When extrapulmonary symptoms
develop, they sometimes result in rheumatologic manifestations,
including but not limited to arthritis, skin lesions, or neuropathy.
Rheumatologists frequently meet sarcoidosis patients when
the presentation is atypical, when multiple organs are affected, or
when a differential diagnosis must be clarified.

## Classification Criteria

No classification criteria for sarcoidosis have been prepared by the
American College of Rheumatology or the European Alliance of
Associations for Rheumatology. However, the American Thoracic
Society (ATS), the European Respiratory Society, and the World
Association of Sarcoidosis and Other Granulomatous Disorders
suggest a diagnosis of sarcoidosis with the following fulfilled criteria: (1) a compatible clinical picture, (2) histologic demonstration
of noncaseating granulomas, and (3) exclusion of other diseases
capable of producing a similar clinical picture.
The ATS has published updated recommendations for diagnosing and detecting
sarcoidosis.

## Epidemiology

Sarcoidosis is a global disease. The worldwide prevalence and incidence of sarcoidosis have been difficult to calculate because of its
clinical heterogeneity and variable diagnostic criteria in different
countries. In Northern Europe, up to 40 cases per 100,000 people
have been reported,
whereas in a study from Eastern Europe,
only 3.68 cases of sarcoidosis per 100,000 people were found.
The incidence of sarcoidosis in Japan is also low, with one study
estimating it to be 3.7 cases per 100,000 individuals.
Within
countries, incidence rates may vary between races. In the United
States the annual incidence of sarcoidosis is more than three times
higher in black people (35.5 per 100,000) than in white people
(10.9 per 100,000).
Furthermore, the disease course is more progressive, with higher mortality in African Americans.
Despite
numerous epidemiologic studies of sarcoidosis, many clinicians
and researchers believe that prevalence and incidence estimates are
lower than actual disease rates because of inaccurate diagnoses or
asymptomatic cases that are never diagnosed.
Although sarcoidosis affects men and women of all ages and
from diverse ethnic backgrounds,
some disparities have been
noted. More women than men have been reported in studies
worldwide; estimates indicate that 57% of patients with sarcoidosis are women.
Relative to men, women with sarcoidosis have
more ocular and neurologic manifestations. People of any age may
develop the disease, but the median age of onset is around 40
years.
A second peak of incidence occurs at around age 65 years,
especially in women.

## Etiology

The exact cause of sarcoidosis remains elusive. Multiple etiologic factors have been identified. The heterogeneity of the disease suggests that multiple causative agents may be responsible
for the variable disease manifestations of sarcoidosis. Given the
immunopathogenic mechanisms that underlie sarcoidosis, such a

disease trigger might be antigen-driven T cell–mediated adaptive
immunity that stimulates the cascade of events leading to granuloma formation. Because the lungs are affected in more than 90%
of sarcoidosis cases, likely an environmental agent (including an
infectious agent, potentially exposed via aerogenic routes) contributes to the onset of sarcoidosis.
Both inorganic and organic environmental factors with antigenic capabilities have been implicated in pathogenesis. Early
studies on the causes of sarcoidosis suggested a link between sarcoidosis and agents associated with a rural lifestyle, such as the
lumber industry and burning wood.
A case-control etiologic
study of sarcoidosis (ACCESS) found that burning agricultural
debris and wood is mainly associated with pulmonary sarcoidosis but not systemic sarcoidosis.
In a different analysis of the
ACCESS trial, radiation, insecticides, mildew, and mold were
environmental factors associated with the systemic sarcoidosis
phenotype.
More recently, a sarcoidosis-like phenotype presented in fire department rescue workers involved in the World
Trade Center attack.
These findings may indicate that each sarcoidosis subtype may have distinct inducing factors.
Because of clinical and histologic similarities, numerous methods have been used to look for an infectious agent as a cause of
sarcoidosis, with most studies focusing on
Mycobacterium tuberculosis
or
Propionibacterium acnes.
When in situ hybridization
was used,
M. tuberculosis
catalase-peroxidase protein (mKatG)
was found in nearly 40% of tissue samples from patients with
sarcoidosis. Recombinant mKatG protein was then used to study
patients with sarcoidosis and measure mKatG antibodies, which
were present in 50% of the patients studied.
Other investigators
have found evidence of an immunologic reaction to additional
mycobacterial antigens such as ESAT-6.
P. acnes
has been
found more frequently in granulomas from patients with sarcoidosis but also can be found in people without sarcoidosis. Thus its
primary role in pathogenesis remains unclear.
These studies
suggest that sarcoidosis may represent overexposure of the patient
to a commonly encountered microorganism associated with a dysregulated immune response. Because several types of bacteria have
been associated with sarcoidosis, some clinicians have attempted
to use antibiotics or antimycobacterial agents to manage the disease. Although skin sarcoidosis has occasionally responded to these
treatments,
their usefulness in treating other forms of sarcoidosis appears minimal.
In addition, serum amyloid A (SAA)
accumulates in granulomas due to the hyperimmune T helper
(Th)1 cell response necessary for killing the infecting agent.
This
mechanism could explain the ongoing chronic granulomatous
inflammation in many sarcoidosis patients.
Furthermore, blood
levels of SAA correlate with the disease stage and thus could serve
as a biomarker or therapeutic target.

## Genetics

Compelling studies of familial clustering and the incidence of sarcoidosis among different racial groups indicate that genetic factors influence susceptibility. In the ACCESS study, first-degree
relatives had a fivefold increased risk of developing the disease.
Associations have been found between the risk of sarcoidosis and
class I and II human leukocyte antigen (HLA) gene products,
which have essential roles in antigen presentation. It is considered
likely that a susceptibility locus for sarcoidosis exists within the
HLA gene region, as is the case with other autoimmune diseases
and cancers, such as Hodgkin’s lymphoma. An intriguing analysis
of data from the ACCESS study identified associations between
genetic factors (HLA alleles), environmental factors, and sarcoidosis phenotypes. A compelling argument can be made for a genetic
factor predisposing individuals to disease and a subsequent environmental exposure that triggers the onset of sarcoidosis per se.
Specifically, investigators found that HLA-DRB1*1101 and insecticide exposure at work are significantly associated with cardiac
sarcoidosis and hypercalcemia.
A similar relationship between
HLA-DRB1*1101, mold and musty odors, and pulmonary sarcoidosis was described.
Specific HLA alleles are protective or associated with increased
susceptibility or disease phenotypes. In Caucasian populations,
HLA-DRB1*01 and HLA-DRB1*04 were protective against the
disease, whereas HLA-DRB1*03, *11, *12, *14, and *15 were risk
factors for sarcoidosis.
Interestingly, HLA-DRB1*11 was found
to be related to the presentation of the mycobacterial antigens
ESAT-6 and mKatG, thus providing a possible mechanistic link
between genetic background and reaction to a pathogen.
Moreover, HLA-DRB1*03 was associated with spontaneous
resolution, whereas HLA-DRB1*14 and HLA-DRB1*15 were
associated with a chronic course of the disease.
In European
patients with Löfgren’s syndrome (LS), which is an acute form
of sarcoidosis characterized by bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy
(BHL), fever, erythema nodosum, and periarticular ankle inflammation, HLA-DRB1*03 is four times more common than in
healthy individuals.
It has been associated with a favorable prognosis. In one study, most DRB1*03
patients experienced resolution of LS within 2 years after diagnosis. By contrast, nearly half of
the patients without this allele had a chronic disease course.
The
mechanism underlying this difference in disease course remains
unknown.
Genetic studies of non-HLA genes have been inconclusive.
The two candidate genes most consistently associated with sarcoidosis are TNF and the interleukin (IL)-23 receptor (IL-23G).
Other possible candidate genes have produced inconsistent results.
The TNF G-308A polymorphism was found to be predictive of
response to treatment with TNF inhibitors.
Analysis of gene-expression profiles to distinguish between
tuberculosis and sarcoidosis has revealed similarities between the
two diseases; however, matrix metallopeptidase 14 could be identified as the most distinctive marker for sarcoidosis.
Ongoing
genome-wide association studies seek to identify additional genes
that may be linked to sarcoidosis onset and susceptibility. To date,
these studies have identified several novel gene candidates. In particular,
BTNL2, ANXA11, RAB23, OS9, CCDC88B, PRDX5,
and
NOTCH4
are potential loci for sarcoidosis susceptibility.
Most
of these genes play a role in the immune system, but their functional role has yet to be characterized.
More recently, epigenetic changes (such as DNA methylation)
that influence gene expression without changing the primary
nucleotide sequence have been identified as an essential mechanism
in many diseases, such as cancer, kidney disease, and autoimmune
diseases.
Post-translational modification of gene expression by
micro-RNAs (miRNAs) is different in sarcoidosis patients compared with controls.
In this study, an miRNA profile identified
by microarray analysis from patients and controls distinguished
sarcoidosis from controls with a positive predictive value of 88%.
In addition, the underexpression of specific miRNAs was associated with peripheral lymphopenia and airflow reductions, which
are predictors of worse outcomes. Taken together, it is likely that
sarcoidosis might be further subclassified in the future depending
on the genetic backgrounds and their respective associations with
distinct immunologic responses.

## Pathogenesis

Granuloma formation is a hallmark of sarcoidosis. The underlying immunologic events include (1) exposure to one or more
(unknown) antigens, (2) activation of antigen-presenting cells
(macrophages and dendritic cells), (3) a T cell response to eliminate the antigen, and (4) granuloma formation. Physiologically,
granulomas act as shields, protecting tissues from pathogens and
inflammatory reactions. Granulomas consist of a core of mononuclear phagocytes, such as epithelioid cells, multinucleated
giant cells, and macrophages, which are encased by lymphocytes,
including B cells, CD4
T cells, and CD8
T cells.
Granuloma formation occurs in stages: initiation, accumulation, effector phase, and resolution.
After antigen exposure,
macrophages detect antigens via pattern recognition receptors
(e.g., Toll-like receptors [TLRs]) and internalize and process these
antigens. In sarcoidosis, mainly TLR2 and TLR4 are involved.
They then present these antigen(s) to the T cell receptor via
HLA molecules. This step requires the presence of costimulatory
molecules (such as CD80, CD83, and CD86) to determine the
immune response (activation or inhibition). The
butyrophilin-like
2 (BTNL2)
gene may mediate one of these inhibitory signals.
Locally activated naïve CD4
Th cells differentiate into activated
CD4
Th1-like cells during the accumulation phase, causing subsequent elevations in Th1-associated inflammatory mediators
such as IL-2, interferon (IFN)-
α
, IFNγ
, monocyte chemotactic
protein-1, macrophage inflammatory protein-1, and granulocytemacrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF). CD4
Th cells
also interact with antigen-presenting cells to initiate the development of granulomas and to preserve granulomas.
Thus, T cells
undergo oligoclonal proliferation in areas of immune system
activity. At this point in the disease process, lymphocyte levels
are typically increased at the sites of inflammation, but peripheral
blood lymphopenia may occur due to sequestration.
The ratio of
CD4/CD8 cells becomes elevated in the lungs and other affected
organs. During the effector phase, T cells and macrophages release
mediators (IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, and TNF), which amplify the
Th1-driven inflammatory response.
Macrophage-derived cytokines contribute to the external signaling milieu that favors the differentiation of CD4
cells to Th1
cells.
Ultimately, a feedback loop from a downstream-cytokine cascade induces macrophages to differentiate into epithelioid
cells, which gain secretory capability, lose phagocytic capacity, and
fuse to form multinucleated giant cells.
These epithelioid cells
form the cellular basis of granulomas. Th2 cells also contribute to
granuloma formation. These cells synthesize fibronectin and C-C
motif ligand 18 (CCL18). During resolution, the release of these
mediators results in a positive feedback loop of CCL18-activated
and macrophage-mediated collagen formation. Although granulomas spontaneously resolve without causing damage in most
cases, this cycle leads to fibrosis in up to 25% of patients with
sarcoidosis.
As patients’ fibrosis becomes more extensive, their
prognosis worsens. Although the mechanisms responsible for
granulomatous fibrosis have not been fully characterized, patterns of cytokine change (upregulation of transforming growth
factor [TGF]-
β
) and Th2 cells may lead to an increased ratio of
CD8 to CD4 cells. Fewer than 5% of patients die of sarcoidosis;
fibrosis leading to respiratory failure contributes to many deaths.
In recent years, other cell populations, such as regulatory T cells
(Tregs) and Th17 cells that release IL-17A, have been detected to
be upregulated in individuals with sarcoidosis.
Interestingly,
nicotine treatment improves TLR responsiveness and normalizes
the numbers of Tregs in individuals with sarcoidosis,
supporting the anecdotal observation that the incidence of sarcoidosis is
diminished in smokers.
Overall, there appear to be two possible immunologic scenarios in sarcoidosis: an intense immune reaction in patients with
active disease, finally resulting in antigen clearance, or chronic
disease with less inflammation but the inability to eradicate the
inciting agent and subsequent chronic stimulation of the immune
response, resulting in organ damage such as lung fibrosis.
The
interplay between T cells, B cells, and macrophages in sarcoidosis is not fully understood, and research is hindered by the lack
of useful animal models mimicking all aspects present in patients
with sarcoidosis.

## Clinical Features

Sarcoidosis can affect every organ system, with the lungs being
the most commonly affected organ. Joint symptoms are common
in sarcoidosis, affecting up to 25% of patients.
The eyes (uveitis and retinal vasculitis), liver (abnormal liver function tests),
lymph nodes (enlargement), and skin (lupus pernio, papules,
nodules, plaques, and scar sarcoidosis [tattoos]) are the most frequent extrapulmonary organs affected (
Table 122.1
).
According to newer data, renal sarcoidosis may be more frequent than
previously reported when patients are specifically assessed for its
presence.
However, rheumatologists are more likely to be confronted with patients with systemic, atypical, primarily musculoskeletal manifestations or disease refractory to standard treatment.
Additional possible symptoms are sarcoidosis-associated fatigue
(present in 50% to 70% of patients)
; small-fiber neuropathy
(44%), which is challenging to diagnose and treat
; and cognitive
dysfunction.
Musculoskeletal involvement can present as osseous sarcoidosis (axial manifestation or sacroiliitis) in up to 13% of
patients
or sarcoid myopathy, with a muscle biopsy demonstrating granulomas in up to 8%. The latter, however, is only rarely
symptomatic, and a clinically overt myopathy is seen in only 1%
to 2% of patients.
Between 15% and 25% of patients with sarcoidosis may develop arthritis, the most frequently observed rheumatologic symptom of sarcoidosis. In patients with sarcoidosis,
arthritis can be acute or chronic; the acute form is most common. Chronic arthritis is typically associated with multisystem
sarcoidosis.

### Acute Sarcoid Arthritis

Febrile arthropathy is the most frequently observed joint involvement in sarcoidosis. Although any joint may be involved, the condition usually is symmetric, involving ankles, knees, wrists, and
elbows, and co-occurs with BHL and erythema nodosum (EN).
Of note, when assessed by ultrasound or MRI, ankle swelling in
LS is most often due to periarthritis or tenosynovitis rather than
true synovitis.

### Chronic Sarcoid Arthritis

The chronic form of sarcoid arthritis is less common than the acute
form, and in the United States, it affects more African Americans
than white people. It most often affects patients with systemic,
multiorgan involvement and typically manifests as oligoarthritis
or polyarthritis (
Fig. 122.1
). Sarcoid arthritis can have a varied
presentation. In some patients, sarcoid dactylitis may be the presenting symptom (
Fig. 122.2C and 2E

soft tissues surrounding affected fingers, erythematous skin, or
tenderness. Individuals with dactylitis also may have nail abnormalities, such as dystrophy. Therefore it is important to rule out
other conditions that affect the nails, such as psoriatic arthritis,
when making sarcoid arthritis diagnoses. The skin findings may
be an essential clue in these patients since skin manifestations of
psoriasis can usually be distinguished from cutaneous manifestations in sarcoidosis.
Other skeletal areas that may be affected by sarcoidosis include
nasal bones, pelvic girdle structures, ribs, and the skull. One retrospective case-control study of bone sarcoidosis showed that
patients with bone sarcoidosis had a higher incidence of liver,
spleen, and extrathoracic lymph node involvement.
The most
commonly affected site was the spine (68.8%), followed by the
pelvis (35.9%), and the hands (15.6%).

been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

cough or dyspnea) and organ dysfunction (e.g., abnormal pulmonary function tests) and not on the mere number of organs
affected (sometimes termed

may also be indicated to avoid continued high-dose glucocorticoid
therapy with its associated complications. For refractory cases,
TNF inhibitors can be used as third-line therapy. The two beststudied agents of this class of drugs are infliximab (INF) and adalimumab (ADA). INF has been studied in two placebo-controlled
trials and improved forced vital capacity (FVC) and dyspnea,
especially in patients with longer disease duration and more dyspnea at baseline.
ADA has shown some effectiveness in pulmonary sarcoidosis.
Etanercept has proven to be ineffective for
pulmonary and ocular sarcoidosis.

TNF inhibitor golimumab and ustekinumab, an antibody against

with refractory sarcoidosis in a small clinical trial,
but it has been

effective in singular cases.

kinase (JAK) inhibitors have been successfully used in cutaneous
and systemic sarcoidosis.
One of these agents, tofacitinib, a
pan-JAK inhibitor, resulted in marked improvement in skin sarcoidosis and stable pulmonary disease in one patient.
Furthermore, tofacitinib also improved internal organ manifestations by
suppressing type 1 immunity, including IFNγ
and IL-6, IL-12,
IL-15, and GM-CSF.
The therapeutic potential for novel members of the class with different JAK specificity is awaited.
A novel promising agent is efzofitimod, a 59-amino acid protein fused to the Fc region of human IgG1.
Efzofitimod’s amino
acid sequence corresponds to the extracellularly active N-terminal
region of the histidyl-tRNA-synthetase.
The binding partner of
this novel agent is neuropilin-2 (NRP-2), a cell surface receptor
whose expression is upregulated following inflammatory insults.
In a phase 1/2 clinical trial in pulmonary sarcoidosis, efzofitimod
had a good safety profile and showed promising results for various
secondary outcomes.
In addition, paradoxical reactions to biologic agents can occur,
including positivity for anti-nuclear antibodies, drug-induced
lupus erythematosus, demyelinating disorders, and sarcoid-like
granulomatosis.
A sarcoid-like reaction also has occurred
with ustekinumab,
suggesting that mechanisms other than
upregulation of interferonα
through TNF inhibition
might
play a role. A genetic predisposition (possibly TNF polymorphisms) is likely, but it is impossible to predict which patients will
experience this reaction. Therefore, a high index of suspicion is
mandatory when using this type of drug.

### Management of Sarcoid Arthritis

No randomized trials have been conducted that could guide
clinical decision-making. To guide clinicians in management,
we propose a treatment algorithm, shown in
Fig. 122.4
.
Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAIDs), MTX, and local
or low-dose systemic corticosteroids are our preferred firstline therapies. Alternatively, hydroxychloroquine and other
DMASDs may be used. Using information obtained from the
diagnostic evaluation, the clinician usually makes treatment
decisions after carefully considering the disease’s severity and
probable clinical course, as revealed by its radiographic progression, and arthritis alone will only rarely be the main driver for
treatment escalation.
The proposed algorithm requires regular patient visits to monitor disease activity, therapeutic efficacy, and tolerability. Responders continue to take first-line agents (usually prednisone or
prednisolone) until disease resolution or treatment failure. Before
nonresponders are prescribed second-line therapies, higher doses
of corticosteroids may be used, or patients may be switched to
methylprednisone, depending on tolerability and manifestations
of toxicity. For patients who require second-line medications, two
options are available: (1) MTX may be administered to MTXnaïve patients, or (2) non-MTX disease-modifying antisarcoid
drugs (e.g., HCQ, LEF, and AZA) as monotherapy or combinations thereof may be prescribed to patients who have inadequate
responses to first-line MTX.
For patients for whom this treatment approach fails, one can
consider an alternative third-line biologic therapy or an aggressive
course of systemic corticosteroids with careful toxicity monitoring.
Alternatively, participating in a clinical trial may be appropriate
for some patients.

### Vitamin D

Vitamin D metabolism is more complex in sarcoidosis than other
rheumatic diseases.
Patients with sarcoidosis might be more
sensitive to calcium and vitamin D substitution,
with the associated risks of hypercalcemia and hypercalciuria, which are present
in up to 10% of patients who do not receive vitamin D supplements. The mechanism usually attributed to hypercalcemia is
increased production of 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D (1,25-OH
D)
by epithelioid cells in the granuloma (1
α
-hydroxylase activity).
This risk must be balanced against the adverse effects of potential
chronic vitamin D deficiency, and patients often require treatment
for osteoporosis because of long-term glucocorticoid use. Bisphosphonates alone may be adequate to treat corticosteroid-induced
osteoporosis.
Patients who do not receive glucocorticoid
therapy do not seem to have a lower bone mineral density per se
because of sarcoidosis.
Recently, higher levels of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25-OHD) were associated with a higher frequency of
fractures in patients with sarcoidosis.
The authors found that
levels between 10 and 20 ng/mL were associated with the lowest fracture risk. Because of the disassociation between 25-OHD
and 1,25-OH
D in sarcoidosis, it seems reasonable to measure
both levels to ascertain which patients should receive vitamin D
supplements.

## Outcome and Prognosis

The overall mortality in sarcoidosis is low (about 5%),
and
many patients with spontaneous regression will never require
treatment. In people who need treatment, important differences
exist between African American and white patients. African American patients with sarcoidosis are known to experience a more
severe and systemic disease; mortality is 12 times higher in this
population.

## Conclusion

Much remains to be learned about sarcoidosis. The causes of this
disease remain unclear, and no medications have been approved
by the FDA for its treatment, except for prednisone and ACTH
gel, which was approved in the 1950s based on a few case reports.
Although increasing attention has been directed to the underlying mechanisms of granuloma formation and the cause (including
an infectious cause), full details of sarcoidosis immunopathogenesis have yet to be determined. Appropriate animal models and
candidate genes are needed to help advance our understanding of
this disease. Developing and evaluating an appropriate primary
outcome measure is required for clinical trial purposes to measure
responses in this remarkably heterogeneous disease.
We have presented an algorithm that can be used in treating
patients with an established diagnosis of sarcoidosis. However,
treatment approaches vary by institution and individual clinicians because of a myriad of conflicting studies published about
sarcoidosis management. The diversity of clinical presentations
and the lack of large, well-designed, randomized, controlled
clinical trials addressing the various clinical phenotypes make it
impossible to provide more specific treatment recommendations.
