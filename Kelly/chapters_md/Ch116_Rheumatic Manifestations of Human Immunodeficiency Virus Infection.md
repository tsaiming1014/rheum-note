## Introduction

In the years since acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS)
was initially described in 1981, human immunodeficiency virus
(HIV) has become one of the leading global health crises. According to new data in UNAIDS Global AIDS Update 2022, trends
in HIV infections and AIDS-related deaths are driven by the
availability of HIV services.
In 2021 new infections fell globally
at only 3.6% compared with 2020—the smallest annual reduction since 2016—demonstrating that global progress is slowing
rather than accelerating. The rates of new HIV infections are still
occurring at alarming rates in certain regions such as southern
Africa, eastern Europe, and central and eastern Asia. An estimated
38.4 million people worldwide are living with HIV (
Fig. 116.1
).
Approximately 1.5 million people became newly infected with
HIV in 2021, and 650,000 people died. Worldwide, there were
0.84 males per female living with HIV in 2019, although the
rate of new infections and deaths from HIV were approximately
the same in both genders. One encouraging note was that the
incidence of new cases and death fell more significantly in SubSaharan Africa (with a 28%–52% decrease in incident cases and a
39%–66% decrease in deaths), although the incidence remained
stable or increased, whereas deaths generally decreased, elsewhere.
Progress in dealing with the HIV epidemic, including in education and in public health awareness, has undoubtedly influenced
the decrease in new infections seen among young people in some
countries in recent years. While availability of newer treatment
strategies and better access to health care has resulted in increased
life expectancy, HIV-1 infection will be managed increasingly
as a chronic illness, and complications, such as musculoskeletal
and rheumatic conditions associated with HIV infection and its
treatment and illnesses associated with the later years of life, are
expected to increase (
Tables 116.1 and 116.2
).
Among the rheumatologic disorders, clinicians face the challenge of treating potentially disabling inflammatory disorders with
immunosuppressive therapy in the face of ongoing viral-induced
immunocompromise. Diagnosing infection is especially important in an immunocompromised patient because the probability
of a musculoskeletal complication of an opportunistic infection
increases with advancing stages of the patient’s HIV disease. At
early stages (CD4
count >200/
μ
L), opportunistic infections are
unlikely, although bacterial infections (especially tuberculosis) can
still occur. Immunosuppressive drugs should be used with caution
in this population.

### Painful Articular Syndrome

Painful articular syndrome is self-limited and lasts less than 24
hours, is associated with few objective clinical findings, and is
characterized by severe bone and joint pain.
It occurs predominantly in the late stages of HIV infection. Its cause is unknown,
and synovitis has not been described in these patients. The knee
is most commonly affected, but elbows and shoulders can also
be involved. Radiographic features are nonspecific; occasionally,
periarticular osteopenia is seen. Treatment is symptomatic. Given
the abrupt nature of this disorder’s onset, these patients are more
likely to be encountered in emergency departments and not in
HIV clinics.

HIV-1 infection is pandemic. In the Congo, where the sero

anti-retroviral treatment.

PsA were infrequently reported in Sub-Saharan Africa before the

in West Africa and 2.8% to 3.5% in South Africa.

this has increased significantly in the HIV-1 era, with more than

### Undifferentiated Spondyloarthritis

Symptoms of reactive arthritis or PsA such as enthesopathy (plantar fasciitis, Achilles tendinitis) are observed in patients in whom
full-blown disease does not otherwise develop. Treatment is symptomatic (e.g., NSAIDs, intralesional corticosteroid injections),
although sulfasalazine should be considered in patients with more
extensive disease.

### Osteoarthritis

With patients living longer and with the increased frequency of
metabolic syndrome, the prevalence of osteoarthritis (OA), particularly of the hip and knee, is rising among patients living with
HIV.
Risk factors for knee OA in the setting of HIV infection
are similar to the non-HIV population and have been described as
including female gender, obesity, and Black or African American
B

race.
Treatment is similar as in non-HIV patients, including
physical therapy, intraarticular corticosteroid injections, and
NSAIDs.
Reduced levels of ghrelin has been associated with
knee OA in HIV-positive individuals.

### Avascular Necrosis of Bone

Case-control studies have shown an association of osteonecrosis after the introduction of highly active anti-retroviral
therapy (HAART), especially in those who have received corticosteroids.
A meta-analysis revealed more than two-fold
increased odds of avascular necrosis among HIV-infected patients
exposed to protease inhibitors
; however, this association has
recently been disputed in a large European cohort of 11,820
individuals followed 86,118 person-years.
Other contributing
factors include dyslipidemia (associated with protease inhibitor
usage), alcohol abuse, use of megestrol acetate,
the presence
of antiphospholipid antibodies,
intravenous drug abuse,
and HIV-1 itself.
The most common presenting symptom of
osteonecrosis is pain on weight bearing and activity, most commonly in the hips. Some patients may be asymptomatic, and the
diagnosis may be based on incidental findings from radiologic
studies. Most patients tend to present when subchondral collapse
already has occurred. Radiographs, computed tomography (CT),
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and nuclear medicine studies
have been used successfully to diagnose osteonecrosis, as in HIVnegative patients.

### Hypertrophic Pulmonary Osteoarthropathy

Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy (HPO) affects bones,
joints, and soft tissues and can develop in HIV-infected patients
with
Pneumocystis jiroveci
pneumonia. It is characterized by severe
pain in the lower extremities; digital clubbing; arthralgia; nonpitting edema; and periarticular soft tissue involvement of the ankles,
knees, and elbows. The skin over the affected areas is glistening,
edematous, and warm. Radiography reveals extensive periosteal
reaction and subperiosteal proliferative changes in the long bones
of the lower extremities. A bone scan may show increased uptake
along the cortical surfaces. In addition, treatment with voriconazole for chronic fungal infections seen in patients with advanced

HIV infection can be complicated by periostitis. When present,
treatment of
P. jiroveci
pneumonia usually alleviates symptoms of
HPO.

### Osteopenia and Osteoporosis

Osteopenia and osteoporosis occur more than three times as
commonly in HIV-infected patients regardless of anti-retroviral
treatment
and can result in pathologic fractures.
One metaanalysis found 15% of HIV-positive patients to have osteoporosis and 52% to have osteopenia.
Risk factors for osteopenia
include use of protease inhibitors, advanced stage of HIV infection, high viral load, low body mass, low bicarbonate levels,
increased alkaline phosphatase levels, and especially, smoking
and vitamin D deficiency.
Clinicians should have a low
threshold to screen for vitamin D deficiency, should provide adequate repletion as necessary, and (if not done already for many
other reasons) counsel against smoking. Bisphosphonates and,
in patients with HIV wasting syndrome, testosterone have been
used to maintain bone density.
Of particular note is a recent
study that showed that bone mineral density in 481 Asians with
well-controlled HIV infection did not differ from those without HIV infection, suggesting that effective control may prevent
premature bone loss.

## HIV-Associated Muscle Disease

Muscle involvement in HIV infection varies from uncomplicated myalgias or asymptomatic creatine kinase (CK) elevation
to severe, disabling, HIV-associated polymyositis or pyomyositis (
Table 116.4
). HIV-1 seroconversion can also coincide with
myoglobinuria and acute myalgia, suggesting that myotropism for
HIV may be present early in the infection.

### Myalgia and Fibromyalgia

One-third of HIV-positive outpatients may report myalgias,
and 11% were found to have fibromyalgia in one old series and
the same in a more recent cohort.
Fibromyalgia is associated
with longer disease duration and a history of depression. Treatment is similar to that for fibromyalgia in the non-HIV setting,
including anti-depressants, exercise, and continued reassurance
and follow-up.

### Non-inflammatory Necrotizing Myopathy and HIV-Related Wasting Syndrome

Severe wasting from chronic infection, malignancy, malabsorption, and nutritional deficiency often accounts for weakness and
disability in patients with AIDS. This wasting leads to loss of muscle mass. Cachexia and muscle wasting associated with HIV constitute “slim disease” in Africa.
A non-inflammatory necrotizing
myopathy of unclear pathogenesis has been described, accounting
for 42% of patients diagnosed with myopathy.
Even in patients
without significant wasting, muscle biopsy specimens have shown
diffuse atrophy, mild neurogenic atrophy, or thick filament loss
without conspicuous inflammation. Whether this condition is
immune mediated, as some have suggested,
or whether it is the
result of metabolic or nutritional factors remains unclear. Corticosteroids restore muscle strength and mass.

### Nemaline Myopathy

Nemaline myopathy is a rare disorder that has been described
in some HIV-positive patients in addition to its occurrence as a
congenital disorder. Nemaline myopathy represents a nonspecific
myofibril alteration resulting from Z band disruption.
Musclebiopsy specimens disclose prominent, randomly distributed, atrophic type 1 fibers with numerous intracytoplasmic rod bodies
in the centers of the fibers, corresponding to nemaline rods on
electron microscopy. Necrotic fibers and inflammatory infiltrates
are usually not found. Some patients have been described to have
associated monoclonal gammopathy.
Although no inflammation is noted, corticosteroids may be useful. In addition, two cases
of successful treatment with intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)
have been reported.

course of HIV infection and may be the presenting feature. In one
large series of HIV-positive outpatients from a county clinic in

In another recent series

with an expected frequency of 3.23 per 100,000.

### Inclusion Body Myositis

Inclusion body myositis (IBM) has been recognized as a complication of HIV infection.
This condition is clinically, histologically, and immunologically identical to sporadic IBM. Muscle
biopsy specimens suggest two concurrently ongoing processes:
an autoimmune process mediated by cytotoxic T cells and a
degenerative process manifested by vacuolated muscle fibers
and deposits of amyloid-related proteins. Of particular interest
has been the finding of elevated mRNA levels and constitutive
expression of Toll-like receptor 3, which is known to mediate
inflammatory stimuli from pathogens and endogenous danger signals. Another is to link the innate and adaptive immune
systems in muscle fibers of patients with HIV-associated IBM
in close proximity with infiltrating mononuclear cells.
One
review of four cases of HIV-associated IBM found that involved
CD8-positive T cells surrounding muscle fibers were virus specific and may cross-react with antigens on the surface of muscle
fibers, suggesting that HIV may trigger a viral-specific inflammatory response that can lead to IBM.
Another longitudinal
study, which included patients with and without HIV infection
and myositis, showed that those with HIV-associated polymyositis may present initially with features characteristic of typical
polymyositis. However, in time nearly all eventually manifested
symptoms more consistent with IBM, including hand and wrist
flexor weakness, rimed vacuoles on biopsy, and anti-NT5C1A
antibodies.
This was not seen in a more recent study where
only 50% of patients with HIV-associated myositis developed
IBM over time.

### Myopathy Associated With Treatment

The three anti-retroviral treatment (ART) medications zidovudine, didanosine, and raltegravir are very well known to cause
myopathy.
Didanosine and zidovudine are in the family of
nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs), and raltegravir is an integrase inhibitor. It is thought that the pathophysiology of the myopathy is dose related due to a mitochondrial
dystrophy causing depletion of mitochondrial DNA. The mechanism is postulated to be multifactorial by inhibition of the bioenergetic machinery of the mitochondria, inhibition of DNA
polymerase gamma, depletion of L-carnitine, and oxidative stress
from zidovudine. Symptoms of myopathy include proximal
muscle weakness, muscle tenderness, and myalgias. Should any
of these symptoms occur while on these medications, they should
be stopped for 4 weeks. If clinical improvement is not seen within
the 4 weeks of cessation, electromyography and muscle biopsies
should be performed next to further assess other etiologies of
myopathy.
Increasingly the integrase inhibitor raltegravir is being added to
ART regimens because of the limited side effect profile, albeit with
an increased incidence of myalgias reported. This is, however, not
associated with elevated CK levels nor is it dose- or time-dependent.
We suggest medication cessation and change to other ART
should myalgias develop while on raltegravir.

### Rhabdomyolysis

Rhabdomyolysis can occur at all stages of HIV-1 infection and
may be separated into three groups: (1) HIV-associated rhabdomyolysis, including rhabdomyolysis in primary HIV infection
and recurrent or isolated rhabdomyolysis, (2) drug-induced rhabdomyolysis, and (3) rhabdomyolysis at the end stage of AIDS,
possibly associated with opportunistic infections of muscle. Drugs
implicated in rhabdomyolysis in HIV-positive patients include
didanosine, lamivudine, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, ritonavir, indinavir, and raltegravir
and, in conjunction with statin
use,
Genvoya
(elvitegravir/cobicistat/emtricitabine/tenofovir

## Vasculitis Associated With HIV Infection

A wide spectrum of vasculitis has been described in patients with
HIV-1 infection.
Fever, malaise, weakness, rash, headaches,
and neurologic symptoms are common in HIV-positive patients
and triggers of vasculitis range from specific infectious agents and
drugs to idiopathic causes. Among infectious causes, cytomegalovirus and tuberculosis are probably the most common.
One series found 34 (23%) of 148 “symptomatic” HIV-positive patients to have vasculitis.
Of these patients, 11 met American College of Rheumatology criteria for a distinct category of
vasculitis, including hypersensitivity vasculitis in six, polyarteritis
nodosa in four, and Henoch-Schönlein purpura in one. Another
series of 98 Chinese patients found that 20% had vasculitis,
including 15 with Behçet’s-like disease, two cases of HenochSchönlein purpura, two cases of digital gangrene, and one case of
central nervous system (CNS) vasculitis.
Granulomatosis with
polyangiitis and pulmonary microscopic polyangiitis can occur in
patients with high CD4
counts and during immune reconstitution (see below). Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis
(Churg-Strauss syndrome) has also been described.
Behçet’s disease and relapsing polychondritis occur in HIV infection
and
were reported as improving with HAART.
Rapidly progressive focal necrotizing vasculitis of the aorta and
large arteries with occlusion and occasionally aneurysm formation
and rupture have been described in Africans with HIV infection.
In one recent study, 90% of HIV-positive patients, but no HIVnegative patients, had evidence of adventitial leukocytoclastic
vasculitis of the vasa vasorum of the femoral arteries.
Giant cell
arteritis likewise has been described in patients with HIV infection with aortic root dilation as well as Takayasu arteritis.
Kawasaki’s disease has been reported in HIV-positive children and
adults.
Cryoglobulinemic vasculitis with associated lymphocytic
interstitial pneumonia occurs with and without hepatitis C coinfection. Patients with isolated CNS angiitis are usually seen with
organic brain syndromes and neurologic deficits.
In children
and in one case report in an adult, HIV-associated cerebral aneurysmal arteriopathy was described as causing multiple fusiform
aneurysms in the circle of Willis.
CNS vasculitis may manifest
as recurrent strokes. Although imaging studies (MRI, angiography) may be helpful, brain biopsy may be necessary to establish
the diagnosis. Necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis not limited to
the CNS has been reported in patients with low CD4 counts that
respond to ART.
Diagnosis is based on a high degree of suspicion and on angiography and/or biopsy of specific organ beds. Similar to immunocompetent patients, perinuclear anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic
antigen (p-ANCA) and cytoplasmic ANCA (c-ANCA) may be
useful with granulomatosis with polyangiitis or microscopic polyangiitis. However, biopsy with cultures is important to rule out
infectious mimics.
Corticosteroids are the mainstay of treatment for HIV-associated vasculitis, although cytotoxic agents such as cyclophosphamide, IVIG, and plasmapheresis have been used in refractory
cases. Painful neuropathy secondary to vasculitis responds well to
high-dose glucocorticoids, in contrast to HIV-associated peripheral neuropathy.

## Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura

Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura is a thrombotic microangiopathy associated with thrombocytopenia and hemolytic
anemias (accompanied by red cell fragments on peripheral blood
smear), which is triggered by an excess of large von Willebrand
factor secreted by the endothelium that results in vascular occlusion and critical organ ischemia, with a high mortality.
It
occurs much more commonly in the setting of HIV infection
and is caused by marked reduction of the metalloprotease enzyme,
a-disintegrin-and-metalloproteinase-with-thrombospondin-motifs 13 (ADAMTS13) whose role is to cleave large multimers of
von Willebrand factor into smaller monomers. Plasma infusion
and plasma exchange are the key components of the treatment,
but the overall mortality is around 40%.

## Primary Pulmonary Hypertension

Pulmonary hypertension is a severe life-limiting disease that
often affects younger patients. Patients with AIDS and primary
pulmonary hypertension present with a higher degree of pulmonary hypertension than non-AIDS patients.
The predominant histopathologic finding has been a plexogenic pulmonary
arteriopathy, although thromboembolic changes have also been
reported. Symptoms include progressive shortness of breath, pedal
edema, nonproductive cough, fatigue, syncope or near-syncope,
and chest pain. Pulmonary function tests show mild restrictive
patterns with variably reduced diffusing capacities. Older studies
suggested a very high mortality; however, with improving treatment of pulmonary hypertension, the prognosis has improved
considerably. Treatment of HIV-associated pulmonary hypertension is similar to that in the non-HIV setting and includes

anti-coagulation, calcium channel blockers, prostanoids, endothelin receptor antagonists, and phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors,
which have reduced the otherwise high mortality associated with
this disease.

## HIV-Associated Musculoskeletal Infection

### Pyomyositis

Pyomyositis is a primary infection of skeletal muscle that does not
arise from contiguous infection; it is presumably hematogenous
in origin and is often associated with abscess formation. Rarely
seen in developed countries, infectious myositis nonetheless is an
important complication of HIV infection in areas most endemic
for HIV, such as Africa and India,
although recent data have
shown pyomyositis to be significantly associated with HIV infection in the United States.
It tends to occur in later stages of the
infection, with CD4 counts less than 200/
μ
L.
Staphylococcus aureus
is the most common pathogen.
Other organisms that have been
implicated include
Streptococcus pyogenes
,
Cryptococcus neoformans
,
Mycobacterium tuberculosis
,
Mycobacterium avium-intra-cellulare
,
Nocardia asteroides
,
Salmonella enteritidis
,
Escherichia coli
,
Citrobacter freundii
,
Morganella morganii
,
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
, and
group A streptococci. Recently microsporidial species have also
been reported as causing infectious myositis in HIV-1-infected
patients with advanced immunosuppression.
The clinical course of pyomyositis can be roughly divided into
three stages: invasive, suppurative, and late. The first stage, which
typically lasts 1 to 3 weeks, is characterized by localized cramps
and induration in conjunction with a low-grade fever. Large muscle groups, particularly those of the lower extremities, are most
often affected. The degrees of pain and fever increase in the second
stage, which is characterized further by the development of edema
and purulence in the affected muscle. Untreated, the disease progresses to the third stage; within 3 weeks of onset, sepsis and death
can occur.
The mortality rate associated with pyomyositis has
been estimated to range from 1% to 20%. Ultrasound and MRI
with contrast enhancement are effective in localizing infection,
although sometimes tagged white blood cell scans may be needed.
Oral and intravenous antibiotics in conjunction with surgical
drainage are often required.

### Bacterial Arthritis and Osteomyelitis

Patients become more susceptible to bacterial infections of the
bones and joints with advanced HIV infections.
S. aureus
is the
most common infectious agent encountered, but parenteral drug
use and not HIV infection per se may account for this.
Osteomyelitis is associated with mortality rates of greater than 20% in
HIV-infected patients. The most frequently involved bones are the
wrist, tibia, femoral heads, and thoracic cage, but other rare sites,
such as the patella and the mandible, have been reported.
Due to the fear of perioperative complications due to immunosuppression from HIV, some orthopedic surgeons are reluctant
to perform joint replacement in HIV-infected individuals. In a
nationwide database survey of HIV-infected patients undergoing
total hip arthroplasty, there was only a slight increase in post-operative complications and length of hospital stay compared with
non-HIV-infected individuals.
In another study of patients
with and without HIV infection undergoing total knee replacement, patients with HIV infection were at an increased risk for
perioperative wound infections and a slight increase in average
length of hospital stay, although they were not at an increased risk
for overall complications.

### Musculoskeletal Tuberculosis

Musculoskeletal involvement, the fourth most common extrapulmonary manifestation of tuberculosis, is found in approximately
1% to 5% of patients with tuberculosis. It can mimic many skeletal diseases and can manifest in various locations. Less than 50%
of reported patients with musculoskeletal tuberculosis have radiographic evidence of pulmonary tuberculosis.
M. tuberculosis
disseminates hematogenously after an acute or reactivated pulmonary
infection. Usually, skeletal tuberculosis lesions in immunocompetent patients are solitary, but in AIDS patients, they may have a
multicentric distribution in approximately 30%.
The vertebrae
are the most common site involved, mostly the lower thoracic or
upper lumbar segments. The frequency of tuberculous spondylitis
is 50% to 66%; peripheral arthritis, 20% to 30%; osteomyelitis,
10% to 20%; and tenosynovitis and bursitis, approximately 1%
to 3%. Treatment includes four-drug anti-tubercular therapy and,
often, surgical intervention.

### Bacillary Angiomatosis Osteomyelitis

Bacillary angiomatosis is a multisystem infectious disease caused
by two closely related organisms:
Bartonella henselae
and
Bartonella
quintana.
It seems to be a disease unique to HIV-infected patients
and, to a lesser degree, to other immunocompromised patients.
Clinical characteristics associated with bacillary angiomatosis and
bacillary peliosis in patients with HIV infection include fever (temperature, >100° F [37.8° C]; 93%), cutaneous or subcutaneous
vascular lesions, lymphadenopathy, osteomyelitis, and abdominal
symptoms associated with profound depression of CD4 lymphocyte counts, anemia, and an elevated serum level of alkaline phosphatase.
Osteomyelitis is found in approximately one-third
of patients in association with skin disease. These lesions are usually
characterized by extensive destruction of the cortical bone, periostitis, medullary invasion, and an overlying soft-tissue mass that might
resemble cellulitis. Complete remission of bacillary angiomatosis
after doxycycline or erythromycin therapy occurs, although bone
lesions may need surgical drainage.

## HAART-Related Immune Reconstitution Syndrome

The immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS) is an
interesting paradox that occurs when the immune system restores
itself after ART has been successfully started. It causes a rapidly
declining clinical status despite increasing absolute CD4 counts
and decreasing viral loads of HIV-1.
It is thought that
IRIS is due to dysregulation that occurs in the setting of rising
CD4 T cells that are highly specific for a certain antigen or from
the hyperresponsiveness of the innate immune system to helper
T-cells.
There is an inverse correlation between the absolute
CD4 cell count and the probability of developing IRIS.
Although IRIS primarily occurs in the setting of infectious
complications of HIV infection, autoinflammation leading to
the development of autoimmune diseases can also occur. Diseases
such as cerebral CD8 lymphocytosis, Guillain-Barré syndrome,
alopecia universalis, and terminal ileitis as well as RA, polymyositis, SLE, autoimmune hepatitis, adult-onset Still’s disease, and
sarcoidosis have been shown to appear de novo after ART has
been initiated. There are criteria that should be used to diagnose IRIS.
One should have the diagnosis of AIDS, currently
be on treatment with anti-retrovirals that result in an increase
in CD4 cell count and decreased HIV viral load, exaggerated
response to infection, and inflammatory symptoms appearing
during therapy that cannot be explained by another cause. A
meta-analysis found that the risk of IRIS is greatest in patients
with a CD4 cell count of less than 50 cells/
μ
L prior to ART
initiation.
Therefore, it is best if ART is administered before
immunodeficiency is advanced. A recent clinical trial suggested
that JAK inhibitors may be effective in preventing inflammatory
complications of ART.

## Laboratory Abnormalities Associated With HIV Infection

Humoral immunologic abnormalities are frequent in patients with
HIV but are rarely associated with severe clinical signs. The most
common laboratory abnormality is polyclonal hyperglobulinemia,
found in 45% of HIV-positive individuals.
RF and anti-nuclear
antibodies, usually in low titer, have been described in 17% of
patients with HIV infection in some series,
although antidouble-stranded DNA antibodies and hypocomplementemia are
rare. Increased titers of ACPA and RF occur in advanced HIV
infection. Although more specific than RF, before immune reconstitution, ACPA is an unreliable diagnostic marker for RA and
does not necessarily predict future RA.
In older series, IgG
anti-cardiolipin antibodies are found in 95% of untreated patients
with AIDS, particularly in patients with advanced disease, and
overall in 20% to 30% of HIV-positive individuals.
They were
rarely associated with thrombotic events. However, in more recent
series in the era of HAART, such high frequencies are no longer
seen and, when anti-cardiolipin antibodies are present, they are
more likely to be associated with thrombosis.
C-ANCAs and
p-ANCAs have been described in the serum of HIV-positive individuals, as have anti–glomerular basement membrane antibodies.
Cryoglobulinemia is decreasing in this population since
the introduction of HAART.
Indeed, with HAART, many of
these serologic abnormalities tend to decrease or resolve.

## Conclusion

The impact of the global HIV pandemic continues to grow, and
rheumatologists need to be aware of the wide spectrum of rheumatic diseases that occur in HIV-positive patients. HAART has
changed the natural history of HIV infection. It has modified the
frequency and expression of some HIV-related clinical syndromes
and has been associated directly (toxicity) and indirectly (immune
reconstitution) with the development of new ones. With longer
survival and newer refinements in treatment, the spectrum of
rheumatic disease seen in HIV-positive patients is very much a
moving target for rheumatologists and is likely to continue to
evolve.
