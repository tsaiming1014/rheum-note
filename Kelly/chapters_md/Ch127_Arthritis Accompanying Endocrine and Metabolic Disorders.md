## Introduction

Rheumatic manifestations are often the initial presentation of systemic illnesses. Each endocrine disorder has its own set of arthritic
symptoms that can mimic or present as definitive rheumatic diatheses, requiring a high degree of vigilance and a thorough assessment of patients presenting with such varied symptoms. Both
rheumatologists and general internists should be well versed in
identifying the manner in which components of the musculoskeletal system are affected by diseases of the endocrine system. Additionally, recognition that endocrinopathies can arise in the course
of treating rheumatic illnesses, such as with the use of corticosteroids, is critical in minimizing or reversing any potential adverse
sequelae in these patients. This chapter will describe the common
musculoskeletal manifestations of endocrine disease encountered
in the clinical setting.

## Adrenal Gland Disorders

In 1932 Harvey Cushing described several rheumatic conditions,
including osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, myopathy, and synovitis, later collectively termed
Cushing’s syndrome
(CS). Primary CS
is usually related to excessive endogenous production of cortisol
and is either adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)–dependent
(pituitary adenomas) or ACTH-independent (adrenal tumor or
adrenal hyperplasia), whereas exogenous CS is secondary to excessive exposure to exogenous glucocorticoids.
Steroid excess increases muscle protein catabolism and inhibits analog signaling, leading to atrophy, especially of type IIa
muscle fibers.
Myopathy presents as extreme muscle weakness
and pain that is most often pronounced around the pelvic girdle
and is reported in 70% of patients with active primary CS.
This
muscle weakness and pain may either occur suddenly or be insidious in onset. Muscle performance such as hand grip strength and
performance on the chair rising test is impaired in patients with
active CS.
Although it is generally agreed that weakness gradually
improves as corticosteroids are discontinued or with biochemical
control of disease, some recent studies using body composition
analysis with MRI and dual-energy x-ray absorptiometry (DXA)
in patients with primary CS showed persistence of lower limb
skeletal muscle mass decline even up to 13 years after achieving
clinical remission.
Avascular necrosis generally occurs after prolonged use of highdose corticosteroids but can be observed months or even years
after the discontinuation of corticosteroids.
Less commonly, it
can be associated with short courses of therapy or intermittent
high-dose intravenous therapy; consequently, the risk for avascular
necrosis should always be considered during clinical assessment.
Although rare, case reports of avascular necrosis in patients with
endogenous CS do exist.
Corticosteroids have inhibitory effects on replication and differentiation of osteoblasts, as well as intestinal and renal calcium
reabsorption. Abnormalities of bone status are commonly seen
in patients with CS, with osteopenia occurring in 40% to 78%
of patients, osteoporosis occurring in 22% to 57%, and fractures occurring in 11% to 76%. The prevalence of osteoporosis is
higher in individuals with adrenal CS than in those with pituitary
CS.
Male patients have a higher prevalence of osteoporosis and

vertebral fractures compared with females.
Several studies show
improvement of bone mineral density (BMD) after biochemical
remission, either through surgical or medical management; however, time to improvement is variable. Several studies show persistently low BMD and altered bone metabolic markers up to several
years after achieving clinical remission.

## Acromegaly

### Clinical Features and Pathogenesis

Acromegaly is caused by hypersecretion of growth hormone (GH),
usually as a result of a GH-secreting pituitary tumor. Other causes,
such as increased GH-releasing hormone (GHRH) production from
hypothalamic tumors, ectopic GHRH production, and ectopic GH
secretion from nonendocrine tumors, are rare. Prevalence has been
reported between 2.8 and 13.7 cases per 100,000 with an incidence
range between 0.2 and 1.1 cases per 100,000 per year. Acromegaly
occurs most commonly in middle-aged men and women, and there
is a median diagnostic delay of 4.5 to 5 years.
Hypersecretion of
GH and insulin-like growth factor (IGF)-1, its primary mediator,
have both somatic and metabolic effects.
GH and IGF-1 are important regulators of bone and cartilage
homeostasis and have a central role in development of bone growth
and bone mass. Musculoskeletal symptoms, including acromegalic arthropathy, are common complications of acromegaly and
affect both weight-bearing and non–weight-bearing joints.
At
the time of diagnosis, arthropathy is one of the presenting symptoms in up to 50% to 70% of patients.
Between 60% and 70%
of patients experience involvement of large peripheral joints (i.e.,
the shoulder, knee, and hip). Back pain may be a particularly
troubling feature of acromegaly. Approximately 50% of patients
have axial arthropathy, mainly affecting the lumbar area.
Carpal
tunnel syndrome (CTS) occurs in approximately 60% of patients
with acromegaly and is frequently bilateral.
GH secreted by the anterior pituitary acts on hepatocytes to
secrete somatomedins, which have stimulatory effects on bone and
cartilage, including chondrocyte replication, synthesis of proteoglycans, collagen, osteoblastic proliferation, and increased bone collagen
(resulting in increased bone mass).
Somatomedins also have insulin-like activity on muscle and fat. There are probably two phases in
the pathogenesis of acromegalic arthropathy: an early and potentially
reversible phase in which elevated GH and IGF-1 levels induce cartilage hypertrophy and laxity of periarticular ligaments and a late irreversible phase where GH excess persists, leading to degenerative joint
disease with scarring, cysts, and osteophyte formation.
Compared
with patients with primary osteoarthritis (OA), MRI studies have
shown that cartilage hypertrophy and composition differ significantly
in patients with acromegalic arthropathy.
Arthropathy symptoms are significantly incapacitating and
lead to a lower quality of life.
Symptoms are chronic, and
current evidence suggests that they can be persistent even after
biochemical control of disease, with more than 70% of patients
having progressive osteophytosis or joint space narrowing despite
successful treatment.

### Bone Mass and Quality in Acromegaly

GH stimulates, either directly or indirectly through IGF-1, the
proliferation of cells of the osteoblastic lineage and the differentiated function of mature osteoblast. It also stimulates the carboxylation of osteocalcin and the production of osteoprotegerin.
GH hypersecretion has different effects on cortical and trabecular bone; elevated levels have a deleterious effect on the former,
whereas bone density tends to be increased in the latter because
of periosteal ossification. These differences, associated with variations in DXA measurements because of bone enlargement in acromegaly patients, are a possible explanation as to why patients with
acromegaly experience fractures even in the presence of normal
BMD.
The increased risk of vertebral fractures in acromegaly has been
confirmed by several studies, which describe its association with
hypogonadism, higher IGF-1 levels, and longer duration of active
disease. Incident vertebral fractures occurred in 42% of patients
followed up for 3 years during the active phase of disease; however,
progression of fractures was observed in up to 20% of patients
with biochemical control of disease.

## Hypothalamic–Gonadal Axis

Many autoimmune disorders are more prevalent in women.
Female-to-male ratios differ among connective tissue diseases. In
rheumatoid arthritis (RA), the incidence is higher in post-menopausal as opposed to pre-menopausal women. Adult men have a
significant delayed onset of disease.
These findings may be due to
gonadal hormones offering protection against RA in younger men
(as a result of testosterone) and a predisposition to systemic lupus
erythematosus (SLE) in women (as a result of estrogens).
Both sex hormones and genes expressed on sexual chromosomes have been proposed as potential explanations for sex- and
age-related effects on musculoskeletal disease presentations. This
is exemplified in Klinefelter’s patients, who have two XX chromosomes (XXY karyotype), reduced levels of androgens, and an
increased risk for the development of autoimmune conditions.
Besides their role in determining biologic sex, androgens also have
specific effects on the immune system related to suppression of
immune reactivity and inflammation, increasing the threshold
for the development of autoimmunity.
One prospective study
reported a higher incidence of autoimmune disease in men with
hypogonadism, whereas other studies have demonstrated low
serum androgen levels in autoimmune disorders.

### Androgens in Rheumatoid Arthritis

In a longitudinal study of the levels of total testosterone, sex hormone–binding globulin, and luteinizing hormone (LH) in 41
males with early RA (naïve to disease-modifying anti-rheumatic
agents [DMARDs] at study entry) over 2 years,
it was only in
male patients younger than 50 years that lower baseline levels of
testosterone were found compared with control subjects. This has
been documented in other studies, in which it was also noted that
men who experience a dramatic loss of serum androgens with age
may develop a more aggressive form of RA.
The possibility
of a causative effect between hypogonadism and the development
of RA is contradicted, however, by studies that demonstrate that
serum androgen levels are not lower before the onset of RA in
all patients.
The notion of low serum androgen levels as a
consequence of RA is supported by increased levels of testosterone with response to treatment and decreased disease activity, as
well as the inverse correlation with inflammatory markers.
The role of androgens in RA susceptibility in women is less well
understood, with some studies showing a protective effect and
others suggesting that androgens may worsen disease severity.
Women predisposed to higher androgen concentrations because of

a polymorphism in the
CYB5A
gene are protected from developing
seropositive RA.
Lower androgen levels have occurred in women
with RA refractory to treatment; however, patients with higher
serum androgen concentrations and a low number of CAG repeats
in androgen receptors developed more aggressive RA.
Low
serum concentrations of androgens have occurred in women with
RA, but no significant abnormalities have been noted before disease onset.
Further prospective studies in the large emerging preRA cohorts will be informative of risk in both males and females.

### Ligamentous Injuries and Estrogen

Estrogen fulfills an important role in the development, maturation,
and aging of bone, muscle, and connective tissue.
Increased risk
of musculoskeletal injury, accelerated bone wasting, and accelerated muscle wasting are well characterized in menopause.
In general, anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) ruptures occur
two to eight times more often in female athletes than their male
counterparts. One explanation may be the increase in knee laxity during pre-ovulatory and ovulatory phases of the menstrual
cycle,
because knee laxity is directly related to estrogen concentrations.
Retrospective studies have shown that patients
with ACL injuries were less likely to be on oral contraceptives.
Interestingly, women suffer fewer muscle injuries than men;
this is thought to be related to decreased muscle stiffness. Further research is needed regarding the possibility and timing of
interventions.

### Shoulder

Frozen shoulder, or adhesive capsulitis (AC), is frequently bilateral in patients with diabetes and is associated with severe pain
and stiffness. Incidence has been reported in 10% to 20% and
3% to 32% of patients with type 1 and 2 DM, respectively, with
a prevalence of 11% to 19% and 2% to 3% in DM versus agematched non-DM patients. A recent longitudinal study showed
an increased risk for the development of AC in individuals with
DM compared with non-DM individuals (hazard ratio [HR],
1.333; 95% confidence interval [CI], 1.24 to 1.44).
Symptoms
are associated with poorer quality of life, depression, and higher
diabetes-specific burden.
DM seems to be an indicator of poor
outcome for a conservative approach, but there seems to be no
difference with respect to surgical management.
Calcific shoulder tendinitis, which results from deposition of
calcium hydroxyapatite crystals predominantly in rotator cuff tendons, is more prevalent in patients with DM. One study reported
a three times higher prevalence of shoulder calcification in patients
with DM compared with controls, but only a third of them experienced any symptoms.
The cause of these tendon-related problems in the shoulder are unknown but may reflect altered matrix
metalloproteinase (MMP)/tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase
(TIMP) expression. More work is required in this area.

### Hands

Limited joint mobility syndrome (diabetic cheiroarthropathy),
also known as diabetic stiff hand syndrome, is not actually an
arthropathy but rather a process that involves periarticular structures. It involves a fibrosing syndrome, resembling scleroderma,
with thick, tight, and waxy skin predominantly over the dorsal
aspect of the metacarpophalangeal (MCP), proximal (PIP), and
distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints of the fingers. The “prayer sign”
comes from the inability to oppose palmar surfaces of hands and
fingers. Larger joints, such as wrists, elbows, knees, and ankles,
can be involved. Prevalence between 30% to 58% and 45% to
76% of patients with type 1 and type 2 DM, respectively, have
been reported, as well as an increase in frequency with age, disease duration, and poor glycemic control.
Nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and steroid injections are not
effective in management, and glycemic control, as well as physical
therapy, is encouraged.

Flexor tenosynovitis, or “trigger finger,” results from the occurrence of fibrosis and thickening of the tendon sheath, particularly
when the tendon passes through the pulley, restricting the movement of the flexor tendon within its tendon sheath and later leading to inflammation, which causes pain and ultimately the locking
of the involved fingers. Prevalence is higher in patients with type
1 and type 2 DM compared with the general population, and
it is also higher in patients with poorly controlled disease than
in well-controlled patients.
Patients with DM are more likely
to have multiple fingers involved simultaneously than non-DM
patients.
Management with steroid injections might not be as
effective, and patients with multiple-digit involvement more often
require surgical management.
Dupuytren’s disease (DD) is classically described as the thickening of the palmar fascia and flexor tendons, as well as pretendinous bands, palmar or digital nodules, and flexion contractures of
fingers, involving the ring and little finger. In patients with DM,
DD is not only more prevalent, affecting 16% to 42% of individuals, but it also more often involves the middle and ring fingers.
CTS is an entrapment neuropathy caused by compression of
the median nerve and, in patients with DM, it is thought to be
related to fibroproliferative connective tissue.
In patients with
DM, the role of inflammation and neovascularization seems to be
more prominent than in non-DM patients.
Prevalence is higher
in patients with DM, especially if co-existent with neuropathy; a
recent systematic review and meta-analysis estimated an increased
risk for patients with DM (odds ratio [OR], 1.69; 95% CI, 1.45
to 1.96).
CTS in DM patients can co-exist with other pathologies, such as trigger fingers. Surgical management outcomes seem
to be similar to non-DM patients.

### Feet

Charcot’s osteoarthropathy (COA), or diabetic osteoarthropathy,
is associated with diabetic neuropathy and is a progressive, often
rapid, and degenerative disease of the foot and ankle joints that
leads to significant damage and deformity if left untreated. Incidence is about 0.1% to 5% in diabetic neuropathy, with prevalence ranging from 0.08% to 8.5%.
COA incidence increases
with duration of diabetes and diabetic neuropathy, and although
it is more common unilaterally, it can involve both extremities
in up to 39% of cases.
Risk of amputation with COA is higher
when associated with ulceration. Incidence of the latter in these
patients is 17% per year.
There are two theories regarding the pathophysiology of COA:
the neurotraumatic and neurovascular hypotheses. The first refers
to cumulative trauma (in the context of loss of protective proprioception) as a causative factor, leading to the release of proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF, IL-1
β
, and IL-6, as well as
upregulation of receptor activator of nuclear factor
κ
B (NFκ
B)
ligand (RANKL), which leads to osteoclastogenesis and excessive
bone turnover.
The latter hypothesis proposes that because
of autonomic dysfunction, patients with diabetes have increased
blood flow to subchondral bone, resulting in increased osteoclastic
activity and bone resorption.
This phenomenon occurs even in
the absence of peripheral vascular disease and creates bone fatigue
and disorganization. Some studies have also shown that receptors
for AGEs in chondrocytes can lead to upregulation of MMPs and
increase inflammation.
Initially, there is swelling, warmth, and erythema of the foot.
Involvement of ankle, tarsal, and tarsometatarsal are most common, but rarely knees, wrists, shoulder, and intervertebral joints
can be affected. Progressive bone resorption, fractures, and dislocation lead to deformity and destabilization of the foot, giving the classic “rocker-bottom” appearance. In addition to plain
radiography, MRI and bone scintigraphy can be useful studies for
evaluating the extent of the disease. The initial approach involves
joint stabilization and offloading. Other measures include antiresorptive therapy and surgery.

### Muscles

Diabetic muscle infarction, a rare condition marked by spontaneous infarction of muscle tissue with no preceding injury,
occurs largely in patients who are considered “brittle diabetics.”
Proposed mechanisms include microangiopathy and
hypercoagulability, from alterations of the coagulation–fibrinolytic system and endothelial dysfunction.
Characteristically,
these patients will have accumulated numerous microvascular
sequelae from their uncontrolled disease. Clinically, patients
will present with abrupt onset of pain and swelling, usually
involving the thigh or calf muscles, and creatine phosphokinase (CPK) levels may be normal or elevated.
MRI using
T2-weighted imaging may be useful in arriving at a diagnosis,
with confirmatory testing involving an incisional muscle biopsy
and demonstration of muscle edema and necrosis. Diabetic
amyotrophy usually presents as weakness involving proximal
muscles of the lower extremities and is also associated with
pain. Treatment usually involves glycemic control and physical
therapy.

### Diffuse Idiopathic Skeletal Hyperostosis

Diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH) is characterized
by ossification of the anterior longitudinal ligament of the spine,
can involve various other extraspinal ligaments, and is generally
accompanied by osteophyte formation (
Fig. 127.1
). Intervertebral
disk spaces and sacroiliac joints, however, are commonly spared.
A high incidence of DISH has been demonstrated in a cohort
of patients affected by both clinically active and subclinical DM
(both type 1 and type 2), as well as smaller case series.
A
more recent study found that individuals in their fifth decade of
life were more likely to be affected by DISH if they were obese,
had a first-degree relative with either DM or hypertension, had
lumbar or thoracic spinal pain, and were affected by enthesopathies or tendonitis.
The likelihood of relatively young patients
with three or more of these clinical parameters being affected by
DISH was six times higher compared with age- and sex-matched
control subjects (
P
= .004). It has been suggested that chronic
elevation of serum insulin and IGF-1 leads to calcification and
ossification in ligaments and enthesis regions exposed to mechanical stress.

described in patients with type 1 diabetes, normal or high

2 diabetes. Despite this, both forms of diabetes are associated
with an increased risk of fragility fractures. An increased risk
of hip fractures has been reported both in men and women

diet and physical exercises need to be reinforced given the important role in the prevention and treatment of both conditions.

## Thyroid Disease

### Hyperthyroidism

Patients with hyperthyroidism because of Graves’ disease may present with pretibial myxedema and ophthalmopathy. The myxedema
appears as nodules (usually 1 cm or larger) over the pretibial surface.
They are composed of hyaluronic acid, are pink to purple in color,
and are painless.
Patients with Graves’ disease can also have
proximal muscle weakness, associated shoulder adhesive capsulitis,
loss of muscle mass, and weight loss. Most of these manifestations will
correct with treatment.
Nail changes, onycholysis, and clubbing
(thyroid acropachy) may be associated with periostitis around the
metacarpal joints, as well as soft tissue swelling of the digits. Unfortunately, this condition may not return to normal when the patient
is treated.
A common and serious manifestation of hyperthyroidism is
osteopenia/osteoporosis. Treating patients with thyroid hormone
to achieve a normal level of thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH),
as well as adjusting the dose of thyroid replacement, is essential to
achieving improvement in BMD (as measured by bone densitometry) in patients with hyperthyroid disease.
Hyperthyroidism frequently overlaps with other autoimmune
diseases. To evaluate for the prevalence of various non–organ-specific autoantibodies in patients with hyperthyroidism, 80 patients
with SLE and RA and a control group of 34 healthy subjects were
examined for serum FT3 and FT4, TSH, and antibodies against
thyroglobulin and thyroperoxidase; in addition, an ultrasound of
the thyroid gland was performed.
Patients with hyperthyroidism had a significantly higher prevalence of anti-nuclear antibody
(ANA) expression than control subjects (45% vs. 14.7%;
P
<
.001). There were no significant differences in the prevalence of
other antibodies between the groups. ANA
patients were younger

than ANA

results indicate that it is clinically important to screen patients
with SLE and RA for the co-existence of thyroid autoimmune
disease.

### Hypothyroidism

The most common cause of primary hypothyroidism in the United
States is Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (HT), also known as
chronic
immune thyroiditis
, with an occurrence of 3.6/100 person-years
in females and 0.8/1000 person-years in men.
Other causes
include pituitary adenoma or Sheehan’s syndrome, surgical resection, iodine deficiency, deficiency in the iodothyronine synthetase
pathway, or use of certain drugs (lithium or amiodarone). Hypothyroidism often presents with a characteristic symmetric arthropathy
involving stiffness of the joints of the hands and knees. Upon palpation, the joints feel “gelatinous,” and aspiration of fluid is usually non-inflammatory and viscous, with high levels of hyaluronic
acid. Calcium pyrophosphate dehydrate (CPPD) crystals are often
found, and hypothyroidism should be included in the differential
diagnosis of joints that demonstrate CPPD crystals upon aspiration.
Other conditions associated with CPPD crystal deposition
include:
• Hyperparathyroidism
• Familial hypocalciuric hyperkalemia
• Hemochromatosis
• Hemosiderosis
• Hypophosphatasia
• Hypomagnesemia
• Hypothyroidism
• Gout
• Neuropathic joints
• Aging
• Amyloidosis
• Trauma, including surgery
In patients with hypothyroidism, a myopathy can occur and
presents with pain, cramps, stiffness, proximal weakness, fatigue,
elevated CPK levels, abnormal muscle disease on biopsy (atrophy of type II fibers and an increase in type I fibers), and hypercholesterolemia.
Hoffman’s syndrome is a rare disorder of
hypothyroidism manifesting with increased muscle mass (pseudohypertrophy in addition to the other aforementioned manifestations). Kocher–Debré–Semelaigne syndrome is a disorder of
infancy (cretinism) presenting with similar pseudohypertrophy
in mothers who have deficient levels of iodine.
Most symptoms of myopathy resolve upon treatment with thyroid hormone. Explanations for the effect of thyroid hormone on muscle
include delayed contraction and relaxation (related to a change
from fast to slow muscle fibers), alteration in myosin heavy chain
gene forms, and impaired glycogenosis.
A neuropathy such
as CTS can also be the initial manifestation of hypothyroidism.
The mechanism for compression is thought to be a result of the
accumulation of glycosaminoglycans within the surrounding tissues. One systematic review of 4908 patients with CTS found a
pooled odds ratio of 1.4 (95% CI, 1.0 to 2.0) for the prevalence
of concomitant hypothyroidism compared with healthy control
subjects.

### Chronic Autoimmune Thyroiditis

Chronic autoimmune thyroiditis (ATD) is an inflammatory thyroiditis that is sometimes characterized by lymphocytic infiltration of the
thyroid gland. The presence of arthritis in patients with ATD (even
those with normal thyroid function tests) is increasingly observed.
ATD encompasses a group of illnesses that include Graves’ disease,
chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis (CLT), and Hashimoto’s disease.
Graves’ disease is characterized by production of antibodies against
thyrotropin receptor, activation of thyroid follicular cells, modest infiltration of the thyroid gland by lymphocytes, and hyperthyroidism.
In contrast, CLT can present with either hyperthyroidism or, more
often, hypothyroidism. Both can have positive ANA autoantibody
expression. Rheumatic complaints in patients with ATD include:
• Carpal tunnel syndrome
• Chondrocalcinosis
• Shoulder capsulitis
• Neck pain
• Generalized stiffness
• Myopathy
• Fibromyalgia
Synovial effusions can occur and are non-inflammatory.
Affected joints are knees, metacarpophalangeal joints, proximal
interphalangeal joints, and metatarsophalangeal joints. Most
patients are hypothyroid, and joint complaints resolve when
treated with thyroid hormone suppression and decrease in TSH.
In a group of euthyroid patients with CLT and no well-defined
connective tissue disease, 98% presented with arthralgia and 59%
with fibromyalgia, 28% had Raynaud’s disease, and 26% had sicca
symptoms.
Arthritis was radiographically present in 88% of these
patients (arthritis of the spine accounted for 45%). TSH levels correlated with anti-thyroid peroxidase (anti-TPO) antibodies but neither erythrocyte sedimentation rate nor anti-TG antibody levels. The
authors concluded that rheumatic manifestations frequently occur in
patients with CLT in the absence of overt thyroid dysfunction.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
Anti-thyroid antibodies are present in 15% to 20% of patients

In both RA and psoriatic arthritis, an increase in subclinical and

Even though these increases in anti-TPO and anti-TG

In the Danbio registry of 1035 RA patients, 11.8% with thyroid disease and other comorbid diseases had significantly poorer

An association

Sjögren’s Syndrome
The prevalence of thyroid disease in patients with Sjögren’s syndrome runs from a low of 10% to a high of 70%; however, this
phenomenon may be related to the demographics of Sjögren’s
syndrome, which usually affects older women. In one study, the
occurrence of hypothyroidism was 14%, with the occurrence of
thyrotoxicosis being 1.8%.
Symptoms of Sjögren’s syndrome,
such as conjunctivitis sicca and xerostomia, have been reported in
up to 32% of patients with autoimmune thyroiditis.
In a large
study of 479 patients with Sjögren’s syndrome, the frequency of

with Graves’ disease. It was also observed that the presence of
Graves’ disease and HT could appear either before or after the
occurrence of Sjögren’s syndrome, making it difficult to ascertain
whether primary Sjögren’s syndrome was a predisposing factor for
the development of autoimmune thyroiditis.

## Parathyroid Disease

### Hypoparathyroidism

A disorder of parathyroid hormone (PTH), hypoparathyroidism
is usually caused by either autoimmune destruction of the parathyroid glands (isolated endocrine deficiency syndrome or failure
of other endocrine glands) or neck surgery with extensive removal
of parathyroid tissue. Two other lesser causes are a congenital
disorder with failure of derivatives of the third and fourth pharyngeal pouch (DiGeorge syndrome) and intra-cellular processing defects or mutations of the calcium sensing receptor. Bone
structure and bone turnover is different from normal and may
result in enthesopathy, as well as paravertebral and/or subcutaneous calcifications.
The clinical manifestations (circumoral
numbness, paresthesias, carpal and pedal muscle spasms, laryngeal
spasm, tetany, and/or seizures), which are seen infrequently, are a
result of hypocalcemia.
Albright’s osteodystrophy or pseudohypoparathyroidism
(pseudo-HoPT) results from end organ resistance (bone and
kidney) to PTH and presents with elevated levels of PTH, hypocalcemia, and hyperphosphatemia. Type 1a HoPT (autosomal
dominant) is inherited maternally and characterized by calcification of the paraspinal ligaments, short stature, and mental retardation. Patients clinically have shortened metatarsal and metacarpal
bones and a defect in the genes encoding the
α
-subunit of the
cell membrane–associated guanine nucleotide stimulating unit of
adenyl cyclase.
Type 1b HoPT also has resistance to PTH but
has a normal phenotype and is inherited paternally.
Soft tissue calcifications that are not clinically relevant (in basal ganglia,
cataracts, shoulder joints, or subcutaneous tissues of the hands)
have occurred in HoPT and infrequently in pseudo-HoPT.
Surgically induced HoPT may also be accompanied by muscle
weakness that is usually related to the degree of hypocalcemia and
is responsive to treatment with vitamin D and calcium.
Renal disease can result in crystal deposition disease (sodium
urate, calcium pyrophosphate, or basic calcium phosphate
hydroxyapatite) as a result of hyperphosphatemia from reduced
glomerular filtration and secondary hyperparathyroidism.
Sodium urate deposition results in acute gout seen more commonly in people with renal insufficiency. Gout is rare in patients
undergoing dialysis but can be seen after renal transplantation
with decreased creatinine clearance and use of calcineurin inhibitors, such as cyclosporine. CPPD deposition is seen less often in
renal disease than gout or hydroxyapatite and is rare in individuals
undergoing dialysis. Hydroxyapatite deposition can cause acute
synovitis and periarticular inflammation. Painful subcutaneous nodules or chronic asymptomatic nodules (uremic humoral
calcinosis) can occur. Prevention is achieved through phosphate
restriction, adequate dialysis, and oral phosphate binding agents.
Renal osteodystrophy is a result of osteomalacia, osteitis fibrosa
cystica, osteosclerosis, aluminum toxicity, osteoporosis, and
β
-
microglobulin amyloid deposition and presents with bone pain,
muscle wasting, and myalgias, as well as bone fractures. To define
the abnormalities in skeletal microstructure and to establish the
potential efficacy of PTH replacement in hypoparathyroidism,
standard histomorphometric and microcomputed tomography
analyses were performed on iliac crest bone biopsies obtained from
patients with hypoparathyroidism. Participants were treated with
teriparatide for 2 years and compared with age- and sex-matched
control subjects with no abnormalities in parathyroid function.
Initially bone density was increased and skeletal features reflected
the low turnover state with greater bone volume/trabecular bone
volume, trabecular width, and cortical width, as well as suppressed
mineralizing surface and bone formation rate compared with control subjects. With teriparatide therapy, bone turnover and BMD
increased in the lumbar spine. Requirements for calcium and vitamin D fell, whereas serum and urinary calcium concentrations
did not change. The investigators concluded that the abnormal
microstructure of the skeleton in hypoparathyroidism reflects the
absence of PTH. Replacement therapy with PTH has the potential to correct these abnormalities and to reduce the requirements
for calcium and vitamin D.

Similarities to Other Rheumatic Diseases
The skeletal abnormalities of hypoparathyroidism are caused by
calcification, which can simulate ankylosing spondylitis with
clinical signs, including morning stiffness, gait, and posture.
Sacroiliitis is not expected, although it is the earliest manifestation
in most patients with ankylosing spondylitis. The patterns of syndesmophytes in patients with hypoparathyroidism can resemble
those of ankylosing spondylitis with an origin from the vertebral
margin and preserved disk space, but more often there is also
involvement of the posterior paraspinal ligament. Spinal changes
in hypoparathyroidism have also been described to be similar to
those in DISH, which is characterized by ossification of the anterior longitudinal ligament of the spine and of various extraspinal
ligaments but is rarely reported before 50 years of age. The pain
is not responsive to immunosuppressive agents and NSAIDs but
may resolve completely upon treatment with calcitriol.
Serum
calcium may need to be included in the diagnostic workup of
patients with inflammatory back pain, especially if they present
with atypical features as previously described. It is important to
differentiate hypoparathyroid-related spondylitis from ankylosing
spondylitis because the management for the two disorders is different. In fact, some of the drugs used for ankylosing spondylitis,
such as bisphosphonates, may worsen hypocalcemia. The mechanism underlying these skeletal changes in hypoparathyroidism is
not well defined. Decreased intestinal calcium absorption caused
by a defect in the action of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D has been
suggested to play a role in a controlled study of paravertebral ligamentous ossification.
Investigators suggested that the ossifying
diathesis of paravertebral ligaments, which is the origin of DISH,
might be initiated or aggravated by hypoparathyroidism.
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
SLE is known to potentially affect any organ system, and hypoparathyroidism has been found to occur in 4.0% to 5.7% of

patients with SLE compared with 1.0% in the general population.
Hypoparathyroidism is underestimated because it is
poorly manifested clinically and can be precipitated by certain
aggravating factors, such as corticosteroid treatment or vitamin D deficiency. Although acute hypocalcemia typically gives
symptoms and signs of musculoskeletal irritability, chronic
hypocalcemia usually gives mild symptoms and can even be
asymptomatic. Despite the possible lack of outward manifestations, hypocalcemia may be a significant health risk as a result
of QT interval prolongation and the consequent risk of sudden death. These cases raise the question of whether periodic
calcium and phosphate screening should be routine care for
patients with SLE.

### Hyperparathyroidism

PTH, along with vitamin D and calcitonin, controls calcium
homeostasis. These hormones are responsible for bone metabolism and remodeling in addition to maintaining serum calcium in
a very narrow range. In primary hyperparathyroidism (HPT), this
balance is altered as a result of changes in the parathyroid gland,
whereas changes in other organs of the body that result in calcium
loss and vitamin D deficiency with increased secretion of PTH are
called secondary HPT.
Approximately 1% to 2% of the population has hypercalcemia, with 10% to 20% of these individuals having HPT.
Most patients with HPT have a single adenoma; carcinoma
is rare, and in secondary HPT diffuse hyperplasia related to
hypocalcemia or resistance to PTH is seen. Secondary HPT
is most commonly the result of osteomalacia, vitamin D deficiency, and renal failure.
Skeletal symptoms include arthralgias, as well as radiographic findings such as osteitis fibrosa
cystica and subperiosteal resorption along the radial side of the
phalanges. In addition, the distal tufts of the phalanges may
also reveal subperiosteal resorption.
Arthritis associated with HPT involves the small joints of the
hand (possibly becoming erosive) and spares the PIP joints. In
this respect, it can mimic RA, but the presence of subperiosteal
resorption is very distinctive. In contrast to RA, these patients

### Tendinopathy

Tendon xanthomas have been described in dyslipidemia types II
and III, and type II has been associated with tendinopathy.
Tendon xanthomata, which are collections of lipid-laden macrophages typically observed over the Achilles tendons, can be either
symptomatic or asymptomatic. These develop during childhood
in homozygote patients but can also be seen in heterozygous
patients later in life. Association with tendinitis is variable but
more common in homozygous patients.
The presence of tendon xanthomata correlates with a 3.2-fold higher risk of cardiovascular disease.
Type III patients present with tuberoeruptive
xanthomata and plantar crease xanthomata, which are typically
asymptomatic and do not involve tendons.

### Arthritis

Several other reports have been published of arthritis in patients
with type II dyslipidemia. A case series of 41 patients followed up
for 4 years described a transient migratory polyarthritis, which
affected both large and small joints in 10 of the patients.
Episodes lasted between 3 to 12 days, with moderate to severe pain.
Non-inflammatory synovial fluid was obtained in some cases, supporting the hypothesis that symptoms arise secondary to periarthritis. This presentation is also supported by other reports in the
literature.
Type IV patients have also developed episodes of
arthritis, presenting as an acute or subacute pauciarthritis of large
and small joints. Fluid from two of these patients was described as
of a mildly inflammatory nature.
Arthritis and musculoskeletal symptoms have responded to lipid-lowering therapy.
