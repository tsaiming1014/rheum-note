## Introduction

Autoimmune diseases represent a significant and increasing health
burden for 3% to 9% of the general population, and rheumatology, perhaps more than any medical subspecialty, encompasses a
broad array of such diseases that affect a wide range of organ systems (
Table 21.1
).
Consequently, rheumatologists have a great
interest in defining the causes and pathophysiology of autoimmunity and in applying this information in the clinic.
The immune system must effectively defend against a diverse
universe of pathogens while simultaneously maintaining tolerance to self-antigens. Recent advances have begun to clarify how
this equilibrium is established and sustained and, importantly,
have identified many critical factors and processes involved in
the pathogenesis of autoimmune diseases. This chapter provides
a general overview of autoimmunity, covering the definition of
the term, general tolerance mechanisms for T and B lymphocytes,
theories of how tolerance can be breached, and ways in which
genetic and environmental factors have been implicated in breaking tolerance and producing disease. Emphasis is placed on manifestations and mechanisms related to rheumatologic diseases.

## Definition and Classification of Pathogenic Autoimmunity

Autoimmunity—that is, the immune response against self—
evokes the specter of “horror autotoxicus,” a term coined by Paul
Ehrlich at the turn of the 20th century to anticipate the disastrous
consequences of this condition.
In fact, autoreactivity is more
nuanced, ranging from a low “physiologic” level of self-reactivity
essential for lymphocyte selection and maintenance of normal
immune system homeostasis, to an intermediate level of autoimmunity that manifests as circulating autoantibodies and tissue infiltrates that are not associated with clinical consequences,
to pathogenic autoimmunity associated with immune-mediated
dysfunction or injury.
From the clinical perspective, it is the
transformation to pathogenic autoimmunity that demarcates significant from insignificant self-reactivity.
The diagnosis of an autoimmune disease is generally based on
the presence of adaptive immune system–mediated disease caused
by self-reactive antibodies, T cells, or both. For many common
autoimmune diseases, more definitive evidence for an autoimmune cause has come from studies showing transfer of disease
by autoantibodies or self-reactive T cells and from animal models exhibiting congruent characteristics. However, no universally
accepted criteria exist, and some less well-characterized diseases
currently considered to be autoimmune may turn out to have
other causes.
An example of disorders that exhibit some characteristics of
autoimmunity but are distinct in their pathogenesis are the autoinflammatory diseases.
These diseases are predominantly rare
monogenic disorders typified by intermittent bouts of fever, rash,
serositis, and arthritis caused by a widening spectrum of defects
in the control of basic inflammatory pathways. Included in this
category are familial Mediterranean fever, the cryopyrinopathies,
hyperimmunoglobulinemia D with recurrent fever, familial cold
urticaria, Blau syndrome, and others. These syndromes can be
viewed broadly along with autoimmune disease as disorders in
which autoreactivity causes tissue damage: the autoinflammatory
diseases mediated entirely through the innate arm of the immune
system comprise one end of the autoreactive spectrum, while at the
other end are the autoimmune diseases that require both the innate
and adaptive responses. Indeed, the possibility of a less stringent

## Tolerance Mechanisms

Immunity must be sufficiently robust and diverse to recognize
and protect the metazoan host from the vast universe of microbial
pathogens. Simultaneously, the immune system must avoid excess
or misdirected activation to avoid injury to the host. Tolerance is
the immune system property whereby reactivity with potentially
tissue-damaging infections or tumors is maintained while reactivity to host molecules and normal tissue is actively restrained.
Observations of immunologic tolerance preceded elucidation of its cellular and molecular underpinnings. In 1945 Owen
observed that shared blood supply between fraternal bovine twins
in utero resulted in adult tolerance of genetically foreign blood
cells.
Billingham and Medawar showed that in utero inoculation with foreign tissue resulted in adult animal ability to accept
foreign skin grafts, advancing the concept of actively acquired
immune tolerance.
Burnet and Medawar then advanced the critical concept that tolerance was imposed by clonal deletion of selfreactive lymphocytes during early development, that is, central
tolerance.
The subsequent discovery that mature B cells undergo
somatic hypermutation in the periphery led to the hypothesis by
Bretscher and Cohn that the production of autoantibodies might
be impeded by the need for both B and T cell compartments to
breach tolerance.
In 1975 while studying allogeneic responses,
Lafferty and Cunningham posited that activation of T cells involved
transduction of a second signal that need not be antigen related,
thereby implicating co-stimulation from antigen-presenting cells
(APCs) as a critical factor in lymphocyte activation.
In 1987
the nature of the co-stimulation, or two-signal, requirement was
further defined when Jenkins and Schwartz showed that engagement of antigen receptor alone without a second signal resulted in
inactivation of T cells.
An elegant mechanism for self–nonself
discrimination was then advanced by Janeway in 1989 when he
hypothesized that APCs remain quiescent unless activated by the
engagement of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) by microbial
products.
This concept was further extended by Matzinger in
1994 to a “danger model” that includes activation of the immune
system by both foreign and endogenous factors associated with
tissue stress and damage.
These early insights laid the theoretical foundation for the current, more complex view of self–nonself
discrimination in which tolerance is imposed by both innate and
adaptive immune systems through layers of mechanisms occurring
at various stages, called “self-tolerance checkpoints,” throughout
lymphocyte development and activation (
Table 21.4
).

### Clone-Specific Self/Nonself Recognition

In contrast to innate immune cells, which are activated primarily by hardwired germline-encoded microbial pattern recognition receptors that recognize broad classes of pathogen-associated
structures (viral nucleic acids, microbial cell wall components,
bacterial metabolic products), rearranged lymphocyte antigen
receptors confer capacity for much more individualized and specific antigen binding. In T and B lymphocytes, capacity for self–
nonself discrimination is generated at the clonal level. To control
potentially damaging consequences of self-recognition, T and B
cells utilize three general mechanistic strategies. First, lymphocyte
developmental stage determines the outcome of antigen receptor
signal transduction. Immature lymphocytes respond to strong
antigen receptor stimulation by undergoing apoptotic cell death,
whereas similar-strength signals in mature cells lead to activation. Antigen receptor signaling–induced cell death thus eliminates most self-reactive immature T clones before cells can join
the mature cell repertoire with potential to cause tissue injury.
For the limited self-reactive clones that do survive immaturity, a
second controlling strategy is utilized: co-stimulatory signaling.
Productive T cell activation of mature lymphocytes requires, in
addition to antigen receptor engagement, a second co-stimulatory
signal, the absence of which results in anergy or cell death.
For
the most part, this requirement limits activation of self-reactive T
cells because co-stimulatory signals are largely provided by cells
of the innate immune system. In the absence of activating signals
(such as infection or inflammation) to the innate immune system,
expression of co-stimulatory molecules on innate immune system
cells is limited. Thus, when quiescent, resting innate immune cells
interact with T cells bearing self-reactive receptors, the isolated
TCR signal induces cellular unresponsiveness (anergy). Third,
both T and B lymphocytes are fine-tuned in various ways by an
extensive list of modulating factors necessary for controlling selfreactive clones.
In B cells, but not T cells, the fine-tuning is
also evidenced by an inverse correlation between antigen receptor
(membrane immunoglobulin [Ig]M) self-reactivity and ability to
induce calcium flux.

### The Innate System and Tolerance

It is not surprising that the innate immune system strongly influences both tolerance and proclivity toward autoimmunity, given
its vital role in initiating and modulating adaptive immune
responses. Several ways in which self-tolerance is influenced by
the innate arm have been defined.
First, activation of the innate immune system under normal
circumstances typically requires engagement of microbial PRRs,
germline-encoded receptors that are normally only triggered by
nonself or foreign antigens.
PPRs recognize common structures
on pathogens (pathogen-associated molecular patterns) and also a
few self-molecules, such as certain damage-associated molecular
patterns—for example, heat shock proteins
—which are released
during cellular stress, injury, or death. PPRs can be classified by
their location into the following types: secreted (e.g., collectins,
pentraxins, and ficolins), plasma transmembrane (e.g., TLRs, certain C-type lectin receptors, and N-formyl methionine receptors),
endosomal (e.g., TLRs), and cytosolic (e.g., retinoic acid inducible
gene I–like receptors, NOD-like receptors, and DNA receptors:
IFI16, AIM2, and the cyclic guanosine monophosphate–adenosine monophosphate synthase [cGAS]–stimulator of IFN genes
[STING] pathway).
PRR subcellular location is likely important for control of self-ligand-induced PRR signaling that may in
turn promote activation of autoreactive lymphocytes. TLR7 and
TLR9 are examples of PRR that equally sense nucleic acids from
both self and foreign sources. When TLR7 is overexpressed, by
transgenesis or gene duplication, systemic autoimmunity results.
A hypothesized mechanism for the regulation of autoimmunity
by the TLR7 expression level is that signaling induced by normal substimulatory amounts of self RNA via endosome-localized
TLR7 may be greatly increased by the overexpressed receptor.
The usual requirement for microbial-originated RNA within
endosomes to productively stimulate TLR7 is bypassed, and the
immune cell activation threshold is effectively lowered. In support
of the notion that threshold modulation in PRR signaling can
drive autoimmunity, a mutation in TLR7 that increases signaling in response to normally subthreshold single-stranded RNA
stimuli causes SLE in both humans and mice.
Second, some cells of the innate immune system actively suppress adaptive immune system activation. For example, both
immature and mature dendritic cells (DCs) have been shown to
promote tolerance by modulating CD4
T regulatory (Treg) cell
function and other mechanisms.

can result in secondary necrosis, leading to an increased
supply of self-derived material including nucleic acids that can
be recognized by autoreactive lymphocytes, and to release of
pro-inflammatory factors, leading to systemic autoimmunity.

(2) the milk fat

In the chronic graft-versus-host disease

and dampen CD8
T cell responses to viral infection and selfantigens.
Deficiency of the cell surface protein and complement

Fifth, innate immune cells could be a major source of selfantigenic material. For example, neutrophil extra-cellular traps
(NETs), the nuclear material-containing weblike structure
extruded by neutrophils in the setting of certain stimuli including
bacterial infection, contain a number of modified autoantigens.
However, understanding of the impact of NETs on SLE and on
other human autoimmune syndromes is incomplete.

### T Cell Tolerance

T cells are critical mediators in not only achieving but also fine
tuning self-antigen tolerance to a high degree of specificity. Mechanisms whereby the tissue-damaging potential of autoreactive T
cells are thought to be controlled may be broadly categorized into
three main areas: central tolerance, wherein T cells first acquire
their antigen receptor; peripheral tolerance, in which T cells
encounter self-antigens not present in the thymus; and postactivation regulation, which occurs when activated and expanded T
cell clones undergo apoptosis or are induced into functional quiescence. Central tolerance, as alluded to earlier, is established in
the T cell compartment during thymocyte development, primarily through deletion of T cell clones bearing receptors with high
affinity for self, but also through anergy induction and potential
editing of the TCR
α
-chain.
Thymus-dependent tolerizing
processes are highly effective but incompletely efficient in generating a mature T cell repertoire capable of both robust responses to
foreign antigens and of muted response to self-peptides. Clearly,
T cells exhibiting autoreactivity—primarily those bearing TCR
with intermediate or low antigen affinity—or T cells that recognize self-antigens poorly expressed in the thymus are permitted to
mature and emigrate from the thymus to the peripheral tissues in
significant numbers. This “leakiness” is probably necessary to generate a broad repertoire, but the resultant vulnerability to autoimmunity necessitates additional mechanisms to maintain tolerance
of mature peripheral T cells.
In the periphery, multiple mechanisms for limiting potentially
tissue-injurious autoreactive T cells have been identified. One is
that segregation of self-antigen away from T cells bearing cognate
self-reactive TCR may effectively maintain tolerance, such that
most self-antigens cannot trigger responses of mature autoreactive
T cells by virtue of their subcellular or tissue antigen location or

their low abundance. This mechanism is supported by experimental findings that transgenic TCR-bearing cells with capacity to
recognize tissue-specific antigens are neither deleted or activated;
nor do they cause autoimmune disease. Yet these so-called “ignorant” T cells are fully functional and respond to self-antigen when
presented in a conventional context.
For a few self-antigens,
such as those expressed in the anterior chamber of the eye, central
nervous system (CNS), gonads, or other so-called immune privileged sites—as originally defined by their ability to accept allograft
transplants—suppression of self-reactivity arises partly from anatomic sequestration caused by limited access of blood-borne cells
and the absence of conventional lymphatic drainage,
although
the latter is not universally accepted.
Nevertheless, other anatomic structures that enable separation of immune cells from the
brain such as endothelial, epithelial, and glial cell barriers that
establish compartments within the CNS have been identified with
higher-resolution imaging.
Sequestration is important because T
cells are typically first activated in secondary lymphoid organs and
subsequently migrate to target organs where reactivation by local
APCs and the production of pro-inflammatory factors lead to tissue damage.
Anatomic barriers alone, however, is not sufficient
for tissues to support immunologic “privilege” and, as is discussed
later, such sites employ additional local mechanisms.
Another peripheral mechanism depends on regulation of the
requirement for both TCR engagement and a co-stimulatory signal usually provided by T cell–expressed CD28 to engender full
T cell activation. High-level surface expression of CD28 ligands
CD80 and CD86 on professional APC is typically induced only
under activated conditions. When co-stimulatory ligands are
expressed at low levels, as is observed on quiescent APC, such
cells may present self-antigen to T cells without providing a costimulatory signal. Functionally, such APC-T cell interaction can
induce tolerance. Experimental support for this mechanism comes
from observations that immature DCs constitutively presenting
low doses of self-antigen on MHCs can cause cell death or induce
anergy of the corresponding T cells.
Peripheral tolerance is also maintained by active suppression
exerted by immunoregulatory cells, among which CD4
regulatory T cells (Tregs), are the best characterized.
Treg comprise a
distinct CD4
αβ
-T cell lineage that expresses the transcription
factor forkhead boxP3 (FOXP3) and that function to continuously suppress inflammation and autoimmunity.
Treg develop
in the thymus as an alternative lineage fate to conventional CD4
T cells, when they receive distinctly timed agonist TCR and
TGF
β
signals.
Treg may also differentiate from CD4
precursors extrathymically (peripheral or pTreg) at barrier sites like the
intestine,
where they enforce tolerance to food antigens and
microbiota. Treg typically express high levels of CTLA-4 and the
IL-2 receptor component IL-2R
α
(CD25) and require IL-2 for
survival. Tregs are activated alongside conventional T cells in all
adaptive immune responses and are critical for maintaining a level
of immune response proportional to the size and strength of infectious challenge. Treg-mediated suppression relies on both cell–cell
interactions and on secretion of soluble factors.
As an example
of the former, Tregs suppress DC function through engagement
of DC CD80/CD86 by Treg-expressed CTLA-4. Treg also inhibit
activation of conventional T cells by competing with them for
IL-2, by production of immunosuppressive cytokines (such as
TGFβ
, IL-10, and IL-35), and by expression of inhibitory surface molecules (CD39, CD73, LAG3, and TIGIT). The net effect
of Treg cell–cell interactions and secreted products is global suppression of immune and inflammatory cells in close proximity.
Conventional T cell suppression occurs regardless of Treg TCR
specificity,
although Treg sharing autoantigen specificity with
conventional effector T cells is more potent, in part because it
can deny access of effector cells to stimulatory antigens.
Treg

Th17, Tfr)
that share differentiation features with conventional
effector CD4
T cells. Generally, Tregs are a highly stable lineage,
but under certain conditions. Treg capacity for conversion to conventional CD4
T cells has been described.
Tissue-resident
subsets of Tregs in adipose tissue, muscle, lung, gut, and skin, in
addition to their immunosuppressive function, have been shown
to express tissue-specific transcription factors and elaborate mediators that promote homeostasis and healing.
Other T cells
occupy regulatory niches in regulating autoimmunity, including Tr1, CD8
Treg, Qa-1/human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-

The ability of Tregs to target specific organs or
cell types, including antigen-specific lymphocytes, has generated
considerable interest in harnessing them for the management of
autoimmune diseases.
Tissues that contain or are surveilled by T lymphocytes employ
mechanisms that locally suppress self-reactivity and that also contribute to the establishment of immune privilege.
These
tissues fall into three general groups. First, certain tissues are decorated with cell surface inhibitory molecules—such as the proapoptotic FasL and TRAIL, lymphocyte inhibitory and Treg-promoting
PD-L2, and complement regulatory proteins CD55 and CD46—
that can potentially eliminate or impede the activation of autoreactive T cells. Second, soluble inhibitors of inflammation and
immune activation are secreted by particular tissues. Notably, in
the aqueous humor of the eye, there is a broad spectrum of such
factors that include TGFβ
,
α

vasointestinal peptide, calcitonin gene-related protein, somatostatin, macrophage inhibitory factor, and complement inhibitors.
Third, resident stromal cells and lymphatic endothelial cells that

T cells that
recognize peripheral tissue-restricted self-antigens.
Thus, it
has been proposed that stromal cells in lymph node and tissues
provide a means to eliminate T cells that bind to tissue-restricted
antigens not expressed in the thymus. In addition to these general
mechanisms, the anterior chamber of the eye elicits a uniquely
altered immune response through a complex multistep process
termed “anterior chamber-associated immune deviation” (ACAID)
that leads to a dampened, less tissue-destructive response.
Although ACAID was long thought to be important for tolerance,
it has been argued that its primary function may be to modulate
the immune response so the eye can respond to infection without
endangering ocular integrity.
Immune deviation, wherein modulation of cytokine signaling skews mature CD4
T cell subset differentiation, constitutes
another possible mechanism to limit tissue damage from autoreactivity. Some T cell–driven autoimmune tissue pathology depends
on the polarization of autoreactive clones toward a Th1 cytokine
secretion profile.
Altering the balance of differentiation statepromoting cytokines may result in, for example, skewing of
pathogenic T cells away from Th1 and toward a protective, Th2
cytokine secretion profile. In experimental autoimmune uveitis,
activation of natural killer T cells with
α

IFNγ

Th1 and Th17 effector responses and protection from experimental autoimmune uveitis.
In these models, autoreactive T cells
are activated but do not produce the pro-inflammatory factors
necessary for tissue damage.
In addition to establishing central and peripheral tolerance in
the resting state, the host has capacity to limit the risk of autoimmune tissue injury posed by broad-based T cell activation and
expansion occurring during appropriate protective responses.
Dynamic regulation of activated T cells is achieved through
upregulation of inhibitory receptors such as CTLA4 and PD-1,
expression of proapoptotic receptors such as CD95, synthesis
of metabolic regulators such as indoleamine-2,3-dioxygenase
(IDO),
and action of intra-cellular proapoptotic factors such as
Bim.
Deficiencies of such mediators that control the magnitude
of T cell responses are associated with expansion of T lymphocyte
populations and with autoimmunity in varying degrees.

### B Cell Tolerance

The importance of B cells for the progression of clinical autoimmunity has been powerfully supported by observed therapeutic
effects of specific B cell–depleting therapies such as rituximab on
human inflammatory arthropathies, anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic
antibody-associated vasculitis, SLE,
and other rheumatologic
autoimmune diseases. Moreover, B cell depletion has shown
promise even in autoimmune states considered to be largely mediated by T cells such as T1DM
and MS.
Therefore, substantial interest in defining both mechanisms of B cell tolerance and
its specific role in autoimmune disease has developed.
Cellintrinsic functions of B cells relevant to autoimmunity include,
most obviously, production of autoantibody, but also the capacities to present antigen to T cells, to stimulate follicular DC, and
to exert regulatory function.
In parallel with T cells, tolerance in B cells is established through
complementary central and peripheral mechanisms. Autoreactivity in B lymphocytes is controlled principally through interactions
of such factors as B cell antigen receptor (BCR) signal strength
and context, location and nature of antigen, and developmental
stage. In contrast to T cell tolerance, the process of antigen receptor–mediated apoptosis (deletion) is comparatively less important
than receptor editing mechanisms for developing and maintaining
the B cell repertoire. Several signaling and developmental checkpoints considered important for controlling autoreactive B cells
and maintaining self-tolerance have been identified.
Central tolerance of B cells is established in the bone marrow
during pre-B to immature B cell transition as cells begin to express
rearranged Ig antigen receptors.
In response to ligation by bone
marrow stromal membrane-bound self-antigen, many cells bearing BCR with high affinity to membrane-bound self-antigen
undergo receptor editing, wherein immunoglobulin light [L]
chain sequence is altered followed by surface Ig reappearance. To
a lesser extent, self-antigens induce B cell clonal deletion. In contrast to membrane-bound antigens, soluble self-antigens induce
both BCR receptor editing and anergy.
Anergic B cells are
detectable in the periphery as an IgD
IgM

or in
mice as splenic transitional 3 (T3) B cells.
Anergic B cells are
short-lived, potentially caused by their downregulated expression
of survival signal-transducing BAFFR. Moreover, anergic cells are
less able to enter B cell follicles, putting them at a competitive
disadvantage with other immature B cells.
Among peripheral B cells, the earliest tolerance checkpoint
occurs at the T1 B stage over a 2-day interval, prior to maturation
to T2 and later naive B cell developmental stages.
T1 B
cells are the immediate bone marrow emigrant population and
are dependent on BAFF for survival. Importantly, autoreactive
T1 B cells undergo apoptosis rather than activation when stimulated, which results in deletion of B cells that recognize peripheral
self-antigens that are not expressed in the bone marrow compartment. This periphery-located mechanism, which is unique to B
cells but which forms a parallel to

to drive cell activation, particularly pattern recognition receptors.
Notably, TLR7 signaling is central to the development of a subset

self-antigens can be deleted by a Fas-dependent mechanism.

### Defective Tolerance

Although it can be inferred that loss of tolerance underlies autoimmunity, the specific defects in tolerance establishment and
maintenance that underlie common autoimmune diseases have
been difficult to delineate, most likely because disease most often
occurs because of multiple partial defects operating at diverse cellular and molecular pathway checkpoints. Nevertheless, studies of
monogenic human diseases featuring autoimmune phenomena,
and of both spontaneous and induced animal models of autoimmune disease, have identified a wide range of defects within the
manifold layers of central and peripheral lymphocyte tolerance
that raise risk of real-world multigenic autoimmune disease. Such
defects are caused by diverse genetic abnormalities and are mediated by a variety of lymphoid and nonlymphoid cell types. A few
representative examples are discussed.
The importance of thymus-dependent central tolerance for the
establishment of immune homeostasis and prevention of autoimmunity has been illustrated after discovery that loss-of-function
mutations in the gene encoding the transcription factor, AIRE is
the cause of autoimmune polyglandular syndrome 1 (also called
autoimmune polyendocrinopathy-candidiasis-ectodermal dystrophy [APECED]). APECED is a rare inherited disease featuring
T cell–mediated damage or destruction of multiple endocrine
organs.
Airedeficient mice and patients harboring AIRE
mutations develop similar autoimmune syndromes. Disease in
both humans and rodents is caused by the reduced thymic stromal
expression of thousands of genes with limited, mostly extrathymic
(e.g., pancreatic beta cell-specific) tissue expression profiles, the
protein gene products of which are termed tissue-specific antigens
(TSA). Reduced transcription of AIRE-dependent genes results
in diminished expression of MHC complexes bound to TSA protein-derived peptides expressed on thymic medullary epithelium.
The resulting failure to eliminate T cells recognizing such peptides
by clonal deletion in the thymus results in maturation and migration to the periphery of CD4
T cells bearing high-affinity TCR
reactive with self-antigens. Within thymic medullary epithelium,
AIRE likely functions by binding repressive transcriptional regulatory complexes such as MBD1-ATF7ip that target specific methylated cytosine-phosphatidyl-guanine (CpG) dinucleotides in
genetic regions encoding tissue-specific antigens. AIRE also promotes positive selection of perinatal thymic generation of CD4
FOXP3
regulatory T cells (Tregs, discussed later) that express
peripheral TSA-reactive specificities.
AIRE exhibits sex hormone–biased thymic expression, and females display lower expression of peripheral tissue-specific genes, consistent with observed
higher female prevalence of disease.
Recently several additional
transcription factors, FEZF2 and PRDM1, were also shown to
regulate expression of peripheral tissue-specific antigens in thymic
medullary cells and to be important for suppressing autoimmune
phenomena in mice.
Autoimmune disease observed in animals bearing mutations in
genes that modulate TCR strength highlights the importance of
TCR signaling for selection of a tolerant T cell repertoire. A prime
example is the SKG arthritis model.
Here, a function-impairing
mutation in the C-terminal SH2 domain of ZAP70, a cytosolic
Syk family tyrosine kinase activated by the TCR complex
ζ

reduces TCR signal strength. The resultant altered thymocyte

conventional T cells and Tregs, and seemingly enhanced positive
selection of autoreactive T cells.
Interestingly, using several

altered by only slight differences in ZAP70-mediated signaling
strength.

presumably because immature B cells continue to undergo negative selection after egress from the bone marrow, allowing developing B cells direct exposure to TSA. A tolerance regulatory role
for lymphocyte-expressed microRNA (miRNA) was suggested in
experiments wherein overexpressed miRNA-148a causes escape
from BCR-induced central deletion.
MiRNA-148a promotes
cell survival by suppressing multiple autoimmune and tumor
suppressors, as well as proapoptotic genes in immature B cells.
High miRNA-148a expression facilitates lethal autoimmunity
in a mouse model of lupus. Defective central tolerance of B cells
in human autoimmunity has been suggested by the findings of
a higher frequency of naive mature B cells with self-reactivity in
human autoimmune syndromes, including SLE and RA.
Whether epigenetic mechanisms contribute to such augmented B
cell autoreactivity is unknown.

### Autoimmunity Caused by Activation of Nontolerant or Partially Tolerant T Cells

The other main theory is that autoimmunity develops through
the conventional activation of self-reactive T cells that have not
been deleted in the thymus and that remain oblivious to the corresponding self-antigen after emigration. Such ignorant T cells,
commonly found in the periphery of both healthy humans and
animals, can be activated by antigen presented by professional
APCs in the context of an innate inflammatory milieu. Once activated, T cells are able to gain access to virtually all tissues and,
when activated again locally, can elaborate pro-inflammatory
factors, causing tissue damage.
Breach of tolerance by this
mechanism is most often associated with organ-specific diseases,
presumably because TSA that are candidate activators for ignorant
self-reactive T cells are less likely to be expressed in the thymus.
This theory is supported by the finding that T1DM is prevented
in BB rats and NOD mice by intrathymic transplantation of
pancreatic islets.
Similarly, EAE can be prevented by intrathymic administration of either the immunizing antigen, myelin
basic protein, or its major encephalitogenic epitope,
and autoantibody production can be delayed in lupus-prone mice following intrathymic injections of polynucleosomes.
Taken together,
these data support the concept that autoimmunity can be caused
by the activation of lymphocytes, which are not susceptible to
central tolerance by virtue of the tissue-specific self-antigens that
they recognize. Notably, this theory does not imply the presence
of a defect in central tolerance, such as would be operative in the
APECED syndrome caused by AIRE deficiency.
Breach of tolerance by ignorant lymphocytes has been shown
to depend on many factors, including the (1) nature of the selfantigen, (2) extent of exposure to antigen, (3) antigen receptor
affinity, (4) frequency of autoreactive lymphocytes, (5) types and
levels of co-stimulatory molecule expression, (6) cytokine and
chemokine profiles, and (7) presence of inflammation.
It should also be emphasized that despite the well-documented
presence of autoreactive T cells in humans and rodents, earlierdescribed peripheral tolerance mechanisms are difficult to overcome. Consequently, supraphysiologic amounts of self-antigen,
strong adjuvant, specific MHC haplotypes, and a susceptible background are all typically required to reveal the functional presence
of peripheral autoreactive T cells and to break tolerance. These
findings are consistent with the fact that autoimmunity does not
develop in most humans or animals despite the presence of autoreactive T cells, and they speak to the robustness of peripheral tolerance. Although it is difficult to prove which of these theoretical
mechanisms are operable in patients, they provide frameworks for
understanding how autoimmunity can develop along with guidance in devising interventions.
As with conventional protective immune responses, key factors for the initiation of autoimmunity are innate inflammatory
and co-stimulatory factors that promote the initial activation
and expansion of resting autoreactive lymphocytes. Release of
self-antigens after cell damage or death, increased MHC/peptide
expression, upregulation of co-stimulatory factors, and activation
of professional APCs all may contribute to both conventional and
autoimmune responses.
Indeed, moderate to severe tissue
necrosis is often associated with some evidence of autoreactivity,
although this condition rarely progresses to clinical disease.
In autoimmune diseases, chronic cell and tissue injury is associated
with a continual release of self-antigens under conditions favorable for antigen presentation; co-stimulatory signaling promotes
epitope spreading and the activation of an expanding repertoire
of lymphocytes that recognize autoantigens beyond the initiating autoantigen.
This process is thought to at least partially
account for the often-progressive course of autoimmunity. Overall, these and other findings support the theory that, under certain
conditions such as infection or trauma, self-antigens are released.
When coupled with the presence of inflammatory factors and an
activated innate immune system, such self-antigen release may
trigger activation and expansion of previously ignorant autoreactive lymphocytes and progress to clinically evident disease.
In addition to the release of sequestered self-antigens, the initial
activation of naive autoreactive lymphocytes has been postulated
to occur in several other ways. Some investigators have suggested
that the initial response may be directed toward certain determinants
based on the concept of “crypticity,” wherein a hierarchy of dominant and cryptic epitopes can be established for a
given protein based on differences in peptide binding affinity to
the MHC, protein processing, preference for presentation by different APCs, and the constitutive prevalence of epitope-specific
T cells.
It is therefore posited that during thymic selection,
T cells that engage the few dominant epitopes are eliminated by
clonal deletion, whereas those recognizing the less antigenic, but
more abundant, cryptic epitopes are spared. The latter T cells then
emigrate to the periphery, where they can be activated by the corresponding cryptic epitope under certain inflammatory conditions.
Another mechanism by which self-antigens can activate nontolerant lymphocytes is through the production of neo self-antigens
after post-translational or chemical modifications. A prominent
example is the formation of citrullinated proteins generated by
the deimination of arginine residues by peptidylarginine deminase (PADI) enzymes. Serum autoantibodies against citrullinated
vimentin, collagen II, enolase, and other citrullinated proteins are
not only present in, and predictive markers of destructive joint

This hypothesis is supported by observations of

## Immunological Mechanisms of Tissue Inflammation and Dysfunction

The same effector mechanisms used by the immune system to
neutralize pathogens are utilized in autoimmunity to inflict a wide
range of deleterious effects on self-molecules, cells, and tissues.
These mechanisms, initially classified by Gell and Coombs in the
1960s, were broadly grouped into hypersensitivity types II to IV,
which encompass antibody-, immune complex-, and T cell–mediated processes, respectively (see
Table 21.2
).
In type II reactions, pathologic autoantibodies bind to self-antigens primarily located on cell surfaces or in tissues and mediate
autoimmune disease by three general mechanisms: (1) altering the

function of the target antigen, (2) promoting cell injury or death,
and (3) inducing inflammation. Blocking or enhancing self-molecule function represents a special kind of type II hypersensitivity response in which autoantibodies alone are causal in generating
pathologic function effects. Examples include the agonist anti-thyroid stimulating hormone receptor antibodies in Graves’ disease that
stimulate thyrocyte growth and production of thyroid hormone,
the anti-acetylcholine receptor antibodies that block neuromuscular transmission in myasthenia gravis, and antiβ
2-glycoprotein I
antibodies in antiphospholipid antibody syndrome that alter the
regulation of anticoagulant activity.
Cell injury or death in type
II hypersensitivity is mediated by the binding of IgM or IgG to surface antigens, leading to cell lysis directly by complement activation
or to phagocytosis via interaction of deposited C3 fragments with
CR1 and CR3 receptors. Bound IgG also promotes phagocytosis
through its interaction with Fc receptors. Examples include autoimmune hemolytic anemia, idiopathic thrombocytopenia, and autoimmune neutropenia. Finally, antibodies bound to tissue antigens
promote inflammation by activating complement, which generates
the chemoattractant C5a and leukocyte-activating C3 fragments,
and by Fc
γ
R binding, which activates immigrating leukocytes such
as neutrophils and macrophages, as well as tissue-resident mast cells
and basophils. These cells produce pro-inflammatory factors that
further expand the inflammatory response by recruiting and then
activating additional circulating leukocytes.
Type III responses are caused by abnormal deposition of
immune complexes of IgG antibody and soluble antigen in tissues.
Such complexes, which also contain bound C3 complement fragments, are normally cleared from the circulation by complement
receptors on red blood cells (RBCs) and by complement receptors
and Fc
γ
Rs on mononuclear phagocytes and platelets. However,
this clearance can be overwhelmed under certain conditions such
as overabundant production or immune complexes composed of
excess antigen wherein less antibody coverage reduces complement deposition and aggregation of Fc regions, leading to less
efficient clearance. Once deposited in tissues, immune complexes
initiate through complement activation and Fc
γ
R binding, the
same inflammatory cascades as type II responses.
Type IV hypersensitivity encompasses cell and tissue injury
mediated by activated T cells. Apart from direct evidence of this
mechanism in animal models using approaches that are not feasible
in human studies, indirect evidence supporting this mechanism
includes a higher frequency of autoreactive T cells with effector
function in patients with autoimmune diseases, immunopathologic
findings similar to T cell–mediated autoimmune models, and inhibition of disease with T cell–blocking agents such as cyclosporin
A.
T1DM and MS are examples of pathologic type IV reactions.
It should be mentioned that these classifications are generalized
and, for some diseases, the underlying effector mechanism may be
more complex. In RA, for example, the mechanism responsible for
injury or dysfunction probably involves more than one hypersensitivity type,
whereas in others, such as SLE, different clinical
manifestations are mediated by different mechanisms; for example,
anti-neuronal antibody-mediated CNS disease is a type II process
whereas glomerulonephritis has features of type III.

## Pathophysiology of Autoimmune Rheumatic Diseases

Although the general principles underlying loss of tolerance and
autoimmunity discussed previously provide a useful conceptual
foundation for understanding pathogenesis of autoimmune phenomena underpinning common rheumatic diseases, pathophysiology observed in patients reflects multicomponent immune
dysregulation that is disease state specific. The generation of
anti-nuclear antibody production in SLE and the progression
of destructive joint disease in RA are notable examples of multiphasic processes that are briefly discussed in the context of their
broader significance to autoimmune diseases.
Several findings provide mechanistic insight into the question
of why anti-nuclear antibodies are virtually always present in SLE
despite substantial genetic and clinical heterogeneity.
First,
autoreactive B cells are activated when self-reactive BCRs bind
to nucleic acid–containing antigens (nucleosomes, oxidized mitochondrial DNA,
or ribonucleoprotein). BCR–antigen complexes are endocytosed, and nucleic acids engage TLR7 and TLR9
within lysosomes. Combined BCR and PRR signaling drive a gene
expression program that includes upregulation of MHC Class II
and T cell co-stimulatory ligands CD80/CD86. The resulting
activated B cells act as potent APCs for T lymphocytes but are also
induced to undergo class-switch recombination and produce IgG
autoantibodies. Plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) and DCs are then activated following uptake of autoantibody-nucleic acid-containing
complexes by Fc
γ
RIIA (Fc
γ
RIII in mice). Elaboration of lupuspromoting cytokines such as type I IFN and BAFF by pDCs and
DCs as well as enhanced antigen presentation are thought to
further drive loss of tolerance, activation of autoreactive B cells,
and higher-affinity autoantibody production, thereby resulting in
an amplification loop. Thus in persons susceptible to SLE, confinement of nucleic acid–binding TLRs to endolysosomes is not
sufficient to block cellular activation by normally substimulatory
amounts of self-nucleic acids. This mechanism provides an explanation for the high prevalence of autoantibodies in SLE that bind
to nucleic acids such as DNA and to antigens physically associated
with nucleic acids such as histones, ribonucleoproteins, myeloperoxidase (ANCA), and surface molecules derived from many
cell types including platelets, lymphocytes, neutrophils, and even
RBCs. Autoantibodies to cell surface epitopes are likely induced
by apoptotic bodies containing nucleic acids.
Importantly, this
model suggests the possibility that other autoimmune diseases
might also be mediated by specific PRRs.
Autoimmune pathophysiology of joint disease in RA is best
understood for the HLA-DR1*401 (DR4)- and anti-cyclic citrullinated protein antibody (ACPA)–positive patient subset, in
which common and specific pathways together underpin disease
progression through three clinical phases.
An initial preclinical phase is thought to be characterized by activation of T
cells and B cells at extraarticular sites including gut, respiratory,
and oral epithelium. Driven by local inflammatory responses to
microbes at these sites, increased citrullination of both bacteria
and host proteins occurs via activity of peptidylarginine deiminase released from apoptosing granulocytes and monocytes. The
HLA-DRB1 shared epitope, which has been shown to specifically
bind and present citrullinated self-peptides, is thought to enhance
likelihood of productive antigen presentation to T cells reactive
with cyclic citrullinated peptides. Isotype-switched IgA and IgG
autoantibodies that react with citrullinated peptides fibrinogen,
vimentin,
α
-enolase, and other host proteins (ACPA) then appear.
The specific triggers of anti-citrullinated peptide-specific T and
B cells leading to a loss of tolerance, proliferation, and activation
observed in RA patients at high risk of erosive joint disease have
been the subject of intense study. Accumulating evidence points
strongly toward the oral, respiratory, and gastrointestinal epithelia

as sites where activated innate immune cells instigate breaches
in T and B cell tolerance, generating a limited ACPA response
prior to joint involvement. Activating stimuli at these sites may
likely include inflammation, such as occurs in periodontal disease (PD),
toxins such as cigarette smoke,
and autoreactive T
cell–inducing intestinal microbiota
(discussed further below).
Multiple lines of evidence support a triggering role for periodontal
disease, which features chronic inflammatory injury to gingival
mucosa. PD is more common in RA, especially in ACPA-positive patients
and in patients with active, treatment-refractory
inflammatory joint disease.
RA patients with PD experience
frequent bacteremia with oral cavity–derived organisms, coincident with increased circulating inflammatory monocytes and with
clinical disease flares.
The oral cavity bacteria repeatedly found
in RA patient blood exhibit extensive citrullination and are recognized by hypermutated ACPA produced by blood-borne plasmablasts. These findings strongly suggest that repeated translocation
of mouth-originated citrullinated bacteria may be foundational to
both triggering innate immunity, and for initiation and expansion
of adaptive immune responses, in RA.
The pre-clinical phase characterized by detectable ACPA and
RFs typically lasts for years, and higher titers of ACPA increase
likelihood of progression. The next, early “clinically evident”
arthritis phase is characterized by inflammation (featuring edema,
cellular infiltrates, hypervascularity) occurring in synovial tissue.
Within joints, immune complexes and activated T cells engender
Fc receptor- and cytokine-mediated stimulation of macrophages,
synovial fibroblasts, endothelial cells, mast cells, osteoclasts, and
platelets,
and FcR-induced activation concurrent with the
eventual production of pro-inflammatory factors including TNF,
IL-1, IFNγ
, IL-17A, IL-23, chemokines, matrix metalloproteinases, and osteopontin that contribute to synovitis, pannus formation, bone erosion, and cartilage destruction. Indeed, unique
populations of PD-1
CXCR5
CD4
T cells in RA synovium and
peripheral blood have been identified that express factors promoting B cell help and that likely contribute to this process.
The combined innate and adaptive immune activation at the
synovium leads to a third chronic “established” RA phase that is
characterized by increased numbers of activated fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS). FLS produce a wide array of pro-inflammatory
mediators that promote recruitment and activation of circulating
and resident cells.
The aggressive and invasive properties of FLS
are independent of B and T cells indicative of appreciable remodeling by epigenetic or other mechanisms.
Trafficking to joints
and proliferation of such activated fibroblast-like synovial cells
may underlie the progressive, additive joint disease observed clinically in RA.
Treatments that target the receptor tyrosine phosphatase sigma that is specifically upregulated on FLS in diseased
synovium can synergize with low-dose TNF inhibition and suppress experimental inflammatory arthritis,
strongly suggesting a
requirement for FLS in destructive synovitis. Molecular and cellular connections between neoantigen (citrullinated peptide)–driven
autoimmunity and the advent of the nonimmune FLS-dominated
destructive joint disease phase in RA remain under study.

## Genetics of Autoimmune Diseases

During the past three decades, substantial insight into genetic
autoimmune disease susceptibility factors has emerged from both
human and animal studies. Progress has been greatly facilitated
by major technical advances such as increasingly inexpensive and
rapid high-throughput DNA sequencing, but also the emergence
of large databases containing annotated whole genome data,
improved definition of genetic variations and haplotype frequencies in human populations, and consortia that coordinated participation of large numbers of autoimmune disease patients and
healthy persons.
Since the early years of the 21st century, the
field of genetic investigation progressed from testing a few specific
candidate susceptibility polymorphisms to genome-wide family
analyses of hundreds of cases, and even larger-scale genome-wide
association studies (GWAS) involving thousands of participants.
These experiments permitted not only verification of
long known genetic associations (e.g., HLA associations with RA
and SLE), but also identified many novel disease-predisposing
variants (typically single-nucleotide polymorphisms [SNPs]) with
modest effect sizes. GWAS have identified more than 100 candidate genes or loci in SLE and RA (metaanalyses and multiple
GWAS reviewed in ref 262) and numerous susceptibility loci in
other rheumatologic diseases such as systemic sclerosis, Kawasaki’s
disease, Behcet’s disease, spondyloarthropathies, and ANCAassociated vasculitis (reviewed in references,
http://www.
genome.gov/gwastudies
). Many autoimmune disease susceptibility alleles have known or suspected cellular biologic functions
within innate and adaptive immune systems. For example, in SLE,
candidate genes are involved in antigen presentation (HLA-DR3),
clearance of self-antigens (C1Q, C2, and C4), B and T cell receptor signaling (
PTPN22

regulation (

(PDCD1)

immune system or lupus. Together, these candidates provide clues
to pathogenetic pathways involved in human SLE, which indeed
has dovetailed well with findings from genetic studies in mice.
From GWAS and other studies, including those in animal
models, several general conclusions can be made about genetic
susceptibility in autoimmune disease. First, autoimmune diseases are associated with many susceptibility genes that have been
shown to have an impact on a wide range of immunologic, cellular, and end organ functions in ways that enhance, modify, or even
suppress relevant pathophysiologic processes.
Second, considerable genetic heterogeneity exists at both the
individual and population levels regardless of whether the phenotype is relatively uniform, as in RA, or diverse, as in SLE. Thus
although there are many predisposing genes, inheritance of only
a subset of these genes is generally sufficient for disease development. Whether this heterogeneity is caused by variants affecting a
few common pathways or altering function in numerous unique
pathways remains unclear.
Third, the vast majority of candidate genes or loci have only
modest effect sizes, with most odds ratios less than 1.5. Low effect
sizes comport well with the notion that genetic susceptibility to
most common autoimmune disease is polygenic, resulting from
concerted function of several, perhaps dozens, of predisposing
loci. Overall, however, for most candidate variants, low effect
size predicts difficulty in defining a clear-cut cellular or molecular
mechanism of variant autoimmune disease–predisposing action.
Fourth, some SNPs and associated markers are shared among
autoimmune diseases, suggesting the presence of common underlying mechanisms.
Noteworthy examples are the association of

RA, SLE, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, Graves’ disease, systemic

Fifth, common SNP-defined variants in aggregate account for
only a portion of overall heritability in autoimmune diseases, that
is, approximately 20% to 60%,
of which the HLA region
by itself typically accounts for a substantial part of overall disease
association. Several explanations for inability to account for missing heritability to date have been proposed. First, failed detection
because of inadequate SNP coverage or the presence of diseasepromoting non-SNP genomic variations such as copy number
variants. Second, a large number of common variants with modest to marginal effects (odds ratio <1.1–1.2) that are undetectable
despite large study sizes given that the statistical power is reduced
by both smaller effect size and lower variant frequency in the population. Indeed, for many traits, common single-nucleotide variations with small effect sizes below what is detectable by GWAS are
suspected to account for the majority of missing heritability.
And third, uncommon variants (1% to 5%) or rare disease-associated risk alleles (<1% frequency) are missed even in large GWAS.
It is estimated that 25,000 cases plus a replication set would be
required for detection of a genetic signal due to a causative rare
variant in a well-powered genome-wide study,
illustrating the
technical hurdles that impede further progress using the GWAS

care.
Sixth, most common SNP variants associated with autoimmune
diseases do not occur within coding regions. This observation
increases the difficulty of determining whether the disease-associated SNP variant sequence itself modulates biologic function and
drives disease susceptibility or, alternatively, the associated variant is in linkage disequilibrium with nearby biologic functionchanging sequence. A subset of disease-associated variants occurs

An example is the

have therapeutic potential.
Seventh, the more recent application of transcriptomics, the
high-throughput profiling of virtually all RNAs derived from
patient cells or tissues, is a powerful approach that complements
genome mapping studies and has led to the identification of therapeutic targets such as type I IFN, IL-1, and IL-17 for autoimmune

The ability to rapidly assess gene expression profiles
associated with clinical phenotypes–disease state/diagnosis, disease activity status, and response to treatment has great potential
for both advancing understanding of basic pathogenesis and for
improving many areas of clinical practice including diagnosis, risk
patient stratification, and monitoring.
Finally, information gleaned from genetic studies to date has
not translated into the clinic with assays for risk stratification
or other personalized medicine purposes, largely because of the
small effect sizes of risk alleles and the relatively high frequency
of known risk alleles combined with incomplete coverage of heritability.
Nevertheless, these data and newer technology-driven
approaches have helped define disease-relevant pathways, and
within that context have provided clues and support for developing therapeutic strategies.

## Sex and Autoimmunity

A significant female bias in autoimmunity has been observed for
as long as the disease states were clinically described. The female
predilection for many autoimmune syndromes was considered
an important clue to disease pathogenesis.
The strength of
this predilection varies among autoimmune disorders, with
female prevalence in the 80% to 95% range for thyroiditis, SLE,
Sjögren’s syndrome, and antiphospholipid syndrome; in the 60%
to 75% range for RA, scleroderma, myasthenia gravis, and MS;
and close to 50% for T1DM and autoimmune myocarditis.
Why some autoimmune diseases are affected more by sex than
others is not known, but the lack of common or distinguishing
characteristics in those diseases with female predominance suggests the possibility of multiple mechanisms.
Indeed, to differing extents depending on the disease, both sex
hormones and sex chromosomes have been implicated in greater
prevalence of autoimmune disease among women.
Substantial evidence from in vitro, animal, and clinical studies supports a
role for both female and male sex steroids in modifying the incidence and severity of autoimmunity. In persons with SLE, both
estrogens and prolactin have been shown to exacerbate disease. In
animal studies, sex steroids promote loss of tolerance and expansion of B cell populations, which, for estrogens, was also shown
to be associated with greater expression of anti-apoptotic factor
B cell lymphoma 2 and enhanced cellular survival.
Importantly, studies of lupus-prone mice involving combinations of
castration and hormone replacement convincingly demonstrated
a disease-promoting effect of female hormones on spontaneous
autoimmunity.
Evidence also supports a role for sex chromosome dosage in
autoimmune disease susceptibility. Persons with Klinefelter’s
syndrome (XXY) have a higher-than-expected incidence of SLE,
whereas there is underrepresentation of SLE in Turner’s syndrome
(XO).
Furthermore, using genetic and gonad manipulation
in mice that allowed direct comparison of the effects of one or two
X chromosomes on autoimmunity, susceptibility to both EAE in
SJL mice and TMPD-induced lupus was shown to be more severe
in mice with XX.
Another potential contributing factor is
the biallelic expression of the X chromosome
TLR7
gene in some
B cells, monocytes, and pDCs due to incomplete X chromosome
inactivation at the single cell level in females.
Biallelic expression in B cells correlates with more TLR7 protein, greater response
to TLR7 ligands, and a propensity to class switch, findings that
support an increased predisposition to lupus. T cells from SLE
patients and from lupus mice also demonstrate incomplete X
chromosome inactivation–associated impaired epigenetic controls and consequent upregulation of numerous X chromosome–
encoded genes.
Thus disruption of epigenetic processes such as
Xist-mediated maintenance of X chromosome inactivation could
lead to augmented lymphocyte signaling and function in SLE.
The influence of sex on the immune response to the microbiome

and other environmental factors has also been implicated in autoimmune disease susceptibility.
Together, these findings suggest that actions of several sex hormones, the number or extent
of inactivation of X chromosomes, and environmental influences
likely each contribute to observed sex bias in SLE.

## Microbial and Other Environmental Triggers

As alluded to previously, considerable evidence indicates that environmental factors exert major, yet variable, influence on the development of common autoimmune diseases. A major pathogenetic
role for the environment is strongly suggested by the low concordance rate (20% to 50%) among monozygotic twins for common
autoimmune diseases. Nevertheless, the nature of environmental
factors and the extent to which they contribute to disease induction and exacerbation remain poorly understood for most autoimmune disorders. The reasons for this lack of knowledge are likely
manifold and, depending on the disease, may include requirements for additive or multiplicative effects of environmental factors that individually contribute only small portions of the overall
effect, a highly variable or prolonged interval between exposure
and disease onset, and a low incidence of autoimmune disease
after exposure to a candidate trigger. Another impediment is
that commonly employed epidemiologic approaches to identifying autoimmune disease triggers can document association with,
but not establish causation by, candidate factors. Because of these
difficulties, animal studies have been invaluable in allowing controlled experimentation to test putative roles of disease-triggering
environmental triggers.
Investigative limitations notwithstanding, a broad range of
environmental factors have been implicated in autoimmune disease causation or susceptibility.
The most prominently cited
candidate factors are infection and exposure to microbial products, which are suggested by epidemiologic and animal studies to either enhance or inhibit autoimmunity depending on
the type of exposure and disease.
Rheumatologic disease
examples of suspected causative links between infection and disease include reactive arthritis after genitourinary or enteric infections, chronic Lyme arthritis resulting from infection with
Borrelia
burgdorferi
, and the association of oral
Porphyromonas gingivalis
(which expresses a peptidylarginine deiminase that can citrullinate proteins) with RA. Several mechanisms by which microbes
are postulated to induce or exacerbate autoimmunity include (1)
molecular mimicry,
as discussed earlier; (2) bystander activation of autoreactive T cells, potentially occasioned by breaches in
gut, respiratory, or mucosal epithelial barriers that normally limit
host–pathogen interactions, by pathogen-activated APC
; (3) inflammation of the target tissue and the release of immunologically hidden self-antigens
; (4) enhanced production of
disease-promoting cytokines, such as IFNα
,
by infectionresponsive cells; (5) expansion of pathogenic immune cells including Th17 T cells and plasmacytoid DCs
; and (6) release
of metabolites that affect the frequencies of autoreactive and regulatory T cells or inflammation.
Clearly, microbes impinge on susceptibility to autoimmune
disease. Gut microbiota, specifically commensal bacteria, are
critical for immune homeostasis in adult metazoan hosts. Tonic
signaling engendered by GM through innate immune receptors
TLR and NOD1 is needed for maturation of both gut barrier
and maintenance of gut-associated lymphoid tissues. Observations of inverse correlations between microbial exposure burden
in early life and incidence of atopic diseases, asthma, and even
autoimmune diseases such as T1D and MS gave rise to the
“hygiene hypothesis” in the late 1980s.
This concept has since
been supported by additional epidemiologic and experimental
data, although individual microbial pathogens or commensals
and type of exposure required for effects remain uncertain.
Alterations in the localization, abundance, and function of gut
microbiota may drive loss of tolerance and tissue damage.
Disrupted signaling through innate sensors and gut translocation by
commensal bacteria leads to colitis and T cell–dependent autoimmune diabetes.
T1DM incidence increases in NOD mice
housed under “clean” laboratory conditions and lessens with exposure to microbial products. Thus alterations in gut microbiota,
which can interact with and modulate the host immune and
inflammatory apparatus, could account for major environmental
effects on disease susceptibility and even influence female sex
bias.
Disease-inducing effects have occasionally been attributed to individual microbes (see below), but most mechanisms
appear to be rooted in dysbiosis or imbalance between the types
of organisms comprising the microflora in aggregate. Additionally, in some animal models, autoimmune disease develops under
germ-free conditions, thereby excluding an absolute requirement
for microbial environment or infection. In such cases, the suspected autoimmune trigger is provided by self-derived antigens,
most likely produced by cell damage. Examples include T1DM in
NOD mice and BB rats, APECED in Aire-deficient mice, IPEX in
the presence of Foxp3 deficiency, and lupus in MRL-lpr mice.
Interestingly, in MRLlpr
mice, the addition of a filtered diet
containing fewer pathogen-associated molecular patterns reduces
disease severity. This finding suggests that microbial products at
least partially affect disease susceptibility in this model and points
to the difficulty of completely excluding a role for microflora.
Striking relationships between the gut microbiome, altered
immune system homeostasis, and autoimmune diseases have been
demonstrated.
Interactions between commensal microbiota,
metabolic intermediaries, and diet components can influence likelihood of autoimmunity. One mechanism whereby gut microbiome can drive or restrain autoimmunity is through metabolism
of dietary elements. In the breast-fed infant, bacteria capable of
utilizing human milk oligosaccharides are key for production of
indole 3 lactic acid, which induces galectin-1 expression in intestinal T cells. Galectin-1 promotes cytokine-mediated skewing
toward Th1 and regulatory phenotypes, and away from intestinal
inflammation-associated Th17 and Th2 fates, likely reducing proclivity for autoimmune disease.
Germ-free NOD mice develop
T1DM as a consequence of dietary gluten, but gluten-dependent
disease requires gut bacteria expression of proteases that can break
down gluten.
Bacterial digestion of gluten generates T cell–activating gluten peptides and enhanced innate immune cell reactivity
to lipopolysaccharides, which are required for disease expression.
Dysbiotic microbiota have been observed in mice bearing multiple lupus susceptibility genes.
When microbiota taken from
diseased lupus mice is transferred into healthy animals, autoantibody production and immune cell activation results. Transferable autoimmunity in this case depends on altered tryptophan
metabolites in dysbiotic feces. A diet low in tryptophan prevents
autoimmunity in the animals, whereas a high tryptophan diet promotes it. These findings illustrate potential for interplay between
microbial dysbiosis, gut microbial metabolism, and genetic susceptibility to lupus.
Another mechanism of microbiome action in autoimmune
pathogenesis is disruption of the intestinal barrier that normally sequesters microbes away from autoreactive lymphocytes.

Pathogenic effects of dysbiosis can trigger SLE. The gut pathobiont
Enterococcus gallinarum
is capable of inducing SLE in animals featuring autoantibody production and tissue infiltrates.
E.
gallinarum
promotes leakiness of the gut mucosal barrier, leading
to colonization of extraintestinal organs and SLE.
Impressively,
lupus was prevented in mice by treatment with antibiotics or vaccination against
E. gallinarum
. In another study, a five-fold greater
representation of
Ruminococcus gnavus
was detected in large SLE
patient cohorts; presence of the bacterium correlated with reduced
taxonomic complexity of the microbiome, antibodies to
R. gnavus
, increased disease activity, and greater severity.
A third mechanism whereby microbiome may impinge on
autoimmune pathogenesis is through recruitment/expansion of
T cells reactive with post-translational modified antigens, such
as cyclic citrullinated peptides. A commensal bacterium,
Ruminococcaceae Subdoligranulum
, was identified in RA patient feces
by virtue of its binding to ACPA.
Germ-free mice colonized
with a single
R. Subdoligranulum
strain exhibit autoantibody production and expansion of splenic Th17 cells. Colonized animals
also develop swollen joints and display Ig and complement deposition in synovium; these features require CD4
T cells and B cells.
Together, these findings suggest that specific bacterial species can
both stimulate gut-based humoral and T cell responses and trigger
joint disease.
The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the potential for
viral infection to drive humoral autoimmunity. Multiple novel
autoantibodies against a range of self-antigens, including IFN
α
prothrombotic proteins, and G protein–coupled receptors, commonly arise in SARS-CoV2-infected patients.
Interestingly,
high-titer autoantibodies against these antigens correlate with worse
COVID-19 disease severity and complications like thromboembolism, whereas infection-driven autoantibodies against chemokines
associate with better outcomes and protection against post-acute
coronavirus syndrome.
Although an array of autoimmune disorders has sporadically been reported following vaccination against
SARS-CoV2, it is notable that vaccination, unlike natural infection, does not drive pre-existing autoantibody titers higher.
Nonmicrobial environmental factors also may play roles in the
development of systemic autoimmunity. These include (1) drugs,
such as procainamide, gold salts, and IFNs, that typically cause
mild autoimmunity that resolves after discontinuation; (2) trauma,
exemplified by sympathetic ophthalmia caused by penetrating
injury to the eye globe; and (3) diverse environmental agents such
as adulterated oil (which caused toxic oil syndrome), ultraviolet
radiation (which exacerbates systemic autoimmunity), iodine
(which causes autoimmune thyroiditis), silica (which is associated
with RA, SLE, and systemic sclerosis), and tobacco smoke (which
is associated with ACPA and HLA-DR4
RA).

## Conclusion

Since 1904, when Donath and Landsteiner reported the first evidence of self-reactivity in paroxysmal hemaglobinuria,
the field
of autoimmunity has progressed tremendously at both the clinical and basic science levels. Notably, substantial progress has been
made since the previous edition of this book in environmental
factors, particularly within understanding of how microbiome
and host–pathogen interactions may promote systemic autoimmunity and pathophysiology and with the continued refinement
of the many pathways that connect cause with immunopathology.
Recent advances suggest how the innate response plays key roles
in both initiating autoimmunity and determining the nature of
adaptive autoimmune response and disease phenotype. Genetic
studies have progressed to larger GWAS projects and with larger
focus on identifying and characterizing causative variants underlying mechanisms. Advances in the understanding of disease
pathogenesis primarily in more common rheumatologic diseases
have further solidified and extended existing frameworks on
which researchers can build on to identify critical pathways and
molecules. Of importance to patient care has been the successful bench-to-bedside translation approach that has resulted in the
introduction of new therapies to the clinic. However, the highly
sought-after objective of specifically reestablishing tolerance in
patients has remained elusive despite considerable effort, and currently used therapies are only partially effective and are frequently
associated with significant side effects. Consequently, the critical
needs for investigation of the basic processes that underpin autoimmune disease, and for leveraging resulting knowledge for development of safer and more effective therapies, remain.
