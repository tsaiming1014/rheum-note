## Introduction

Hemoglobin (Hb) has a tetrameric structure consisting of two
α
-globin and two non–
α
-globin polypeptide chains, each associated with a heme group. The interaction of these chains is responsible for the quaternary structure of the Hb molecule and its
normal function. The fundamental role of Hb is oxygen transport.
Hemoglobinopathies are inherited diseases caused by mutations in globin genes resulting in varying defects in the synthesis
and structure of hemoglobin. Abnormalities in hemoglobin can
alter red cell shape, deformability, and viscosity. Approximately
1000 mutations have been described
; however, most are
not
associated with clinical disease. The gene mutations that result in
sickle cell disease and thalassemia syndromes cause diseases that
often involve the musculoskeletal system and will be discussed in
this chapter.

## Thalassemia

The term
thalassemia
is derived from a Greek term that roughly
means “the sea (Mediterranean) in the blood” and was used to
describe anemias in people from Italy and Greece. The term is
now used to refer to inherited defects in the synthesis of one or
more of the
α
- or
β
-hemoglobin subunits.
Individual syndromes
are named according to the globin chain of which the synthesis
is adversely affected. For example,
α
-globin chains are absent or
reduced in patients with
α
-thalassemia;
β
-globin chains are absent
or reduced in patients with
β
-thalassemia;
δ
- and
β
-globin chains
are absent or reduced in patients with
δβ
-thalassemia; and so on.
Thalassemias are inherited as pathologic alleles of one or more of
the globin genes located on chromosomes 11 (
β
) and 16 (
α
) and
range from total deletion or rearrangement of the loci to point
mutations that impair transcription, processing, or translation of
globin messenger RNA.
As a consequence of reduced globin chain production, a reduction of functioning Hb tetramers occurs. Impaired globin synthesis leads to an imbalance of the individual
α
- and
β
-subunits.
Free or “unpaired”
α
-,
β
-, and
γ
-globin chains are either highly
insoluble or form homotetramers (Hb H and Hb Bart’s) that cannot release oxygen normally or are relatively unstable and precipitate as the cell ages. Excess
α
-globin chains, for example, continue
to accumulate and precipitate in individuals with
β
-thalassemia.
Unpaired subunits are the major sources of morbidity and mortality (
Tables 125.1 and 125.2
). Several genotypes are associated
with thalassemia. Hypochromia and microcytosis are found in all
patients with thalassemia. In milder forms of the disease, these
changes may be subclinical, causing few or no symptoms (thalassemia minor), while patients with severe disease typically present
with transfusion-dependent anemia due to hemolysis (thalassemia
major).
Thalassemias have been encountered in virtually every ethnic
group and geographic location but are most common in the Mediterranean basin and tropical or subtropical regions of Asia and
Africa. The “thalassemia belt” extends from the Mediterranean
across the Arabian Peninsula, extending through Turkey, Iran, and
India to southeastern Asia and southern China. The prevalence of
thalassemia in these regions ranges from 2.5% to 15%. As with
sickle cell anemia, thalassemia is most common in areas affected
by endemic malaria. Malaria occurring in thalassemia heterozygotes results in milder infection and confers a selective advantage on reproductive fitness. The gene frequency of thalassemia
has become fixed and remains highest in populations exposed to
malaria over many centuries.

although in half of the cases, no precipitating factor is found.
Repeated splenic infarctions in childhood result in “autosplenectomy” and loss of splenic function by age 6 to 8 years in approximately one-half of patients.

### Epidemiology

Sickle cell disease is the most common hemoglobinopathy worldwide. The prevalence is higher in Sub-Saharan Africa, the Mediterranean basin, Middle East, and Asian/India, due to the protection
the mutation provides against severe malaria.
In Europe, it is estimated that 20,000 to 25,000 individuals are affected with sickle
cell disease, although numbers are increasing due to immigration,
particularly in Northern Europe.
Sickle cell disease is also found
in Greece and southern Italy, where
β
-thalassemia is also endemic.
It is estimated that 72,000 to 98,000 individuals in the United
States have Hb SS, and around 300,000 babies are born with
the disease worldwide each year; the numbers are expected to
increase with gains in life expectancy related to systematic newborn screening and earlier intervention. Although life expectancy
has improved with sickle cell disease, mortality remains high from
both acute and chronic complications.

### Screening and Diagnosis

Preconception, antenatal, and neonatal screening have been implemented in the United States, Europe, India, and in some parts
of the middle East and Africa. Preconception screening identifies
asymptomatic parents whose newborn might be affected by sickle
cell disease. Antenatal screening is performed with chorionic villus
analysis, but new techniques have been in development to detect
abnormal fetal DNA as early as week 4 of gestation. Universal
newborn screening is mandatory in the United States and in most
European countries for both sickle cell disease and thalassemia.
Diagnosis is confirmed using DNA analysis to detect hemoglobin
variants. Such screening tests vary in other areas of the world; some
African counties, for example, have developed screening programs
that are not universal and have encountered multiple challenges.

### Treatment

Optimal care for patients with sickle cell disease and thalassemia
includes a multidisciplinary approach to treat the multiple manifestations and prevent end organ damage. In addition to supportive measures including red blood cell transfusion, a growing

number of medications have been demonstrated to provide therapeutic benefit.
Hydroxycarbamide (hydroxyurea) continues to be the first-line
management to treat sickle cell disease. It reduces the number
of painful episodes, transfusions, and hospitalizations, but only
about two-thirds of adult patients have a response to this drug.
In recent years, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration
approved three novel therapies for the treatment of sickle cell disease. l-Glutamine was approved in 2017 for patients 5 years or
older with sickle cell disease. It decreases the frequency of painful
episodes and hospitalizations.
Crizanlizumab is a monoclonal
antibody that binds to P-selectin. Triggered by inflammation,
P-selectin expression is increased in endothelium and platelets and
promotes vaso-occlusion in sickle cell disease. It was approved in
2019 to reduce the frequency of painful episodes in patients with
or without concomitant hydroxyurea. Voxelotor is a polymerization inhibitor also approved in 2019 for the treatment of sickle cell
disease in patients 12 years or older. Voxelotor increases oxygen
affinity, thus decreasing hemolysis and improving hemoglobin levels without increasing the frequency of vaso-occlusive episodes.
Hematopoietic stem cell transplant with a fully matched donor
represents a potential cure for sickle cell disease,
although transplants are expensive, are not widely available, and have associated
risks such as infections and life-threatening immune-system reactions with nonautologous donors.
Gene therapy is emerging as a potential treatment and possible cure for sickle cell disease.
Conventional gene therapy, also
known as gene addition, typically involves inserting new genes.
Modification of harvested stem cells with a viral vector that is
modified with the gene to be inserted. The virus then searches
out the cells and inserts the gene into the cells’ DNA, correcting the sickle cell mutation, after which the cells are transplanted
into the patient. Gene editing is more specific when the faulty
DNA sequence is removed and a piece of laboratory-created DNA
is inserted. In both approaches, the modified DNA dictates the
formation of a normal, working protein. A pilot study
reported
reduction or elimination of clinical manifestations using a viral
vector in six patients with sickle-cell disease.

## Musculoskeletal Involvement: Sickle Cell Disease

### Vaso-occlusive Crisis of Bone

Bone involvement in sickle cell patients is the most common clinical manifestation of the disease (
Table 125.3
), both as an acute
process and as a chronic symptom.
Painful osteo-articular crisis was the most common reason for emergency care of children
with sickle cell disease in an African population (58.6%).
Nearly
all patients experience painful vaso-occlusive crises, which usually begin in late infancy and continue throughout life. Patients
typically complain of pain; the episode can affect any area of the
body, with the chest, low back, and extremities most commonly
affected. Abdominal pain may mimic acute abdomen of other
causes. Fever is often present, even in the absence of infection.
Although microvascular occlusion can occur in any organ, it is
common in tissues with low blood flow, such as the bone marrow,
where deoxygenation and polymerization of Hb S leads to bone
thrombosis, infarction, and necrosis. Spinal cord infarction can
occur.

### Dactylitis

In children younger than 7 years of age, vaso-occlusion can manifest in the bone marrow of the small bones of the hands and feet,
resulting in dactylitis
(also called hand–foot syndrome). Patients

### Growth Disturbance

A majority of children with sickle cell disease show declines in
growth compared with normal peers.
Puberty onset is delayed

on average by 12 to 24 months, as is skeletal age. Factors such as
nutritional status and inactivity can potentially be optimized and
to decrease the adverse effects on growth. Delayed skeletal maturation in children can also occur.

### Osteopenia and Osteoporosis

Osteopenia and osteoporosis are common in Hb SS disease, especially
given the young age of disease onset in patients, overlapping with the
critical period of bone development. The lumbar spine appears to be
the site most often affected. Low body mass index is a consistent correlate of decreased bone density, as are low vitamin D levels.

### Osteonecrosis

Vaso-occlusion resulting in infarction of articular surfaces of
long bones occurs most commonly in the femur, followed by the
humerus, knees (distal femur or proximal tibia), and small joints
of the hands and feet.
Although previously thought to occur
with increased frequency in Hb SC disease, its prevalence is greatest in patients with Hb SS disease.
By the age of 33 years, 50%
of patients experience avascular necrosis of the femoral head. The
presence of concurrent deletional
α
-thalassemia and a history of
frequent vaso-occlusive crises are classic risk factors for femoral
avascular necrosis. Patients present with chronic joint pain with
a progressive decrease in range of motion of affected joints. Multiple joints are commonly involved. The vast majority of untreated
patients with avascular necrosis progress to femoral head collapse
within 5 years. Symptoms nearly always precede hip collapse. The
duration to collapse appears to be shorter with more advanced
stages at study onset, with a majority of patients progressing to
symptomatic avascular necrosis that requires intervention.
Avascular necrosis has been treated with a number of modalities including core decompression, osteotomy, bone grafting,
surface arthroplasty, and joint replacement.
The only randomized trial in avascular necrosis compared core decompression
and physical therapy versus physical therapy alone and did not
show a difference in outcome between the two arms; however,
follow-up was short, and a significant number of stage III hips
were included. Core decompression is a useful option in earlystage avascular necrosis. Several studies associate total hip replacement in individuals with sickle cell disease with a higher rate of
orthopedic and medical complications
; however, other studies
show a lower rate of orthopedic complications.
Structural bone
diseases in people with sickle cell disease make joint replacement
technically challenging.

### Iron Metabolism

Red blood cell transfusion is a mainstay in the treatment of
sickle cell disease complications.
Most patients receive at least
one transfusion in their lifetime, usually for acute complications.
Blood transfusions increase arterial oxygen pressure and Hb oxygen affinity, thereby reducing red blood cell sickling and improving
microvascular perfusion. Furthermore, regular blood transfusion
regimens suppress endogenous erythropoiesis and therefore the
production of red cells containing Hb S. Iron metabolism and
consequently patterns of iron loading differ in sickle cell disease
and thalassemia.
Erythropoiesis is variably increased in sickle cell disease, but it
is not ineffective. Unlike patients with thalassemia, patients with
nontransfused sickle cell disease do not develop systemic iron
overload because of increased iron absorption, and they may actually present with iron deficiency, possibly related to intravascular
hemolysis and the resulting excessive urinary loss of iron. Inflammation, which is part of the pathophysiology of sickle cell disease,
increases synthesis of hepcidin and consequently decreases iron
absorption and enhances retention of iron within the reticuloendothelial system. As a result, tissues and organs are less commonly
affected by iron overload in sickle cell disease than in thalassemia.
When it does occur, musculoskeletal manifestations of iron overload include a symmetric, polyarticular arthropathy with bony
enlargement. Notably, iron overload preferentially affects the second and third metacarpophalangeal joints in addition to the proximal interphalangeal joints, wrists, elbows, shoulders, and hips.

## Musculoskeletal Involvement: Thalassemia

### Decreased Bone Density and Osteoporosis

Skeletal changes in people with untreated thalassemia are related
to bone marrow hyperplasia, cortical resorption, rarefaction of
cancellous bone with coarsening of the trabeculae, and a generalized decrease in bone density (
Table 125.4
).
In patients with
thalassemia major, onset of the radiographic changes can be seen
in their first year of life. Involvement of the spine, skull and facial
bones, ribs, and metaphyses of long bones are typical. Osteoporosis is present in a substantial proportion of individuals with thalassemia intermedia (49% in one cohort), with higher bone density
values in those never transfused, as well as in those who had not
undergone splenectomy.
In the spine, osteoporosis and cortical thinning are associated
with vertebral compression fractures (
Fig. 125.4
). After an initial
increase in the height-to-width ratio of the vertebral body, thinning of the subchondral bone plates occurs and the bones become
biconcave and wedge-shaped as multiple compression fractures
occur. Patients can experience scoliosis and kyphosis.
Lateral plain
radiographs of the spine may show a “bone-within-a-bone” appearance as the end plate depression is evident (
Fig. 125.5
). MRI demonstrates a predominance of red marrow in the vertebrae and early
degeneration of the intervertebral discs of the lower thoracic and
lumbar spine.
Abnormal intravertebral iron deposition can also
be seen. One small clinical trial enrolled women with thalassemia
major–related osteoporosis and demonstrated increased bone mass
and decreased bone turnover with strontium ranelate therapy.

## Iron Metabolism

The anemia of thalassemia major typically becomes symptomatic between 6 months and 2 years of age, requiring the institution of a regular transfusion program. As a consequence of
continuous anemia, erythropoiesis, although inefficient, can be
intense; the bone marrow undergoes an marked expansion with
consequent distortion of facial features, and the plasma volume
increases. In addition, hepatosplenomegaly develops. A regimen
maintaining a minimum Hb concentration of 9.5 to 10.5 g/
dL can prevent the aforementioned complications and fosters
normal growth at least until puberty. Transfusion therapy, however, may result in iron overload in various tissues. Thus iron
chelation therapy prevents accumulation of iron, promotes normal growth, and improves survival in patients with thalassemia
requiring chronic transfusions.
Deferoxamine chelation is
effective but challenging to use because of its cost and the inconvenience of prolonged subcutaneous infusions. Deferiprone,
an oral iron chelator, is more often used and offers improved
compliance over deferoxamine. Mild arthralgias and/or arthritis
involving the knees may occur in up to 38% of patients receiving deferiprone,
which is usually self-limited or responsive to
conservative management with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
agents. Some patients, however, can develop severe arthropathy
related to deferiprone, requiring discontinuation of iron chelation therapy. Subchondral erosions can be seen on radiographs
of the long bones of the hands in symptomatic children treated
with deferiprone.

## Comorbid Rheumatic Disease

### Autoimmune Disorders

Autoimmune diseases, including rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), have been reported in
patients with hemoglobinopathies. Up to 50% of patients are
positive for anti-nuclear antibodies.
Several cases of mild recurrent arthritis have occurred in patients with thalassemia.
RA
is uncommonly diagnosed in patients with sickle cell disease.
They can present with similar symptoms, although some authors
have reported RA in younger patients with sickle cell disease.
Patients carrying both diagnoses have more hospital admissions
and more radiographic erosive changes.
The prevalence and
clinical features of RA in patients with thalassemia is unknown.
One group reported that rheumatoid factor was detected in 23%
of 90 patients, while anti-citrullinated protein antibodies were
detected in only 2% in patients with thalassemia or sickle cell
disease followed over a decade in Greece.
Patients with RA
had no other specific features that distinguished them from the
patients with only hematologic disease. Patients with sickle cell
disease and co-existing RA benefit from disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs), but data are lacking regarding the
safety profile of the available therapies. Methotrexate has been
used with improvement of the disease activity, pain, and quality
of life.
The safety of etanercept was preliminarily examined in
a small study of eight patients with sickle cell disease and concomitant RA.
Autoimmune disease in patients with sickle cell disease may
go undiagnosed, or diagnosis may be delayed as a result of shared
clinical manifestations and multiorgan involvement. The presence of SLE and sickle cell disease, although uncommon, has been
reported.
Recognition of an autoimmune disease in patients
with sickle cell disease may require the use of immunosuppressive
medications including glucocorticoids that have been linked to
exacerbations of sickle cell disease.
Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) is rarely recognized in patients
with sickle cell disease. One small study observed AIH in 13 of 77
patients, suggesting a higher prevalence than in the general population.
Standard immunosuppressive therapy is used in patients
with concomitant AIH and sickle cell disease with successful outcomes reported in patients undergoing liver transplantation.

### Gout

Increased red blood cell turnover in sickle cell disease leads to
hyperuricemia, as can renal tubular involvement and a decrease
in uric acid excretion. Hyperuricemia is a risk factor for declining renal function in patients with sickle cell.
A retrospective
review observed a 14% prevalence of gout in patients with sickle
cell disease. In this study, gout was more common among those
with Hb SS, hyperuricemia, and low hemoglobin.
As with concomitant autoimmune disease, gout can be underdiagnosed due
to low index of suspicion or confusion with other manifestations
of sickle cell disease.

## Conclusion

Hemoglobinopathies, particularly sickle cell disease and the thalassemias, are often associated with significant and varied musculoskeletal manifestations, including osteonecrosis, osteomyelitis,
arthropathies, and vertebral fractures. Perhaps under-recognized
in practice, awareness of the clinical presentations associated with
these manifestations is essential to facilitate early recognition and
effective management.
