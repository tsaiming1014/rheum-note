## Introduction

Immune function depends on the biologic activities of numerous small glycoprotein messengers termed
cytokines
, as well as
smaller peptides called
chemokines.
Broadly speaking, cytokines
can alter immune cell function and phenotype, and chemokines
are involved in the tissue-specific migration of leukocytes in vivo.
Structurally the cytokine family is diverse but can be divided into
multiple structurally conserved subfamilies. In contrast, the chemokine family is structurally relatively homogenous and defined
by the presence of a conserved cysteine motif in the mature amino
acid sequence. However, chemokines can be generally divided
into two subfamilies, namely CC and CXC (along with CX3C)
based on the location of cysteines. Typically, cytokines exhibit
broad functional activities that not only mediate effector and
regulatory immune function but also have wider effects across a
range of tissues and biologic systems. Additional features are the
capacity to work in synergy one with another and to utilize shared
signaling components.
As such, cytokines play a role not only
in host defense but also in a variety of normal physiologic and
metabolic processes. By this means they integrate de facto host
defense and host metabolic function. In contrast, chemokines
have a very specific role in regulating cellular recruitment at rest,
during inflammation, and during the development of adaptive
immune responses. Here we review the biology and biochemistry
of cytokines and chemokines and discuss their current and potential therapeutic use in immune and inflammatory disease.

Cytokine regulation within the cell can be usefully considered
at several levels (see
Fig. 29.2

on the recruitment of discrete transcription factors to the cytokine

of stimuli. Several transcription factors (e.g., nuclear factorκ

cytokine expression between individuals that could confer selective advantage against infection but could also increase susceptibility to, or progression of, autoimmunity. In general, the net effect
of haplotypes may be more important at the functional level or
only play a role in the context of networks where multiple minor
polymorphisms can synergize, particularly when their relevance
to disease entities is considered. Finally, a number of microRNAs
have been identified that regulate cytokine release in the context
of rheumatic diseases.
Post-transcriptional regulation is important in determining
longevity of cytokine expression. This regulation may operate by
promoting translational initiation, mRNA stability, and polyadenylation. AU-rich elements (AREs) within the 52 or 32 untranslated regions (UTRs) of cytokine mRNA are crucial for stability.
For TNF, regulatory proteins bind AREs to mediate such effects.
HuR and AUF1 exert opposing effects, stabilizing, and destabilizing ARE-containing transcripts.
TIA-1 and TIAR have been
identified as RNA recognition motif family members
that function as translational silencers. Macrophages from TIA-1–deficient
macrophages produce excess TNF, whereas TIA-1–deficient lymphocytes exhibit normal TNF release, suggesting distinctions in
mRNA regulation in discrete cell types.
Alternatively, cytokines
may generate stable mRNA a priori to assist with subsequent rapid
response in tissues. The IL-15 mRNA 52 UTR contains 12 AUG
triplets that significantly reduce the efficiency of IL-15 translation.
Deletion of this sequence permits IL-15 secretion.
Post-translational regulation also modulates cytokine expression via several mechanisms. Patterns of glycosylation are
important for cytokine function and may regulate intra-cellular
trafficking.
Modified leader sequences can alter intra-cellular
trafficking of cytokines. Some cytokines are translated without
functional leader sequences. Their secretion depends on nonconventional secretory pathways that are poorly understood. IL-1
β
employs, among other pathways, a purine receptor–dependent
pathway (P2X7) for cellular release.
Enzymatic activation of
cytokines is common, whereby nonfunctional promolecules are
cleaved to generate functional subunits. Examples include the
cleavage by caspase 1 of pro–IL-1
β
to generate active IL-1
β
and,
similarly, of pro–IL-18 to generate an active 18 kDa species.
This organized process occurs both sequentially (in time) and by
orientation within the cell (in space). IL-1 processing occurs in
a protein complex within the cytosol termed the
inflammasome.
The latter has attracted considerable interest as a therapeutic target in conditions such as crystal-induced arthritis and in diseases
that arise from mutations in certain inflammasome genes (e.g.,
cryopyrin; see
Chapters 11 and 18
).
Alternative processing pathways for cytokines include the serine proteases, proteinase 3 and elastase, and adamalysin family
members. Enzyme cleavage pathways operate within and outside
cells, providing for extra-cellular cytokine activation. Similarly, cell
membrane enzymes serve to cleave membrane-expressed cytokine.
Members of the adamalysin family regulate TNF release; a TNFconverting enzyme cleaves and mediates the release of TNF and
its receptors.
Extensive molecular machinery exists to regulate
tightly not only the production and stability of cytokine mRNA
but also its translation and cellular expression and distribution.
At each level, opportunities exist for intervention and therapeutic
cytokine modulation; thus far, however, no successful medicines
have emerged from this approach.

### Chemokine Nomenclature System

A systematic nomenclature system has been developed for chemokines.
This nomenclature system defines the chemokines
as ligands. Thus CC-chemokines are referred to as CC ligands,
or CCLs, CXC ligands as CXCLs and XC and CX3C ligands as
XCLs and CX3CLs. The numbering system simply refers to the
order in which the cDNA sequences for the chemokines were
deposited in the genomic databases. Thus, for example, CCL1
was the first CC-chemokine identified, and CCL28 was the most
recent and probably the last of the CC chemokines. This nomenclature system has been universally adopted by chemokine biologists, immunologists, and others working with these molecules.

### Chemokines in an Evolutionary Context

Chemokines and their receptors are vertebrate-specific molecules, and
pre-vertebrate species do not possess chemokines. Chemokines first
appeared in evolution approximately 600 million years ago in jawless fish,
and the primordial role for the first chemokine (almost
certainly CXCL12) was to regulate stem cell migration during development,
a function for which it is also essential in mammalian
embryos. Thus the ancient and highly conserved role for chemokines
was to regulate stem cell migration during embryogenesis.
From CXCL12, and through a process of gene duplication, the
chemokine system has expanded to the point that mammals have
approximately 45 different chemokines that involve themselves in
exquisitely subtle ways in regulating in vivo leukocyte migration. Nevertheless we retain the essential dependence on CXCL12 for successful embryologic development. In terms of receptors, the most ancient
of these is almost certainly the CXCL12 receptor CXCR4, which is
expressed on stem cell populations required to migrate within the
embryo, as well as ACKR3, which fine tunes CXCL12 expression
and presentation to finesse the developmental process.

### How Do Chemokines Attract Cells to Specific In Vivo Destinations?

С
hemokines have to perform two distinct functions in terms of
supporting leukocyte migration to specific tissues. First, they have
to inform cells in circulation that they are at the right place to
leave the circulation and enter the tissue under either inflammatory or homeostatic conditions, and second, they need to facilitate movement of extravasated cells within the sublumenal tissue.
These will be dealt with separately.

Chemokines in the Leukocyte Adhesion Cascade
Leukocytes use combinations of molecules to determine, in time
and space, where to exit the vasculature and enter tissues.
Leukocytes initially role on the lumenal endothelial surface via “stuttering” interactions with selectins. When the rolling leukocyte
encounters the chemokine for which it has the appropriate receptor, the chemokine induces “inside-out” signaling, which activates
cell surface integrins and mediates firm adhesion to the vascular
lumen.
This is then followed by transendothelial migration,
through which leukocytes traverse the endothelial layer and enter
the sublumenal tissue.

## Conclusion

Cytokines and chemokines are the key regulators of inflammatory
cell phenotype, function, and migration. The pleiotropic functions
of cytokines and their propensity for synergistic interactions and
functional redundancy render them intriguing therapeutic targets.
Thus far, single cytokine targeting has proven useful in several
rheumatic disease states. Further elucidation of the biology and
functional interactions within this expanding family of bioactive
moieties is likely to prove informative in resolving pathogenesis
and in generating novel therapeutic options. In particular, biologic agents that target cytokines will increasingly unravel a novel
molecular taxonomy for the rheumatic diseases. In contrast, chemokine and chemokine receptor targeting has not yet proved to be
therapeutically useful in the context of immune and inflammatory
diseases. This is despite very significant successes in targeting chemokine receptors in other pathologies. It is likely that enhanced
understanding of the role for chemokines and their receptors in
inflammation and immunity will lead to more specific targeting of
this family of molecules in immune and inflammatory disease and
the development of successful and important therapeutic agents.
