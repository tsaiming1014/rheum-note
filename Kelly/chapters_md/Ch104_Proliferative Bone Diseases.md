## Introduction

Proliferative bone diseases encompass a variety of conditions
characterized by exuberant bone and entheseal ossifications and
calcifications. New bone formation is the main feature in diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH) and hypertrophic
osteoarthropathy (HOA) and is a common finding in osteoarthritis. New bone formation may also accompany some spondyloarthropathies such as ankylosing spondylitis (AS), psoriatic
arthritis, and sternoclavicular syndrome, also known as SAPHO
(synovitis, acne, pustulosis, hyperostosis, and osteomyelitis).
New bone formation has also been described in endocrine diseases such as thyroid disorders, acromegaly, and hypoparathyroidism (

## Diffuse Idiopathic Skeletal Hyperostosis

DISH is a condition characterized by calcification and ossification
of soft tissues, mainly ligaments and entheses. This condition was
described by Forestier and Rotes-Querol in 1950
and was termed
senile ankylosing hyperostosis.
It has a marked predilection for the
axial skeleton, particularly the thoracic spine. Recognition that the
condition is not limited to the spine and may involve peripheral
joints led researchers to coin the name
DISH,
a term now widely
used.
DISH is characterized by the production of coarse flowing
osteophytes involving, in particular, the right side of the thoracic
spine with preservation of the intervertebral disk space, as well
as by ossification of the anterior longitudinal ligament. Calcification and ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament seem
to be additional skeletal manifestations of DISH. Other entheseal
regions may be affected, such as the peripatellar ligaments, Achilles tendon insertion, plantar fascia, olecranon, and others.
Diagnosis is usually based on the definition suggested in
1976.
This radiographic approach requires the presence of flowing, coarse osteophytes on the right side of the thoracic spine,
connecting at least four contiguous vertebrae, or ossification of
the anterior longitudinal ligament, preserved intervertebral disk
height in the involved segment, and the absence of apophyseal
joint ankylosis and sacroiliac joint involvement (
Table 104.2
).
An MRI study suggested that degenerative disk changes are common in DISH. It is logical to consider that in the characteristic
DISH age group, degenerative disk changes should not be a deterrent from diagnosing this entity.
CT scans of the SIJs showed that
bony ankylosis of the SIJs was also common.
Taken together,
these new data stress the need to update the classification criteria.
Another set of criteria, for epidemiologic purposes, was suggested.
These criteria also consider peripheral enthesopathies.
With these criteria, a definite diagnosis of DISH is established
by criteria similar to those previously suggested. A probable diagnosis of DISH is possible, however, with continuous ossification
or calcification, or both, of the anterolateral aspect of at least two
contiguous vertebral bodies and bilateral well-corticated enthesopathies in the heel, olecranon, and patella. It has also been suggested that peripheral enthesopathies might represent early DISH,
which may evolve, over time, to its full radiologic appearance.

### Etiology and Pathogenesis

The cause and pathogenesis of DISH are unknown. Several
genetic, metabolic, and constitutional factors, as well as other
bone formation–promoting peptides, are associated with this
condition. They all might contribute to bone and entheseal new
bone formation.
Genetic Factors
Genetic factors might play a role in the pathogenesis of DISH.
It is known that the prevalence of canine DISH in the Boxer
breed is about 40%, whereas the overall prevalence of DISH in
dogs is 3.8%, suggesting a genetic basis for the risk of developing canine DISH.
Familial clustering of DISH or families with
early presentation of DISH suggest a genetic background for this
disorder in humans.
Ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament is closely related to DISH, and the two conditions
can coexist.
COL6A1,
which is the candidate gene for ossification
of the posterior longitudinal ligament, was significantly associated
with DISH in Japanese patients but not in Czech patients
Although the role played by
COL6A1
has not been fully elucidated, it might be involved in ectopic bone formation in ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL), as well as in
DISH. Many other candidate genes for the promotion of OPLL
have been reported, but their association with DISH has not been
established yet.
These findings would suggest that other factors
might play an important role in the genetic predisposition for the
development of DISH.
Metabolic Factors
DISH has been linked to metabolic and constitutional factors
such as obesity, a high waist circumference ratio, hypertension,
type 2 diabetes mellitus (DM), hyperinsulinemia, dyslipidemia,
elevated growth-promoting peptides, hyperuricemia, and use of
retinoids (
Table 104.3
).
The association of DISH with excess
body weight has been well known since the early descriptions
of the disease.
This association was confirmed in a study in
which patients with DISH were compared with healthy people
and patients with spondylosis.
The association of DISH with
type 2 DM has been reported in several studies.

Other Bone Formation–Promoting Pathways
Bone morphogenetic protein (BMP)-2 is a potent osteogenic factor that promotes differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells into

fibroblasts and chondroblasts. It stimulates cell proliferation, alkaline phosphatase (ALP) activity, and collagen synthesis.
Its
ability to promote mineralization is inhibited by matrix Gla protein, which is highly expressed in bone and cartilage. Matrix Gla
protein deficiency or its altered carboxylation may cause a high
level of BMP-2 activity that leads to hyperostosis.
Another
possible explanation is the upregulation of BMP-2 and BMP-4
messenger RNA expression after tensile stress on ligaments in
patients with OPLL.
The enthesis may also be under the influence of other growthpromoting peptides. Elevated growth hormone levels have
occurred in DISH. Growth hormone is capable of inducing
osteoblast cell proliferation and may promote local production of
IGF-I, which mediates the action of growth hormone and can
stimulate ALP activity in osteoblasts.
ALP promotes the
calcification process during bone formation and is considered a
good indicator for the maturation stages of osteoblasts.
It has
not yet been determined why the new bone formation is localized
mainly at the ligamentous and entheseal sites. In male patients
with DISH, growth hormone serum levels are not elevated compared with patients with OA, but they are much higher in the
synovial fluid.
In the spine, vertebral blood supply could be
a factor in the onset or progression of DISH.
Intraerythrocyte
growth hormone levels may exceed serum growth hormone levels
and could be transported to the vertebral site.
The expression of various genes involved in cell division and
growth is regulated by nuclear factorκ
B (NFκ
B), which is capable
of regulating the differentiation of multipotential cells. Activation
of environmental factors such as platelet-derived growth factor
(PDGF)-BB and transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
1 in ligament cells stimulates the activation of NFκ
B, which influences
the osteoblastic differentiation of mesenchymal cells. This event is
accompanied by elevation of ALP activity in cells of patients with
DISH and serves as an indicator of maturation stages of the osteoblast.
Inflammatory cytokines such as PDGF-BB, TGFβ
1, and
others may be related to the onset of non–insulin-dependent DM
and may be the link between this condition and the occurrence of
DISH.
The Wnt–

Wnt signaling can be inhibited by the endogenous secreted protein dickkopf-related protein 1 (DKK-1; also known as
dickkopf-1

Wnt pathway might have a role in the development of extensive
bony spinal bridges in a subset of patients with DISH.
Vitamin A and its derivatives have been implicated in the pathogenesis
of DISH because of their ability to promote new bone formation.
Levels of vitamin A were higher in patients with DISH compared
with control subjects, and some reports showed DISH-like manifestations in young patients treated with vitamin A or its derivatives.
The role of vitamin A in the pathogenesis and clinical
manifestations in DISH is still controversial.
Larger prospective
studies are necessary to elucidate the role played by this vitamin in
the pathogenesis of DISH.
Prostaglandin I
(PGI
) is a potent inhibitor of bone resorption, and endothelin-1 might regulate ectopic calcification of the
vasculature but may also regulate the proliferation of osteoblasts
and osteoclasts. In vitro studies have suggested that endothelin-1
and PGI
might play roles in the pathogenesis of DISH by induction, through various mechanisms, of ALP activity and osteogenic
differentiation in spinal ligament cells.
There are no convincing explanations for the predilection of
the hyperostotic process to affect the anterolateral aspect of the
thoracic spine. The limited range of motion of the thoracic spine
has been cited as a possible cause for the predilection to this site.
This assumption cannot explain the involvement of the extremely
mobile cervical spine or the lumbar spine, however. The less
frequent involvement of the left side of the thoracic spine was
ascribed to the pulsation of the aorta. This assumption was based
on a few reports that described left-sided bridging osteophytes in
cases with a right-sided aorta, suggesting that the aortic pulsations
interfere with the production of the osteophytes.
This hypothesis
has been recently reiterated. Furthermore, a CT study suggested
that the degree of thoracic spine new bone formation depends on
the distance of the aorta from the spine. When the aorta is adjacent to the vertebra, little or no new bone formation was seen.
The relationship between parathyroid hormone (PTH) and DISH
has not been elucidated yet. However, in an animal model it promotes bony ankyloses.
Calcifications, ossification, and subsequent stiffening of ligaments and joint capsules have important pathogenetic implications. Recently patients with DISH have a significantly higher risk
of developing aortic valve sclerosis.
Whether this finding might
share common pathogenetic mechanisms or only reflects the usual
risk factors has yet to be determined (
Fig. 104.2
).
Osteoarthritis may have pathogenetic features in common
with the peripheral joint manifestations of DISH. It was suggested
that in the small non–weight-bearing joints in osteoarthritis, the
process is caused by an increased intra-articular pressure and the
subsequent development of “crash” forces.
This development
was attributed to thickening of the collateral ligaments of the
joints that enforce a constraint movement not to primary damage to the cartilage. It seems reasonable that the joints affected
by DISH may develop the same crash forces operating in small
osteoarthritis joints as a result of this mechanism. This mechanism
might explain the involvement of “atypical” joints that are not
commonly affected by osteoarthritis and the hypertrophic osteoarthritic changes in the commonly affected joints.

### The Inflammatory Theory

It has been proposed that the initial event at the site of the enthesis
might be a local inflammatory process that precedes the ossification process. The theory is supported by imaging findings in which
it was shown that MRI studies of the spine in patients with DISH
might detect early or late vertebral corner lesions compatible with
the Assessment for Ankylosing Spondylitis (ASAS) definitions for
axial spondyloarthritis.
Furthermore, it seems that the rates of
progression of DISH and AS are similar, which suggests possible
similarities also in the initial pathogenetic mechanisms.
A single
ultrasonographic study of the peripheral entheses in patients with
DISH found an increased tendency for inflammatory signs such
as positive power Doppler and to some extent erosions supporting the theory of a local inflammatory process as the promotor of
entheseal new bone formation.
These and other findings suggest
that this theory should be also pursued.

In
some studies, up to two-thirds of the patients with DISH experience morning stiffness and spinal pain, and nearly half have a
reduced range of motion.

Extraspinal Involvement
Clinical manifestations similar or identical to those of osteoarthritis are prominent features of DISH in the peripheral joints.
The peripheral joints affected by DISH have features that distinguish them from primary osteoarthritis, however. One is the
more frequent involvement of joints that are not usually affected
in osteoarthritis such as the metacarpophalangeal joints, elbows,
and shoulders (
Fig. 104.5
).
Other features are a more severe
hypertrophic disease that may result in a reduced range of motion
in the affected joints and calcified and/or ossified prominent
enthesopathies.
As described previously, the primary event in DISH is thickening, calcification, or ossification of ligaments and entheses. In
particular, enthesopathy affecting the peripheral joints has been
described.
The radiographic appearance of peripatellar, cruciate
ligament insertion, and pericapsular osseous enthesopathies are
some examples of the contribution of DISH to stiffening of the
soft tissues surrounding a joint (
Fig. 104.6
).
Entheseal ossification at various sites other than joints such as the heel, ribs, and
pelvis is a common finding in DISH. The clinical implication of
the peripheral involvement is not clear and is controversial. However, the enthesopathies may become symptomatic, exhibiting
pain and swelling in the affected region. A high probability for the
presence of spinal DISH was noted for ossification of the iliolumbar and sacrotuberous ligaments and with bony overgrowth of the
inferior acetabular rim.
A higher level of soft tissue tenderness
and lower functional status have occurred recently in patients with
DISH compared with patients who do not have DISH.

Patients with DISH often have higher body weight and body
mass index, waist circumference, and systolic blood pressure.
These factors and the metabolic abnormalities described earlier
put patients at an increased risk for cardiovascular diseases.

The diagnosis of DISH should be suspected in patients with
osteoarthritis in atypical locations (e.g., the elbow), in patients
with hypertrophic osteoarthritis, and in patients with large enthesopathies and entrapment neuropathies of uncertain origin. This
is particularly true for patients with the associated diseases and
metabolic abnormalities previously discussed. Chest radiographs
may serve as a screening tool for the diagnosis of DISH with a
sensitivity of 77% and a specificity of 97%.
Fracture Risk and Heterotropic Ossification
The sites of ossification and the subsequent production of large
osteophytes may result in spinal stenosis
and spinal stiffening.
Under these circumstances, force cannot be dissipated, and the
spine behaves like a long bone with an increased risk of fracture.
The increased spinal stiffness may play a more important role than
the actual bone mineral density in the increased fracture risk and
the fracture patterns observed in individuals with DISH.
These
fractures may be unrecognized, unstable, and associated with
treatment delays and permanent neurologic deficits.
The tendency for new bone formation puts the patient at risk for
the development of heterotopic ossification after joint surgery
and may jeopardize the outcome. Although it is not clear what
causes these ossifications, it has been suggested that the surgical
trauma–induced TGFβ
release at the lesion site promotes recruitment of osteoblast precursor cells and determines the overexpression
of BMP-2 in the surrounding skeletal muscle, inducing a further
osteogenic differentiation.
The use of irradiation or prophylactic
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) treatment has been
suggested to prevent heterotropic ossifications for patients at risk.

growth hormone and IGF-I might be a useful surrogate marker
for assessing DISH progression and remission.
It was estimated
that a period of at least 10 years is necessary for the pathologic
process to evolve completely.
This notion has been recently corroborated by a CT study
and implies that a long observation
period is necessary to show that a therapeutic intervention prevents the development of the disease, arrests its progression, or, it
is hoped, reverses the pathologic changes.
Few reports have been published about remedies to alleviate
the symptoms of the disease, but some investigators have reported
on the beneficial effects of light exercise, heat, analgesics, and
NSAIDs.
Exercise therapy failed to show a significant improvement in the spinal range of motion except for lumbosacral flexion.
More recently, the use of locally acting NSAIDs for the
treatment of osteoarthritis was as effective as the same product
by oral route, suggesting that locally acting NSAIDs also might
be successfully used for the symptomatic relief of pain and stiffness that may be present in the peripheral joints of patients with
DISH.
Treatment of symptomatic enthesopathies might be
necessary to alleviate local pain and swelling; this treatment can be
achieved through use of local soft applications such as insoles for
plantar spurs or protective bandages at other sites. Infiltration of a
local anesthetic with long-acting corticosteroids may offer at least
temporary relief in people with severe symptoms. When multiple
sites are involved, the same therapeutic modalities mentioned for
osteoarthritis may be used.
It seems appropriate to screen patients with DISH for known
cardiovascular risk factors and to treat them when appropriate.
General measures such as weight reduction, adequate physical activity, and a diet low in saturated fat and carbohydrates all
might be important in preventing or arresting the progression
of DISH. Some of these factors may have pathogenetic implications and may become therapeutic targets. On the basis of present understanding, therapeutic interventions should aim at a
reduction of insulin secretion and insulin resistance. In patients
with non–insulin-dependent DM, the use of biguanides, which
decrease insulin resistance, may offer an advantage compared
with the use of sulfonylureas, which increase insulin secretion.
When coexisting hypertension is treated, medications that might
improve insulin resistance such as angiotensin-converting enzyme
inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, and
α
-blockers are preferred
over medications that may worsen insulin resistance, such as thiazide diuretics and
β
-blockers.
Some growth factors that might
have a role in the development of DISH such as NFκ
B, PDGFBB, TGFβ
1, growth hormone, and IGF-I may become targets for
specific therapeutic interventions.
In some patients, several complications can be avoided if they
are taken into consideration. Aspiration pneumonia can be partially avoided if instructions in proper deglutition and preservation of an upright position after meals are carefully explained to
the patient. Physicians familiar with DISH can avoid or minimize damage to the cervical spine or to soft tissues in patients
who might need certain diagnostic or therapeutic interventions
such as upper gastrointestinal endoscopy or endotracheal intubation. It is reasonable to adopt common measures to prevent falls
and trauma, especially in elderly patients. Heterotopic ossification
after orthopedic surgeries, in particular hip arthroplasty, is common in patients with DISH. Several therapeutic interventions
aimed at abolishing heterotopic ossification, such as administration of NSAIDs, anti–vitamin K, and irradiation, have been
reported with variable success.
Patients at high risk of developing this complication, such as patients with DISH, should be
considered candidates for one of these regimens. The therapeutic
approach in patients with fractured ankylosed spine requires an
aggressive intervention but with extreme caution due to the high
risk of neurologic complications.

The research of DISH is hampered by its late diagnosis and the

### Surgery

C-spine large anterior osteophytes may cause a significant reduction in quality of life, in particular with dysphagia. Since there
is no known medical therapy that can reverse the excessive bone
formation, surgery might represent a feasible therapeutic option.
Recurrence of the osteophytes is of concern, although the data are
not clear about the time to recurrence due to different follow-up
periods.
It emerged, however, that a shorter time from the onset
of symptoms and a milder severity of the symptoms may improve
the final outcome.
It has also been suggested that postoperative administration of indomethacin or irradiation might reduce
recurrences in a similar way it might prevent postsurgical heterotropic ossifications.

Secondary Hypertrophic Osteoarthropathy
Secondary forms of HOA manifest as isolated clubbing or with the

### Pathogenesis

The pathogenesis of HOA is not fully scrutinized, yet three pathogenic processes have been suggested: pathologic vascularization,
ongoing hypoxia, and connective tissue hyperplasia.
Pathologic examination of distal digits found numerous platelet microthrombi with dilated capillaries in some biopsies.
Another
microcapillary photo examination study revealed dilated capillaries and arborized loops compared with controls, emphasizing
the role of pathologic vascularization as part of this syndrome.
Thickened skin reported in primary HOA found hyperkeratosis
irregularities, acanthosis, hyperkeratotic plugs, and multiple pustules within the epidermis with sebaceous hyperplasia.
Various hypotheses have been generated in an attempt to
explain the development of HOA. One group
suggested that
megakaryocytes are fragmented into platelets during their passage in the lung capillaries. In severe lung diseases or right-to-left
shunts, however, megakaryocytes or platelet aggregates bypass the
pulmonary capillary bed and lodge in the peripheral vasculature
of the digits.

### Clinical Manifestations

HOA is often asymptomatic, and sometimes it is the patient who
notes changes in the shape of his or her fingers. Symptomatic
patients report a deep-seated pain in the lower extremities and
over the long tubular bones, which is exacerbated by palpation.
Upper limb digits might feel tingling and burning sensations.
It was postulated that new-onset HOA, mostly among cancer
patients, is the most symptomatic type. Large-joint effusions are
common, and the synovial fluid is thick, with few white blood
cells.
The absence of inflammatory exudate or synovial hypertrophy supports the paucity of joint pain or stiffness. Skin hypertrophy may be confined to the nail beds or may involve the face or
larger areas overlying the tubular bones or joints.
The most common and apparent clinical manifestation is digital clubbing. The bulbous deformity of the fingertips is accompanied by a convex nail (watch-crystal nail). The skin around
the nail bed becomes shiny and thin, with disappearance of the

creases. Palpation of the base of the nail bed yields the sensation of
a “floating” nail within the soft tissue. Cases of advanced clubbing
can be identified easily. Several methods have been developed to
diagnose early phases of the condition. Among those techniques,
the digital index and the phalangeal depth ratio have been most
widely used.
The digital index measures the ratio between
the circumference at the level of the nail bed and the circumference at the distal interphalangeal joint of the 10 fingers. A sum
of ratios greater than 10 suggests clubbing. The phalangeal depth
ratio measures the ratio between the depth of the distal phalanx
and the depth of the distal interphalangeal joint of the index finger. A ratio greater than 1 is considered abnormal.

### Imaging and Labs

Radioisotope bone scanning can be useful for diagnosis and for
evaluating the extent of the process. Hand and leg radiographs
show soft tissue swelling of the extremities, bulbous deformation of the distal fingers, and sometimes acro-osteolysis, which
is thought to be related to bone remodeling in the more chronic
states.
Radiographs of the tibia and fibula show periostosis,
which is limited to the diaphysis at early stages and progresses to
the epiphysis later. Characteristically, there is no reduction in joint
space or erosions.
Although not needed for the diagnosis, information about
other imaging modalities such as scintigraphy and MRI is available. In scintigraphy, there is an increased, linear uptake around
the cortex of long tubular bones, sometimes in the form of splints
(
Fig. 104.9
). Tracer uptake in the digits was also reported.
MRI
findings point to the periosteal reaction, which exhibits a low to
intermediate signal intensity in T1-weighted images. Fluid-sensitive sequences, such as T2-weighted and short tau inversion recovery (STIR), show hypo-intense lines representing the elevated
periosteum. Soft-tissue high-intensity T2 signal represents paraosseous tissue edema.
No specific laboratory tests are available to
diagnose HOA, but in cases of secondary HOA, laboratory tests
might reveal the underlying diagnosis predisposing to HOA (such
as Grave’s disease or cancer biomarkers).

### Treatment

Asymptomatic people do not need to be treated. A recent trial
showed that NSAIDs are safe and effective in the treatment of
primary HOA.
Although not thoroughly investigated, NSAIDs
are sometimes useful in symptomatic patients.
Case reports have
suggested that significant pain relief was observed after treatment
with octreotide or bisphosphonates.
In secondary cases of
HOA, all features and symptoms promptly regress with successful treatment of the primary disease, such as correction of a heart
malformation, removal of tumors, and therapy of infective endocarditis or inflammatory bowel disease. The role played by VEGF
in the pathogenesis of HOA suggests that treatment with VEGF
inhibitors might improve the symptoms of this condition.

SAPHO syndrome is a chronic inflammatory disorder, often
relapsing, of unknown cause. The term
SAPHO
was coined to
include several associated clinical manifestations: synovitis, acne,
pustulosis, hyperostosis, and osteitis.
The most common sites
of bone involvement are in the anterior chest wall—mainly the
clavicles, sternum, and sternoclavicular joint.

The condition is uncommon, with an estimated prevalence of

There is an unclear association or overlap of SAPHO with other

(CRMO) or chronic nonbacterial osteomyelitis (CNO).
Furthermore, it is presumed that the prevalence of SAPHO syndrome
is underestimated, particularly in patients with mild or absent skin
manifestations.

### Etiology and Pathogenesis

The cause of SAPHO syndrome is unknown. An infectious association has been proposed on the basis of the isolation of
Cutibacterium acnes
(formerly termed
Propionibacterium acnes
), a slowly
growing anaerobic microorganism often found in acne, from sternal osteosclerotic lesions.
It has been postulated that
P. acnes
might act directly, inducing bony erosions through chronic indolent inflammation. It has also been suggested that
P. acnes
may
trigger complement activation and promote production of interleukin (IL)-1, IL-8, and TNF, which contribute to both humoral
and cellular pro-inflammatory responses. It has been suggested
that immune inflammation in SAPHO patients may be related
to the depletion of natural killer (NK) cells and the imbalance

of T helper (Th)17 and T regulatory (Treg) cells. A reduction of
peripheral NK cells may exacerbate the disease progression by
not being inhibited by Th17 cells.
Another theory suggested a link between impaired FoxO1 levels, leading to defective
autophagy of the bacteria, which leads to inflammatory activation
of autoinflammatory pathways within bones. These defects might
also be genetically predisposed.
Genetic susceptibility has
not yet been found. Specifically, no associations with
HLA-B27,
other alleles often associated with psoriatic arthritis, or other candidate genes have been identified.
In patients with SAPHO syndrome, the reported prevalence
of common autoantibodies encountered in autoimmune diseases
was 22%. The autoantibodies did not show a specific profile
but suggest a possible relationship between autoimmunity and
SAPHO.
It has been suggested that the increased association of
SAPHO syndrome with inflammatory bowel disease might link
SAPHO to the seronegative spondyloarthropathies, but this possibility has not yet been confirmed.

### Clinical Manifestations

SAPHO syndrome is characterized by the combination of skin
and osteoarticular manifestations. The skin manifestations include
palmoplantar pustulosis, severe acne, suppurative hidradenitis, and
at times psoriasis. The skin manifestations can precede or follow
the osteoarticular manifestations, at times by many years.
The osteoarticular features of SAPHO syndrome are hyperostosis, aseptic osteitis that occasionally involves the adjacent joints,
and synovitis. The early histologic findings of the bone lesions are
similar to those of osteomyelitis with periosteal bone formation.
Subsequently the lesions evolve into chronic inflammation with
a preponderance of mononuclear infiltrate; it is only in the late
phases that prominent marrow fibrosis, sclerosis, and enlarged
bone trabeculae ensue.
The most characteristic clinical manifestation is pain in the
anterior chest wall as a result of common involvement of the
clavicles, sternum, and sternoclavicular joints.
Other common sites of involvement in the axial skeleton are the SIJs, vertebral bodies with vertebral sclerosis; hyperostosis; spondylodiskitis;
nonmarginal syndesmophytes, at times with anterior bridging;
the bone adjacent to the sacroiliac joint; and the pubic symphysis.
Specifically the sacroiliac joint involvement is usually
more unilateral and affects mostly the iliac bone.
Extraspinal
involvement is infrequent but may be observed in the form of
osteitis involving the tibia, femur, or mandible and with arthritis involving the knees, hips, ankles, or small joints of the hands
and feet.
The diagnosis is highly suspected in cases that
feature usually sterile, multifocal, recurrent osteomyelitis, with or
without cutaneous lesions; arthritis associated with palmoplantar
pustulosis, pustular psoriasis, or severe acne; and osteitis associated
with severe acne, palmoplantar pustulosis, or pustular psoriasis.

### Laboratory and Imaging

Laboratory investigations may reveal a moderately elevated
erythrocyte sedimentation rate or acute phase reactants such as
C-reactive protein, C3, and C4. These findings may reflect the
inflammatory nature of the condition but are less reliable than in
other inflammatory rheumatic disorders.
The diagnosis is usually established with the correlation
between the clinical presentation and imaging findings. Plain
radiographs may detect advanced lesions, but a CT scan enhances
the ability to detect these lesions, in particular in flat bones.
Radiographic studies of the anterior chest lesions show osteitis
in the form of osteosclerosis with a homogeneous fibrillary pattern, hyperostosis in the form of periosteal reaction, and cortical
thickening leading to bone hypertrophy. These lesions with eventual erosive arthritis (due to either primary arthritis or extension
of the adjacent osteitis) classically involve the sternoclavicular
joint and the upper costo-sternal and manubriosternal junctions.
The spine, which is involved in about a third of the patients,
presents with a similar radiographic appearance with vertebral
sclerosis, hyperostosis, spondylodiscitis, nonmarginal asymmetric syndesmophytes, and at times hyperostosis in the form of
osseous bridging along the anterior aspect of the spine.
Bone
scintigraphy has sufficient sensitivity to detect the anterior chest
wall involvement. The “bull’s head” sign is characteristic but not
entirely sensitive.
MRI is suggested to be the favored imaging modality in SAPHO because of its specificity in demonstrating osteitis during the inflammatory phase and fatty changes
in more chronic phases. The anterior chest wall MRI exhibits
involvement of the sternoclavicular, sterno-costal, and manubriosternal joints. Entesitis, synovitis, and osteitis are well visible
with MRI.
Spinal MRI features include corner vertebral erosions, which are also frequently demonstrated with involvement
of the vertebral end plates and at times adjacent vertebra.
The
relative paucity of paraspinal soft tissue involvement that can be
examined by MRI helps to differentiate spinal SAPHO involvement from infectious causes.
Entheseal ossifications, osteitis,
osteosclerosis, and periosteal new bone formation have all been
reported with variable frequency in long bones as well. Ultrasound can be used to demonstrate sternoclavicular and peripheral joint involvement in SAPHO.

### Treatment

There are no randomized controlled trials targeting SAPHO for
specific treatment; thus the treatment should be guided by an
expert rheumatologist and joined with other disciplines, mainly
dermatologists. NSAIDs are usually the first line of treatment
and may improve symptoms. However, they often fail to control
the disease, and additional therapy modalities are required.
Several empiric therapeutic approaches have occurred in SAPHO,
including NSAIDs, corticosteroids, bisphosphonates, sulfasalazine, colchicine methotrexate, antibiotics, anti-TNF, anti–IL-1,
anti–IL-17, anti–IL-12/23, and Janus kinase inhibitors, and
yielded mixed results.
Treatment with antibiotics, usually
azithromycin, was beneficial in patients with SAPHO syndrome.
However, many patients were also treated with other diseasemodifying anti-rheumatic drugs, and the response faded after discontinuation of the antibiotic.
Bisphosphonates, in particular
pamidronate, elicit a good or partial response in patients affected
by SAPHO syndrome.
It seems that beyond their ability to
prevent bone resorption, bisphosphonates are capable of suppressing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines such
as IL-1, TNF, and IL-6.
The body of data about treatment
with TNF inhibitors is growing, and they are effective therapy
mainly for synovitis, enthesitis, and osteitis. All anti-TNFs were
reported in small series, and a meta-analysis summarized it as an
effective modality.
In patients failing TNF inhibitors, IL-1
inhibitors and biologics targeting the IL-17/IL-23 axis could be
used.
