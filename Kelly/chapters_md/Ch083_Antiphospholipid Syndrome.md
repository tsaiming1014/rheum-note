## Introduction

Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS) is a systemic autoimmune
disease characterized by varied clinical manifestations, most
commonly thrombosis or pregnancy morbidity, in the setting of
persistent antiphospholipid antibodies (aPL), documented by a
solid-phase serum assay (anti-cardiolipin antibody [aCL] or antiβ
-glycoprotein-I antibody [a
β
GPI] immunoglobulin [Ig]G or
IgM), a coagulation assay (inhibitor of phospholipid-dependent
clotting—the lupus anti-coagulant [LA] test), or both.
APS can occur as an isolated diagnosis (primary APS), or it can
be associated with other systemic rheumatic diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). Individuals with aPL positivity
may present with heterogeneous disease manifestations, with varying aPL types and titers, and clinical manifestations ranging from
arterial or venous thrombosis, microvascular disease, nonthrombotic manifestations such as thrombocytopenia, catastrophic
antiphospholipid syndrome (CAPS), and severe placental insufficiency resulting in pregnancy morbidity.
The original Sapporo classification criteria for APS were developed in 1999
and revised in 2006.
Given various limitations
of the Revised Sapporo criteria, an international multidisciplinary
effort to develop new APS classification criteria was completed in
2023, resulting in newly developed classification criteria approved
by the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) and European
Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR) to identify
homogeneous cohorts of APS patients for research purposes.

## Epidemiology

Although the epidemiology of primary APS is still under investigation, based on a few international studies, the current estimated
annual incidence is approximately 1 to 2 per 100,000 personyears, and prevalence is 50 per 100,000 person-years.
However,
due to significant study design limitations, an updated targeted
literature review using applied methodology to derive a best available estimate noted a much higher incidence of APS in the United
States of approximately 7.1 to 13.7 per 100,000 person-years.
Population-based studies are needed to confirm these estimates.
Transient aPL positivity can be induced by drugs and infections.
Low-titer, usually transient aCL occurs in up to 10% of
normal blood donors
; however, persistent moderate- to
high-titer aCL/a
β
GPI or a positive LA test occurs in less than
1%. The prevalence of positive aPL tests increases with age, with
an estimated mean age of 50 years for APS, although this requires
further confirmation.
The peak age of incidence may be lower
in women (35 to 39 years) than in men (55 to 59 years); other
studies report that APS incidence increases with age only in men,
peaking at the age of 70 to 79 years.
Although the distribution of
APS by gender varies based on the cohorts studied and depends
on exclusion of patients with concomitant SLE or obstetric APS,
the female-to-male ratio appears to be close to 1:1.
Prevalence
of positive aPL is higher in specific disease-related subgroups. Up
to 40% of SLE patients and approximately 20% of rheumatoid
arthritis patients have positive aPL tests
; however, the incidence of APS is relatively low.

Based on a limited number of uncontrolled and non-risk-stratified studies, available data suggest an annual thrombosis incident
rate ranging from 0% to 5.3% for aPL-positive patients, including those with another systemic autoimmune disease; however, in
aPL-positive individuals with no other systemic autoimmune disease or thrombosis risk factors, the annual risk of first thrombosis
is probably very low (<1%/year).
The aPL profile and patients’
clinical characteristics (presence or absence of other acquired or
genetic provoking thrombosis risk factors) influence the individual risk of thrombosis.
Although it is difficult to determine the association of the highrisk aPL profile in general-population patients (without concomitant autoimmune diseases) with aPL-related clinical outcomes
due to the lack of robust data, systematic literature reviews and
meta-analyses estimated the overall aPL frequency is 6% for pregnancy morbidity, 14% for all stroke, 17% for young (<50 years
old) stroke, 11% for myocardial infarction (MI), and 10% for
deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
In the United States APS is estimated to cause approximately 50,000 pregnancy losses, 110,000
strokes, 100,000 MIs, and 30,000 DVTs annually.
CAPS, a lifethreatening condition characterized by the rapid development of
systemic thromboses, is observed in less than 1% of APS cases.

## Etiology

Activation of vascular and immune cells by aPL is widely considered to be the driving force for pathologic outcomes in APS.
Low levels of aPL may be present normally; one of the functions
of normal aPL may be to participate in the physiologic removal of
oxidized lipids. However, thrombotic events occur only occasionally in patients with persistently present aPL, and aPL are considered not only disease markers but also implicated in disease
pathogenesis. However, aPL infusions in experimental models
notably fail to induce thrombosis when given in isolation,
suggesting that the antibodies are necessary but insufficient and that
other factors may play an important role in the development of

The “two-hit” model of thrombosis associated with aPL proposes
that an initiating “first hit” disrupts the endothelium and a “second hit” potentiates thrombosis
in both animal models and in
humans with an underlying genetic predisposition. Second hits
or triggers for APS may include environmental factors (such as
infection, estrogen-containing hormones), inflammation (from
concomitant systemic autoimmune diseases), lifestyle and other
traditional cardiovascular risk factors (smoking, obesity, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia), and prothrombotic factors (surgery,
immobility, acute medical illness, pregnancy, active malignancy),
although these remain to be confirmed.
Because high levels of aPL may persist for years in asymptomatic
individuals, it is likely that vascular injury, endothelial cell activation, or both immediately precede the occurrence of thrombosis in
those bearing the antibody. Of note, at least 50% of APS patients
with vascular factors possess other acquired thrombosis risk factors
at the time of their events.
Additionally, familial studies support
the concept of a genetic predisposition to APS, in part accounted
for by the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system, with the most
consistent associations being HLA-DR4 and HLA-DRw53.
Other genes may also play a role in APS pathogenesis, including
encoding interferon regulatory factor 5 and encoding signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT) 4.
Animal models suggest a clear role for antibodies against
β

phenotype. Molecular mimicry between bacterial or viral structures and

In experimental animal models, passive or active

## Pathogenesis

Current evidence demonstrates a broad range of effects of aPL
on a variety of cells, coagulation factors, regulatory proteins, and
inflammatory mediators. The process begins with activation or
apoptosis of platelets, endothelial cells, monocytes, neutrophils,
fibroblasts, or trophoblasts, during which phosphatidylserine (a
negatively charged phospholipid) migrates from the inner to the
normally electrically neutral outer cell membrane. The main antigen to which aPL bind is not a phospholipid but rather a phospholipid-binding plasma protein, namely,
β
GPI. In vivo,
β
GPI
binds to phosphatidylserine on activated or apoptotic cell membranes, including those of trophoblasts, platelets, and endothelial
cells. Under physiologic conditions,
β
GPI may function in the
elimination of apoptotic cells
and as a natural anti-coagulant.
Although mice lacking
β
GPI are fertile, the success of early pregnancy is moderately compromised;
β
GPI is believed necessary for
optimal implantation and placental morphogenesis.

### Activation of Complement Cascade

aPL also initiate complement activation. aPL–
β
GPI dimer binding to cell surfaces activates the classical complement cascade and
thus initiates an intra-cellular signaling cascade, probably through
the C5a and
β
GPI surface receptors. Activated complement fragments recruit and activate inflammatory effector cells, including
monocytes, neutrophils, and platelets, leading to the release of
pro-inflammatory products (e.g., TNF, oxidants, proteases) and
the induction of a prothrombotic phenotype.
Complement-mediated disruption of endothelial and trophoblast function partly explains pregnancy complications and microthrombosis associated with aPL. Around the time of a thrombotic
event, patients with APS demonstrate increased complement activation and cell surface deposition of terminal complement C5b-9;
additionally, rare germline mutations in complement regulatory
genes that limit activation of the alternative pathway can be identified in patients with CAPS.
Placental thrombosis and aPL
interactions with decidual cells may also contribute to pregnancy
complications.
In experimental animal models, aPL cause fetal resorption
and increase the size and duration of trauma-induced venous and
arterial thrombi.
Inhibiting complement activation prevents
experimental aPL-induced fetal death and angiogenic dysregulation–associated abnormal placental development and pre-eclampsia; C5 knockout mice carry pregnancies normally despite aPL,
implying that a complement-mediated effector mechanism is an
absolute requirement for fetal death to occur. Complement activation triggers production of inflammatory mediators such as
TNF and influences placental development and vascular remodeling.
In addition, the reduction of heparin-binding epidermal
growth factor–like expression mediated by aPL leads to defective
placentation in APS.
A role for complement in pathogenesis of
pre-eclampsia associated with APS is supported by the presence
of C4d, a complement fragment and a marker of classical complement pathway activation, in the placentae of women with SLE
and/or APS and women with pre-eclampsia, whereas it is absent
in healthy controls.
Recent data from a large cohort of pregnant patients also suggest that circulating markers of complement
activation, particularly the alternative pathway (Bb and C5b-9),
contribute to adverse pregnancy outcomes in patients with SLE
and/or aPL positivity.
The role of local thrombosis in pregnancy complications is
less clear. Complement activation is also required for experimental thrombosis.
Compared with control mice, mice deficient
in complement factors C3, C5, and C6 demonstrate reduced
thrombotic responses following aPL administration and vascular
injury.
Complement activation in APS may be modulated by
rivaroxaban, a direct factor Xa inhibitor, based on recent evidence
that levels of activation markers C3a, C5a, SC5b-9, and Bb fragment were reduced in thrombotic APS patients after switching
from warfarin to rivaroxaban treatment in the Rivaroxaban in
Antiphospholipid Syndrome (RAPS) trial.
In addition, heparin attenuates activation of complement.

### Activated Protein C Resistance

Although the most common antigens recognized by aPL are the
phospholipid binding proteins
β
GPI and prothrombin, the anticoagulant protein C pathway, which plays a central role in regulation of coagulation and maintenance of blood fluidity, may also
be a key aPL target. Activated protein C resistance strongly predisposes to venous thromboembolism (VTE).
In vitro, aPL induces
activated protein C resistance; the autoantibodies compete with
activated protein C for phospholipid binding, thereby limiting the
access of protein C to its substrates.
The effect in vivo is currently unknown. Thrombotic APS patients have greater resistance
to both recombinant human-activated protein C and activation of
endogenous protein C, suggesting that high-avidity anti–protein
C antibodies, associated with greater activated protein C resistance, may provide a marker for a severe thrombotic phenotype.
Animal models are needed to investigate the role of aPL-induced
activated protein C resistance in the development of VTE in APS
patients.

### Other Mechanisms

Substantial research continues to unravel underlying mechanisms
of aPL-induced thrombosis and targets for treatment. Other
possible contributory mechanisms of aPL-mediated thrombosis
include oxidative stress, impaired function of endothelial nitric
oxide synthase, increased expression of glycoprotein IIb/IIIa,
decreased tissue factor inhibitor activation on monocytes, reduction of fibrinolysis, and disruption of the annexin V anti-coagulant
shield.
Neutrophils may also be directly activated by a
β
GPI that
recognize and bind to cell-surface
β
GPI antigen, stimulating the
formation of neutrophil extra-cellular traps (NETs) via the development of reactive oxygen species and TLR4.
NETs—consisting of webs of nuclear DNA, histones, and microbicidal proteins
extruded by activated neutrophils—may be major contributors
to thromboinflammation in APS.
NETs activate platelets and
endothelial cells and form an important part of venous and arterial clots.
Increased NET formation and higher circulating
low-density granulocytes in APS patients may lead to increased
type 1 interferon
; NETs are also found infiltrating placental
intervillous spaces, in association with inflammatory and vascular
changes in individuals with SLE and with pre-eclampsia.
Data
from a single-center study demonstrates that some APS patients
develop anti-NET antibodies (anti-NET Abs) that impair NET
clearance and may amplify inflammatory responses.
Although it
remains to be determined whether anti-NET Abs can serve as biomarkers in addition to traditional aPL, recent data demonstrates
high levels of anti-NET Abs in 45% of aPL-positive patients, suggesting a possible role in disease pathogenesis.
Gene expression studies elucidating immune-thrombosis
mechanisms in APS are limited. Two studies provided data from
RNA-sequencing of neutrophils and plasmacytoid dendritic cells
in 9 and 10 patients with primary APS, respectively.
Recent
data aiming to identify gene signatures in thrombotic primary

APS patients show a potential role for upregulated type I and
II interferon-regulated genes in peripheral blood to discriminate thrombotic primary APS from healthy controls.
Further
research is needed to investigate the roles for these mechanisms as
potential therapeutic targets in APS.
Microvascular manifestations, such as vasculopathy in APS,
may be explained in part by upregulation of the mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) on endothelial cells, a kinase that integrates a variety of signaling pathways to regulate cellular growth,
proliferation, and survival.
As demonstrated in patients with
aPL-associated nephropathy, molecular markers consistent with
activation of mTOR and downstream signaling were found in
intrarenal vascular endothelium.
Prospective studies of novel measures of potential targets for
thrombosis development, including anti–Domain I and aPS/PT,
may reveal mechanistic roles for these antibodies. For instance,
some studies suggest that Domain I–specific a
β
GPI are the aPL
most likely to cause thrombosis and obstetric morbidity compared with antibodies directed against other regions of
β
GPI.
While some studies also suggest a predictive role for anti–Domain
I antibody positivity for the first thrombotic event in aPL carriers,
further research is needed to confirm these findings.
Additionally, measuring antibodies to prothrombin, detected using
aPS/PT, may be useful in establishing thrombotic risk of patients
with previous thrombosis and/or history of SLE, as well as in a
subset of patients who test negative for the recommended criteria
aPL tests.

## Clinical Features

Clinical manifestations in APS are heterogeneous. Patients with
APS may present with a spectrum of often overlapping clinical
phenotypes. While the principal manifestations of aPL are venous
or arterial thromboses (thrombotic APS) and pregnancy events
(obstetric APS), APS can range from asymptomatic aPL positivity to microvascular events (microvascular APS), to CAPS (multiple thromboses occurring over days). Symptomatic patients may
experience a combination of the above findings and thus should
not be evaluated and managed as if they have a single disease.

### Macrovascular Thrombosis

APS can affect all organ systems. DVT (with or without pulmonary embolism) and stroke are the most common clinical manifestations of APS and have strong positive associations with aPL.
Except for the youth of affected patients, the unusual anatomic
locations (e.g., Budd-Chiari syndrome, sagittal sinus and upperextremity thromboses), venous thromboses in APS do not differ
clinically from thromboses attributable to other causes. Similarly,
arterial thromboses differ from non-aPL-associated thromboses
only by their recurrent nature, unusual locations, and occurrence
in young patients. Thrombosis of renal vessels may lead to severe
hypertension,
and occasionally peripheral artery thrombosis
may occur.

### Microvascular Antiphospholipid Syndrome (Without Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Syndrome Classification)

Ten to twenty percent of aPL-positive patients develop livedo, the
most frequently associated cutaneous manifestation in patients
with APS.
While most studies do not distinguish between
livedo reticularis (uniform, reversible, unbroken, and symmetric
discoloration) versus livedo racemosa (nonuniform, irreversible,
broken, and asymmetric discoloration), “primary” livedo reticularis is typically considered physiologic or benign, whereas livedo
racemosa is considered pathologic. Another relatively uncommon
cutaneous finding linked to APS, known as livedoid vasculopathy, is characterized by purpuric macules and papules, cutaneous
nodules, and/or painful ulcerations on the lower legs and feet,
and is observed primarily in young to middle-age women with
APS
(
Fig. 83.2
).
Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (DAH) is a rare microvascular
manifestation of aPL, characterized by the red blood cell leak from
alveolar capillaries into the intraalveolar space. While prevalence
estimates range from 2% to 12% in APS patients, it may be difficult to calculate due to nonspecific clinical and radiographic findings and the inconsistent ability to perform invasive diagnostic
testing (i.e., bronchoscopy).
Pulmonary capillaritis with or
without microthrombosis has been detected in
∼
50% of the lung
pathology specimens from aPL-positive patients with DAH.
Biopsy-proven aPL-associated nephropathy, which develops in
∼
3% of aPL-positive patients,
can present as acute (thrombotic
microangiopathy [TMA] in glomeruli or arterioles/arteries) or
chronic (i.e., organized arterial or arteriolar microthrombi with
or without recanalization, organized glomerular thrombi, fibrous
and fibrocellular [arterial or arteriolar] occlusions, focal cortical atrophy with or without thyroidization, and fibrous intimal
hyperplasia) lesions
(
Fig. 83.3
). Chronic aPL nephropathy is
typically characterized as slowly progressive kidney disease with
proteinuria, microscopic hematuria, and/or hypertension.
Additionally, aPL-positive patients can rarely develop myocardial ischemia/infarction with nonobstructive coronary artery
disease due to microvascular thrombosis, adrenal infarction/hemorrhage, or acute ischemic encephalopathy.

### Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Syndrome

CAPS is a rare, abrupt, life-threatening complication. It consists of
multiple thromboses (e.g., stroke; cardiac, hepatic, adrenal, renal,
and intestinal infarction; and peripheral gangrene) occurring over
a period of days and is commonly associated with microvascular disease with or without systemic TMA.
In a review of 220
patients with CAPS, the main clinical manifestations included
renal (70%), pulmonary (66%), cerebral (60%), cardiac (52%),
and cutaneous involvement (47%).

to adrenal hemorrhage may be the initial clinical event. Some

### Pregnancy Morbidity

Pregnancy losses in patients with aPL typically occur after 10
weeks’ gestation (fetal loss) and are more specific when accompanied by signs of vascular insufficiency such as severe pre-eclampsia
or severe placental insufficiency. Although recurrent early pregnancy losses (<10 weeks’ gestation) are reported in aPL-positive
patients, they are commonly due to chromosomal and other
genetic defects. Pregnancy in those with APS is often normal until
the second trimester, when fetal growth slows and amniotic fluid
volume decreases. Intrauterine growth restriction due to placental insufficiency in APS patients is also elevated, ranging from
15% to 40%, although the definition of placental insufficiency
remains controversial.
APS patients may develop severe, early

### Classification

Antiphospholipid Syndrome
The revised Sapporo classification criteria for APS required clinical (thrombosis or pregnancy morbidity) and laboratory (LA, aCL

### Antiphospholipid Antibody Profile

Not every positive aPL test result is considered “clinically relevant”—an important concept in APS laboratory evaluation that
incorporates the type, isotype, titer, persistency, and number of
positive aPL tests. Although aPL prevalence approaches 10% in
a general healthy population, persistent LA or moderate- to hightiter aCL/a
β
GPI positivity is uncommon. In a review of how aPL
positivity is defined in the literature, 60% of papers did not apply
a moderate- to high-titer cutoff for aCL, and only 30% of papers
demonstrated aPL “persistence.”
Certain aPL profiles, such as
LA positivity, moderate- to high-titer aCL/a
β
GPI IgG, or triple
aPL positivity, are more strongly associated with aPL-related clinical events and provide a strong confidence level for APS diagnosis
in the appropriate clinical setting. Insufficient data exist regarding
the clinical significance of aCL or a
β
GPI IgG/M titers that are
above the upper limit of the reference range but are less than 40
units.
The significance of IgA isotypes is controversial. Moderate- to high-titer aCL/a
β
GPI IgG/M are defined as titers equal
to or above 40 ELISA units, according to the 2023 ACR/EULAR
APS classification criteria.
The LA test is a more specific but less sensitive predictor of
thromboses than is the aCL test; it correlates better with aPLrelated clinical events.
LA is the strongest aPL test for prediction of pregnancy complications; the predictive power of aCL is
due to its association with LA.
However, both false-positive and
false-negative LA test results may occur in anti-coagulated (including vitamin K antagonists, heparin, and direct oral anti-coagulants
[DOACs]) patients and patients with severe inflammation. Documentation of LA requires a four-step process: (1) demonstration
of a prolonged phospholipid-dependent coagulation screening
test, such as activated partial thromboplastin time or dilute Russell viper venom time (however, low-level abnormalities are not
clearly linked to APS); (2) failure to correct the prolonged screening test by mixing the patient’s plasma with normal platelet-poor
plasma, demonstrating the presence of an inhibitor; (3) shortening or correcting the prolonged screening test by the addition of
excess phospholipid, demonstrating phospholipid dependency;
and (4) exclusion of other inhibitors.
If there is doubt in LA
test result interpretation, they should be discussed with an experienced physician and/or laboratory personnel. Approximately 80%
of patients with a positive LA test have aCL, and 20% of patients
with aCL have a positive LA test.
A false-positive test for syphilis does not fulfill the laboratory
criteria, but it should alert physicians to order the aPL tests previously described, especially in patients with a history of aPL-related
clinical manifestations.
Underlying Comorbidities
In patients presenting with thrombosis, performing a thorough
history evaluating additional VTE and cardiovascular disease
(CVD) risk factors are integral to an accurate assessment and
should be incorporated in diagnostic decision making in all
aPL-positive patients, given that independent coagulopathies
further increase thrombotic risk in patients with aPL. Furthermore, genetic thrombophilias such as deficiency of protein C,
protein S, and anti-thrombin III and the presence of the factor V Leiden (A506G), prothrombin (G20210A), and methylene tetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR, C677T) mutations
are alternative explanations for venous or arterial thrombotic
events but are less common causes of fetal loss.
Arterial occlusion may occur in patients with atrial fibrillation, thrombotic
thrombocytopenic purpura, infected or sterile emboli of cardiac or vascular origin, septicemia, hyperhomocysteinemia,
myxoma, Takayasu’s arteritis, polyarteritis nodosa, and severe
Raynaud’s disease. Thoracic outlet syndrome with external arterial or venous compression can result in upper-extremity thromboses of the arteries or deep veins, respectively. Other systemic
autoimmune conditions, serious infections, and solid organ or
hematologic malignancies should also be considered, as well as
medications or toxic exposures. In these or similar situations
with increased baseline thrombosis risks, even with a high-risk
aPL profile, the APS diagnosis cannot be confirmed with high
confidence.
Attribution of pregnancy loss to APS is most certain when
no coexisting plausible explanation is known, when the loss
occurs after demonstration of a fetal heartbeat (10 weeks),
when a significant aPL profile is repeatedly positive before and

after pregnancy, and when the placenta shows vasculopathy and
infarction. A single pregnancy loss before 10 weeks’ gestation
in a patient with a low-positive anti-cardiolipin test is more
likely to be attributable to fetal chromosomal abnormalities,
infection, or maternal hormonal or anatomic abnormalities,
poorly controlled maternal conditions such as diabetes or thyroid diseases, and parental karyotype abnormalities. Alternative
explanations for fetal death (after 10 weeks) such as fetal genetic
anomalies, feto-maternal hemorrhage, umbilical cord accidents,
placental abruption, pre-term rupture of membranes, congenital infections (e.g., syphilis, toxoplasmosis, or cytomegalovirus),
chorioamnionitis, or red cell alloimmunization should also be
considered.
Infection-induced aCL is usually transient and is more commonly IgM than IgG.
Transient aPL or low-titer aCL is
inconclusive for diagnosis. Research laboratories can distinguish
autoimmune from infection-induced aPLs by determining the
antibody’s
β
GPI dependence.
CAPS has few mimics. Among them are sepsis, disseminated
intravascular coagulation, thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura,
hemolytic uremic syndrome, heparin-induced thrombocytopenia,
and disseminated embolization from myxoma, atrial thrombus, or
atherosclerotic plaque. However, as these factors may overlap with
CAPS, the diagnosis can be particularly challenging and requires
a high index of suspicion with early and aggressive treatment. Previous APS diagnosis and/or persistent clinically significant aPL
positivity is of great importance for the CAPS diagnosis; however,
almost half of the patients who develop CAPS do not have a history of aPL positivity.

Antinuclear and anti-DNA antibodies occur in approximately

mandate the additional diagnosis of SLE if the patient has no
clinical indicators of SLE.
A small number of patients exist with classic features of APS

diagnostic accuracy of the syndrome.

## Management

### Macrovascular Thrombosis

Primary Thrombosis Prevention
Persistence of aPL for decades without clinical events is well documented. The probability that an asymptomatic person incidentally found to have aPL will eventually develop the syndrome is
likely low.
Anti-coagulation is not indicated for the prophylactic treatment of asymptomatic aPL-positive individuals. For
those with moderate- to high-titer persistent aPLs, education
about the meaning of abnormal tests is appropriate, as is a discussion of warning signs to be reported. Elimination of reversible
thrombosis risk factors and prophylaxis during high-risk periods,
such as surgical procedures, are crucial. The protective effect of
low-dose aspirin for primary thrombosis prophylaxis prevention
is not supported by prospective or randomized controlled data.
A recent meta-analysis demonstrated that the risk of first thrombosis is decreased by low-dose aspirin among asymptomatic aPL
individuals, patients with SLE, or obstetric APS; however, no significant risk reduction was observed when considering only prospective studies or those with the best methodological quality.
In the only randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial,
low-dose aspirin (81 mg) appeared to be no better than placebo
in preventing first thrombotic episodes in persistently asymptomatic aPL-positive patients; the incidence rate of first thrombosis
was relatively low.
Currently, there is no clinical trial evaluating
direct oral anti-coagulation for primary prophylaxis among aPLpositive individuals, and these agents are not recommended.
Hydroxychloroquine may have protective mechanistic properties in aPL-positive patients, including protecting the endothelial
surface, decreasing binding of aPL to placental syncytiotrophoblasts, and modulating inflammation.
While hydroxychloroquine appears to be protective against thrombosis in aPL-positive
SLE patients, its role in aPL-positive individuals without systemic
autoimmune diseases remains uncertain. Statins downregulate
pro-inflammatory and prothrombotic biomarkers in APS patients
and may have a role in aPL-positive individuals with high CVD
risk.
Two risk-stratification tools have been developed to predict
the thrombotic and obstetric risk among aPL-positive patients:
the antiphospholipid score (aPL-S) and the global APS score
(GAPSS).
Both were derived from retrospective cohorts of
patients with systemic autoimmune diseases and included noncriteria aPL as part of their scoring. aPL-S quantified risk associated
with various aPLs and assigned points to each test. GAPSS not
only quantified risk associated with aPL but also included cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension and hyperlipidemia) in its
scoring model to quantify risk of thrombosis and obstetric events.
Recent validation studies have observed that higher aPL-S and
GAPSS scores are associated with thrombosis.
However,
further prospective validation among ethnically diverse asymptomatic persistently aPL-positive individuals is needed to test their
true clinical performance. At this time, their clinical utility in this
population remains uncertain.
Although drugs that induce lupus (hydralazine, phenytoin)
may also induce aPL, they may be prescribed for patients with
aPL if no alternatives are available. Drugs that promote thrombosis (estrogen and estrogen-containing oral contraceptives) are not

currently deemed safe, even for asymptomatic women serendipitously known to have high-titer antibodies. This advice does not
translate to a recommendation to test all normal women before
prescribing such medications, but it does suggest that special
attention and further evaluation should be provided to those with
family histories or clinical suggestions of rheumatic disease, livedo
reticularis, biologic false-positive tests for syphilis, or borderline
thrombocytopenia. No reliable information is available regarding
the safety of progestin-only contraception, “morning after” contraception, or the use of raloxifene, bromocriptine, or leuprolide
in APS patients. A small retrospective review of women undergoing artificial reproductive technology (in vitro fertilization) procedures demonstrated no thrombotic events.
Secondary Thrombosis Prevention
Anti-coagulation with heparin is the treatment for acute thrombosis in APS patients. Warfarin, occasionally in association with
low-dose aspirin in the setting of additional CVD risk factors, is
the standard treatment for secondary thrombosis prophylaxis in
APS. Two randomized, controlled trials demonstrated that moderate warfarin (international normalized ratio [INR] 2 to 3) and
high-intensity warfarin (INR 3 to 4) are equally protective against
recurrence in APS patients after the first thrombosis.
The
intensity of anti-coagulation for aPL-related arterial thrombosis
is still a matter for debate because, in both studies, patients with
arterial events constituted less than half of the study population.
Although APS patients with arterial thrombosis who are at high
risk for recurrence may require high-intensity anti-coagulation, in
the absence of risk-stratified studies,
high risk
has no consensus
definition and currently is based solely on clinical judgment.
For patients with APS and a history of venous thromboses, our
approach is to recommend initial therapy with unfractionated or
low-molecular-weight heparin, followed by long-term treatment
with a vitamin K antagonist such as warfarin (target INR 2 to 3).
In two randomized trials, higher-intensity warfarin therapy (target
INR 3 to 4) does not further reduce the risk of recurrent thrombosis.
For arterial thromboses outside the cerebral vasculature,
we recommend the use of vitamin K antagonists. For APS characterized by stroke, risk stratification by aPL profile and concurrent cardiovascular risk factors are important. Although warfarin
(target INR 2 to 3) is typically used in patients with clinically
significant aPL profiles, with or without low-dose aspirin, in certain situations aspirin alone may be considered. For example, in
older patients with stroke and a single low-titer aCL, aspirin alone
may be as effective as warfarin.
As dual anti-thrombotic
therapy (i.e., the addition of aspirin to anti-coagulant therapy)
may increase the risk of major hemorrhage, it is often reserved for
patients with clinically significant cardiovascular risk factors and/
or those in whom warfarin failed to prevent recurrence. Treatment

versus low-dose warfarin for 12 months.

in 6 of 23 patients on apixaban versus 0 of 25 patients on warfarin. Thus apixaban is not recommended to be substituted to prevent recurrent thromboses, especially arterial events, in patients
with thrombotic APS.

definitive evidence is needed to better understand the subgroup
of thrombotic APS patients that DOACs can be considered.

patients with single- or double-positive aPL, following the first
VTE episode, DOACs may be continued while awaiting confirmation (on two occasions 12 weeks apart), as long as the physician
and patient discuss the potential risks, benefits, and uncertainties
related to anti-coagulation choice. The task force also determined
that further studies are required to investigate the potential role
of DOACs for patients with thrombotic APS, accounting for the
clinical heterogeneity and factors associated with thrombotic risk
assessment.
Currently available data, although retrospective and not risk
stratified, indicate that lifelong anti-coagulation of APS patients
who have had vascular events is appropriate.

triggers, a Task Force on Prevention and Management of Thrombosis recommended that in cases of first venous event, low-risk
aPL profile, and a known transient precipitating factor, anti-coagulation could be limited to 3 to 6 months.

### Microvascular Antiphospholipid Syndrome (Without Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Syndrome Classification)

Although no uniform approach to the management of microvascular APS exists due to its heterogeneous manifestations, rarity,
and lack of supporting evidence, based on the presumed pathogenesis, some physicians prescribe anti-coagulation for patients
with microvascular manifestations of aPL such as livedoid vasculopathy, leg ulcers, or aPL nephropathy. The efficacy of anticoagulation is unknown in these conditions and many patients
develop microvascular disease while receiving anti-coagulation
therapy. Given improved understanding of the underlying mechanisms related to microvascular APS (e.g., aPL-induced endothelial cell, platelet, monocyte, neutrophil, complement, and
coagulation system activation), immunosuppressive therapies
are being used with variable success.
One small, descriptive,
cross-sectional study provides evidence that B cell depletion with
rituximab is well tolerated and can be effective for refractory
thrombocytopenia and skin ulcers in aPL-positive patients.
An open-label phase IIa descriptive prospective pilot study
(RITAPS) assessing the effectiveness and safety of rituximab in
patients with noncriteria and/or anti-coagulation-resistant manifestations of aPL demonstrated that rituximab, even without
inducing substantial change in aPL profiles, may be effective in
controlling some noncriteria manifestations of aPL.
Similarly,
reports of belimumab, another B cell depleting agent, suggest a
potential role in treating primary APS patients with microvascular involvement such as livedoid vasculopathy and DAH.
Use
of other immunosuppressive agents, including mofetil mycophenolate, azathioprine, cyclophosphamide, and sirolimus, have
been described in retrospective studies or case reports in patients
with
microangiopathic APS
(MAPS) or nonthrombotic APS. In
some patients with MAPS, especially those with DAH, glucocorticoids induce remission, but steroid-sparing immunosuppressive medication is required in at least half of these patients.
Cyclophosphamide- or rituximab-based regimens appear most
effective, but intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), plasma
exchange,
mycophenolate mofetil
(MMF), and/or azathioprine
can be considered as well.

In general, our outpatient approach for mild chronic microvascular disease, such as chronic DAH or chronic aPL nephropathy,
is to initiate glucocorticoid treatment (up to 60 mg daily) along
with MMF with or without rituximab. Rituximab may be added
for persistent symptoms or aPL-related hematologic or microvascular manifestations. IVIG can be added on for refractory disease. For DAH in particular, the risk-to-benefit ratio of stopping
anti-coagulation therapy needs to be carefully considered. Sirolimus can be considered in patients with aPL nephropathy unable
to tolerate MMF or rituximab; given the high risk of toxicity,
cyclophosphamide is generally avoided except in refractory cases.
Despite the lack of strong data, for patients with MAPS we also
typically add hydroxychloroquine to reduce thrombosis risk and
a statin to reduce pro-inflammatory and prothrombotic markers.

### Catastrophic Antiphospholipid Syndrome

The onset of CAPS is usually sudden and immediately life threatening. Early diagnosis can be a challenge, especially in patients
with no history of APS. However, early diagnosis is critical
because, in contrast to other causes of multiple organ dysfunction
syndrome, appropriate therapy includes anti-coagulation and corticosteroids in combination with repeated plasma exchange, IVIG,
and, in desperate situations, other modalities such as cyclophosphamide or rituximab.
Rituximab, typically reserved for disease
refractory to anti-coagulation and glucocorticoids, can be particularly helpful in cases of microvascular involvement and/or severe
thrombocytopenia, both of which are also common in thrombotic
thrombocytopenic purpura.
A case report described improvement of post–kidney transplant TMA in an eculizumab (terminal
complement inhibitor)-treated APS patient, suggesting a role for
anti-complement therapy.
In another CAPS patient resistant
to anti-coagulation, immunosuppression, plasmapheresis, and
rituximab, eculizumab successfully blocked complement activity,
aborted progressive thrombosis, and reversed thrombocytopenia.
Since then, supportive data include small series and additional case reports, which have provided some encouraging data.
Of 39 patients with CAPS from the “CAPS Registry” treated with
eculizumab, 74% recovered, 23% worsened, and 5 died
; other
studies have demonstrated improvements in hematologic parameters such as microangiopathic hemolytic anemia and thrombocytopenia but not renal disease.
While further studies are needed,
complement inhibition can be considered when the CAPS event
also includes a systemic TMA picture (e.g., thrombocytopenia) in
addition to the organ involvement.

### Pregnancy Morbidity

Heparin is indicated at the diagnosis of pregnancy in an aPLpositive woman who has had prior pregnancy losses attributable
to APS. Because warfarin is teratogenic, only unfractionated or
low-molecular-weight heparin is used for the treatment of affected
pregnancies in the United States; in other countries, converting to
warfarin after the first trimester may be considered acceptable.
Low-molecular-weight heparin is the treatment of choice due to
decreased risk of thrombocytopenia and osteoporosis.
Patients with prior fetal losses later than 10 gestational weeks
should be treated with prophylactic heparin (enoxaparin 30 to 40
mg subcutaneously once daily), together with low-dose aspirin;
this regimen increases the fetal survival rate from 50 (untreated)
to 80%.
Women who have had prior thromboses must be
fully anti-coagulated (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg subcutaneously twice
daily or 1.5 mg/kg subcutaneously once daily) throughout pregnancy because the risk of new thrombosis markedly increases both
during pregnancy and during the postpartum period. Even with
treatment, prematurity and fetal growth restriction may occur.
Clopidogrel and newer anti-thrombotic agents have not been
cleared for use in pregnancy. IVIG and hydroxychloroquine can
be considered in patients who fail heparin treatment.
In aPL-positive women with prior thrombosis, warfarin is
changed to heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin before conception, if possible, or at the first missed menstrual period. In
aPL-positive women without prior thrombosis, if heparin is indicated during pregnancy, treatment begins after confirmation of
pregnancy, continues until 48 hours before anticipated delivery
(to allow epidural anesthesia), and resumes for 8 to 12 weeks during the postpartum period. Some physicians recommend the initiation of heparin before conception; no clinical trial supports this
recommendation, however, and the risk associated with longerduration heparin therapy is considerable. Patients in most published series received low-dose aspirin as well as heparin, but the
benefit of adding aspirin is unknown.
Because of the risk of postpartum thrombosis, it is prudent
to continue anti-coagulation for 8 to 12 weeks during the postpartum period and then to discontinue it by tapering the doses.
If desired, conversion from heparin to warfarin may be accomplished after the first or second postpartum week. Breastfeeding is
permissible with both heparin and warfarin.
No studies unequivocally justify the treatment of women with
aPLs during a first pregnancy, women with only very early losses,
or women whose aPL titers are low or transient. Nonetheless, it is
common to offer such patients low-dose aspirin.

## Outcome

Pulmonary hypertension, neurologic involvement, myocardial
ischemia, nephropathy, gangrene of extremities, and CAPS are
associated with a worse prognosis. During long-term follow-up,
serious morbidity and disability occur in an unpredictable proportion of primary APS patients who experience major vascular events
and in those who have delays in diagnosis and treatment. Thus the
long-term functional outcome of primary APS patients is poor; at
10 years, one-third of patients develop permanent organ damage,
and one-fifth are unable to perform everyday activities.
CAPS has a high fatality rate if untreated; even with aggressive
treatment, mortality may exceed 30%, primarily due to cerebrovascular, cardiac, and infectious etiologies.
Survival may be
improved with early recognition and rapid initiation of appropriate treatment. Relapse of CAPS has been shown in up to one-third
of patients; while studies on prevention are limited, reducing the
likelihood of CAPS relapse may result from minimizing triggers
such as periods without anti-coagulation, untreated infections,
or exposure to procoagulants such as estrogen-containing oral
contraceptives.
Long-term outcomes of children born of APS pregnancies
are not known. In many patients with long-standing APS, the
development of severe cardiac valvular disease necessitates valve
replacement, and rare patients develop renal failure due to TMA.
Immediate thrombosis may cause loss of a transplanted kidney
or other organs; aPL positivity correlates with poor graft survival
after renal transplantation in SLE patients.
Serious perioperative complications may occur despite prophylaxis in aPL-positive patients because they are at additional
risk for thrombosis when undergoing surgical procedures. Thus

perioperative strategies should be clearly identified before any
surgical procedure is performed; in addition, pharmacologic
and physical anti-thrombosis interventions should be vigorously
employed, periods without anti-coagulation should be kept to
an absolute minimum, intravascular manipulation for access
and monitoring should be minimized, and any deviation from
a normal course should be considered a potential disease-related
event.
Optimal management of anti-coagulation and proper
use of bridging therapy based on perioperative guidelines is associated with fewer thrombotic and hemorrhagic complications in
patients with APS undergoing surgical procedures.
