# Reactive Arthritis

ALAN P. HUDSON AND JOHN D. CARTER

KEY POINTS

> Reactive arthritis is a form of spondyloarthritis triggered by any of

> several particular infections.

> Reactive arthritis is often self-limiting, can become chronic, and can

> have both peripheral and axial features.

> Diagnosis of reactive arthritis depends on symptoms and signs of

> spondyloarthritis, including various extra-articular disease aspects,

> as well as evidence of a preceding infection; chlamydial infections

> are often difficult to demonstrate, however.

> Treatment of reactive arthritis is conservative in early self-limiting

> disease but more aggressive in chronic disease.

## Introduction and Historical Background

Reactive arthritis (ReA) is an inflammatory syndrome that by formal definition can result from certain gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections. The classic triad of symptoms comprising ReA
includes acute inflammatory arthritis, inflammatory eye conditions (conjunctivitis), and dysuria. However, most patients who
develop the arthritis do not present with this triad. Indeed many
other clinical manifestations of ReA have been described.
Interestingly and as developed next, acute inflammatory arthritis in
concert with other possible clinical manifestations following specific gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections has been known
for centuries. Importantly, ReA is one of the first systemic disease
processes to be recognized as resulting from a classic host–pathogen interaction.
The term
reactive arthritis
was introduced in 1969 to describe
this syndrome.
However, considerable ambiguity exists in the
literature in terms of the nomenclature of the condition, and
many terms and eponyms have been utilized. Perhaps the most
common eponym used until relatively recently was
Reiter’s syndrome
. In 1942 two Harvard researchers, Bauer and Engelmann,
recognized a case of ReA, and upon their review of the literature,
they discovered that Dr. Hans Reiter had described the same syndrome in 1916.
Reiter’s patient, a German army officer, developed acute arthritis, nongonococcal urethritis, and conjunctivitis
after an episode of dysentery. In recent years the designation
Reiter’s syndrome
has become problematic, because after World War
II Hans Reiter was arrested, tried, and convicted at Nuremburg
for war crimes. It also has been suggested that health care providers too often have associated the term
Reiter’s syndrome
exclusively
with the “classic triad” of symptoms, thereby resulting in under-/
misdiagnosis (see below). Currently, ReA is classified among the
spondyloarthropathies, and it has been given several different clinical designations, including, originally,
Reiter’s disease
or
syndrome
.
More usually, however, the arthritis has been referred to simply
as ReA. Some reports indicate that about half of all ReA cases are
due to genital infection with
Chlamydia trachomatis,
and in such
cases the disease is simply designated
Chlamydia
-induced arthriInterestingly, several reports have pointed out that some cases

of ReA can be attributable to infection with the related respiratory
pathogen
Chlamydia pneumoniae
.
The historical literature is replete with descriptions of patients
with ReA. Importantly and more recently, recognition of the
critical interplay between host and pathogen has become common in that literature. The earliest description of ReA may be that
from circa 460 B.C., when Hippocrates wrote: “A youth does not
suffer from gout until sexual intercourse”; at the time, the term
gout
was used indiscriminately to describe inflammatory arthritis
of any origin.
Several authors have speculated that Christopher
Columbus may have had ReA in 1494, when he developed fever
and severe arthritis of the lower extremities after a case of dysentery.
Several others have described similar cases, including van
Forest’s description of a case of “secondary arthritis and urethritis” in 1507,
Sydenham’s association of arthritis with diarrhea in
1686,
Stoll’s documentation of arthritis following dysentery in
1776,
and Yvan’s description of a French captain who developed
“ophthalmia” and inflammatory arthritis of the lower extremities
after a venereal infection.
Perhaps the earliest and most eloquent
description of ReA in the modern clinical literature was penned
by Brodie in 1818 when he described five patients with urethritis,
arthritis, and conjunctivitis.
In 1824, Cooper proposed a relationship between venereal infections and arthritis, particularly of
the lower extremities, and in 1916 two French physicians (Feis
singer and Leroy) described a clear case of ReA.
In addition to
the descriptions of ReA by both Reiter and the French physicians
in the same year more than a century ago, the literature clearly
includes description of cases of ReA many years before either
Reiter or Feissinger and Leroy (for further discussion, see ref. 17).
The standard etiologic agents triggering ReA have been defined
and include various species of gastrointestinal pathogens from the
genera
Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter,
and
Yersinia,
as well
as the genitourinary pathogen
C
.
trachomatis.
Importantly, many
additional bacterial pathogens and several viral pathogens have
been implicated recently as likely or potential triggers for ReA as
well, as discussed below.

## Epidemiology

Despite extensive description over the centuries, as well as the
clear realization that ReA results from a bacterial infection, epidemiologic studies of the disease remain problematic. As suggested
above, significant ambiguity has long existed concerning the exact
nomenclature for this clinical entity, and similar ambiguity occurs
in terms of an apparent over-reliance on the so-called classic
triad of ReA in diagnosis. Further, several studies have suggested
a similar over-reliance on the presence of the HLA-B27 antigen
to diagnose ReA.
Because ReA represents the classic interplay
between host and environment and because bacterial populations
and genetics vary worldwide, the incidence and prevalence of the
disease vary widely. The majority of ReA cases are transient in
nature, adding to the difficulty in gathering epidemiologic data.
In the case of
Chlamydia
-induced ReA specifically, emerging data
strongly suggest that the initial infection can be clinically silent,
rendering the ensuing diagnosis extremely difficult.
The percentage of patients with ReA who develop peripheral arthritis versus
axial arthritis versus a combination of both also remains poorly
understood. Most importantly, no universally accepted sets of
classification or diagnostic criteria exist at this point.
The first attempt to develop diagnostic criteria for ReA took
place in 1979, when a group of experts met and analyzed 249 cases;
however, they were unable to define universally accepted criteria.
The American College of Rheumatology proposed diagnostic criteria for ReA in 1981, but these simply specified a seronegative
arthritis that develops after an episode of urethritis, cervicitis, or
diarrhea. The Third International Workshop on ReA convened in
1995, and one of its goals was to propose new diagnostic criteria.
The criteria proposed were somewhat vague, but they did
state that the patient must have the typical peripheral arthritis
(asymmetric oligoarthritis predominately of the lower limbs), as
well as evidence of a preceding infection (clinical diarrhea or urethritis within the preceding 4 weeks); in cases in which no clear
clinical infection has been established, laboratory confirmation of
infection is essential. It should be noted that the specification of 4
weeks was somewhat arbitrary, since some patients develop their
arthritis 6 or more weeks after infection, but it was agreed that the
extension of the time beyond 4 weeks would make the criteria less
specific. The group recognized the weakening association of HLAB27 with ReA even then and specifically stated that its determination should play no part in the diagnostic criteria. The Fourth
International Workshop met in 1999, adding some clarity to the
nomenclature and classification of ReA.
Agreement was reached
to use the term
reactive arthritis
; it was also agreed that clinicians
should differentiate between acute and chronic ReA using a recommended cutoff of 6 months’ duration.
Because the majority of individuals exposed to causative bacteria do not develop ReA, the concept of “attack rate” is important.
The attack rate represents the percentage of people exposed to a
specific bacterial infection who develop ReA. Attack rates can vary
widely among the causative bacteria and in various populations,
but it generally ranges from 1% to about 30%, depending on the
study and causative organism in post-enteric ReA
; that of
post-chlamydial ReA ranges from 4% to 8%.
Because the
prevalence of the bacterial triggers can also vary widely in populations and over time, especially with enteric organisms, the incidence and prevalence also vary accordingly.
The sex ratio for post-enteric ReA is nearly 1:1, but venereally
acquired ReA is more often diagnosed in men. This is primarily a function of the requirement for confirmed genital infection,
and such infections are frequently subclinical in women.
Adults are more likely to develop ReA than children.
In Scandinavia, an annual incidence of 10/100,000 to 30/100,000 has
been reported.
In one of those studies, incidence rates of postenteric ReA and post-chlamydial ReA were essentially equal at
5/100,000 and 4.6/100,000, respectively.
In the United States,
the estimated age-adjusted annual incidence of ReA in males
younger than age 50 was 3.5/100,000
; however, no female cases
were identified, strongly suggesting an underestimate. A German
study indicated a prevalence of 10/100,000.
ReA has become
more common in Africa in the wake of the AIDS epidemic, without any association with B27, an allele that is rare in these populations.
The expected number of cases of ReA compared with
those actually diagnosed strongly argues that ReA is underdiagnosed overall; perhaps this is most true with post-chlamydial ReA.
Because acute genital
C. trachomatis
infections are a reportable
disease in the United States, the annual incidence of such infections is well described. An estimate of the incidence of
Chlamydia
-
induced ReA in the United States using the known attack rate
suggests significant mis- or underdiagnosis.
Initial reports suggested that 60% to 85% of patients who
develop ReA secondary to
Shigella, Yersinia,
or
C. trachomatis
infection are HLA-B27 positive.
However, other studies suggest
a lower prevalence of B27 in
Salmonella
-induced ReA, and one
study suggested no such association in
Campylobacter
-induced
ReA.
Several more recent community-based or commonsource epidemic studies demonstrate the prevalence of B27 in
ReA to be less than 50%.
Data regarding HLA-B27 in the
setting of ReA, however, are convincing that this HLA antigen
lends to disease chronicity.
It also appears to render the host
with more fulminant acute symptoms, and it more often predisposes to the classic triad of symptoms.
It should be emphasized at this point that, in addition to the bacterial pathogens
classically understood to be the etiologic agents for ReA, recent
studies have associated a large and diverse array of infectious
organisms with this clinical entity. Although many of these studies are case reports that include only small numbers of patients,
we consider it important to provide some initial discussion of this
emerging etiologic issue.

## Etiology and Triggering Organisms

Bacteria long-recognized to be definitive etiologic triggers of ReA
include several species from the genera
Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter,
and
Yersinia,
as well as the genital pathogen
C
.
trachomatis
.
However, many other infectious agents have been implicated
as potential causes of ReA, including
C. pneumoniae, Ureaplasma
urealyticum, Helicobacter pylori,
various intestinal parasites,
Escherichia coli
,
Clostridioides difficile
,
and intravesicular
Bacillus
Calmette–Guerin (BCG).
Further and as mentioned above,
additional infectious organisms have been postulated to elicit
ReA, including HIV,
Cryptosporidium, Giardia lamblia, Cyclospora
cayetanensis, Entamoeba hartmanni, Vibrio parahaemolyticus, Orientia tsutsugamushi, Rickettsia conorii, Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Staphylococcus haemolyticus,
and importantly
severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS–CoV-2)
(for detailed discussion of these organisms, see ref.
51
). Again,
many of these latter associations appeared only in one or a few
case reports, with few patients described. More information will
be required before these pathogens are established in the etiology
of ReA, and if any are so established, some revision in the formal
definition of this clinical entity will be required.

C. trachomatis
is the most common etiologic agent causing ReA
in the United States.
Interestingly, the clinical features of ReA
appear to be congruent whether the inciting infection is enteric
or venereal. The post-dysentery form of ReA is always preceded
by a symptomatic infection, and many studies suggest that the
more severe the initial gastrointestinal infection, the more likely
ReA is to develop.
In contrast and as mentioned above, initial
genital
C. trachomatis
infections are often asymptomatic,
especially in women, and data indicate these initial asymptomatic
infections do indeed engender ReA.
The four enteric organisms known to cause ReA represent a
large proportion of the cases of ReA as a group.
Salmonella
are
gram-negative, rod-shaped, motile bacteria that are widespread in
animals and environmental sources. Because
Salmonella
outbreaks
are relatively common worldwide, these organisms are the most
frequently studied enteric bacteria associated with ReA. The attack
rate for
Salmonella
-induced ReA has ranged between 6% and 30%
in different studies.
All four
Shigella
species (
S. flexneri, S. dysenteriae, S. sonnei,
and
S. boydii
) can cause arthritis. The attack rate
for
Shigella
-induced ReA has been estimated at 7% to 9%.
Campylobacter
spp. also are gram-negative motile bacteria, and they
are an extremely common cause of enteric infections. In spite of
high infection frequency, these organisms appear to be somewhat
less arthritogenic, with an attack rate of 1% to 5%.
Yersinia
spp.
have been estimated to have an attack rate of 12% to 23%,
but these organisms apparently have been a less frequent cause of
ReA in recent years.
C. trachomatis
is an obligate intracellular gram-negative pathogen; this bacterial species is comprised of 14 strains/serovars (A,
B, Ba, and C are ocular/trachoma serovars, D to K are genital
serovars), plus 3 biovars of lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV;
L1 to L3). Chlamydial infections are not usually clonal, and these
organisms can and frequently do exchange DNA among strains,
making the production of new substrains common. The attack
rate for genital
C. trachomatis
in ReA is 4% to 8%.
C. trachomatis
has been demonstrated in about half of patients with a preceding
symptomatic urogenital infection who developed ReA.
Importantly, two previous studies with similar results demonstrated that
asymptomatic initial infections can lead to ReA, perhaps in the
majority of cases.
Historically, infection with LGV has been
relatively uncommon in the United States and Europe, although
these organisms have been identified increasingly in anal infections in men who have sex with men; some recent studies have
implicated LGV as the source for ReA in patients with such infections.
We again note that the related respiratory pathogen
C.
pneumonia
has been implicated as a cause of ReA, although available data indicate that it does so at lower frequency than does
C.
trachomatis
.
ReA has been described as a “sterile” arthritis that develops following infection by these known bacterial triggers, the apparent
sterility due to the inability to grow the organisms by routine culture methods from the synovial fluid in patients with the arthritis.
However, many studies have demonstrated that the causative chlamydiae in ReA can infect monocytic cells at the site of primary
infection, and they are held intracellularly during dissemination
from the genital tract to the synovium. The processes that allow
the bacteria to remain intact within monocytic cells have yet to
be elucidated, but it has been clearly demonstrated that in this
context the organisms undergo a transcriptional arrest for certain
genes governing cell division late in the intracellular developmental cycle.
Analyses using polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and
reverse transcriptase-PCR have shown that synovial chlamydiae
within their monocytic host cells are viable and metabolically
active, albeit in an aberrant state termed
persistence
. These aberrant synovial chlamydiae have been detected by electron microscopy.
Many of the gene products that are produced at high
levels during a normal acute chlamydial infection are down-regulated strongly when the organisms are in the persistent state.
Persistent chlamydiae have been demonstrated in other organ tissues and other disease states, suggesting that they may be involved
in additional clinical entities. Several well-controlled studies of
ReA have demonstrated consistently that the PCR positivity rate
for chlamydiae DNA is significantly higher in ReA patients compared with controls. One surprising recent report indicated that
synovium-based persistent chlamydiae uniquely belong to the
ocular, not genital, serovars.
This unusual observation has not
yet been confirmed, but ocular chlamydial strains in the initial
infectious material may explain in part two unusual features of
Chlamydia
-induced ReA. First, ocular serovars are included only
rarely as part of the inoculum in genital infections (1% to 5% of
the time), potentially explaining the attack rate. Second, patients
with
Chlamydia
-induced ReA often develop ocular symptoms.
Interestingly, recent studies have shown that increased tryptophan
levels can reactivate persistent chlamydiae to the normal biphasic
developmental cycle, and a significant difference is known to exist
in the capacity of ocular and genital
C. trachomatis
serovars to
counter tryptophan deprivation. Clues to the apparent arthritogenicity of the ocular chlamydial serovars may have been seen years
ago. In the 1960s and early 1970s, “Bedsonia” (now know to be
C. trachomatis
) recovered from patients with ReA caused arthritis
100% of the time when injected into rabbits. Congruently, intraarticular injection of these same organisms often caused ocular
involvement.
As with chlamydiae, extensive efforts have been made to detect
the enteric pathogens in synovial tissue or fluid of patients with
post-enteric ReA. Bacterial DNA or bacterial degradation products have been identified, but unlike the case for chlamydiae,
viable organisms have not been demonstrated.
The single
possible exception to this was published in a study targeting
Yersinia
; to our knowledge, this observation has not been replicated
in another similar study.
Whether these synovially based persistent organisms or bacterial fragments trigger an autoimmune
response or whether they actually drive the inflammatory process
remains to be determined.
In all established and possible etiologic contexts, ReA represents a classic interplay of host and environment. The environmental triggers outlined above play an undeniable role in
disease genesis and possibly propagation. However, genetic susceptibility also plays a role. As mentioned, the role that HLAB27 plays in the pathophysiology of ReA has been overstated.
Certainly, B27-negative patients can and do develop ReA, but
it is important to remember that HLA-B27 has multiple alleles
that could influence host response and disease susceptibility.
Few studies have analyzed the specific HLA-B27 alleles in the
context of ReA. One recent such report, however, suggested that
HLA-B*2705 is the most common allele observed in B27-positive ReA patients.
Another study suggested that HLA-B*5703
increases the risk for the classic triad of symptoms of ReA in a
specific population.
Much remains to be learned about other
HLA loci and non-HLA genes that may be important in the
pathophysiology of ReA. A good deal of data exists that indicate
that patients with HIV are at increased risk for ReA and that the
symptoms can improve with anti-retroviral HIV therapy (ref.
78
and see below).

## Clinical Features

In the context of ReA elicited by the classic enteric and venereal
pathogens, the disease shares many clinical features in common
with the other spondyloarthritides (SpAs). In spite of the fact that
ReA has several distinct etiologic agents, as well as some differences in the pathophysiology depending on the classic triggering
microbe, clinical features of ReA are quite congruent regardless of
the initiating infection. The clinical syndrome that encompasses
ReA can involve many different organ systems, although it has
a predilection for the synovium, urethra, eye, and skin (
Table
77.1
). Symptoms typically become apparent within 1 to 4 weeks
of the initial infection, but occasionally the delay can be longer.
If a patient experiences symptoms of longer than 6 months’ duration, they are diagnosed as having chronic disease. Traditionally,
rheumatologists have felt that 90% or more of cases of ReA resolve
spontaneously within weeks to months, but more recent observations suggest that a higher percentage of patients, although still a
minority, proceed to develop chronic disease.
Typically,
patients have more significant symptoms during the acute phase
of the disease, and these can include constitutional symptoms
(malaise, fatigue) and fever. If symptoms persist, the long-term
manifestations tend to be milder. In these chronic cases, it is not
unusual for symptoms to wax and wane. In cases in which apparent ReA is elicited by a noncanonical organism, the initial clinical
features can differ somewhat from those given above. For example,
in the case of SARS–CoV-2 infection, the arthritis may not manifest itself for as long as 3 weeks post-infection; involvement may
be oligoarticular, as in enteric or venereal elicitation, and psoriatic lesions along with enthesitis may be present.
Culture and
molecular analyses targeting the virus in synovial fluid and related
areas are negative in many cases.
Of all the SpAs, the phenotypic articular manifestations of ReA
are most similar to psoriatic arthritis (PsA). These articular-based
inflammatory features include synovitis, enthesitis, and dactylitis.
While both ReA and PsA can present as peripheral or axial arthritis or a combination of both, the peripheral arthritis of ReA most
typically involves the large joints of the lower extremities (
Fig.
77.1
). Epidemiologic data are not available by which to determine
the frequency of peripheral arthritis vs. axial involvement alone
vs. a combination of both, but many rheumatologists agree that
peripheral involvement with an asymmetric oligoarticular distribution is the most common. Nearly all patients with ReA have
peripheral symptoms at presentation, with about half showing
axial symptoms.
Of those with peripheral involvement, some data suggest that
enthesitis is more common than synovitis.
Two different types of
entheses (bony attachment of a tendon or ligament) are known in
the human body, and both seem to be susceptible to inflammatory
disease in ReA.
Common sites of enthesitis include the Achilles
tendon (
Fig. 77.2
), plantar insertion/fascia, and patellar tendon;
this also appears in many cases of arthritis resulting from SARS–
CoV-2.
Some authors have argued that sacroiliitis is, at least in
part, a form of enthesitis. Evidence of enthesitis can often be seen
on plain radiographs in patients with chronic ReA (
Fig. 77.3
).
Dactylitis is a relatively common finding in ReA, as it is in
other types of SpA, especially PsA (
Fig. 77.4
). One series of studies suggested that it was present in 28% of patients presenting
with ReA.
The underlying pathophysiology has been difficult
to define, but elements of synovitis, enthesitis, and tenosynovitis
all have been recognized.
Dactylitis also is helpful with the differential diagnosis, in that it is only seen in a few other conditions
(sarcoidosis, gout, sickle cell disease, and a variety of infections).
Dactylitis can be refractory to treatment.
The most common extra-articular features of ReA are mucocutaneous and ocular in nature. Urethritis or cervicitis occurs rather
frequently in post-chlamydial ReA, but these same symptoms are
rarely seen in the post-enteric variety. However as noted above, the
absence of these symptoms does not rule out
Chlamydia
-induced
ReA. Typical skin lesions include keratoderma blenorrhagicum, circinate balanitis, and nail changes; more rarely, oral ulcers can occur.
Keratoderma blenorrhagicum is clinically indistinct from palmoplantar pustular psoriasis (
Fig. 77.5
). Relatively little information
is available to define how frequently this rash occurs in ReA, but
early reports describe it in about 20% of cases.
The same is true
for circinate balanitis; estimates suggest it occurs in
∼
10% to 15%
of cases
(
Fig. 77.6
). Interestingly, the histologic features of circinate balanitis resemble pustular psoriasis,
and chronic cases can
look like plaque psoriasis. Nail changes include onycholysis and
pitting, and these occur in about 10% of patients.
The relationship between distal interphalangeal arthritis and nail involvement
is well recognized in PsA
; the same also is likely to be true for
ReA, but no definitive data exist in this regard. The most common form of ocular involvement includes conjunctivitis and anterior uveitis, but many other manifestations have been described.
Ocular involvement is unilateral in about two-thirds of cases, and
all ocular manifestations can become chronic and/or recurrent,
requiring systemic therapy. The relatively limited available observations indicate that many of the extra-articular features elicited
by noncanonical pathogens are not seen; for one example, in the
case of ReA elicited by
C. pneumoniae,
no ocular features or skin
lesions have been observed. Extant data for arthritis apparently due
to other organisms is incomplete and requires further definition.

## Laboratory, Radiographic, and Advanced Imaging Findings

No diagnostic test for ReA is currently available. The diagnosis
remains clinical despite the lack of validated/accepted diagnostic criteria. Every effort should be made to culture the triggering organism from genital or stool specimens in patients with
acute ReA, but attempts to document the causative bacteria are
often unsuccessful. Historically, this is particularly true in postchlamydial ReA. These triggering bacteria can be cultured from
synovial fluid or tissue only rarely, but as detailed above these bacteria or bacterial products have been demonstrated in such synovial specimens using PCR. PCR has been suggested for analysis
of urethral or cervical specimens in patients with suspected
Chlamydia
-induced ReA, but to date no data are available to know
the sensitivity or specificity of such testing (see refs. 91 and 92
for review). Because these bacteria traffic to synovial tissue in an
apparently rapid manner, genital specimens may test negative even
by PCR. Acute phase reactants (APRs) are often elevated in the
acute phase.
In chronic ReA, stool and urogenital sampling to assess causative organisms has been analyzed, but many patients test negative, thus limiting the utility of this approach.
No data are
available to assess whether PCR analysis of urethral or cervical
specimens in patients with chronic disease might be useful. PCR
analysis of synovial specimens might be useful in chronic patients,
but this approach currently is utilized only as a research tool. Further, serologic testing for antibodies to the causative organisms
is unreliable.

## Treatment

The role that triggering organisms play in the genesis of ReA is
undisputed. However, whether these bacteria drive the inflammatory process or trigger a related but independent immune response
remains to be resolved. Treatment for acute ReA is relatively
straightforward, but the ambiguity that surrounds the pathophysiology is mirrored in the various treatment strategies for those who
develop chronic disease.
Most cases of ReA are self-limiting, so the initial treatment
is often conservative. As in other types of SpA, nonsteroidal
anti-inflammatory drugs are effective for most cases as an initial
treatment approach. Two randomized clinical trials have been performed demonstrating their efficacy. The first was a double-blind
crossover study comparing azapropazone to indomethacin in
patients with both PsA and ReA.
Both medications were efficacious, with a trend favoring indomethacin. The second compared
ketoprofen to indomethacin in 50 patients with ReA
; both were
efficacious with no significant difference between the two. However, indomethacin does tend to have more side effects than other
NSAIDs, so treatment with any NSAID is appropriate.
In those patients who do not respond to NSAIDs, corticosteroids have been utilized. Because ReA can present as a mono- or
oligoarthritis, intra-articular steroids are often quite effective.
Enthesitis may benefit from intralesional glucocorticoids. It has
been suggested that systemic corticosteroids are more effective
for the peripheral symptoms rather than the axial manifestations.
Topical corticosteroids are the initial treatment of
many of the extra-articular features of ReA, including iritis/uveitis, keratoderma blenorrhagicum, and circinate balanitis.
In patients with HIV infection and ReA, many of whom have
severe skin lesions, the skin lesions in particular respond to antiretroviral therapy.
Data also demonstrate that prompt treatment of the triggering
infections with antibiotics decreases the likelihood of the development of ReA.

## Conclusion

ReA is unique in that it is a systemic disease with known etiologic triggers. It represents the classic interplay between host
and environment. Because the bacterial triggers usually cannot
be detected with routine culture from synovial fluid, it has been
referred to as a “sterile” inflammatory arthritis arising after such
infections. However, recent advances in our understanding of the
pathophysiology of ReA have called into question whether this is
truly the case. Because acute ReA is often self-limiting, the initial
treatment approach should be conservative, but more aggressive
treatment is warranted in those with chronic disease. Importantly,
much emphasis has been placed on understanding host genetics
in the determination of disease susceptibility, but specific bacterial
characteristics appear to be uniquely arthritogenic. Recent studies
suggesting new etiologic organisms for the arthritis may require
significant rethinking regarding the host and pathogen contributions to disease development.
