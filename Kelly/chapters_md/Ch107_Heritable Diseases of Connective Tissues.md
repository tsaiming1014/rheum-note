## Introduction

Heritable disorders of connective tissues are a heterogenous group
of disorders characterized by abnormalities in skeletal tissues,
which include cartilage, bone, tendon, ligament, and muscle. Disorders of connective tissues have been classified based on clinical
features, radiographic findings, and molecular criteria.
Historically, they are subclassified into those which primarily affect cartilage and bone, which are the skeletal dysplasias, and those that
have a more profound effect on connective tissue, which include
the Ehlers–Danlos syndrome group, Marfan syndrome, among
others. The skeletal dysplasias are associated with abnormalities
in the size and shape of the appendicular and axial skeleton and
frequently result in disproportionately short stature. Until the
early 1960s, most individuals with short stature were considered
to have pituitary dwarfism, achondroplasia (short-limb dwarfism), or Morquio disease (short-trunked dwarfism). Presently, if
genetic syndromes with significant skeletal findings and osteochondrodysplasias are considered together, there are more than
750 well-characterized disorders that are classified primarily on
the basis of clinical, radiographic, and molecular criteria. With
advances in genomic technologies, including exome and genome
analyses, newer findings have made classification of these disorders more complicated and nuanced. There are disorders that are
genetically heterogenous, resulting from mutations in multiple
genes and with differing modes of inheritance. Further, the same
gene, (e.g.,
fibrillin-1
), produces numerous conditions associated
with both tall and short statures.
This expansion in our understanding of the genetic basis of heritable disorders of the skeleton
has led to a deeper appreciation of molecular mechanisms that
underlie these disorders. Mutations in these genes encode extracellular matrix proteins, transcription factors, signal ligands and
their respective cognate receptors, signal transducers, intracellular/
cytoskeletal adaptor molecules, enzymes, molecular chaperones,
RNA processing molecules, ciliary proteins, and genes of lesserknown function.

## Skeletal Dysplasias: Overview

The skeletal dysplasias, or osteochondrodysplasias, are defined as
disorders that are associated with a generalized skeletal abnormality. While each skeletal dysplasia is relatively rare, collectively the
birth incidence of these disorders is almost 1 in 5000.
These disorders range in severity from precocious arthropathy to perinatal
lethality due to respiratory failure. Individuals with these disorders
can have significant complications including orthopedic, neurologic, craniofacial, and psychologic effects. Many of these individuals seek medical attention for orthopedic complaints due to
fractures, ongoing pain, arthritic complaints in large joints, and
back pain due to ongoing abnormalities in bone, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. Other organ systems can be affected, and in
addition, as these patients age, addressing medical comorbidities
is an important element of clinical care.

### Embryology

The human skeleton (from the Greek
skeletos
, meaning “dried
up”) is a complex organ consisting of 206 bones (126 appendicular bones, 74 axial bones, and 6 ossicles). The musculoskeletal
system—including tendons, ligaments, and muscles in addition
to cartilage and bone—has multiple embryonic origins and serves
many key functions throughout life, including linear growth,
mechanical support for movement, a blood and mineral reservoir,
and protection of vital organs.

The patterning and architecture of the skeleton occurs during fetal development. During that period, the number, size, and
the shape of the future skeletal elements are determined, and this
process is under complex and exquisitely timed genetic control.
Uncondensed mesenchyme undergoes cellular condensations
(the cartilage anlagen) at sites of future bones, and this occurs
by two mechanisms.
In the process of endochondral ossification,
mesenchyme first differentiates into a cartilage model (anlagen),
and then the center of the anlagen degrades, mineralizes, and is
removed by osteoclast-like cells. This process spreads up and down
the bones and allows for vascular invasion and influx of osteoprogenitor cells. The periosteum in the midshaft region of the bone
produces osteoblast progenitors, which then synthesize the cortex.
This is known as the primary ossification center. At the ends of the
cartilage anlagen, a similar process leading to the removal of cartilage occurs (secondary center of ossification), leaving a portion of
the cartilage model “trapped” between the expanding primary and
secondary ossification centers. This area is referred to as a cartilage
growth plate or epiphysis.
There are four chondrocyte cell types in the growth plate:
reserve, resting, proliferative, and hypertrophic. These growth
plate chondrocytes undergo a tightly regulated program of proliferation, hypertrophy, degradation, and then replacement by bone
leading to the primary spongiosa. The periosteum is a specialized
cell layer that surrounds the cartilage growth plate and is a critical signaling center and source of progenitor cells to both cartilage growth and bone formation.
This is the major mechanism
underlying skeletogenesis and is how bones increase in length and
how the articular surfaces increase in diameter. In contrast, the
flat bones of the cranial vault and part of the clavicles and pubis
form by intramembraneous ossification, during which fibrous tissue, derived from mesenchymal cells, differentiates directly into
osteoblasts, which then directly lay down bone. These processes
are under specific, temporal, and genetic control and abnormalities in the genes that encode these pathways frequently lead to
skeletal dysplasias.

### Cartilage Structure

Collagens are the most abundant proteins in mammals. Collagen
accounts for two-thirds of the adult weight attributed to articular
cartilage and provides significant strength and structure to tissues.
Collagens are a family of 29 proteins that consist of single molecules (monomers) that associate into three polypeptide chains to
form a triple-helix structure. In the triple helix, every third amino
acid is a glycine residue, and the general chain structure is denoted
as
Gly-X-Y
, where the
X
and
Y
amino acids are commonly proline and hydroxyproline. The collagen helix can be compromised
of identical chains (homotrimeric), as in type II collagen, or can
consist of different collagen chains (heterotrimeric), as seen in collagen type I.
Collagens are widely distributed throughout the body, and 45
collagen gene products are expressed in a tissue-specific manner.
Collagens are further classified by the structures they form in the
extracellular matrix. The most abundant collagens are the fibrillar
types (I, II, III, V, and XI), and their extensive cross-linking provides mechanical strength that is necessary for high-stress tissue
such as cartilage, bone, and skin.
Another collagen species is the
f﻿iibril-associated collagens with interrupted triple helices (FACIT),
which includes collagen types IX, XII, XIV, and XVI. These collagens interact with fibrillar collagens, as well as other extracellular
molecules including aggrecan, cartilage oligomeric matrix protein
(COMP), and other sulfated proteoglycans.
Collagens types VIII
and X are nonfibrillar, short-chain collagens, and type X collagen is the most abundant extracellular matrix molecule expressed
by hypertrophic chondrocytes during endochondral ossification.
The major collagens of articular cartilage are fibrillar collagen
including types II, IX, XI, and X. In developing cartilage, the core
fibrillar network is a cross-linked copolymer of collagens II, IX,
and XI
and forms the backbone complex to which other structural proteins bind. Mutations in genes that encode these collagens
result in varying skeletal dysplasias and highlight the importance
of these molecules in skeletal development.

### Classification and Nomenclature

As mentioned above, in the 1970s, there was recognition of the
genetic and clinical heterogeneity of these disorders, and there was
a new awareness of the complexity of heritable conditions leading
to skeletal dysplasia. As a result, there have been multiple attempts
to classify these disorders in a manner that clinicians and scientists
could effectively use to diagnose and determine their pathogenicity (International Nomenclature of Constitutional Diseases of
Bone, 1970, 1977, 1983, 1992, 2001, 2005, 2010, 2019, and
2023).
Initial categories were purely descriptive and clinically based. With recent advances in the ability to determine the
genetic bases of these diseases, classification has evolved into one
that combines the older clinical approach (including the eponyms
and Greek terms) and incorporates these disorders into families that share a common molecular basis or pathway. The most
widely used method for differentiating the skeletal disorders has
been through the detection of skeletal radiographic abnormalities.
Radiographic classifications are based on the different parts of the
long bones that are abnormal (epiphyses, metaphyses, and diaphyses) (
Fig. 107.1
). These epiphyseal, metaphyseal, and diaphyseal
disorders can be further differentiated depending on whether or
not the spine is involved (spondyloepiphyseal, spondylometaphyseal, or spondyloepimetaphyseal dysplasias). Moreover, each of
these classes of conditions can be further differentiated into distinct disorders based on other clinical and radiographic findings.

### Clinical Evaluation and Features

The skeletal dysplasias are generalized disorders of the skeleton
and usually result in disproportionately short stature. As a general
comment, most individuals with disproportionately short stature
have skeletal dysplasias, and those with proportionate short stature
are more likely to have an endocrine, nutritional, prenatal-onset
growth deficiency, or another nonskeletal dysplasia genetic disorder. However, there are exceptions to the rule, such as congenital hypothyroidism, which is associated with disproportionately
short stature. Disorders such as mild osteogenesis imperfecta and
hypophosphatasia can be associated with relatively normal body
proportions.
A disproportionate body habitus may not be immediately
visible on physical examination. Therefore anthropometric measurements, such as the upper/lower segment (U/L) ratio, sitting
height, and arm span must be measured when considering the
possibility of a skeletal dysplasia, and should be measured in
centimeters (
Fig. 107.2
). Sitting height is an accurate measure
of head and trunk length, but it requires special equipment for
precise measurements. Upper/lower ratios are easy to obtain and
provide an accurate measurement of proportion. The lower segment is measured from the symphysis pubis to the floor at the
inside of heel. The upper segment is measured by subtracting the

As in any disorder that has a genetic basis, it is critical to obtain
an accurate family history, and this should include any history of
previously affected children or parental consanguinity. The skeletal dysplasias are genetically heterogenous and can be inherited
as autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked recessive,
X-dominant, or Y-linked. Less frequent genetic mechanisms of
disease, including germline mosaicism, somatic mosaicism, uniparental disomy, microdeletion syndromes, and mutations in regulatory microRNAs (miRNAs) have been observed.
For many
patients and families, accurate diagnosis and recurrence risk can
have significant impact on reproductive decisions. Another consideration for patients with short stature is that there is increased
nonassortative mating, which leads to reproductive outcomes
that have been previously unknown.
For example, homozygous
achondroplasia is usually lethal, and many newborns that inherit
two dominant mutations (compound heterozygotes) are often
more significantly affected due to the synergistic effects of two
simultaneous conditions on the skeleton.
It is also important
to obtain accurate history relative to the onset of short stature
and whether it developed immediately in the postnatal period or
was noticed at age 2 or 3. Of the more than 750 skeletal dysplasias, many are prenatal in onset, but there are also many that do
not result in disproportionately short stature and joint discomfort
until childhood.
A detailed physical examination may reveal a diagnosis or help
differentiate the most likely group of possible diagnoses. It is critical, once disproportion and short stature has been established and
the limbs are involved, to determine which segment is involved:

upper segment (rhizomelic; humeri and femora), middle segment
(mesomelic; radii, ulnae, tibiae and fibulae), and distal segment
(acromelic; hands and feet). Numerous craniofacial features are
seen in the skeletal disorders. Affected individuals frequently have
disproportionately large heads or relative macrocephaly compared
with their height percentile. Frontal bossing and a flattened nasal
bridge is very characteristic of achondroplasia, one of the most
common skeletal dysplasias.
Cleft palate and micrognathia are
commonly found in the type II and XI collagen abnormalities.
Abnormal flattening of the midface with a turned-up nose is frequently found in the chondrodysplasia punctata disorders,
and
abnormal swollen pinnae are seen in diastrophic dysplasia.
Individuals with skeletal dysplasias should be screened for ophthalmologic and hearing abnormalities since many of these disorders are
associated with vision deficits and hearing loss.
Further evaluation of the hands and feet can further differentiate these disorders. Postaxial polydactyly is characteristically found
in chondroectodermal dysplasia (Ellis–Van Creveld syndrome)
and the short-rib polydactyly syndrome disorders. Short, hypermobile, radially displaced thumbs and medially deviated halluxes
are seen in diastrophic dysplasia. Nails can be abnormally hypoplastic in chondroectodermal dysplasia, short and broad in cartilage hair hypoplasia, and almost absent in nail patella syndrome.
Club feet or equinovarus can be seen in many disorders including
Kneist dysplasia, spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia congenita, Larsen
syndrome, and diastrophic dysplasia. Fractures may occur in several types of disorders including those that result from undermineralized bone (e.g., osteogenesis imperfecta, hypophosphatasia,
achondrogenesis type 1A) or those that result from over mineralized bone (e.g., osteopetrosis syndromes and dysosteosclerosis).
Other conditions with abnormal bone shape (e.g., gracile bone
dysplasia) or genetic disorders with poor bone quality (e.g., Glass
syndrome) may also be associated with fractures.
Other organ systems can be involved, though not commonly.
Congenital cardiac defects are seen in chondroectodermal dysplasia (e.g., atrial septal defects), the short-rib polydactyly syndrome
disorders (complex outlet defects including isolated ventricular
septal defects), and Larsen syndrome (ventricular septal defects).
Gastrointestinal anomalies are rare among the skeletal disorders,
but congenital megacolon can be seen in cartilage hair hypoplasia,
malabsorption syndrome in Schwachmann–Diamond syndrome,
and omphaloceles in oto-palato-digital syndrome and atelosteogenesis I. Causes of respiratory dysfunction are multifactorial in
etiology. Craniofacial abnormalities (e.g., cleft palate, midface
hypoplasia, micrognathia) may lead to obstructive apnea. Cartilage defects can lead to laryngo-tracheo-broncho-malacia. Intrinsic lung disease may be present if the genetic mutation involved
adversely effects connective tissues in the lung, resulting in abnormal alveolar structure. Abnormal chest wall compliance and deformity (e.g., kyphoscoliosis) can compromise tidal volumes.

### Diagnosis and Testing

After obtaining a thorough family history and physical examination, the next step is to obtain a full set of skeletal radiographs or
genetic skeletal survey. A full series of skeletal views, including
anterior, lateral, and Towne (angled anteroposterior) views of the
skull, anterior and lateral views of the entire spine, and anteroposterior views of the pelvis and extremities, with separate views of
the hands and feet, is optimal, especially after the newborn period.
Most of the important clues to diagnosis are in skeletal radiographs that are obtained prior to puberty. Once the epiphyses have
fused to the metaphyses, determining the precise diagnosis can be
exceedingly challenging. If an adult is evaluated, attempts should
be made to obtain any available radiographs from childhood.
There are many subtle clues in these skeletal radiographs that can
lead to a precise diagnosis. For example, punctate calcifications in
the areas of the epiphyses in the chondrodysplasia punctata disorders, which are not seen in adulthood, multiple ossification centers of the calcaneus in many disorders,
and the pattern of hand
shortening can aid in differentiating many disorders.
After obtaining radiographs, close attention should be paid
to the specific parts of the skeleton (spine, limbs, pelvis, skull)
involved and to the location of the lesions (epiphyses, metaphyses, and spondyl components) (
Fig. 107.3
). As mentioned above,
these radiographic abnormalities can change with age, and if possible serial radiographs that span age periods should be reviewed.
The presence of fractures is a clue to diagnosis, for example, in
osteogenesis imperfecta and severe hypophosphatasia. In older
individuals, fractures may be seen in disorders associated with
increased mineralization, such as the osteopetrosis syndromes.
Morphologic studies of chondro-osseus tissue have revealed
specific abnormalities in many of the skeletal dysplasias,

done with much less frequency. However, histologic evaluation

### Management and Treatment

The optimal management of this diverse set of disorders requires
an understanding of their natural history. This is often best
accomplished by centers that have a multidisciplinary approach,
including adult and pediatric physicians, as well as geneticists,
orthopedists, rheumatologists, otolaryngologists, neurologists,
neurosurgeons, and ophthamologists who are committed to the
care of these patients.
Most medical complications in these disorders result from skeletal manifestations and vary depending on the specific disorder.
For example, in disorders associated with significant odontoid
hypoplasia such as Morquio disease, type II collagenopathies,
metatropic dysplasia, and Larsen syndrome, flexion–extension
films should be monitored at regular intervals to assess for C1–C2
subluxation. If there is evidence of significant subluxation, surgery
for C1–C2 fixation is indicated. Some providers support flexion
and extension radiographic evaluation, including MRI of the neck
in all cases of skeletal dysplasia, since so many of these disorders

Frequently, patients with these disorders, which involve

weight control in adults with short stature is an ongoing issue
with obesity contributing to inactivity, loss of function, and
adult-onset diseases such as diabetes, hypertension, and coronary artery disease.
Achondroplasia is the most common of the nonlethal skeletal
dysplasias (approximately 1 in 20,000) and serves as an example
of how to approach these disorders, particularly because the incidence and type of medical complications evolve over the lifespan
of the individual. People with achondroplasia have typical intelligence and typical lifespan and can lead independent and productive lives. The mean final height in achondroplasia is 130 cm
for men and 125 cm for women. Diagnosis-specific growth and
developmental charts have been developed for tracking linear
growth, head circumference, weight, and gross-motor and development milestones.
In early infancy, there is a potential for serious compression of
the cervico-medullary spinal cord secondary to a narrow foramen
magnum, cervical canal, or both. Clinically, these infants show
central apnea, sleep apnea, profound hypotonia, motor delay,
excessive sweating, or neurologic abmormalities (e.g., clonus, unilateral strength differences, hyper-reflexia, or head preference).
Flexion/extension MRI with flow studies is necessary to assess
for stenosis or myelopathy. If present, decompression surgery is
indicated. Other complications include upper airway obstruction,
otitis media (serous or infectious), thoracolumbar kyphosis, and
hydrocephalous in a small number of individuals.
Recurrent
ear infections can lead to chronic serous otitis media and hearing
loss. Tympanic membrane tube placement is indicated in many of
these patients. From early childhood, and as the child begins to
walk, they can develop several orthopedic manifestations, including progressive genu vara, exaggerated lumbar lordosis, and hip
flexion contractures. Craniofacial abnormalities lead to dental
malocclusion, and appropriate treatment is necessary. As adults,
the main potential medical complication is impingement of the
spinal root canals, which can manifest with lower limb paresthesias, claudication, clonus, bladder, or bowel dysfunction. It

is critical that these complaints are addressed in a timely fashion
since decompression surgery may prevent paralysis.
Growth hormone replacement has not been effective in
increasing height in children with achondroplasia.
Surgical
limb lengthening has been employed successfully to increase

Presently, there is

Vosoritide is

other skeletal dysplasias and their families experience various
psychosocial challenges.
This can be addressed by specialized

### Biochemical and Molecular Abnormalities

Based on similarities in clinical, radiographic, and histomorphologic characteristics, heritable disorders of connective tissue have
been placed into bone dysplasia “families.”
These families share
common pathophysiologic or pathway mechanisms. In recent
years, there have been substantial improvements in our understanding of the basic biology of these disorders. This has resulted
from advances in the human genome project that improved various methodologies allowing for identification of the responsible
genes. Genes associated with the osteochondrodysplasias can be
organized into several categories to facilitate understanding of
their pathogenesis: (1) defects in extracellular proteins; (2) defects
in metabolic pathways (enzymes, ion channels, and transporters); (3) defects in folding and degradation of macromolecules;
(4) defects in hormones and signal transduction; (5) defects in
nuclear proteins; (6) defects in oncogenes and tumor suppressor
genes; (7) defects in RNA and DNA processing molecules; (8)
defects in intracellular structural proteins; (9) defects in chaperones; (10) defects in ciliary components; and (11) genes coding
proteins with functions that remain unknown. Examples of the
molecular mechanisms involved in select skeletal dysplasias are
provided below.
Defects in Extracellular Structural Proteins
Type II Collagen and Type XI Collagen.
Since type II collagen is found primarily in cartilage, the nucleus pulposus, and
the vitreous of the eye, it has been hypothesized that skeletal
disorders with significant spine and eye abnormalities would
result from defects in type II collagen. Indeed, type II collagen
defects have been identified in a spectrum of disorders ranging
from lethal to mild arthropathy, including achondrogenesis II,
hypochondrogenesis, spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia congenita,
platyspondylic dysplasia Torrance type, spondlyoepimetaphyseal dysplasia (COL2A1-related), Kniest dysplasia, spondyloperipheral dysplasia (COL2A1-related), spondyloepiphyseal
dysplasia with metatarsal shortening (COL2A1-related), Stickler syndrome (COL2A1-related), and dysplasia of the proximal
femoral epiphyses (COL2A1-related). Together, these disorders
are referred to as
type II collagenopathies,
and all result from heterozygosity for mutations in the gene
COL2A1
that encodes type
II collagen.
Mutations that result in a substitution for a triple-helical glycine residue appear to be the most common type of mutation.
There are some correlations between the location of the mutation and the disease phenotype. In spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia
congenita (SEDC), glycine substitutions are scattered throughout
the molecule. However, in Kniest dysplasia, mutations are more
prominent in the amino-terminal end of the molecule.
Stickler syndrome is genetically heterogenous and results from mutations in
COL2A1, COL11A1,
and

.

## Specific Heritable Disorders of Connective Tissue

### Osteogenesis Imperfecta

Osteogenesis imperfecta (OI) or brittle bone disease is a heritable
disorder of bone and was one of the first genetic disorders hypothesized to result from a defect in collagen.
While OI represents
a form of osteochondrodysplasia, it will be discussed separately
from the other chondrodysplasias delineated above. OI is a generalized disorder of connective tissue that predominantly affects the
skeletal system and affects approximately 1 in 20,000 individuals. OI is a genetically and phenotypically heterogeneous disorder
resulting from mutations in at least 22 different genes.
More
than 80% of OI cases result from dominantly inherited mutations in
COL1A1
and
COL1A2,
which encode the
α
1(I) and
α
2(I)
chains of type I procollagen.
The products of the remaining
OI-associated genes are involved in type I procollagen biosynthesis, post-translational modifications, trafficking, cross-linking of
telopeptide residues, osteoblast differentiation and function, and
processes involved in bone mineralization. All forms of OI share
the features of low bone mass, propensity to fracture, and short
stature.
While there is extensive genetic heterogeneity, many clinicians prefer the clinical categorizations of patient phenotypes
into mild, moderate, severe, and very-severe/lethal groups. Since
there is enormous clinical variability in these types, the subtypes
will be discussed separately.
Mild Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Type I)
Individuals with OI type I account for the majority of OI cases
overall and demonstrate a relatively mild disease phenotype in
terms of clinical course, the extent of skeletal deformity, and the
radiologic appearance of the skeleton. Individuals usually have
shortened stature for age or compared with unaffected family members, although reductions in stature are less than that
observed in other forms of OI. In general, these patients experience an estimated 5 to 15 major fractures before puberty, as well
as several minor traumatic fractures of the digits or involving small
bones of the feet. Characteristically, the fracture rate falls dramatically after puberty only to increase during later life. Broadly speaking, prepubertal children average 1 fracture/year.
In OI type I,
long bone fractures usually heal without significant deformity,
infrequently requiring the insertion of intramedullary rods and
rarely resulting in fracture nonunion.
Although this form of OI is inherited in an autosomal dominant fashion, an individual may be the first affected in their family
due to incomplete penetrance. Physical examination findings in
type I OA include blue sclerae that can transition to gray to pale
blue in adulthood. Arcus senilis, unrelated to lipid abnormalities,
may occur in some patients. Other reported ocular defects include
scleromalacia, keratoconus, and retinal hemorrhage.
Teeth can
show dentinogenesis imperfecta (DI) due to the effects of mutations on tooth dentin.
Deciduous and permanent teeth (usually
less affected) can have an opalescent and translucent appearance,
which tends to darken with age. While tooth enamel is normal,
the dentin is dysplastic, leading to chipping of enamel, erosion,
and breakage. DI can be seen in all forms of OI. The prevalence of
DI increases with OI severity.
During the second and third decades of life, a characteristic high-frequency sensorineural or mixed hearing loss can be
detected.
The incidence of mitral valve prolapse is not increased
in these patients compared with the population at large, but

effects of mutations on both the skin and the cutaneous blood

Mildly affected patients may not have fractures at birth,
although clavicular fractures are occasionally detected post-delivery. Radiographically, affected newborns can have wormian bones
seen on lateral views of the skull, and osteopenia is often detected
through the skeleton, especially the spine (

on the physical activity levels, the need for immobilization after
lower extremity fractures, the attitude of the family toward independent activity, and genetic background. Mild scoliosis approximating 20 degrees is common.
Measurement of bone mineral density (BMD) by dual-energy

decrease associated with increased fracture risk.

by the World Health Organization (WHO). Z scores can be used
in children to control for age, race, and sex in the analysis of BMD.
Molecular Pathology.
OI type I results from mutations affecting the COL1A1(I) and COL1A2(I) polypeptide chains of type
I collagen. Cultured fibroblasts from individuals with mild OI
synthesize reduced quantities (approximately one-half) of the
expected concentration of type I collagen. Most of the reported
mutations in OI type I are nonsense and frameshift mutations
and thus are predicted to lead to premature termination codons,
although there are some exceptions.
Very Severe/Lethal Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI Type II)
Approximately 10% of OI patients have the very-severe/lethal
neonatal form of the disease. Most cases result from sporadic
mutations; however, recently recessively inherited forms of the disease have been documented.
Infants with OI type II present
with severe bone fragility, multiple intrauterine fractures at various
stages of healing, deformed extremities, and occasionally hydrops
fetalis. Radiographic features include wormian bones, multivertebral fractures, concertina-like compression fractures of the long
bones and beading of the ribs due to healing callus formation.

Most cases occur
de novo,
as new dominant mutations; however, autosomal recessive forms have been
established, as has recurrence based on germline mosaicism.
The

Progressively Deforming Osteogenesis Imperfecta
(Type III)
The deforming variant represents the classic form of OI. Similar

kyphoscoliosis, thorax deformity, and a significantly short stature. The extent of growth retardation is remarkable, and in many
adults the height may not surpass 3 feet (90 to 100 cm). Abnormal cranial molding occurs in utero and during infancy, producing frontal bossing and a characteristic triangular shaped facies.
Radiographically, wormian bones and delayed closure of the fontanelles may be observed well into the first decade.
Pulmonary function can be diminished because of distortion of the spine and thorax and can progress over time and lead
to restrictive lung disease and sleep apnea. In the neonatal and
infant periods of life, in addition to the skeletal deformities, there
is increased chest wall compliance that may require respiratory
support. Over time, and with treatment, the increased chest wall
compliance may resolve. Unfortunately because of diminished
vital capacity, pulmonary insufficiency is a leading cause of death
in subjects with OI type III. Many patients with scoliosis greater
than 60 degrees develop respiratory compromise and require pulmonary support, including supplemental oxygen.

external ear canals to slant upward as the base of the skull settles on
the cervical vertebrae. This distortion may lead to communicating

and lower motor neuron lesions. Headache, diplopia, nystagmus,
cranial nerve neuralgia, decline in motor function, urinary dysfunction, and respiratory compromise are complications of basilar

III patients have white sclera as adults. Approximately 75% of
patients with type III OI have DI, necessitating constant dental
care throughout childhood. Severe hearing impairment occurs in
20% of patients, although milder degrees of hearing loss are more
common.
The skeleton in these patients has significant osteopenia, leading to multiple fractures in the upper and lower extremities and
vertebral bodies, particularly prior to puberty. Unlike type I OI,
in which fractures tend to heal without complication, fractures in
type III OI frequently lead to skeletal deformity. In addition to
marked osteopenia, radiographs demonstrate thinning of cortical
bone, narrowing of the diaphysis, and widening of the metaphysis, which can merge into a dysplastic epiphyseal zone filled with
whorls of partially calcified cartilage (i.e., popcorn deformity).
Osteoporosis leads to collapse of vertebral end plates, contributing
to worsening kyphoscoliosis. Pectus excavatum or pectus carinatum adds to thoracic deformity.
In addition, lack of weightbearing increases the severity of osteoporosis and increases the risk
of fracture. Many individuals with type III OI become wheelchair
bound at an early age or walk with mechanical assistance.
Molecular Pathology.
The molecular basis of OI type III is
very similar to OI type II. Most of the cases result from heterozygosity for mutations in
COL1A1
and
COL1A2.
These mutations
are glycine substitutions scattered through the triple helix, as well
as in-frame deletions. As in OI type II, familial recurrences result
from recessively inherited mutations.
Moderate Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Type IV)
Clinically, the phenotype of patients with moderately severe OI
(OI type IV) falls between the milder OI type I and the more
severe OI type III, and this diagnosis is somewhat subjective. In
most cases, this type is usually inherited in an autosomal dominant

fashion. Fractures can be seen at birth, although some patients
may not experience an initial fracture until beginning to bear
weight or ambulate. The extent of skeletal deformity involving the
spine, thorax, and extremities is usually intermediate between that
of types I and III OI, and these patients also have short stature and
frequently have scoliosis. Patients may have mild facial dysmorphisms, although typically not as severe as OI type III. Hearing
loss occurs but is less frequent than in OI types I and III.
Most fractures occur during childhood and may recur during
the postmenopausal period in women or in men older than age
50 years.
Long-bone deformities tend to develop after fractures,
which may lead to a difficulties with ambulation. Radiographs of
the long bones and vertebral bodies demonstrate osteopenia with
vertebral collapse. Although there is marked cortical thinning,
bowing, and coarsening of trabeculae, the overall architecture of
the bone is fairly typical.
Molecular Pathology.
Molecular mechanisms underpinning
OI type IV are similar to other forms of OI. Causal mutations are
found in
COL1A1
and
COL1A2
and include glycine substitutions
and in-frame deletions. At the present time, there is no evidence
for an autosomal recessive form of OI type IV.

Osteogenesis Imperfecta Type V
This phenotype was first reported in 2000 as a variant within the
heterogeneous group classified under OI type IV.
In the initial
report of seven OI patients, the phenotype was distinguished by
the following criteria: moderate fracture history, hyperplastic callus formation, limitation in forearm pronation and supination
due to progressive intramembraneous bone formation at the joint,
normal sclera, and no DI. Radiographs can demonstrate a dense
subphyseal sclerotic band, which is specific for this form of OI.
Bone biopsy showed a meshlike appearance of irregularly spaced
lamellae, different from the woven bone seen in OI types II, III,
and IV.
Molecular Pathology.
The etiology of this rare form has been
established and results from heterozygosity for mutations in interferon-induced transmembrane protein 5
(IFITM5)
.
Histopathology of Bone in Osteogenesis Imperfecta
The range of histologic appearances of bone in the different OI
phenotypes is as variable as the clinical phenotypes. Undermineralization and overmineralization of bone have been recognized
within the same specimen.
Bone histomorphology appears relatively normal in OI type I, but osteopenia due to thin lamellar
plates and diminished cortical width is evident. Immature woven
bone and lamellar disarray are characteristic of more severe OI
phenotypes.
Treatment
Over the years, there have been multiple attempts to treat OI
with a variety of vitamins, hormones, and other agents, none of
which has been successful. The list includes administration of
mineral supplements, fluoride, androgenic steroids, ascorbic acid,
and vitamin D. Since the mid-1990s, bisphosphonates administered parenterally to children has demonstrated favorable results.
Intravenously administered pamidronate increases bone mass,
decreases skeletal pain and decreases fracture incidence in children
with severe OI.
Dosage regimens in different series for children
and adults have varied from 1 to 3 mg/kg, administered intravenously at 2- to 4-month intervals, and lower-dose regimens
also have been reported.
Beneficial treatment effects are most
marked in the spine, where vertebral remodeling may improve
vertebral height. Metabolic studies by have demonstrated transient decreases in serum ionized calcium and increases in serum
parathyroid hormone (PTH) with treatment. Major side effects
of intravenous bisphosphonate include an acute-phase response
occurring most often during the first treatment cycle. In addition to the use of a bisphosphonate, current recommendations
also include adequate calcium and vitamin D supplementation to
maintain serum vitamin D levels within normal limits. Recently
the use of anabolic treatment to build bone has been tested using
teriparatide in adult patients in OI, and the study showed that
patients with mild OI, but not severe OI, benefitted from treatment.
Multiple other types of therapies are being developed or
studied in OI and include antibodies targeting receptor activator
of nuclear factor kappa-B ligand (RANKL), sclerostin, and transforming growth factor (TGF)
β
.
The use of surgery to correct deformities and to facilitate
weight-bearing has been the subject of several reviews.
Multiple
osteotomies and realignment of deformed bone over intramedullary rods is an option for many children with severe bowing.
Indications include frequent fractures at the apex of the bow,
impaired standing, or limb-length inequality due to bowing.
Expanding (telescoping) rods are best for growing children, for
they require fewer revisions. Spinal deformities are common and
usually progressive. Surgical stabilization is most advisable in the
teen years or early adulthood when patients are best able to tolerate
these complex reconstructions.
Early basilar invagination may
be halted with prophylactic posterior fusion of the occipital–cervical junction with plate fixation.
Patients with severe brainstem
compression may require anterior transoral decompression, as well
as posterior instrumented fusion. Patients with various types of OI
appear to be at increased risk of premature osteoarthritis, although
the reasons for this are not clear. Total joint arthroplasty is usually
successful in these patients; thus referral is appropriate.
Every child with OI benefits from appropriate rehabilitative
therapy.
Muscle strengthening exercises are essential as primary
care and after immobilization for fracture. Perhaps the most beneficial programs have been developed around swimming, preferably in
heated pools and as part of continuous rehabilitative medical care.

Classic Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome
Classic EDS (cEDS) is inherited in an autosomal dominant manner. Cardinal features of cEDS are skin hyperextensibility, atrophic scarring, and generalized joint hypermobility. The skin tends
to be soft and doughy to the touch, with increased fragility, manifesting as splitting of the dermis with relatively minor trauma.
Wound healing is often poor. Recurrent joint dislocations occur
frequently and usually resolve spontaneously or are easily managed by the affected individuals. However, over time, recurrent
joint dislocations, periodic joint effusion related to trauma, and
the eventual appearance of osteoarthritis pose significant management problems. Synovial thickening has been observed in EDS
accompanied by the accumulation of small masses of crystalline
material in synovial villi. It has been observed that EDS patients
constitute up to 5% of all patients in a pediatric arthritis clinic
In children, other features include hypotonia with
population.
delayed motor milestones, easy fatigability, and easy bruising.
Mitral valve prolapse is reported infrequently and is not usually
clinically significant. Aortic root dilation has been reported and is
rarely progressive.
In EDS, the skin has a characteristic soft or “velvety” feel that
can be appreciated by stroking the forearms. Thin, atrophic corrugated, and hyperpigmented scars are often found on the forehead,
under the chin, and on the lower extremities (known as cigarette
paper or papyraceous scars), although this is not a uniform finding. Typically, skin lesions heal slowly after injury or surgery. Molluscoid pseudotumors (violaceous subcutaneous tumors ranging
in size from 0.5 to 3 cm) may be palpated in tissue over pressure
points on the forearms and lower extremities and may be seen on
radiographs. Although many patients claim to bruise easily, ecchymoses distributed on the extremities are found only in patients
with more severe forms of the disorder. Severe bilateral varicose
veins are a common problem. Associated pulmonary complications of EDS include spontaneous pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, and subpleural blebs.

Hypermobile Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome
Joint laxity with instability associated with subluxations and
dislocations are common in Hypermobile Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome (EDSHMB) and may occur spontaneously or with minimal impact, sometimes requiring surgical repair. The shoulders,
patellae, and temporomandibular joints are frequent sites of dislocation. There is an increased risk for degenerative joint disease,
and chronic pain is a major concern, sometimes disabling from a
physical and psychological standpoint. The musculoskeletal pain
may mimic conditions like fibromyalgia, amplified pain syndrome
(AMPS) and chronic regional pain syndrome (CRPS). Affected
individuals typically have normal skin without scarring, but skin
may be soft and mildly hypertextensible with easy bruising. Multisystem complaints include headaches, functional bowel disorders,
psychiatric diagnoses, and autonomic dysfunction manifesting as
postural dizziness or postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome
(POTS). Mild aortic dilation may be seen but is typically mild,
with low risk for dissection.
The diagnosis of hypermobile EDS is based on clinical findings and family history with no known genetic or biochemical
testing available to help confirm or rule it out. Diagnostic criteria
were revised by the International EDS Consortium in 2017; the
presence of three criteria is required: generalized joint hypermobility; evidence of syndrome features, musculoskeletal complication,
and/or family history; and exclusion of alternative diagnoses.
In
light of decreasing joint range of motion with age, the Beighton
scale described previously often leads to overdiagnosis in children
and underdiagnosis in older adults.
Therapeutic interventions in hypermobile EDS, including the
use of pain medications, muscle strengthening, individualized
physical therapy, assistive devices, and other measures of joint
protection, are mainstays of improving joint stability and alleviating pain. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation in addition
to low-impact weight-bearing exercise helps to maximize bone
density, which is often decreased. Regular dual x-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) is recommended for individuals with or at risk for
bone loss. Many of these patients benefit from multidisciplinary
care that includes relevant expertise in clinical genetics, cardiology,

rheumatology, orthopedic surgery, gastroenterology, neurology,
and gynecology specialists.
Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome, Vascular Type
Formerly referred to as
EDS type IV
, vascular EDS (vEDS) is
inherited in an autosomal dominant manner and is considered
to be one of the most severe types of EDS, because of its high
mortality rate. Characteristic features include arterial, intestinal,
and/or uterine fragility; thin, translucent skin; and bruising.
Arterial rupture may be preceded by aneurysm, arteriovenous
fistulae, or dissection; however, it may occur spontaneously.
Common sites involved are the iliac, splenic, and renal arteries
or the aorta, resulting in either massive hematomas or death.
Patients are also susceptible to rupture of internal viscera and
may experience repeated rupture of diverticula on the antimesenteric border of the large bowel. Problems with pregnancy vary
from preterm delivery to uterine or vascular rupture, although
delivery is uneventful in many instances.
Typical causes of
death in EDS families have included gastrointestinal rupture,
peripartum uterine rupture, rupture of the hepatic artery, and
vascular ruptures.
In contrast to the other forms of EDS, vEDS is not associated
with hyperextensiblity of large joints, although small joints may
be minimally hypermobile. These patients have thin, soft, transparent skin, through which a prominent venous pattern is seen,
especially on their chest walls. In contrast to classic EDS, the skin
is not “doughy” in vEDS, although excessive bruising may occur.
There is an increased risk for spontaneous hemopneumothorax
in vEDS associated with hemoptysis and mitral valve prolapse.
Surgical repair of ruptured vessels or internal viscera is extremely
difficult because of friable tissues. Anesthetic and surgical difficulties related to intubation, spontaneous arterial bleeding during surgery, and the ligation of vessels that tear under pressure
complicate surgical maneuvers. Similarly, arteriography may be
dangerous in these subjects. Imaging studies may be reveal normal
appearing aorta or other large vessels that rupture shortly after a
“normal study.”
The diagnosis of vEDS is based on identification of a heterozygous pathogenic variant in
COL3A1.
Clinical diagnostic
criteria are a useful guide and include both major and minor
criteria.
Major diagnostic criteria include arterial aneurysm,
dissection, or rupture; intestinal rupture; uterine rupture during pregnancy; and family history of vEDS. Minor diagnostic
criteria include thin, translucent skin (especially noticeable on
the chest/abdomen), characteristic facial appearance (thin vermilion of the lips, micrognathia, narrow nose, prominent eyes),
acrogeria, carotid–cavernous sinus arteriovenous fistula, hypermobility of small joints, tendon/muscle rupture, early-onset
varicose veins, pneumothorax/hemopneumothorax, easy bruising (spontaneous or with minimal trauma), chronic joint subluxations/dislocations, congenital dislocation of the hips, and
talipes equinovarus (clubfoot).
It is important to have a high index of suspicion for complications in vEDS, and it is recommended that affected individuals
seek medical attention for any sudden, unexplained pain. Patients
should carry documentation of their diagnosis such as a Medic
Alert device, emergency letter, and/or vEDS passport. Vascular
surveillance may include ultrasound, magnetic resonance angiogram (MRA) or computed tomography angiogram (CTA) with
and without venous contrast. Regular blood pressure monitoring
should be undertaken to facilitate the early detection and treatment of hypertension. There is ongoing research investigating
medications that may decrease arterial complications and improve
life expectancy.
Arthrochalasia Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome (EDSARTH1 and
EDSARTH2)
Formerly known as
EDS VIIA
and
VIIB
, arthrochalasia Ehlers–
Danlos Syndrome (EDSARTH1 and EDSARTH2) represent
autosomal dominant forms of EDS resulting from mutations in
the
COL1A1
and
COL1A2
genes that cause faulty processing
of type I collagen at the N terminus.
This type of EDS is
characterized by pronounced and generalized joint hypermobility,
skin hyperelasticity, moderate bruising, short stature, and subtle
dysmorphic features including round facies with midface hypoplasia. The skin feels doughy and is usually fragile. Kyphoscoliosis
and muscle hypotonia are frequently present. Affected individuals experience multiple dislocations, particularly involving large
joints, including the hips, knees, and ankles, often presenting in
the newborn period. Patients frequently require orthopedic surgery for joint dislocation with increased risk for complications due
to tissue friability.
EDSARTH1 and EDSARTH2, collectively termed the
arthrochalasia type
, result from mutations involving the N-terminal propeptide cleavage site of type I collagen. The arthrochalasia type
of EDS has provided insight into the process of normal type I
collagen fiber formation. The initial observation was of an accumulation of unprocessed procollagen within the dermis of affected
individuals.
With subsequent recognition that procollagen had
both N- and C-terminal extension propeptides and that separate
enzymes were responsible for their removal, the syndrome became
more sharply defined as an accumulation of procollagen with the
N-terminal peptides still attached (pN collagen).
Of the two
distinctly different genetic abnormalities resulting in procollagen
accumulation, the more frequent form is the mutational resistance
of a procollagen cleavage site to the action of the N-terminal procollagen peptidase. The resistance results from an amino acid substitution or deletion in the proCOL1A1 or pro2COL2A1chain
(type VIIA and VIIB, respectively), leading to a portion of the collagen chains containing an abnormal N-terminal extension. This
results from mutations in
COL1A1
or
COL1A2
in exon 6 of the
molecule that alter the proteinase cleavage site. Those individuals
with mutations in exon 6 of
COL1A1
are more severely affected
than those with similar mutations in
COL1A2.
Dermatosparaxis Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome
Dermatosparaxis EDS (EDSDERMS), formerly known as
EDS
VIIC
, unlike the aforementioned types, is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner. In this type, the skin is extremely fragile,
soft, and doughy with easy bruising. Other features include blue
sclerae, marked joint hypermobility, micrognathia, large umbilical
hernia, epiphsyeal delay, and mild hirsutism.
This type of EDS
results from a deficiency of the procollagen
N
-propeptidase, in
contrast to the arthrochalastic form, which involves the enzyme
cleavage site; individuals have been identified who are homozygous for mutations in the gene. Interestingly, this defect is homologous to the defect in sheep and cattle with dermatosparaxis.
Kyphoscoliotic Ehlers–Danlos Syndrome
Kyphoscoliotic EDS (EDSKSCL1 and EDSKSCL2) is inherited
in an autosomal recessive manner. The findings in this disorder
include severe kyphoscoliosis noted at birth, recurrent joint dislocations, hyperextensible skin and joints, poor tone, and reduced
muscle mass.
The skin is grossly abnormal and has been

described as pale, translucent, and velvety. Upon trauma, the skin
shows gaping wounds that heal poorly. Kyphoscoliotic EDS is
characterized by significant ocular involvement with microcornea,
retinal detachment, and glaucoma, leading to blindness in some
individuals. Severe kyphoscoliosis may lead to respiratory and cardiac compromise and ultimately cardiorespiratory failure.
Kyphoscoliotic EDS results from lysyl hydroxylase deficiency.
A variety of mutations within the lysyl hydroxylase gene
have been defined and include premature stop codons, amino acid
substitutions, and internal deletions with compound heterozygosity reported.
Defective lysyl hydroxylase impairs the conversion
of lysyl residues to hydroxylysine on procollagen peptides. The
consequence of deficient hydroxylysine content of collagen is the
effect it has on cross-linking, which helps stabilize the mature collagen molecule.

### Marfan Syndrome

Marfan syndrome (MFS) is one of the most common hereditary
diseases of connective tissue (
Table 107.3
). It is inherited in an
autosomal dominant manner with an estimated prevalence of 1 in
3000 to 5000 individuals.
There is a wide spectrum of clinical
presentations from the severe infantile form to individuals who
are only mildly affected. Although the most impressive findings
are related to musculoskeletal, cardiac, and ocular manifestations,
affected individuals may also have pulmonary, neurologic, and
psychologic complications. MFS has become one of the genetic
disorders for which there has been advocacy for treatment to slow
disease progression and improve life expectancy. It is important
for health care providers to recognize the condition early to prevent presentation with life-threatening emergencies.
Clinical Features
MFS can be difficult to diagnose, with both under- and overdiagnosis being an issue, especially when based solely on outward
appearance. Stringent criteria for this diagnosis were proposed in
1996 and revised in 2010.
In the absence of a known family
history of MFS, the diagnosis is made for an individual with a
pathogenic variant in the
fibrillin-1 (FBN1)
gene and either of the
following: aortic root enlargement (

≥
2.0) or ectopic lentis
and a systemic score

7.

Major morbidity and early mortality in MFS are
related to cardiovascular complications including aortic dilation
at the sinus of Valsalva with an increased risk for aortic tear and
rupture, mitral and tricuspid valve prolapse, and pulmonary artery
dilation.
Aortic root dilation is found in approximately 50%
of children with MFS and is progressive; it is also noted in >80%

extends the entire length of the aorta. Severe and prolonged valvular regurgitation can lead to ventricular dysfunction and possible
heart failure. Of all patients with MFS, 60% to 70% have mitral
valve prolapse with regurgitation. Pregnant women are at particular risk for aortic dissection, particularly those who already have

Molecular Biology of Marfan Syndrome
Fibrillin-1 (FBN-1) protein is an important component of both
elastic and nonelastic connective tissues throughout the body.
It is the main protein of a group of connective tissue microfibrils
that are essential for normal elastic fibrillogenesis. In nonelastic
tissues, the Fibrillin-1–containing microfibril functions as an
anchoring fiber.
FBN-1
mutations are associated with a wide range of phenotypes including acromicric dysplasia, ectopia lentis, geleophysic
dysplasia type 2, MASS syndrome, stiff skin, and Weill–Marchesani syndrome type 2.
The majority of mutations in MFS
and related syndromes are private mutations (occur genetically
independent with no “hot spot” in the molecule). Exceptions
include early-onset or rapidly progressive MFS, previously termed
neonatal Marfan syndrome
), with mutations clustering between
exons 24 and 26 and exon 32,
as well as acromicric and geleophysic dysplasias, which result from mutations in exons 41 and
42.
Heterozgyosity for missense, frameshifts, deletions and
insertions, splice site alterations, and nonsense mutations have all
been reported.
Treatment
In 1972, the life span of untreated patients with classic MFS was
approximately 32 years. The early mortality in the MFS results
primarily from complications associated with aortic dilation.
This symmetric dilatation of the sinuses of Valsalva is progressive throughout life and is often detectable in infancy. In the early
1970s, it was suggested that surveillance and treatment could
lessen the complications of aortic dissection in MFS. There have
been many treatment trials with beta blockers. When compared
against the control group, the treated individuals demonstated a
significantly slower rate of dilation of the aortic root and improved
survival, with fewer treated patients reaching a clinical end point
(death, congestive heart failure, aortic regurgitation, aortic dissection, or cardiovascular surgery).
Data generated from mouse models of MFS suggest that excessive signaling by the TGF
β
family of cytokines plays a central
pathogenic role.
A large randomized controlled trial suggested
that losartan (an antihypertensive and angiotensin II receptor
blocker) attenuates TGF
β
signaling and may have advantages
over beta blockers. However, a randomized controlled study in
MFS comparing atenolol to losartan failed to show any difference in efficacy between the two agents.
Animal and human
data suggest that calcium channel blockers may negatively affect
aortic complications. Affected mice treated with calcium channel
blockers showed expansion of aneurysms and increased rupture
risk with increased risk also noted in patients with MFS.
Regular echocardiography is the mainstay of surveillance for
the aortic root complications of MFS. Elective repair of aortic root
disease before enlargement to 4.5 cm is typically suggested and
is preferable to emergency repair required for marked dilation or
dissection.
Mitral valve replacement is frequently accomplished
during the same procedure. Most importantly, repeated trials have
shown that patients who undergo elective repair, versus emergent
repair, have a substantially better prognosis. Retrospective studies
of patients with MFS undergoing thoracoabdominal CT or MRI
as part of their clinical care showed that one-third had findings of
peripheral vascular aneurysms, with approximately half requiring
interventions.
Correction of scoliosis may be attempted with bracing; however,
surgical repair, including fixation with rods, should be considered
when the curvature exceeds 40 degrees. Arthropathy associated
with excessive joint mobility may require orthopedic intervention.
Dislocated lenses should not be removed surgically unless more
conventional means of correcting vision are ineffective.

Differential Diagnosis
Homocystinuria.
Homocystinuria is an autosomal recessive
inborn error of metabolism that shares several skeletal and ocular
features with MFS and is therefore part of diagnostic consideration.
Characteristic features include a marfanoid habitus with
joint laxity, scoliosis, lens dislocation, early-onset osteoporosis,
mild intellectual disability, vascular thrombosis affecting arteries and veins due to increased clotting activity, and the cytotoxic
effect of homocysteine on vascular endothelial cells.
Cystathionine
β
-synthase (CBS) deficiency is the most common cause of homocystinuria
and leads to elevated levels of
homocystine and methionine levels, whereas cystathionine and
cysteine levels in blood are decreased. Importantly, this disorder is
differentiated from MFS primarily because the direction of ectopia lentis is different, usually laterally or inferiorly, and progressive
aortic root dilation is not seen.
Loeys–Dietz Syndrome.
In 2005, individuals with a previously
undescribed autosomal dominant aortic aneurysm syndrome were
reported.
This disorder, now referred to as Loeys–Dietz syndrome (LDS), is characterized by generalized arterial tortuosity
with ascending aortic aneurysm and dissection, hypertelorism,
bifid uvula, and/or cleft palate. Other findings include craniosynostosis, pectus deformity, joint laxity, talipes equinovarus, cervical spine instability, easy bruising, translucent skin, and abnormal
wound healing. There is an increased risk for widespread and
aggressive aneurysms, as well as uterine rupture and death during pregnancy. Of note, affected individuals have also been found
to have a predisposition to allergic and inflammatory conditions
such as food allergies and eosinophilic esophagitis. The phenotype
has been considered to overlap with MFS and vEDS and similarly,
there is marked inter- and intrafamilial variability.
Heterozygosity for mutations in
TGFBR1, TGFBR2,
TGFB2,TGFB3, SMAD2,
and
SMAD3
have been identified
(
Table 107.1
).
From a management perspective, timely
diagnosis is important because individuals with LDS are managed more aggressively than in MFS.
Unlike with MFS, which
is associated with slowly progressive arterial disease, in LDS the
aneurysms tend to be particularly aggressive and rupture at an earlier stage and size.
Diagnostic differentiation between LDS and

MFS is therefore very important. All individuals with LDS require
frequent echocardiography to monitor the ascending aorta. MRA
or CTA are also often deployed for surveillance in affected individuals, particularly if there are concerns of vascular involvement
beyond the ascending aorta. Aortic aneurysms are corrected at
smaller sizes (4 cm) than in MFS, and complaints such as abdominal pain and headache should be thoroughly investigated given
the high risk for widespread vascular complications. The peripartum period can be dangerous for women with LDS and should be
accompanied by an increased frequency of aortic imaging.
Congenital Contractural Arachnodactyly.
Congenital contractural arachnodactyly (CCA) is inherited in an autosomal dominant manner and characteristic features include arachnodactyly, a
marfanoid habitus, and multiple flexion contractures.
There is
a characteristic “crumpled ear” deformity due to a flattened helix
with partial obliteration of the concha. Marked deformity of the
chest cage also occurs, and kyphoscoliosis is often progressive and
severe. For unknown reasons, the contractures tend to become less
severe with age. Radiographically, osteopenia can be seen. Aortopathies are less often seen.
This disorder results from heterozygosity for mutations in
Fibrillin-2 (FBN2)
.

## Conclusion

The hereditary disorders of connective tissue represent a heterogenous group of conditions characterized by abnormalities in skeletal
tissues, which include cartilage, bone, tendon, ligament, muscle,
and skin. Clinical phenotypes vary across a broad clinical spectrum
that ranges from extremely short stature to excessively tall individuals, and the types of altered genes span the genome and involve a
diverse number of biologic pathways. Affected individuals usually
require close medical surveillance and treatment across their lifespan. Greater understanding and appreciation of disease pathogenesis and the unique constellation of medical issues posed by each
of these disorders will ultimately lead to improvements in quality
of life and survival for those with heritable disorders of connective
tissue.
