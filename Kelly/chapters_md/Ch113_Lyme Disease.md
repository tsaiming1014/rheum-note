# Lyme Disease

VAIDEHI R. CHOWDHARY AND LINDA K. BOCKENSTEDT

KEY POINTS

> Lyme disease is a spirochetal infection transmitted by deer ticks.

> It presents most often as an expanding macular skin lesion at the bite

> site and less commonly as disseminated infection affecting the

> nervous system, heart, and/or joints.

> Myalgias and arthralgias are common at all stages of infection, but true

> arthritis is a sign of late disseminated disease.

> Lyme disease should be suspected when a person who has been

> exposed to ticks in an endemic area is seen with signs and

> symptoms of

> Borrelia burgdorferi

> infection.

> Lyme serologic tests may be negative with early infection but are

> positive in most patients with infection longer than a 1-month

> duration.

> Patients on B cell–depleting therapies are more likely to have

> disseminated infection and may be seronegative.

> Most patients, including those who are immunosuppressed, can be

> treated effectively with 2 to 4 weeks of antibiotic therapy, although

> symptoms may resolve slowly.

> Persistent arthritis in an involved joint after antibiotic treatment for

> Lyme disease occurs in less than 10% of patients and responds to

> disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs.

> A post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome occurs in a minority of

> patients and continued antibiotics provide no benefit over placebo.

## Introduction

Lyme disease is a complex multisystem disorder caused by the
tick-borne spirochete
Borrelia burgdorferi.
The disease first came
to medical attention in the United States in the late 1970s with
the investigation of a clustering of cases of juvenile arthritis in
the region of Lyme, Connecticut.
A characteristic skin rash,
described as single or multiple expanding red macules, often heralded the onset of arthritis. This rash, termed
erythema migrans
(EM), had been linked in Europe to the bite of
Ixodes
ticks and
the subsequent development of neurologic abnormalities.
Further investigation revealed that arthritis was one manifestation
of a systemic disorder affecting the skin, heart, joints, and nervous system. In 1982 Burgdorfer isolated the causative agent,
the spirochete
B. burgdorferi
, from
Ixodes
ticks.
Demonstration
that antibodies to this organism developed in patients with Lyme
disease and its eventual culture from skin and cerebrospinal fluid
(CSF) confirmed the infectious etiology of the disorder.
It is
now the most common vector-borne disease in the United States
and Europe.

## Ecology and Epidemiology of Lyme Disease

Lyme disease has a worldwide distribution, with most cases
reported in North America, Europe, and Asia.
On each of these
continents, hard-shelled ticks of the
Ixodes
family are the only
known vectors for the disease. Other arthropods and bloodsucking insects such as mosquitos cannot transmit the infection,
and person-to-person transmission has not been documented.
The incidence of Lyme disease varies geographically and is determined by the prevalence of
B. burgdorferi
–infected ticks. In the
United States cases of Lyme disease have been reported in all 50
states and the District of Columbia, but most are clustered in the
Northeast and mid-Atlantic region from Virginia to Maine and
westward through Pennsylvania, the upper Midwest (Wisconsin
and Minnesota), and foci in northern California, Oregon, and
Washington state. Cases of Lyme disease outside these regions are
mostly acquired after travel to highly endemic areas. More than
90% of U.S. cases reported to the Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention (CDC) are from Connecticut, Delaware, Maine,
Maryland, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York,
Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, West Virginia,
and Wisconsin. The number of reported cases underestimates the
actual case incidence, which is estimated to be about 476,000
annually.
The spirochetes associated with Lyme disease reside within the
genus
B. burgdorferi sensu lato
(sl), and the vast majority of cases
are caused by
B. burgdorferi sensu stricto
(ss),
Borrelia garinii
, and
Borrelia afzelii
.
All three genospecies can be found in Europe,
whereas
B. burgdorferi
ss and rarely
B. mayonii
, a recently emerging species found only in the upper Midwest, are the only species
causing Lyme disease in North America.
Variation among the
genospecies may account for the differences in clinical expression
of Lyme disease between the two continents:
B. garinii
and
B.
afzelii
are more commonly associated with neurologic disease and
late skin involvement, respectively, whereas
B. burgdorferi
ss is
associated with arthritis.
B. mayonii
species gives rise to atypical
disseminated EM lesions and high levels of bacteremia.
Because
of the prominence of musculoskeletal manifestations with
B.
burgdorferi
ss infection, this chapter focuses mainly on Lyme disease in North America.

## Ticks and Lyme Disease

Lyme disease is found primarily in temperate climates where an
enzootic cycle maintaining
B. burgdorferi
in
Ixodes
ticks is established.
Ixodes
ticks have a 2-year life span in which they pass

through three developmental stages—larva, nymph, and adult—
feeding only once per stage.
B. burgdorferi
is not passed transovarially and is maintained by passage between reservoir hosts and
ticks. Small rodents are the main reservoirs for
B. burgdorferi
ss
and
B. afzelii,
whereas birds are the principal haven for
B. garinii
.
Larvae acquire
B. burgdorferi
after feeding on an infected mammal in late summer and then molt to nymphs, which lay dormant
until the following late spring and summer. The peak incidence
of Lyme disease is in the summer months, when humans come
in contact with questing nymphs.
Engorged nymphs molt into
adult ticks, which feed mainly on deer but are capable of transmitting infection to humans.
B. burgdorferi
does not persist in deer,
which serve to maintain and propagate the tick population.

## Pathogenesis

### Borrelia burgdorferi Invasion of the Mammalian Host

The onset of tick feeding signals
B. burgdorferi
residing in the tick
midgut to undergo global changes in gene expression, multiply,
and migrate to the salivary glands, where they pass with saliva into
the blood meal host.
Relatively few spirochetes—estimated in
the hundreds—are deposited at the bite site, a process that takes at
least 24 hours.
From the skin, spirochetes can disseminate via
the blood and lymphatics to establish foci of infection elsewhere
in the body. Spirochete virulence, growth conditions that allow
persistence at a particular site, and host factors that modulate the
inflammatory response determine disease expression. Clinical disease mainly involves the skin, heart, nervous system, and joints.
B. burgdorferi
has no known virulence factors common to
other bacterial pathogens.
Its genome encodes an abundant
array of putative lipoproteins, some of which play critical roles in
spirochete colonization of its different hosts. Outer surface protein (Osp) A is a midgut adhesin required for spirochete infection of ticks.
Osp C is essential for initial infection of the
mammal but is dispensable after spirochetes have disseminated
and colonized other tissues.
To do so,
B. burgdorferi
harnesses
host plasmin to move through tissues
and expresses adhesins,
including decorin-binding proteins A and B, BBK32, and p66,
which allow it to bind to extra-cellular matrix (ECM) proteins
and integrins on cells.
B. burgdorferi
expresses a protease with
aggrecanase activity and induces the host expression of matrix
metalloproteinases, which degrade ECMs.
Expression of VlsE,
an Osp that undergoes antigenic variation, is required for infection to persist.

### Pathology of Lyme Disease

With the exception of
B. mayonii
, which can be associated with
visible bacteremia on blood smears,
few spirochetes can be
seen in infected tissues of patients with Lyme disease. Because
B. burgdorferi
secretes no known toxins, the host inflammatory
response is believed to underlie the pathology seen in Lyme disease.
Innate immune cells responding to the tick bite site orchestrate the inflammatory response and can kill spirochetes. By the
time patients present with symptoms of Lyme disease, however,
adaptive immune cells dominate tissue pathology.
Histopathologic studies of EM lesions, cardiac tissue, synovial biopsy
specimens, and limited nervous system tissue (meninges, spinal
cord, and nerve roots) reveal varying degrees of monocytic and
lymphoplasmacytic infiltrates, especially perivascular, that include
macrophages, T cells, and B cells.
The proportions of responding
immune cells differ among EM, CSF, and synovial fluid samples,
suggesting a tissue-specific immune response to
B. burgdorferi
infection. Although neutrophils are apparent in EM lesions of less
than 24 hours’ duration, these cells diminish in number as the
lesion matures, giving way to lymphoplasmacytic infiltrates associated with macrophages and dendritic cells.
A lymphocytic
pleocytosis is seen in CSF of patients with central nervous system (CNS) involvement, whereas synovial fluid exhibits elevated
leukocyte counts with a predominance of neutrophils.
Lyme
arthritis synovium resembles that of rheumatoid arthritis (RA),
with chronic inflammation mediated by mononuclear cell infiltration and pseudolymphoid follicles formed by T cells, B cells, and
plasma cells. In the synovium and, less commonly, the epineural
area, perivascular infiltrates can be associated with endarteritis
obliterans.

### Immune Response to Borrelia burgdorferi

Host cell recognition of
B. burgdorferi
occurs mainly through
engagement of the Toll-like receptor (TLR) family of pattern
recognition receptors, especially TLR2/TLR1 heterodimers (lipoproteins), TLR5 (flagellin), TLR7 and TLR8 (RNA), and TLR9
(spirochete DNA).
Activation of TLRs on innate immune cells
results in the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines, including interleukin (IL)-1
β
, TNF, IL-2, IL-6, and type I and type II
interferons, that recruit inflammatory cells to the site of infection.
Both TLR-dependent and non-TLR-dependent pathways induce matrix metalloproteinases that promote tissue injury
and spirochete dissemination in murine models.
Other pattern recognition receptors may be engaged by
B. burgdorferi
after
its ingestion by phagocytes, including the intra-cellular NOD2
receptors, which respond to peptidoglycan and potentiate the
inflammatory response in vitro and ex vivo.
Humoral immunity is a key host defense against
B. burgdorferi
infection.
B. burgdorferi
lipoproteins are B cell mitogens, and
antibodies that arise in the absence of T cell help are sufficient
to resolve inflammation and prevent challenge infection in the
mouse model of Lyme disease.
With the induction of adaptive
immunity,
B. burgdorferi
-specific immunoglobulin (Ig)G helps
clear spirochetes from infected tissues.
In humans, IgG1 and
IgG3 subclasses are dominant.
Recent single-cell immunophenotyping of the B cell response in EM lesions revealed infiltrating
memory B cells and plasma cells, with features suggesting both
local antigen-presenting cell function and local antibody production.
Some of these cells had clonal relatives in the circulation.
IgG-containing immune complexes are concentrated in the joints
of patients in whom Lyme arthritis develops, and it is likely that B
cells and plasma cells in the synovium also contribute to local antibody production.
The B cell–recruiting chemokine CXCL13
can be found in the CSF of patients with neuroborreliosis
; B
cells constitute approximately 15% of the nucleated cells, an order
of magnitude higher than in other infectious and inflammatory
neurologic diseases.
Intrathecal production of
B. burgdorferi
–
specific antibodies is found and some of these antibodies can also
bind neural antigens.
B. burgdorferi
infection primes CD4
and CD8
T cells, and
the predominance of interferon (IFN)-
γ
producing T helper (Th)
1 response correlates with more severe arthritis and neuroborreliosis.
Th17 cells are also involved. In the CSF, T cells represent more than 60% of the responding cells, with CD4
Th1
cells a majority; approximately half of patients with neurologic

involvement have elevated levels of IL-17 as well.
B. burgdorferi
neutrophil-activating protein A is abundant in synovial
fluid and can elicit IL-17 from synovial fluid T cells ex vivo.
EM lesions and synovial fluid also contain other T cell subsets,
including CD25
Foxp3
T cells, CD8
T cells,
γδ
T cells, and
natural killer (NK) T cells, which may regulate the inflammatory
response.
B. burgdorferi
induces dendritic cell production
of indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase, an enzyme necessary for tryptophan catabolism that is also capable of promoting the differentiation of tolerogenic T cells that produce IL-10.
In both mice and
humans, elevated IL-10 levels appear to correlate with less severe
disease.

### Mechanisms of Spirochete Persistence

When visualized in vivo,
B. burgdorferi
resides primarily in the
ECM in connective tissue.
Despite occasional sightings of spirochetes inside cells,
an intra-cellular phase of the
B. burgdorferi
life cycle has not been shown.
B. burgdorferi
uses immune evasion
strategies of an extra-cellular pathogen, which are directed toward
deterring phagocyte ingestion and antibody- and complementmediated lysis.
B. burgdorferi
proteins inhibit multiple components of the complement pathway. These include OspC and
BBK32, which bind complement component C4b
and C1q,
respectively, to inhibit the formation of C3 convertase, and Erp
and complement regulator–acquiring surface proteins that bind
host factor H
and FH-like factor 1 to prevent complement-mediated lysis.
B. burgdorferi
infection in mice, a reservoir host,
is associated with a delay in germinal center formation, thereby
deterring specific antibody-mediated killing and interfering with
the development of long-lived plasma cells that provide protective immunity from reinfection.
To impede antibody-mediated clearance,
B. burgdorferi
undergoes antigenic variation
and reduces expression of lipoproteins as infection progresses.
The
VlsE
gene undergoes random rearrangement of its expression
locus, producing antigenically distinct variants of VlsE.
In the
chronic phase of
B. burgdorferi
infection in mice, spirochetes can
be visualized in the ECM of connective tissue, especially in the
skin, without an associated inflammatory response.

## Clinical Features of Lyme Disease

Lyme disease occurs in stages that reflect the immune response
to spirochetes as they establish infection in the skin and later disseminate to distant organ sites (
Table 113.1
). Presenting clinical
manifestations depend on the stage of the illness in which patients
first seek medical attention. A characteristic feature of Lyme disease is that clinical signs can resolve without specific therapy, and
patients may be seen in later stages of the illness without exhibiting signs of early disease.

### Early Localized Infection

The hallmark of Lyme disease is the skin lesion EM, which is
present in up to 80% of patients (

The lesion arises
within 1 month (median, 7 to 10 days) at the tick bite site, which
is often in skin folds or where clothes bind. In children, the tick
bite site can also be around the hairline. EM begins as a red
macule that expands at the rate of 2 to 3 cm/day, enlarging to
more than 70 cm in diameter. Characteristic lesions greater than
5 cm in diameter in an appropriate clinical setting are sufficient
for establishing the diagnosis of Lyme disease.

usually a consistent complaint.

Cardiac Disease
Cardiac involvement is clinically apparent in less than 1% of
patients with Lyme disease.
It most often occurs within the
first 2 months of infection (average 21 days) and manifests as varying degrees of atrioventricular block, occasionally accompanied by
mild myopericarditis. Both atrial and ventricular arrhythmias can
occur, and sudden death has been reported.
Electrophysiologic
studies have mapped the conduction defect to the area above the
bundle of His and involving the atrioventricular node, although
multiple levels can be affected.
Overt congestive heart failure is
rare, and chronic cardiomyopathy, reported in Europe, has not
been documented in the United States.
Patients with Lyme
carditis often have a history of EM and may have concomitant
arthralgia and myalgia at the time of presentation. Absence of
valvular heart disease helps distinguish Lyme carditis from acute
rheumatic fever, and prominent myocardial dysfunction or pericardial involvement should suggest other infectious etiologies.

Nervous System Involvement
Acute neurologic Lyme disease occurs in less than 10% of patients
and most commonly manifests as cranial nerve palsy or meningitis.
Although alterations in memory and cognitive function can
occur in the setting of disseminated Lyme disease, such symptoms
are most likely a consequence of systemic inflammation rather
than actual CNS infection.
Parenchymal CNS involvement
(encephalomyelitis) is rare in the United States; the largest case
series of U.S. patients with neurologic Lyme disease in whom brain
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) was performed revealed that
findings of scattered white-matter lesions occurred at a frequency
similar to age-matched controls, suggesting that these are nonspecific.
Other rare manifestations include ischemic stroke and
cerebral vasculitis, which are primarily reported in Europe.
Cranial palsy occurs in 9% of reported cases of Lyme disease
and is the most common neurologic manifestation.
The seventh
nerve is most frequently affected, resulting in unilateral or bilateral facial palsy. Even in endemic areas, however, onset of seventh

Other Organ System Involvement
A variety of other organs can exhibit pathology with disseminated
B. burgdorferi
infection, including the eye (keratitis, uveitis, papillitis), the ear (sensorineural hearing loss), the liver (hepatitis),
the spleen (necrosis), skeletal muscle (myositis), and subcutaneous
tissue (panniculitis).
In general, more classic manifestations of
Lyme disease are present concurrently or have been present in the
recent past to suggest the diagnosis.

### Late Disease

Months after the onset of infection, untreated patients can
develop late manifestations of Lyme disease, usually involving the
joints (discussed separately later), nervous system, and the skin. At
this stage of infection, two-tier serologic testing (enzyme-linked
immunosorbent assay [EIA] and IgG immunoblot) for
B. burgdorferi
should be positive.
Late Neurologic Disease
Late neurologic Lyme disease is now rare; patients may be seen
with encephalomyelitis, peripheral neuropathy, or encephalopathy.
Encephalomyelitis, seen predominantly in Europe with
B. garinii
infection, is a slowly progressive, unifocal, or multifocal inflammatory disease of the CNS, with increased T2 signals
in the white matter on MRI.
CSF examination often reveals a
lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, and normal glucose;
serum
B. burgdorferi
–specific IgG and intrathecal production of
B. burgdorferi
IgG can also be found. These findings help distinguish Lyme encephalomyelitis from multiple sclerosis, which
may rarely be associated with positive IgG reactivity to
B. burgdorferi
in serum and CSF samples but without intrathecal antibody production.
Multiple sclerosis patients with positive
Lyme serologies do not respond to antibiotics used for neurologic
Lyme disease, and routine testing for Lyme disease in patients who
present with multiple sclerosis symptoms is not recommended.
Late peripheral nervous system involvement manifests as a
mild sensorimotor neuropathy in a “stocking and glove” distribution, with evidence of a mild confluent mononeuritis multiplex
on electrophysiologic studies.
Patients may have intermittent limb paresthesias and occasionally radicular pain. The most
common finding on physical examination is reduced vibratory
sensation in the lower extremities. Serum IgG to
B. burgdorferi
should be present, but CSF examination is normal. Patients with
this form of neuropathy should be evaluated for other infectious
diseases (syphilis, HIV, and hepatitis C virus), metabolic disorders
(especially vitamin B
deficiency, diabetes mellitus, and thyroid
disease), and autoimmune diseases.
Although late Lyme encephalopathy was reported in the
1980s, it is now rare. Patients complain of memory impairment
and cognitive dysfunction that are best shown by formal neuropsychological testing.
Occasionally, CSF examination can
reveal elevated protein, lymphocytic pleocytosis, and intrathecal
antibody to
B. burgdorferi
, but it can also be normal. Serum IgG
to
B. burgdorferi
should be present, however, to consider the diagnosis. The mild cognitive dysfunction seen in patients with Lyme
encephalopathy must be distinguished from neurocognitive deficits secondary to chronic stress, sleep deprivation, fibromyalgia,
chronic fatigue syndrome, or aging. As for any chronic encephalopathy, toxic-metabolic causes should be excluded. Brain imaging
studies are generally normal or show only nonspecific abnormalities and are not useful in establishing a diagnosis of encephalopathy associated with Lyme disease.
Late Skin Disease
The late skin lesion acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans (ACA)
is found mainly in Europe because of its association with
B.
afzelii
infection, although any
B. burgdorferi
species can cause the
lesion.
ACA develops insidiously over years and is most often
found on an extremity (
Fig. 113.3

to atrophic, cellophane-like skin with prominent appearance of
the blood vessels (

). Allodynia is considered a characteristic feature of the skin lesion. Approximately 60% of patients
also have a peripheral sensory neuropathy affecting the involved
extremity. A prominent lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate is shown on
the skin biopsy specimen. Antibiotics can lead to improvement in
pain and swelling, but atrophic skin remains.

## Lyme Arthritis and Other Musculoskeletal Manifestations of Lyme Disease

Musculoskeletal symptoms are common in all stages of Lyme disease and include migratory pain in joints, tendons, bursae, and
muscles.
Typically, musculoskeletal pain affects one or two
sites at a time, lasts only hours to a few days at any one location, and can be associated with significant fatigue. Although
an early study reported that frank arthritis occurred in 60% of
patients with untreated EM, the annual reported incidence has
been around 30% for several decades.
EIA and IgG immunoblot for
B. burgdorferi
are positive when arthritis appears, and
B. burgdorferi
DNA can be detected by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) in synovium and synovial fluid, even though cultures
are negative.
Although Lyme arthritis can resemble pauciarticular juvenile arthritis or reactive arthritis, patients generally test
negative for anti-nuclear antigen (ANA), rheumatoid factor, and
anti-citrullinated protein antibodies and do not have an increased
frequency of human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-B27 alleles. Joint
fluid analysis and synovial histopathology cannot distinguish these
entities. Axial and sacroiliac joint involvement is not a feature of
Lyme disease, but enthesitis can be seen.
Arthritis usually begins months or years after
B. burgdorferi
infection (average 6 months) and is pre-dated by migratory arthralgias in half of patients.

### Post-infectious Lyme Arthritis

Approximately 10% of patients treated with standard antibiotic
regimens for Lyme arthritis have persistent joint inflammation
and proliferative synovitis that does not respond to further antimicrobial therapy.
The pathogenesis of “post-infectious” Lyme
arthritis (previously called “antibiotic-refractory” or “post-antibiotic” Lyme arthritis) is unknown, but persistent infection is considered unlikely. Current theories center on the development of
a dysregulated inflammatory response, possibly related to initial
virulence of the infecting spirochete strain, inefficient clearance of
B. burgdorferi

A genetic predisposition to post-infectious Lyme arthritis had

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of Lyme disease should be considered in individuals
who are seen with an appropriate clinical history and who have a
reasonable risk of exposure to
B. burgdorferi
–infected ticks (
Fig.
113.4
).
Supporting serologic evidence is necessary to secure

B

the diagnosis at all stages of infection except when EM is present
or, in the setting of immunosuppression, direct detection of
B.
burgdorferi
by culture or detection of its DNA by PCR.
Routine laboratory tests are nonspecific, with some patients exhibiting
mildly elevated white blood cell (neutrophil) counts, erythrocyte
sedimentation rates, and liver function tests. Findings of thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, neutropenia, anemia, and/or elevated
indirect bilirubin levels should raise concern for co-infection with
B. microti
or
A. phagocytophilum
or an alternative diagnosis.
Microscopic visualization of spirochetes in clinical samples is not
sensitive enough for routine use in diagnosis.

### Culture

B. burgdorferi
is difficult to culture from clinical specimens.
Culture of a skin biopsy specimen taken from the leading margin
of an EM lesion is an exception, with
B. burgdorferi
detected in
more than 40% of samples. The rate of positivity of culture of
any other tissue or body fluid specimen is too low to recommend
culture as a routine diagnostic test for Lyme disease. The validity
of a novel serum culture assay for detecting
B. burgdorferi
in blood
samples has been questioned and should not be used.

### Serologic Testing

Serologic assays that detect
B. burgdorferi
-specific antigens make
up the only U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) cleared
or approved diagnostic tests for Lyme disease.
Presence of
antibodies to
B. burgdorferi
at best indicates previous exposure to
the organism, however, and should not be considered evidence of
active infection. Earlier studies in endemic areas found that
∼
5%
of people had asymptomatic IgG seroconversion to
B. burgdorferi
;
this estimate may be higher now.
After antibiotic therapy,
serum IgM and IgG titers to
B. burgdorferi
generally decrease
slowly but can remain positive for years.
Repeat serologic
testing is not recommended as a means for assessing response to
treatment.
The CDC recommends a two-test approach to maximize clinical utility as no single serologic test is sensitive or specific on its
own.
The standard two-tier serologic testing (sTTT) consists
of an EIA or an indirect immunofluorescence antibody assay to
detect IgM and IgG reactivity to
B. burgdorferi
as an initial screening test, followed by an immunoblot (Western blot) to confirm that
positive or equivocal results are the result of antibodies that bind
B.
burgdorferi
antigens. EIA and immunofluorescence assays are highly
sensitive tests but lack specificity because of the cross-reactivity of
antibodies induced by other bacterial pathogens with
B. burgdorferi.
A positive or equivocal EIA or immunofluorescence assay should
be confirmed by IgM or IgG immunoblot analysis of
B. burgdorferi
proteins. Banding patterns characteristic of early infection include
antibodies to the 41 kilodalton (kDa) flagellin protein and Osp C.
With disseminated infection, and especially with late Lyme disease,
IgG reactivity to an expanding array of
B. burgdorferi
proteins can
be seen.
A drawback to the sTTT is that the interpretation of
Lyme immunoblots is subjective and weak bands, especially on the
IgM immunoblot, can erroneously be scored, leading to a false-positive result. Many patients and healthy individuals have bands on
immunoblots that do not meet criteria for a positive result but can
create confusion for patients and health care providers. A positive
IgM immunoblot alone (in the absence of a positive IgG immunoblot) should not be used as evidence of active disease in patients with
illness greater than 1 month. The rationale for the “1-month rule”
is that most patients with active, untreated infection for >1 month
should have a positive IgG response. Positive IgM serologies alone
after 1 month of illness are most often false-positive tests, which
occur in the setting of other infectious diseases (especially infectious
mononucleosis and other spirochetal and tick-borne infections),
RA (with or without rheumatoid factor), and conditions associated
with a positive ANA (systemic lupus erythematosus).
The sTTT
has overall sensitivities of 25% in EM during the acute phase; 55%
for EM in the convalescent phase; and greater than 95% in neurologic, arthritis, and other manifestations of late disease phase.
In an effort to develop more sensitive and specific serologic
assays for early Lyme disease, peptide-based assays have been
developed, most often involving invariant regions of the OspC
and VlsE proteins. The C6 (IR6) peptide-based EIA uses a highly
conserved invariant region of the VlsE protein.
As a single assay, the C6 peptide EIA has increased sensitivity but slightly
lower specificity in early infection compared with the whole-cell
lysate EIA.
To enhance specificity, a modified two-tiered serologic testing (mTTT) algorithm is now available as an alternative strategy
to the sTTT.
This approach utilizes two different EIA
sequentially or concurrently and provides a categorical result
(positive, negative, or equivocal) in each tier, thereby eliminating the need for immunoblot confirmation. The assay can also be
performed more quickly than the sTTT. One limitation of the
mTTT approach is that it does not provide the detailed information about antibody reactivity with the broad array of spirochete
proteins that can be detected on immunoblot, although in practice, health care providers generally rely on laboratory reporting
of immunoblot positivity to support a diagnosis of Lyme disease.
Some commercial laboratories have developed assays using
recombinant antigens or use criteria for the interpretation of
immunoblots that have not been validated.
For this reason, the
CDC advises using only validated tests cleared or approved by the
FDA for serologic diagnosis of Lyme disease.

### Antibodies to Borrelia burgdorferi in Cerebrospinal Fluid

Serum antibody testing for Lyme disease by two-tier methodology is usually positive when patients present with Lyme neuroborreliosis and is the preferred testing method when considering that
diagnosis.
Intrathecal antibody production to
B. burgdorferi
is a highly specific test for CNS Lyme disease.
Positive
tests are more commonly found in European neuroborreliosis
than in North American Lyme disease, which may be the result
of the higher association of
B. garinii
in CNS infection than
B.
burgdorferi
ss.
Intrathecal antibody production is measured by
performing EIA using paired CSF and serum specimens to evaluate the ratio of
B. burgdorferi
–specific IgG in CSF and serum. A
limitation to the use of this test is that methods have not been
standardized across laboratories, and measurement of
B. burgdorferi
–reactive antibodies in the CSF without a concomitant serum
sample or assessment of antibody production by immunoblot can
be misleading. Furthermore, antibodies to
B. burgdorferi
may persist in CSF after treatment for Lyme disease and should not be
used to assess efficacy of therapy.

### Polymerase Chain Reaction

PCR has been used to detect
B. burgdorferi
DNA in a variety of
clinical specimens with variable success.
The greatest utility

of PCR clinically is in Lyme arthritis, where its sensitivity for
detection of
B. burgdorferi
DNA is 85% (range 40%–96%).
Although patients with Lyme arthritis have strong IgG responses
to
B. burgdorferi
at the time of presentation, seropositivity at best
indicates previous exposure to the organism and cannot distinguish past infection from current infection. In this situation, a positive synovial PCR test can be used to support a diagnosis of acute
Lyme arthritis. Sensitivity of PCR is lower than serologic testing
and synovial PCR is generally considered when there is a known
history of Lyme disease in the past that has been treated with
antibiotics or in the case of immunosuppression when patients
may be unable to mount the appropriate antibody responses (see
“Lyme Disease in the Immunosuppressed Host” section). Synovial PCR can remain positive for weeks to months after successful
treatment and therefore even this test should not be equated with
active infection in all instances.
B. burgdorferi
DNA can generally
no longer be detected by PCR after 1 month of intravenous (IV)
antibiotics or 2 months of oral antibiotics.
The sensitivity of PCR for detecting
B. burgdorferi
DNA in
other body fluids (blood and CSF) is low.
PCR of urine
specimens is not recommended because of documented nonspecific amplification of non–
B. burgdorferi
DNA targets.
PCR testing is useful for establishing exposure to
B. burgdorferi
, but positive results are not necessarily a reflection of active
infection because
B. burgdorferi
DNA can persist for months after
spirochetes are killed with antibiotics in vitro and in vivo.
There
are currently no FDA-approved tests for PCR-based molecular
techniques for detecting
B. burgdorferi
DNA in patient specimens.

### Other Laboratory Tests

A urine antigen test, immunofluorescent staining for cell wall–
deficient forms of
B. burgdorferi
, lymphocyte transformation
assays, or quantitative CD57 lymphocyte assays are offered by
some commercial laboratories to aid in the diagnosis of Lyme disease. These tests have not been adequately validated for accuracy
or clinical usefulness and should not be used.

### Imaging

Imaging studies have a limited role in the evaluation of patients
with Lyme disease because no feature is sufficiently distinctive to
confirm the diagnosis. Plain radiographs of arthritic joints show
changes consistent with an inflammatory arthropathy, including
joint effusions, synovial hypertrophy, and periarticular osteoporosis; with advanced duration of arthritis, cartilage loss, bony erosions, and calcified entheses can be seen.
MRI of arthritic joints
can confirm the radiographic findings and reveal associated myositis and adenopathy, which may be useful in distinguishing Lyme
arthritis from septic arthritis in children.
An ultrasound study
found prominent hamstring involvement in patients with Lyme
arthritis involving the knee.
Cranial and spinal MRI findings in neuroborreliosis can
reveal focal nodular lesions or patchy white-matter lesions on
T2-weighted images, consistent with inflammatory or demyelinating processes.
These lesions typically resolve after treatment for Lyme disease, in some cases only after several years.
Nonspecific findings of small white-matter lesions reported in
Lyme neuroborreliosis are indistinguishable from those commonly
seen in patients with risk factors for vascular disease and occur
at the same frequency as in patients without Lyme disease.
Positron-emission tomography and single-photon emission CT
studies are often normal or show only nonspecific changes with
subcortical and cortical hypoperfusion.

## Treatment

Guidelines for the clinical assessment (
Fig. 113.5
) and treatment
(

) of Lyme disease have been updated.
Because many
of the manifestations of Lyme disease resolve without specific
therapy, the goal of antibiotic treatment is to hasten resolution of
signs and symptoms and to prevent later clinical manifestations.
Generally, oral antibiotics are the recommended initial therapy for
most manifestations of Lyme disease, except parenchymal involvement of the brain or spinal cord and Lyme carditis requiring hospitalization. Disseminated infection and late manifestations of
Lyme disease may require longer courses of antibiotics, and there
is often a greater lag time to symptom resolution compared with
early disease. Optimal antibiotic choice and duration of treatment
have not been determined for patients who have underlying disorders requiring B cell–depleting therapies or chemotherapy.
Doxycycline is the antibiotic of choice in nonpregnant adults
and children age 8 years and older because it is also effective
against
A. phagocytophilum
, which may occur concomitantly with
early Lyme disease.
In European studies, doxycycline is the only
oral antibiotic that is as equally effective as parenteral antibiotics
in the treatment of acute neurologic disease without parenchymal CNS involvement.
Amoxicillin and cefuroxime axetil
are acceptable alternatives for the treatment of EM, facial palsy,
and other nonneurologic manifestations of Lyme disease. Macrolide antibiotics are less effective than other anti-microbials and
should be used only in individuals who cannot take doxycycline,
amoxicillin, or cefuroxime axetil. First-generation cephalosporins
are not effective therapy for Lyme disease.
Parenteral antibiotic therapy is an alternative choice over oral
doxycycline for patients with acute neurologic disease manifesting as Lyme meningitis, cranial neuropathy, or radiculoneuropathy.
The decision to use parenteral antibiotics should be based
on individual factors because oral doxycycline is effective in this
situation when patients are not on concomitant B cell–depleting therapies.
If parenteral antibiotics are administered initially,
the course can be completed with oral doxycycline. Documented
parenchymal brain or spinal cord disease should be treated with
parenteral therapy.
Ceftriaxone administered intravenously for 2
to 4 weeks is the preferred parenteral anti-microbial,
although
parenteral cefotaxime and penicillin G are acceptable alternatives.
Repeat treatment is not recommended for chronic neurologic
abnormalities unless objective signs of relapse are present.
Patients with symptomatic cardiac involvement (chest pain,
shortness of breath, syncope) or with significant conduction system disease (first-degree atrioventricular block with P-R intervals
≥
0.3 msec or second-degree or third-degree block) should be hospitalized for cardiac monitoring and IV antibiotic therapy.
Consultation with a cardiologist is recommended, and placement of a
temporary pacemaker may be necessary. Oral antibiotics can be
substituted for IV antibiotics when there is clinical improvement
to complete the course of therapy.
For arthritis, a 28-day course of oral doxycycline or amoxicillin
is recommended as initial therapy.
The rate of arthritis resolution varies, with
∼
25% of patients exhibiting mild residual joint

## Special Considerations

### Pregnancy and Lyme Disease

Pregnant and lactating women with Lyme disease can be treated
with the same antibiotic regimens recommended for nonpregnant
patients except that doxycycline should be avoided.
Maternal–
fetal transmission of
B. burgdorferi
can occur but, in contrast
to syphilis in pregnancy, there is no evidence that the organism
causes a congenital syndrome.
Pregnant patients should be reassured that with recommended therapy for Lyme disease,
B. burgdorferi
infection in the mother causes no harm to the fetus.

### Lyme Disease in the Immunocompromised Host

Immunosuppressed individuals pose unique challenges to the
diagnosis and management of Lyme disease. For individuals who
reside or who have recently traveled to endemic areas, a history of
tick bites and skin rashes suggestive of EM should raise suspicion
for Lyme disease. Several case-control studies have been published
from a single European center on the course and outcome of EM
in individuals on TNF inhibitor therapy, chemotherapy for malignancies, or after solid organ transplantation.
Patients receiving TNF inhibitor therapy had a shorter time from tick bite to
the onset of EM (median 7.5 vs. 14 days), smaller diameter of
lesions (median 10.5 cm vs. 15.5 cm), and more frequent signs
of disseminated infection (18.8% vs. 0%).
No differences were
noted in presence of serum IgM and/or IgG antibodies against
Borrelia in patients receiving TNF inhibitors compared with a
control group (42.9% vs. 51.7%). Treatment failure, most often
described as persistence of EM for more than 2 months after a first
course of antibiotic therapy, was found more frequently in those
on TNH inhibitor therapy (4/16, 25% vs. 0%), especially when
immunosuppression was continued while antibiotics were administered, but resolved with a second course of therapy.
Patients
with EM and hematologic malignancies, especially those with
lymphoid neoplasm, more often had signs of disseminated Lyme
disease or treatment failure, especially in those receiving active
chemotherapy.
Patients receiving rituximab had higher rates
of disseminated Lyme disease (43% vs. 8%) and positive borrelial
blood cultures (40% vs. <2%) compared with a historical cohort
of immunocompetent patients presenting with EM from the same
center.
Lower rates of
B. burgdorferi
antibodies were noted
among patients receiving rituximab versus the historical controls
(29% vs. 65%). Treatment failure was reported in one in seven
patients whose EM lesion persisted for >2 months but responded
to retreatment with another antibiotic. Nevertheless, the outcome
of early Lyme disease after 1 year was excellent in all patients.
Patients with solid organ transplant and immunosuppression had a smooth course of illness and favorable outcome
but remained seronegative at 1 year.
In another case-control study, a heterogenous group of patients presenting with
EM who had autoimmune diseases, chronic infection, or
organ transplantation requiring immunosuppressive therapy
was compared with immunocompetent individuals matched
for age, sex, and antibiotic treatment.
No differences in
response to therapy, production of anti–
B. burgdorferi
antibodies, or clinical outcomes were found.
There are now several case reports of seronegative Lyme neuroborreliosis in patients receiving rituximab therapy alone or in
combination with chemotherapy, most often in the context of
lymphoid malignancies.
Typical presentations involved the
nervous system with meningoradiculitis or meningoencephalitis
and exhibited CSF lymphocytic pleocytosis with negative serum
and CSF borrelial antibodies. The diagnosis was often delayed
because of initial suspicion for relapse of their underlying malignancy. Residence in a Lyme-endemic area and/or a history of prior
tick bites raised suspicion for neuroborreliosis. The diagnosis can
be supported by a positive PCR for
B. burgdorferi
sl DNA in CSF
and resolution of signs and symptoms after empiric treatment
with ceftriaxone. Although
B. burgdorferi
PCR on CSF samples
from immunocompetent hosts with neuroborreliosis is insensitive (10%–30%), these cases illustrate that this may not be true
for patients receiving B cell–depleting immunotherapies. It is also
important to remember that co-infections that can be seen with
Lyme disease, especially babesiosis, have significant morbidity and
mortality in those with asplenia or functional B cell deficiency due
to congenital immunodeficiency or anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody therapy. Judicious testing to exclude other tick-borne disease
should be considered.

## Outcomes

Most patients treated for Lyme disease, including those who are
on immunosuppressive therapies, resolve the disease with recommended courses of antibiotics.
Some patients may experience
a Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction, a self-limited worsening of symptoms within 24 to 48 hours of initiation of antibiotic therapy.
Within the first week of treatment, patients may rarely show evolution of disease, such as the development of new EM lesions or facial
nerve palsy, but these signs should improve as therapy progresses.
In one study of immunocompetent hosts, EM that recurred after
completion of antibiotic therapy for Lyme disease was found to
be due to reinfection rather than relapse of a partially treated initial infection.
The onset of noninfectious autoimmune arthritis
(RA, psoriatic arthritis, or spondylitis) has been reported after an
episode of Lyme disease diagnosed in an early stage of infection.
Patients with Lyme arthritis most often resolve joint inflammation with antibiotic therapy, and the minority who experience
post-infectious Lyme arthritis can be successfully treated with
DMARDs or biologics. These medications can be discontinued
without disease recurrence.
Two reports from Europe evaluated
the short-term (6 months) and long-term (median, 5 years) outcomes of children who had Lyme neuroborreliosis.
Overall, complete clinical resolution was seen in the majority during
the long term, although mild residual deficits occurred in some
(incompletely resolved facial palsy or mild motor or sensory deficits). Importantly, nonspecific symptoms, such as fatigue, headache, and cognitive issues, were no more frequent in patients who
had a history of neuroborreliosis than in age-matched controls,
and residual deficits did not affect school performance.
Patients with Lyme disease may be co-infected with another
tick-borne pathogen, most often with
B. microti
or
A. phagocytophilum
.
Patients with co-infection tend to be more symptomatic
at presentation and can have a delayed resolution of symptoms
compared with patients with Lyme disease alone. Co-infection
should be suspected when patients present with high fever or have
laboratory abnormalities—such as leukopenia, anemia, elevated
indirect bilirubin, or thrombocytopenia—that are not normally
seen in Lyme disease.
The impact of co-infection with the newly

emergent
Ixodes
tick-borne relapsing fever spirochete
Borrelia
miyamotoi
on Lyme disease is not yet known.

## Post-treatment Lyme Disease Syndrome

A minority of patients treated for Lyme disease may have fatigue,
musculoskeletal pain, and complaints of memory impairment
despite conventional or prolonged courses of antibiotic therapy, a
condition referred to as post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome.
This entity should be distinguished from “chronic Lyme disease,”
which originally referred to patients who presented with latestage manifestations of untreated Lyme disease, months after the
onset of infection.
Use of the term “chronic Lyme disease” is
discouraged because it now encompasses a broader group of people, including both those who may have post–Lyme disease syndrome and those for whom a diagnosis of Lyme disease cannot
be established by clinical or laboratory criteria.
Occasionally,
patients may have other conditions such as RA or fibromyalgia,
for which therapy has been delayed because of a misdiagnosis of
Lyme disease.
Latent autoimmunity also may be triggered by
B.
burgdorferi
infection. RA, reactive arthritis or psoriatic arthritis,
developing months after initial infection have been reported.
Musculoskeletal pain is common in the general population; the
estimated prevalence of fatigue ranges from 7% to 45%.
In the
absence of a clinical history with objective manifestations of Lyme
disease, supporting positive two-tiered serologic tests (or PCR or
culture in the case of known immunodeficiency), definitive attribution of symptoms present for more than 1 to 2 months to
B.
burgdorferi
infection should not be made.
In several controlled, population-based cohort studies that
used validated standardized measures of outcomes (e.g., Short
Form-36), patients with Lyme disease had more joint pain, symptoms of memory impairment, and worse functional status because
of pain compared with controls.
Follow-up studies suggest
that quality-of-life measures improve with time.
Psychological factors and the presence of psychiatric comorbidity in patients
with Lyme disease correlate with poor functional outcomes after
treatment.
A European study demonstrated that the frequency
of nonspecific symptoms at 6 and 12 months after treatment for
early Lyme disease was no greater than that of the control group,
which included family members without a history of Lyme disease.
Children are less likely than adults to have persistent complaints after treatment for Lyme disease.
Several controlled trials of antibiotics for persistent symptoms after
Lyme disease failed to demonstrate sustained improvement.
In
one study, the frequency of nonspecific symptoms was similar among
patients treated for Lyme disease and a healthy control group, raising
the possibility that in some cases attribution of symptoms to a previous diagnosis of Lyme disease may be anchoring bias.
Prolonged antibiotic use is not without risk. Minor side effects
are common, and serious adverse events such as biliary complications from ceftriaxone therapy or indwelling catheter–related
infections occur at high enough rates to warrant only judicious
use of antibiotics.
Our evolving understanding of the role of
intestinal dysbiosis, which can be a consequence of antibiotics, on
human health and disease raises additional concerns over the use
of long-term antibiotics.

## Prevention

The most effective way to prevent Lyme disease is to reduce
exposure risk to
B. burgdorferi
–infected ticks through personal
protective measures and environmental controls.
These measures include avoiding tick habitats such as wooded areas, stone
fences, woodpiles, and tall grass; wearing protective clothing; and
performing daily surveillance, including of pets, which can bring
ticks into the home, and prompt removal of ticks (within 24 hours
of feeding).
Bathing within 2 hours of an outdoor activity with
possible tick exposure is also effective. Other effective measures
include use of insecticides that contain DEET, picaridin, IR3535,
or oil of lemon eucalyptus applied directly to the skin, or permethrin applied to clothing. Yearly application of acaricides to
property can kill ticks but should not be relied on as a means to
prevent infection.
A single 200 mg dose of doxycycline (or 4.4 mg/kg to as
much as 200 mg for children age 8 years and older) reduces
the incidence of Lyme disease after a recognized tick bite but
only when the patient presents within 72 hours of removal of an
identified high-risk tick bite.
High-risk bites are defined as the
bite of an identified
Ixodes
tick from a highly endemic area where
infection rates are more than 20% and the tick is engorged and
attached for
≥
36 hours. A recombinant Osp A–based vaccine
to prevent Lyme disease was approved by the FDA in 1990 and
later withdrawn because of low market demand and concern for
potential vaccine-related side effects.
A new OspA-based vaccine named VLA15 has been developed based on the six most
common serotypes of
B. burgdorferi
sl species in North America and Europe.
This vaccine has been modified to eliminate
potential cross-reactivity with human proteins and a Phase III
clinical trial is presently ongoing in endemic areas in the United
States and Europe.

## Conclusion

Lyme disease is a localized or systemic infection that usually manifests with skin and musculoskeletal signs and symptoms, but it
can involve other organ systems, especially the nervous system and
more rarely the heart. The diagnosis should be based on objective clinical findings consistent with Lyme disease and supporting serologic tests. Patients receiving immunosuppressive therapy,
especially B cell–depleting therapies, for other conditions are
more likely to present with disseminated infection and may not
mount antibody responses. In these situations, direct measures
such as PCR for
B. burgdorferi
DNA or culture may help support the diagnosis. Most patients are cured within 2 to 4 weeks
of antibiotic therapy, although the time to disease resolution may
be prolonged, especially for individuals in whom therapy was
delayed; irreversible tissue damage may occur. A poor response to
antibiotic therapy should raise concern for alternative diagnoses or
co-infection with other tick-borne pathogens. Less than 10%
of patients with Lyme arthritis develop persistent synovitis for
which continued antibiotic therapy is of no benefit. Treatment
with NSAIDs, hydroxychloroquine, or other DMARDs usually resolves arthritis and often these patients can discontinue
DMARDs within a year. Some patients treated for Lyme disease
experience a post–Lyme disease syndrome of fatigue, headaches,
mild memory impairment, and musculoskeletal pain. Ongoing
infection cannot be shown, and controlled treatment trials show
no benefit of prolonged antibiotic therapy over placebo. Referral to an academic medical center with experience in the diagnosis and treatment of Lyme disease should be considered when
patients do not respond as expected to therapy.
