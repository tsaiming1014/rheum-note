# Treatment of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

ANTONIS C. FANOURIAKIS, GEORGE BERTSIAS, AND
DIMITRIOS T. BOUMPAS

KEY POINTS

> Treatment in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) should aim at

> remission or low disease activity and prevention of flares.

> Drug toxicity, especially from glucocorticoids (GCs), should be minimized.

> Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) is recommended for all patients who do not

> have contraindications.

> HCQ dose should not exceed 5 mg/kg real body weight with ophthalmologic

> evaluation at baseline, 5 years later, and yearly thereafter.

> SLE with severe, organ-threatening disease is usually managed

> with high-dose GCs and pulse therapy with intravenous

> cyclophosphamide (CYC).

> In moderately severe lupus nephritis (LN), mycophenolate mofetil (MMF)

> is equally effective and has a better toxicity profile than CYC.

> Either MMF or azathioprine (AZA) is recommended for maintenance of

> renal response in LN.

> Belimumab should be considered in cases with persistent disease activity or

> frequent flares despite standard of care, when GCs cannot be tapered to

> an acceptable daily dose or as an add-on to standard therapy in LN.

> Anifrolumab may be considered in patients with moderate to severe

> nonrenal SLE who are receiving standard therapy.

> Rituximab (anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody) may be used in severe or

> refractory cases.

> Preconception counseling and pregnancy planning are essential for the

> successful completion of pregnancies.

## Clinical Course and General Treatment Strategy

KEY POINTS

> Treatment of moderate or severe systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)

> includes initial intensive induction immunosuppressive therapy

> followed by less intense maintenance therapy.

> Only a small proportion of SLE patients achieve prolonged periods of

> disease quiescence.

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) usually follows a relapsing–remitting course. Recent studies have shown that an equal
number of patients have continuously active disease, and only a
small proportion experience long periods of disease quiescence.
Despite improvements in overall survival (85% to 90% during
the first 10 years),
patients with SLE are still at risk for premature death.
Persistent inflammation results in irreversible major
organ damage, which is linked to decreased quality of life and
increased mortality.
Accordingly, therapeutic strategies should
aim at reducing overall burden of systemic inflammation. Achieving these goals requires (1) accurate assessment of disease activity
and flares, (2) stratification of patients according to severity of
target organ involvement, (3) use of safe and effective drugs to
induce remission promptly and prevent flares, and (4) prevention
and management of disease and treatment-related comorbidities.
In general, patients with mild lupus manifestations (skin,
joint, and mucosal involvement) are treated with anti-malarials
or disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs), alone or
in combination with low-dose oral glucocorticoids (GCs). Severe
SLE with major organ involvement typically requires an initial
period of intensive immunosuppressive therapy to control aberrant
immunologic activity and halt tissue injury, followed by a longer
period of less intensive and less toxic maintenance therapy to consolidate remission and prevent flares. Immunosuppressive therapy
helps reduce the use of GCs, thus reducing their deleterious effects.

## Patient and Physician Preferences

Patients with SLE experience poor quality of life, which is only in
part associated with disease activity and organ damage.
Indeed,
patients considered to be in “remission” by their treating physicians often report a considerable residual symptom burden;
important contributors include fatigue, fibromyalgia, depression,
and cognitive dysfunction.
Treating physicians should regularly
address these issues and engage symptomatic or remedial therapies, as indicated. The realization that a significant proportion
of patients have disease- and treatment-related comorbidities has
shifted the attention of physicians to adjunct therapies and primary prevention strategies, such as infection and cardiovascular
disease (CVD) risk reduction measures.
Health professionals are encouraged to involve patients in
treatment decisions, recognizing them as experts with a unique
knowledge of their own health and their preferences for treatments
and outcomes.
This approach may be particularly challenging
in patients with severe lupus, who might benefit from cytotoxic
therapy, which is associated with significant toxicity.
Physicians
should provide patients with balanced information to ensure accurate perception of the risks and benefits of prescribed medications.

### Glucocorticoids

GCs exert broad inhibitory effects on immune responses mediated
by T and B cells, as well as on the effector functions of monocytes
and neutrophils. On the basis of these effects and their rapid onset
of action, GCs have been remarkably efficacious in managing
acute SLE manifestations.
Low-dose GC therapy (
≤
10 mg prednisone equivalent per day) is
generally used when initial therapies (mainly anti-malarials) are not
tolerated or are inadequate to control disease activity. In moderate to
severe disease, GCs are used preferably in combination with immunosuppressive agents, at doses of 0.5 to 0.7 mg/kg in a single dose,
usually in the morning. When combined with immunosuppressive
agents, the GC dose should rarely exceed 0.5 to 0.6 mg/kg due to
concerns for infections and other toxicities. Tapering of GC dose may
start after the first 4 weeks of therapy, targeting a maximum daily dose
of 7.5 mg after 6 months. Concomitant use of immunosuppressive
agents facilitates tapering and decreases cumulative GC toxicity.
In severe, rapidly progressing disease or when doses greater than
0.6 mg/kg/day prednisone equivalent are required to control disease
activity, GC pulse therapy may be introduced. Intravenous (IV)
pulses of methylprednisolone (MP) (250 to 1000 mg daily for 1 to
3 consecutive days) are used, as they allow for lower cumulative oral
(per os [PO]) dose and less damage accrual without compromising
efficacy.
In addition to expediting remission, IV MP pulses may
allow for the use of lower GC doses during the induction period.
GC toxicity may involve early (mood effects, acne, myalgias, infections), later (metabolic), and late-onset (osteoporosis, avascular bone
necrosis, cataract, CVD) adverse events.
Although confounded by
increased systemic disease activity, most effects are considered to be
dependent on the cumulative dose and duration. Chronic exposure to
GC doses higher than 6 to 7.5 mg/day of prednisone equivalent is clearly
associated with an increased risk for irreversible organ damage.
Thus, the European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(EULAR) recommendations for the management of SLE suggest
that the chronic maintenance dose of GC should be less than 7.5
mg/day prednisone equivalent, if GC cannot be totally withdrawn.
Although minimization of exposure to GCs is a universal target of
therapy, tapering should be done with caution and may take considerable time. Abrupt withdrawal of 5 mg/day prednisone in SLE clinically quiescent for more than 1 year increased the risk of subsequent
flare (relative risk [RR] in patients maintained on 5 mg/day prednisone 0.2; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.1 to 0.7).

### Anti-malarial Drugs

Anti-malarial drugs, mainly HCQ, are considered as anchor
drugs for the treatment of SLE due to their multifaceted beneficial effects. Importantly, prospective observational studies have
reported an inverse association between use of HCQ and accrual
of irreversible organ damage, as well as mortality.
Additional
effects, ranging from the prevention of congenital heart block to
a more favorable metabolic profile and reduction of the risk of
thrombosis in patients with antiphospholipid antibodies (aPLs),
have also been described.
In a randomized controlled trial
(RCT) in stable active patients with SLE on HCQ treatment,
those who achieved blood HCQ levels greater than or equal to
1000 ng/mL had a tendency for reduced SLE flares (20.5% vs.
35.1%) during a 7-month period.
HCQ is typically well tolerated, with low rates of mild gastrointestinal and skin adverse events. The major concern arising
from its use is the risk for retinal toxicity. Advanced, more sensitive screening techniques for the detection of HCQ-induced retinopathy (10-2 visual fields and spectral domain-optical coherence
tomography) suggest that the latter may be more prevalent than
originally believed; the risk may reach 10% or more among longterm users (more than 10 years).
A daily dose greater than
5 mg/kg real weight and a duration of use of more than 5 and,
especially, more than 10 years, are particularly associated with
heightened risk of retinal toxicity. Patients with chronic kidney
disease, pre-existing retinal or macular abnormalities, or concomitant use of tamoxifen are at increased risk. Thus the daily dose
should currently not exceed 5 mg/kg real body weight, although a
recent study suggested that doses of
≤
5 mg can be associated with
increased risk for flares.
Routine ophthalmologic evaluation is
recommended at baseline, 5 years later, and yearly thereafter, if
baseline examination is normal.
More frequent monitoring is
warranted in patients with pre-existing retinal abnormalities or
other risk factors.

### Immunosuppressive and Cytotoxic Drugs

Methotrexate
Methotrexate (MTX) is a steroid-sparing treatment in mild to
moderate SLE, particularly for articular and cutaneous manifestations.
MTX is administered once weekly orally or parenterally.
A systematic literature review reported significant reduction of
the SLE Disease Activity Index (SLEDAI) among MTX-treated
patients compared with controls (odds ratio [OR], 0.44), followed
by significant reduction in the average dose of GC (OR, 0.34).

Use in Lupus Nephritis.
RCTs with long-term follow-up have
shown that intermittent pulse IV CYC therapy is effective for
moderate to severe proliferative lupus nephritis (PLN).
CYC
delays renal scarring, preserves renal function, and reduces the risk
for progression to end-stage kidney disease (ESKD).
Following
induction therapy, a maintenance regimen is necessary to decrease

the risk of flares.
A combination of pulse therapy with IV CYC
and IV MP improves renal outcomes without increasing toxicity.
Severe forms of proliferative LN (i.e., with acute reduction
in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) and/or adverse renal biopsy
findings) may be treated with seven monthly pulses of IV CYC
(0.5 to 1 g/m
), followed by maintenance therapy (see later in the
chapter). In most severe cases, monthly pulses of IV MP may be
given during the induction period.
In the Euro-Lupus Nephritis Trial, which studied Caucasian
patients with milder forms of LN, low-dose IV CYC (six fortnightly [every 2 weeks] pulses at a fixed dose of 500 mg each in
combination with three daily doses of 750 mg of IV MP), followed by azathioprine (AZA) for maintenance, had comparable
efficacy and less toxicity than high-dose IV CYC (eight pulses).
Mean patient survival rate at 10 years was 92% for both groups;
ESKD and doubling of serum creatinine (Cr) rates did not differ
between the two groups.
Therefore, low-dose IV CYC may be an
alternative option for patients with moderately severe LN, as it has
the additional benefit of less gonadotoxicity.
IV CYC has also been used in membranous lupus nephropathy, in an RCT comparing cyclosporin A (CsA), IV CYC (0.5 to
1 g/m
× six bimonthly doses), and GC alone, in 42 patients.
All patients received alternate-day oral prednisone (1 mg/kg every
other day for 8 weeks, then tapered). At 1 year, the cumulative
probability of remission was 27% with prednisone, 60% with IV
CYC, and 83% with CsA. Rates of nephrotic syndrome relapse
were 10 times higher in patients treated with CsA versus those
treated with IV CYC.
Use in Extrarenal Disease.
CYC has demonstrated efficacy in
life-threatening or refractory extrarenal lupus manifestations such
as severe thrombocytopenia (platelet count < 20,000/mm
), neurologic disease, severe cardiopulmonary manifestations, and extensive skin disease.
Investigators
have reported the superiority
of IV CYC versus IV MP for severe nonthrombotic neurologic
SLE (seizures, peripheral neuropathy, optic neuritis, transverse
myelitis, brainstem disease, coma, internuclear ophthalmoplegia).
Azathioprine
AZA inhibits the conversion of purine bases such as inosine to
adenine and guanine ribonucleotides. AZA (2 to 2.5 mg/kg/day)
is safe in the long term with no significant increase in the risk for
infections; a study in patients with inflammatory bowel disease
has linked AZA to an increased risk for cancer but with no evidence of causality.
Gastrointestinal complaints are frequent, and
mild liver enzyme elevation may occur, but severe liver injury is
infrequent. Reversible, dose-related bone marrow toxicity is also
common; leukopenia is encountered in 4.5% and thrombocytopenia in 2% (see
Table 82.1
) of patients, respectively. AZA toxicity has been associated with genetic polymorphisms, resulting
in decreased thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) activity and
impaired ability to detoxify intermediate metabolites.
Genetic
testing is available—although not widely—for TPMT variant alleles, so that the starting dosages of AZA can be adjusted
accordingly. Concomitant use of allopurinol, which inhibits xanthine oxidase (the enzyme that metabolizes AZA), substantially
increases toxicity and should be avoided.
Patients with manifestations of moderate severity, such as constitutional symptoms, thrombocytopenia with platelet count 30
to 50 × 10
/mm
, and serositis may respond to AZA, usually in
combination with moderate to high starting GC doses (see
Table
82.2
). AZA has also been used in a controlled study of European
patients with proliferative LN who were randomly assigned to
receive pulse IV CYC plus prednisone versus IV MP plus AZA
plus a tapering dose of prednisone. Both groups received maintenance therapy with AZA plus prednisone after the second year.
Despite no significant differences between groups in rates of sustained doubling of Cr, ESKD, or mortality after a median of 9.6
years, renal relapses occurred more frequently in the AZA group
(hazard ratio [HR], 4.5 compared with IV CYC).
Repeat renal
biopsies showed that progression of chronic lesions was more
effectively halted by CYC than by AZA.
Thus, AZA has been
abandoned as a therapeutic option in the induction phase of LN
unless other options are not available or are contraindicated, such
as during pregnancy.
AZA can be used for maintenance of response in LN.
Two
RCTs have compared AZA versus MMF as maintenance regimens in PLN. In the MAINTAIN trial, which included European patients who received induction with low-dose IV CYC,
both agents were equally efficacious in terms of time-to-renal
flare, severe flares, renal remission, and doubling of Cr (median
follow-up 110 months).
Repeat kidney biopsies failed to
reveal histologic differences between the two groups.
In contrast,
the Aspreva Lupus Management Study (ALMS), which included
a larger number of patients with multiethnic backgrounds who
received induction therapy with either IV CYC or MMF, reported
increased renal flares in the AZA versus the MMF group (see also
next section).
To this end, both agents can be used after initial therapy (3 to 6 months)
on the basis of availability, clinical
experience, and potential for pregnancy, because MMF is associated with an increased risk of spontaneous abortion and fetal
malformation.
Mycophenolate Mofetil
Pharmacology.
Mycophenolate is a prodrug of mycophenolic acid (MPA), a potent inhibitor of inosine monophosphate
dehydrogenase that is indispensable for the
de novo
synthesis of
guanosine nucleotides. It is orally administered either as MMF
or as enteric-coated mycophenolate sodium (eMPS). A 720-mg
dose of eMPS is roughly equivalent to a 1-g dose of MMF. The
lack of any salvage nucleotide synthesis pathway in lymphocytes
renders them a selective target for MMF. MMF has excellent oral
bioavailability, with peak levels occurring within 1 to 2 hours after
administration and a half-life of 17 hours. Although therapeutic drug monitoring to guide MMF dosing has been proposed in
renal transplantation, validation of therapeutic MPA monitoring
in LN is still required.
MMF is strongly teratogenic and should
not be administered during pregnancy.
Use in Lupus Nephritis.
Several RCTs have demonstrated
equal or even superior efficacy of MMF versus CYC in inducing
renal response in PLN. Nevertheless, the ALMS, one of the largest
and most racially diverse RCTs in LN, failed to demonstrate the
superiority of MMF (2 to 3 g/day) versus monthly pulses of IV
CYC (0.5 to 1 g/m
), both combined with oral prednisone (maximum starting dose, 60 mg/day).
At 6 months, response rates
were 56% for patients treated with MMF and 53% for patients
treated with IV CYC. Post hoc analysis suggested superiority of
MMF in black and Hispanic patients and noninferiority in the
subgroup of patients with poor renal function at baseline.
Meta-analyses of the previously mentioned and other RCTs have
concluded that MMF is as effective as CYC for induction treatment of PLN and is associated with lower risks of leukopenia,
amenorrhea, and alopecia but not infections.
Based on these
data, MMF is now considered as first-line treatment in PLN, especially when gonadal toxicity is an issue.

A pooled analysis of 84 patients with pure membranous LN
(MLN) who participated in two RCTs has demonstrated comparable anti-proteinuric effects and nephrotic syndrome remission rates in MMF- and IV CYC–treated patients.
Due to more
favorable toxicity profile compared with CYC, MMF is recommended as first-line treatment of MLN.
Meta-analysis of RCTs comparing MMF versus AZA as
maintenance therapy in PLN showed comparable rates of renal
remission, doubling of Cr, and renal remission during a 3-year
period.
However, treatment with AZA was associated with
increased risk for renal relapses (RR, 1.45) and leukopenia (RR,
7.1) compared with MMF. Both MMF and AZA can be used for
maintenance of response in PLN on the basis of availability and
potential for pregnancy. Early (before 18 to 24 months) discontinuation of MMF or switching to other immunosuppressant is
associated with increased risk for renal flare.
Use in Extrarenal Lupus.
A randomized study compared
mycophenolate (in the form of eMPS) and AZA, in combination
with both GC (starting dose 20 to 30 mg/day) and anti-malarials,
in 240 Caucasian SLE patients without renal involvement. Most
patients had musculoskeletal and mucocutaneous manifestations,
but almost 30% also had cardiorespiratory disease.
The primary
endpoint of the study was the rate of remission (measured by SLEDAI-2K and BILAG) at 3 and 24 months, and the results were
remarkably in favor of the mycophenolate arm; 71.2% of patients
reached a clinical SLEDAI = 0 at 24 months compared with
48.3% in the AZA group. Mycophenolate also reduced the occurrence of flares more than AZA (71.7% vs. 50% for BILAG A or
B flares) without excess toxicity.
Post hoc analysis of the ALMS
data showed that MMF was equally efficacious with IV CYC pulse

Intravenous Immunoglobulin
Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) exerts immunomodulatory
effects by interaction with anti-idiotypic antibodies, interference
with complement and cytokines, cytolysis of target cells, induction of apoptosis through Fc receptors, and modulation of costimulatory molecules.
A systematic review of 13 studies concluded
that treatment with IVIG resulted in significant reduction in the
SLE disease activity scores and improvements in serum complement levels.
Improvements in cytopenias, especially immune
thrombocytopenia and hemolytic anemia, proteinuria, arthritis,
fever, and mucocutaneous manifestations, have been reported.
Common adverse events include fever, myalgia, headache, and
arthralgia; less common are aseptic meningitis, nephropathy in
patients who receive sucrose-containing preparations, and thromboembolic complications in older patients with atherosclerotic
risk factors.

### Biologic Therapies

Belimumab
Belimumab is a fully human monoclonal antibody against the B
lymphocyte stimulator (BlyS) protein. Following successful RCTs,
it is approved either for IV (10 mg/kg/month) or subcutaneous
(200 mg/wk) administration.
Enrolled patients in RCTs had
moderately active SLE (average SLEDAI 9 to 10; severe renal or
neuropsychiatric lupus was excluded) and were receiving combinations of HCQ and GC, with or without immunosuppressants.
Belimumab treatment resulted in a modest yet statistically significant reduction in disease activity (difference in primary endpoint, the composite SLE responder index [SRI], ranging from
9% to 14.7% in the three studies, OR to attain SRI response at
52 weeks 1.31 for belimumab).
Stronger effects were observed in
the musculoskeletal (arthritis), mucocutaneous (alopecia, ulcers,
malar rash) and immunologic domains, and skin vasculitis.
Belimumab-treated patients exhibited significantly reduced risk for
severe SLE flares (HR, 0.74), modest reductions in GC dose, and
improvements in fatigue and health-related quality of life.
Post hoc analysis showed that drug effectiveness was higher in
patients with baseline SLEDAI scores of
≥
10 or serologic activity
(low C3/C4 and/or high anti–double-stranded DNA [dsDNA])
or those who were on GCs upon enrollment.
A series of observational studies have subsequently confirmed efficacy of the drug
in real-life patient populations.
Belimumab should be considered in extrarenal disease with inadequate control (ongoing disease activity or frequent flares) to first-line treatments (typically
including combination of HCQ and prednisone with or without
immunosuppressive agents) and the inability to taper GC daily
dose to acceptable levels (i.e., maximum 7.5 mg/day). Long-term
follow-up data (up to 7 years) suggest that belimumab is generally
well tolerated, with no significant increase in risk for serious infections, although infusion reactions can be encountered, especially
during the first three infusions.

Rituximab
Rituximab (RTX) is a chimeric mouse-human monoclonal antibody against CD20 on B-cells. Controlled clinical trials both in
extrarenal SLE and in LN have failed to show statistically significant
clinical benefit, but multiple observational studies have provided
evidence for drug efficacy. In extrarenal disease, uncontrolled studies including more than 1200 patients suggest the efficacy of RTX,
which is mainly used as add-on therapy to GCs, other immunosuppressive (IS) agents, or both.
Complete or partial responses
are noted in 64% to 91% of patients within 3 to 9 months, particularly in the musculoskeletal (arthritis), hematology (autoimmune
hemolytic anemia [AIHA], thrombocytopenia), and immunology
domains. As a rule, more than one IS drug need to have failed
before RTX administration, except in cases of severe autoimmune
thrombocytopenia, due to the drug’s demonstrated efficacy both in
lupus and in isolated immune thrombocytopenia.
In LN, observational data from 300 patients with active LN despite treatment
with GCs plus immunosuppressives (CYC, MMF, or AZA) support RTX efficacy.
After an average of 60 weeks, rates of complete and partial renal response were approximately 40% and 34%,
respectively, with lower responses in mixed class V and III to IV or
pure class V LN. In a nonrandomized study, patients with active

proliferative LN received induction treatment with GCs plus either
RTX (1000 mg on days 0 and 14), MMF (2 to 2.5 g/day), or lowdose IV CYC (3 g), followed by maintenance with AZA, MMF,
or CsA.
Renal response rates at 12 months were comparable
between the RTX, MMF, and IV CYC groups (71%, 53%, and
65%, respectively). In lupus membranous nephropathy, a retrospective multicenter study of RTX monotherapy in 15 patients reported
a complete response (CR) rate of 58% at 12 months.
Prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, RTX was associated with
a slightly increased risk for infections, most of which were mild,
with no serious signal for serious or opportunistic infections. Nevertheless, during the pandemic, it became clear that treatment with
RTX is accompanied by increased risk for hospitalization and other
adverse sequelae, across a spectrum of rheumatic conditions, including SLE, due to its impact on the patient’s capacity to produce
neutralizing antibodies.
This reality, combined with the drug’s
off-label status, suggests that RTX should currently be considered
only after failure of other therapies in multidrug refractory SLE.
Anifrolumab
Anifrolumab is a monoclonal antibody against the type I interferon receptor, thus inhibiting signaling from all type I interferons, which have a central role in SLE pathogenesis. Following a
very successful phase 2 trial,
the drug was tested on two large

### Mucocutaneous and Joint Disease

For all forms of cutaneous involvement (including photosensitivity) in SLE, the use of sun protection measures (broad spectrum
sunscreens and protective clothing) cannot be overemphasized.
Smoking cessation is essential, as it also interferes with efficacy of
anti-malarials in cutaneous lupus.
Topical GCs reduce redness
and scaling. Topical CNIs (TAC, pimecrolimus) are a useful alternative, especially for the face, because they cause fewer atrophic
and rosacea-like effects than topical steroids.
First-line systemic treatment for skin disease consists of antimalarials, if not already prescribed, which can be used alone or
in combination with oral GCs at moderate doses.
HCQ is the
anti-malarial of choice due to its additional beneficial effects in
SLE; in cases of incomplete response or evidence of retinopathy,
quinacrine may be used (in the former case, as add-on therapy
to HCQ), although it is not available in all countries.
Almost
40% of patients will not respond to this first-line treatment. In
such cases, MTX can be added, as it has shown efficacy in refractory subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE) and discoid
lupus erythematosus.
Alternative choices for conventional synthetic drugs are retinoids, dapsone, and MMF.
From biologic drugs, anifrolumab seems to have particular efficacy in skin
disease, although formal real-life evidence is still pending. Belimumab and RTX have also shown efficacy in mucocutaneous manifestations, but reported studies did not use a validated assessment
for response of skin lesions; RTX may be less efficacious in chronic
cutaneous lupus.
Finally, thalidomide should be reserved for
severe recalcitrant CLE cases, because of its serious and potentially
irreversible neurotoxicity (
Fig. 82.2
).
In mild lupus arthritis, anti-malarials will usually suffice. In
persistent disease, utilization of DMARDs is advocated, particularly MTX.
Patients with inadequate response to DMARDs may
benefit from belimumab treatment, although the latter is less efficacious in cases of severe arthritis resembling RA.
In such cases,
or in SLE–RA overlap syndromes refractory to DMARDs, RTX
can be used instead.

### Lupus Nephritis

Traditional therapeutic strategies in LN include an initial treatment period, aimed at substantially improving disease activity (or

voclosporin/TAC plus MMF, or belimumab plus MMF or CYC).

### Treat-to-Target in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Similar to RA, the principle of treating to target has been proposed for SLE.
Despite the phenotypic heterogeneity, certain
goals of treatment may be applicable to all patients. These include

toxicity, and (5) improvement of patient quality of life (

Risk Factors and Dialysis
A systematic review and meta-analysis demonstrated decreased
risks of LN-associated ESKD between the 1970s and 1990s
(15-year risk from over 30% to 22%), which was followed by
a plateau and then, surprisingly, by an increased risk in recent
years.
Class IV LN confers the highest risk for ESKD development. Another study showed that all-cause mortality due to

(mortality hazard ratio, 0.51 to 0.68, depending on patient
race).
Infections and cardiovascular events constitute the
main causes of death, although their incidence has declined
accordingly.
Clinical predictors for ESKD include baseline-reduced GFR
and hypertension, diabetes mellitus,
persistent isolated C3

and failure to achieve renal remission,

may have an impact on long-term renal survival.
Preemptive
transplantation should be considered because it is associated with
superior graft and patient outcomes.
There are no prospective studies comparing various immunosuppressive regimens after renal transplantation in SLE. CNIs are
generally included in the induction phase (6 to 12 months) to
prevent acute transplant rejection.
Their use should be minimized during the maintenance phase, and other immunosuppressive agents (MMF, AZA) should preferentially be employed.
Surveillance biopsy studies have shown that subclinical recurrence
of LN (usually mild class I/II) in the renal allograft is not rare,
but this is rarely a significant clinical problem.
Risk factors for
recurrence include black race, female sex, and living-donor transplantation.
APS has been associated with post-transplant renal
thrombosis and poor graft outcome and warrants anti-coagulation
therapy.

### Cardiovascular Morbidity

SLE is an independent risk factor for CVD due to both traditional
and disease-related risk factors, such as persistent disease activity,
presence of aPL and use of GC. Biomarkers such as proinflammatory high-density lipoprotein (HDL) or hsCRP are elevated in
lupus patients and have been correlated with carotid intima media
thickness (CIMT), a surrogate marker for subclinical atherosclerosis.
Mendelian randomization and network analysis show
shared genetic association between lupus and coronary artery
disease.
Tight control of traditional CVD risk factors is recommended
for patients with SLE.
Primary CVD prevention should follow the principles applied to the general population. In all lupus
patients, the 10-year CVD risk should be calculated, using one
of the validated prediction scores. Importantly, because many
SLE patients are young, RR will give a more accurate prediction than absolute risk.
The importance of smoking cessation
cannot be overemphasized. Hypertension should be controlled
below 130/80 mm Hg.
For dyslipidemia, two RCTs in adult
and pediatric SLE have failed to show benefits of atorvastatin in
CIMT and coronary artery calcium.
Accordingly, lipidlowering therapy in lupus should follow the guidelines for the
general population.
Low-dose aspirin reduces the risk for incident CVD in SLE (HR = 0.24 in one retrospective study),
but
its universal use for primary CVD prevention is discouraged by
the recent studies in the general population, which showed the
benefits of aspirin to be largely counterbalanced by a significantly
larger bleeding hazard.

### Osteoporosis

Persistent disease activity, premature menopause caused by use of
gonadotoxic drugs, vitamin D deficiency, and use of GCs all contribute to reduced BMD in SLE.
Lifestyle modifications, such
as exercise uptake and maintaining a normal body mass index, are
important, as are smoking cessation and reduction of alcohol consumption. Vitamin D supplementation is recommended in cases
of deficiency (<10 ng/mL) or insufficiency (<20 ng/mL), to maintain 25-OH vitamin D levels greater than 30 ng/mL.
For the prevention of GC-induced osteoporosis, the ACR
guidelines for the general population include fracture risk stratification, including calculation of the FRAX score for patients over
40 years. If treatment is warranted, bisphosphonates are generally
considered the drugs of first choice.

### Malignancy in Lupus

Patients with SLE have a slightly increased risk for overall cancer
development with pooled RR of 1.18 (95% CI, 1.00 to 1.38)
from 3694 events across >80,000 patients.
Certain types of
malignancies occur more commonly in patients with SLE, in particular non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma (NHL) and cervical and lung
cancer. Immunosuppressive therapy, viral exposures, and the disease per se have been implicated for this altered risk.
NHL is associated with SLE (systemic inflammatory response
syndrome [SIRS], 3.6 to 4.3), even in pediatric-onset disease,
with diffuse large B cell lymphoma being the most common subtype. Hodgkin’s lymphoma (HL) is also more frequent (SIRS,
3.2). Accordingly, a high index of suspicion is necessary for early
detection of lymphoma, followed by aggressive investigation and
a low threshold for lymph node biopsy.
Cervical dysplasia is increased in women with SLE (pooled
OR, 8.7
) as a result of impaired clearance of HPV. Screening
cervical cytology with HPV testing is recommended for all female
patients with SLE; for women with detectable HPV DNA, subsequent screening with cervical cytology should be performed every
6 months. For the remaining patients, annual rescreening is sufficient. SLE patients under 25 years should receive vaccination
against HPV, similar to the general population. Increased risk for
lung cancer may be smaller than previously believed yet is higher
in smokers (SIRS, 1.16 to 1.30).
On the contrary, patients
with lupus seem to have a slightly reduced risk of certain nonhematologic cancers (breast, ovarian, endometrial, and prostate:
SIRS, 0.66 to 0.77).
Based on the previously mentioned, preventive strategies to minimize malignancy risk are warranted in all
lupus patients (disease control, judicious use of cytotoxic agents,
smoking cessation, and HPV vaccination).

## Evidence and Expert-Based Recommendations in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Due to the systemic nature of SLE, multiple medical specialties are
involved in the care of these patients, dictating a multidisciplinary
approach. To this end, EULAR has developed recommendations
covering the most important aspects of diagnosis, management,
and monitoring.
These recommendations are based on
a combined evidence-based approach and expert opinion consensus. Similar sets of recommendations have also been published by

the ACR and the Pan American League of Associations for Rheumatology (PANLAR).

## Current Trends and Future Perspectives

This decade has witnessed major advances in defining risk factors
and phenotypes, elucidating pathogenesis, and optimizing treatment in lupus. Studies have created novel pathogenesis and treatment paradigms that have benefited the entire field of medical
inflammation and autoimmunity. New classification criteria have
allowed the earlier recognition for diagnosis and classification for
clinical studies. Recognition of adjuvant-like factors that promote
the production of type I interferon offers new targets for therapy
in addition to those targeting B and T cells, cell metabolism, and
various populations of effector cells. Similar to multiple sclerosis
targeting of brain microglia through new drugs or drug repositioning may represent a therapeutic frontier in neuropsychiatric
disease. Despite the frustrating results of the phase 3 baricitinib
trials, the recent successful phase 2 study of deucravacitinib may
gain a momentum for kinase inhibitors in lupus. Lupus nephritis
is currently witnessing a major breakthrough, while neuropsychiatric lupus emerges as a major frontier in more recent cohorts.
Fatigue is another frontier that needs to be better documented
and treated. Exploration of the genetic and environmental factors
(especially smoking) that determine susceptibility to disease may
eventually lead to the identification of individuals at risk and elucidate the primary events that cause autoimmunity.
In contrast to monogenic diseases, the expansion of personalized medicine in lupus awaits a more complete description
of predisposition. Genome-wide, next-generation sequencing
efforts of single cells in target tissues now underway will provide
within the next few years a more comprehensive description of
the relations between genome sequence variation and clinical
phenotypes.
