## Introduction

Angiogenesis is a dynamic multistep process that contributes to
normal physiologic processes but is also a key mechanism in a number of pathologic conditions including inflammatory arthritis.
Angiogenesis is the formation of new blood vessels whereby new
capillaries sprout from existing blood vessels. The early development
of a rich vascular network facilitates leukocyte extravasation into the
joint in addition to supplying the nutrients required for the expansion of resident cells. Angiogenesis is tightly controlled by both
pro- and anti-angiogenic factors and is driven by numerous growth
factors and pro-inflammatory cytokines in the inflamed joint. Activation of endothelial cells (ECs) by proangiogenic stimuli results
in the secretion of degradative enzymes that dissolve the EC basement membrane and the extra-cellular matrix (ECM) components.
Migration of ECs through the connective tissue matrix results in
the formation of primary capillary sprouts. This is followed by EC
proliferation, migration, tip-stalk cell selection, sprout elongation,
synthesis of basement membrane, and lumen formation.

## Endothelial Cells

### Endothelial Permeability

ECs line the lumina of blood vessels; thus they separate and connect the blood and the ECM of the vessel wall. The main functions
of ECs are to contain and distribute blood, facilitate gas and fluid
exchange, regulate coagulation cascades, interact with circulating
leukocytes to facilitate inflammation, and interact with smooth
muscle cells/pericytes to regulate vascular tone. The endothelium
is involved in numerous homeostatic mechanisms and pathologic

In inflammatory states, such as arthritis, ECs interact with
other cell types including leukocytes, fibroblasts, and pericytes.
ECs express numerous cell adhesion molecules (CAMs), adhere to
ECM components, and secrete a number of inflammatory mediators, such as cytokines, nitric oxide (NO), prostaglandins, endothelin 1 (ET-1), and proteases, and thus regulate inflammation in
the surrounding tissue. ECs are also active responders to external
stimuli as they may become targets of inflammatory mediators
produced by leukocytes.
Vascular endothelium undergoes morphologic changes during the inflammatory response including vasodilatation and
increased permeability (leakage), which result from several mechanisms including EC contraction and retraction and leukocyte- or
anti-EC antibody (AECA)-mediated vascular injury and regeneration.
ECs release vasodilatory mediators including prostacyclin, NO, and platelet-activating factor (PAF).
Factors
triggering endothelial leakage include, among others, histamine,
serotonin, complement factors, bradykinin, leukotrienes, PAF,
and AECA. EC adhesion accompanied by cytoskeletal reorganization occurs upon EC activation by pro-inflammatory cytokines
such as interleukin (IL)-1, TNF, or interferon (IFN)-

### Endothelial Injury and Regeneration

In inflammation, endothelial injury is caused by inflammatory
cells and soluble mediators.
Various mediators produced by
ECs themselves and other cells lead to endothelial injury. ECs synthesize NO, prostaglandins, ET-1, and other substances.
During inflammation, ECs and leukocytes produce high amounts
of NO,
which interacts with reactive oxygen intermediates
(ROI).
EC injury also involves ROI and matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) enzymes produced by inflammatory leukocytes.
Asymmetric dimethylarginine (ADMA) is a
naturally produced amino acid that circulates in the plasma.
Increased ADMA production is associated with vascular injury
and inflammatory rheumatic diseases.
ET-1, a vasoconstrictor peptide, activates leukocyte adhesion and is involved in ECM
remodeling and vascular injury.

## Hypoxia, Cell Metabolism, and Angiogenesis

### Hypoxia

The hypothesis that the inflamed joint is hypoxic was originally proposed based on studies demonstrating increased surrogate markers
of hypoxia in the synovial cavity or synovial fluid of patients with
RA.
This was confirmed in subsequent studies using specific
oxygen probes, which demonstrated that the inflamed synovium

### Cell Metabolism

Evidence for a key role of metabolism in the regulation of synovial
inflammation has emerged, where proliferation and rapid activation of immune and stromal cells require a switch in their cell
metabolism from oxidative phosphorylation to glycolysis.
While in the context of synovial ECs very little is known, tumor
studies have demonstrated that the three EC subsets (tip, stalk,
and phalanx cells) differ in their energy, biomass, and redox
requirements.
It is now apparent that metabolic changes
in ECs dictate their phenotypic differentiation into tip, stalk, or
phalanx cells, with tip and stalk cells showing increased glycolytic
rates compared with phalanx ECs.
Indeed the primary form of
energy source required by activated ECs is glycolysis, with 85%
of the adenosine triphosphate (ATP) requirements generated by
glucose’s conversion into lactate. Coordinated communication
between tip, stalk, and phalanx cells is required for a proper
functioning vessel, with the metabolic profile of each dictating
their specific function (migration, proliferation, and quiescence,
respectively).
Both the “tip” and the “stalk” cells, which facilitate EC migration and proliferation, rely on glycolysis for energy.
However, once the blood vessel matures and proper blood flow
is established again, ECs revert back to a quiescent phalanx cell
phenotype, which utilizes oxphos
(
Fig. 28.4
).
Despite their exposure to high oxygen levels, tip and stalk cells
utilize anaerobic glycolysis as an energy source as it allows more
rapid production of ATP, in addition to the generation of biomolecules and metabolic intermediates, which are required to maintain the activated phenotype of these specific EC subtypes during
new blood vessel formation. Indeed, the metabolic by-products—
including enolase, succinate and lactate,
which are known
to be elevated in the synovial fluid of patients with inflammatory arthritis
—can further promote synovial angiogenesis to
maintain a self-perpetuating chronic disease.
In inflammatory arthritis, synovial blood vessels display
enhanced expression of the glucose transporter GLUT1, glycolytic enzymes, metabolic intermediate receptors, and components of the mitochondrial electron transport chain, NADPH
oxidase.
In particular, two glycolytic rate-limiting
enzymes—6-phosphofructo-2-kinase/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase 3 (PFKFB3) and hexokinase 2 (HK2), which regulate EC
angiogenesis—are significantly increased in synovial vascular
regions and mediate VEGF induced-sprout formation.
Indeed, cancer studies have shown that these glycolytic enzymes
are compartmentalized to the tip cell with high localized expression in the sensing filopodia, thus rapidly generating ATP for tip
cell migration.
Blockade of PFKFB3 inhibits angiogenic
tube formation, secretion of proangiogenic mediators, and key
signaling pathways in RA fibroblast-like synoviocytes and ECs.
Consistent with these studies, PFKFB3 and/or HK2 inhibition
reduces disease severity in animal models of RA, psoriasis, and
inflammatory bowel disease that are driven by pathogenic angiogenesis.
Enriched synovial vascular expression of another
metabolic enzyme, glucose-6-phosphate isomerase (G6PI), which
plays a crucial role in glycolysis and gluconeogenesis, has also
recently been shown, with in vitro G6PI loss-of-function assays
demonstrating the requirement of G6PI in mediating hypoxiainduced angiogenesis in RA.
Finally, in animal models of
arthritis the metabolic intermediate succinate induced synovial
angiogenesis through VEGF-dependent HIF-1
α
pathways.

### Single-Cell RNA Sequencing Demonstrates Differential Endothelial Cell Clusters

Advances in technologies such as single-cell RNA sequencing and
CITE (cellular indexing of transcriptomes and epitopes) sequencing for cellular profiling in tandem with metabolomics and spatial
imaging technologies will further facilitate our understanding of
EC function in relation to pathotypes and possibly response to
therapies. However, only a limited number of studies have performed single cell analysis of synovial ECs to date. Global analyses of RA and PsA synovium have revealed six EC clusters in
the inflamed joint.
Differential frequency of clusters was
demonstrated between RA and PsA, with one cluster showing
enhanced expression of key growth factors and NOTCH family
members in RA. Differences were also observed in transcriptional
factor usage, with significant differences observed for TEA domain
1, myocyte enhancer factor 2A, and FOXP1, which may be reflective of the distinct synovial vasculature observed in RA and PsA.
Another recent study focused on RA identified four main clusters
that displayed distinct genes associated with transendothelial cell
migration but also implicated a key role for NOTCH signaling.
The importance of NOTCH signaling, not only in the context of
EC function but also in the regulation of articular inflammation,
was further demonstrated in an eloquent study, where EC-derived
NOTCH was shown to be a critical receptor in dictating synovial fibroblast differentiation, phenotype, and function in the RA
joint.

## Targeting Angiogenesis

Currently, anti-inflammatory and disease-modifying anti-rheumatic agents such as rofecoxib, dexamethasone, chloroquine,
sulfasalazine, methotrexate, azathioprine, cyclophosphamide,
leflunomide, thalidomide, tacrolimus, and minocycline and antiTNF therapies all nonspecifically suppress angiogenesis.
The anti-IL-6 receptor antibody tocilizumab reduced serum levels
of VEGF
and synovial microvessel density in RA.
Another
cytokine that both directly and indirectly induces synovial angiogenesis is IL-18, blockade of which reduces secretion of proangiogenic mediators from synovial cells and inhibits disease progression
in a CIA model of arthritis.
While phase 1 clinical trials of
humanized neutralizing antibodies against IL-18pb showed dosedependent pharmacokinetics with no adverse events in moderate
to severe RA, further trials have not been performed.
Studies
have also shown that tofacitinib (a Janus kinase [JAK] inhibitor)
reduces the secretion of proangiogenic mediators in  vitro and
in  vivo.
Furthermore, JAK-STAT signaling pathways also
interact with both HIF-1
α
and NOTCH signaling,
in addition
to specific glycolytic enzymes.
Several studies have examined blocking angiogenic pathways
such as VEGF, its receptors, or the angiopoietins in models of
arthritis, both in vitro and in vivo.
Bevacizumab, an antiVEGF monoclonal antibody,
is approved for colon, kidney,
and lung cancer; however, efficacy has not been observed in RA.
A soluble Tie2 receptor transcript delivered via an adenoviral vector attenuated arthritis incidence and severity in CIA models.
A double anti-angiogenic protein (DAAP; a dimeric decoy receptor) that strongly binds to ANG1, ANG2, and VEGF had better
protective effects than VEGF-Trap or Tie2-Fc on both inflammation and bone destruction in CIA.
This study also showed
that DAAP yielded benefit when used in combination with TNF
inhibition, suggesting that coordinated blocking of angiogenic
activities by DAAP with an anti-TNF agent might represent a
promising therapeutic approach in RA.
Inhibitors against tyrosine kinases, NFκ
B signaling, mitogenactivated protein kinase–phosphoinositide 3-kinase (MAPKPI3K) signaling, and FAK signaling have all been developed.
Sunitinib, a multitargeted receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitor that
is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for treatment of specific cancers, inhibits synovial neoangiogenesis and
joint destruction in CIA models. Other tyrosine kinase inhibitors approved for cancer include pazopanib and PD166866;
however, they have not been tested in inflammatory diseases.
Recent studies have examined noncanonical NFκ
B pathways,
with studies showing that mice deficient in NFκ
B-inducing
kinase have a reduction in synovial blood vessels, paralleled by
significantly less inflammation in the adjuvant-induced arthritis model.
MAPK inhibitors including tacrolimus, the ERK
inhibitor-FR180204, and a p38 inhibitor reduce EC proliferation and migration and secretion of growth factors and inhibit
radiographic damage in animal models of arthritis.
Despite
this data, many of these inhibitors failed in RA clinical trials due
to lack of efficacy or toxic effects.
Given the role of NOTCH signaling in tip-cell selection
and filopodial protrusion, studies have also examined targeting
NOTCH signaling in addition to integrin-FAK mediated pathways. Inhibition of NOTCH signaling with
γ
-secretase inhibitors
and nanoparticle delivery significantly reduced cytokine/chemokine and growth factor expression, in addition to attenuation of
disease severity in RA animal models.
Several in vitro studies
have shown that FAK inhibitors (Pf-573,228 and FAK inhibitor-14) decrease VEGF-induced EC migration and angiogenic
tube formation. Several FAK inhibitors, including defactinib,
TAE226, PF-562,271, and GSK225-6098, have been developed
; however, these peptides have yet to be tested in clinical
trials. Vitaxin (MEDI-522), a humanized monoclonal IgG1 antibody that specifically binds to the
α
v
β
3 integrin that is expressed
on synovial blood vessels at an early stage of disease, inhibits synovial angiogenesis and disease severity in animal models of arthritis.
These pre-clinical studies led to a phase II clinical trial in
RA; however, its efficacy was limited.
As highlighted earlier, chemokines play a key role in proangiogenic mechanisms in inflammatory arthritis, with many studies focusing on the CXCL12–CXCR4/CXCR7 signaling axis.
Monoclonal antibodies or antagonists against CXCL12, CXCR4,
or CXCR7 have demonstrated significant reduction in blood vessel numbers, T cell infiltration, and joint inflammation in various
animal models of arthritis.
Neutralization of MIF suppresses
synovial vascularization and joint swelling in experimental arthritis.
Milatuzumab (an anti-CD74 monoclonal antibody), which
targets CD74, a part of the MIF receptor, is currently in phase I
clinical trials for the treatment of systemic lupus erythematosus.
While several pharmacologic inhibitors of this axis are in phase I
and II clinical trials for different cancers, they have not yet been
tested in RA.
Finally, metabolic pathways have been implicated in disease
pathogenesis. In particular, PFKFB3 and HK2 inhibition reduced
angiogenic mediators in synovial cells in vitro/ex vivo, in addition
to reducing severity of disease in animal models of arthritis.
Furthermore, targeting metabolic intermediates such as succinate
is also a promising therapeutic avenue, where their cellular accumulation regulates synovial angiogenesis and synovial fibroblast
invasiveness.

## Conclusion

In summary, angiogenesis is one of the primary events in synovial
pathogenesis, activation of which facilitates leukocyte infiltration,
leading to pannus expansion and joint invasion. Many therapeutic
approaches have successfully resolved arthritic joint vascularization
in pre-clinical models; however, this has not translated successfully
to the clinic. It is likely that novel strategies that target multiple
pathways or combining anti-angiogenic therapies with current treatments may be utilized in future inflammatory arthritis therapies.
