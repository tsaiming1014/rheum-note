## Introduction

Spondyloarthritis (SpA) is an umbrella term referring to a heterogeneous group of related, yet distinct, inflammatory disorders:
psoriatic arthritis (PsA), reactive arthritis (ReA), arthritis associated with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), and ankylosing
spondylitis (AS).
These inflammatory conditions share similar
clinical characteristics, genetic features, and pathophysiological
mechanisms and can cluster among families and even within the
same individual.
The shared clinical features include involvement
of the spine, peripheral arthritis (typically asymmetric oligoarthritis), enthesitis, and an over-representation of three specific extramusculoskeletal manifestations: acute anterior uveitis, psoriasis,
or IBD. The relationship among these conditions was further
strengthened on the discovery of a shared association, of varying degrees, with human leukocyte antigen-B*27 (HLA-B*27).
Experimental models supported this relationship, with HLAB*27 transgenic rats developing clinical features seen in humans
with SpA.
This chapter outlines how classification criteria in SpA
evolved and discusses the current epidemiologic data.

## Classification Criteria

The purpose of classification criteria is to provide a standardized
definition of a condition, with the primary intention of creating well-defined homogeneous cohorts of affected individuals,
which will allow clinical research to be performed.
Such criteria
should only be applied in scenarios where there is already an
established diagnosis.
They are not intended to capture
all
possible manifestations of a disease but rather the majority. This
allows interpretation of findings and comparison between studies examining similar populations.
By their nature, classification criteria are designed to be highly specific in order to limit
the number of people without the disease who are wrongly classified as positive (i.e., false positives). The drawback of classification criteria is that with a clear yes or no result, there will
invariably be individuals with the disease who do not fulfill the
classification criteria.
Therefore classification criteria can limit
the extrapolation of the results of studies to real-life individuals with the diagnosis, as affected individuals may not share all
aspects of the disease.
In contrast, diagnostic criteria are intended to be used to
guide the care of individual patients.
They provide clinicians
with signs, symptoms, and diagnostic tests that can be used in
a clinical setting to arrive at a diagnosis with a certain level of
probability.
If all patients with the disease are to be captured,
they must be sufficiently broad, reflecting the variations that can
present with all diseases. They are also more flexible, as they
take negative findings and expert opinion into consideration.
Rheumatology is faced with unique challenges in that many
rheumatic conditions share features with other nonrheumatic
conditions. Therefore diagnostic criteria can be challenging to
develop, and as a result there are very few validated diagnostic
criteria in rheumatology. Due to the difficulties associated with
validating diagnostic criteria, the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) no longer endorses these criteria.
More commonly, a physician’s clinical acumen is employed to establish a
diagnosis based on the interpretation of combinations of signs,
symptoms, available diagnostic results, and current knowledge
of the prevalence and epidemiology of conditions.
However,
in reality diagnostic and classification criteria represent opposite
ends of a continuum.

### Moll and Wright Criteria: Seronegative Spondarthritides

The term
seronegative spondarthritides
was introduced in 1974
by one group to identify a group of nonrheumatoid diseases that
shared clinical and familial features, with the prefix
sponddenoting an association between each included condition and AS.
Criteria to be included in Moll’s “seronegative spondarthritis”
group included being negative for rheumatoid factor, absence of
rheumatoid nodules, inflammatory peripheral arthritis, sacroiliitis, presence of two or more specific clinical features, and familial
aggregation.
Knowledge and terminology have since evolved and
spondyloarthritis is now the preferred term to describe this group
of conditions.

### Ankylosing Spondylitis: The Prototypic Spondyloarthritis Condition

AS was the prototypic SpA condition and has several eponyms,
including Bechterew’s disease and Marie-Strumpell’s disease.
Pathologic changes of AS in skeletal remains have been described as far
back as the Egyptian era,
although it is likely that some of the skeletal changes reported as AS were more degenerative in nature. The first
family study of AS was that of the Medicis, a famous Florentine family, and took place when their bodies were removed from the Medici
chapel in 1945. At least two male members of this family, spanning
the years 1389 to 1516, almost certainly had AS.
Irish physician
Bernard Connor gave the first clear description of AS in 1693, which
was based on a skeleton with the disease: “all the bones
…
united to
make but one bone without articulation.”
There was an early
belief in the United States that AS represented a variation of rheumatoid arthritis (RA).
However, in Europe, the opinion was that AS
represented a disease entity independent to RA, a belief subsequently
confirmed with the discovery of rheumatoid factor.

### Modified New York Criteria

In 1960, the first attempt at classification criteria for AS was made,
following meetings at the National Institutes of Health in the
United States and in Rome, Italy; these were subsequently amended
at a third symposium in New York in 1966, removing thoracic pain
because of low specificity and uveitis because of low sensitivity, culminating in the New York criteria.
They were further amended
in 1984, incorporating inflammatory back pain (IBP) instead of
low back pain (LBP) as a clinical feature, and were referred to as
the modified New York (mNY) criteria.
To satisfy these criteria,
individuals were required to fulfill radiologic criteria (bilateral grade
2–4
or
unilateral grade 3–4 sacroiliitis on plain radiographs of the
pelvis), in addition to at least one clinical criteria (

### Assessment of SpondyloArthritis Society Criteria

Although the ESSG and Amor criteria were an improvement
on the mNY criteria, they did not capture all forms of disease,
particularly those with IBP but no radiograph changes. It was
increasingly recognized that in many adults with AS, radiographic
sacroiliitis could take many years to develop from the onset of
IBP. The introduction of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in
the assessment of AS confirmed that individuals with IBP symptoms but without radiograph changes were experiencing inflammation in the sacroiliac (SI) joints and/or spine.
In addition, the
presence of inflammation of the SI joints on MRI at baseline was
associated with 5-year SI joint damage.
An examination of 416
individuals from the DESIR (acronym in French for recent-onset
SpA) cohort demonstrated only 5% progressed from nonradiographic to radiographic sacroiliitis.
One group demonstrated
no overall progression of radiographic sacroiliitis over 6 years in
an early SpA cohort,
and another group showed a net progression of only 1.9% to sacroiliitis by mNY criteria over 4.4 years of
follow-up.
Furthermore, it was recognized that the presence or
absence of radiographic sacroiliitis did not primarily dictate burden of disease and quality of life.
It was therefore concluded that
the presence and absence of radiographic sacroiliitis represented
a continuum of a single disease, rather than two separate disease
entities, and that individuals with predominantly axial symptoms
but no radiographic changes of the SI joints should be regarded as
the same disease entity as AS individuals.
Thus the Assessment
of SpondyloArthritis Society (ASAS) developed new classification
criteria to account for this, with two tracks depending on whether
the prominent symptom is axial or peripheral.

Peripheral Spondyloarthritis
ASAS criteria for peripheral SpA (pSpA) were developed in 2011
to account for individuals with predominant peripheral manifestations (
Fig. 75.4
). Entry requirements for these criteria are the
presence of peripheral manifestations only: peripheral arthritis compatible with SpA, and/or enthesitis, and/or dactylitis. Additional
SpA features are also required, divided into two separate sets, with
individuals required to have one of set A or two of set B to fulfill the
criteria (see
Fig. 75.4
). The arthritis in these criteria typically refers
to predominantly lower limb and/or asymmetric arthritis.
In contrast to the axSpA criteria, an entry age cutoff was not
applied to these criteria, as it was felt to not be as relevant in pSpA
as axSpA. Sensitivity of these criteria was 77.8% and specificity
was 82.2%. When combined with the axSpA criteria, sensitivity
was 79.5%, and specificity was 83.3%. When the ASAS criteria
were first published, there was strict separation between the axSpA
and pSpA sets of criteria. Individuals could not have current back
pain; if this was present, the axSpA criteria were instead applied.

### Undifferentiated Spondyloarthritis

Prior to the publication of the new ASAS classification criteria,
individuals who had symptoms and/or signs of AS but did not
fulfill the criteria were classified as undifferentiated spondyloarthritis (uSpA).
Often over time, the eventual diagnosis of these
individuals would become clear. However, as the ASAS criteria
have been more widely adopted, the term uSpA has been phased
out, as almost all individuals with SpA can instead be classified as
either axSpA or pSpA.

### Psoriatic Arthritis

PsA was included as a separate disease entity in classification of
rheumatic disease for the first time in 1964,
under the heading
“polyarthritis of unknown etiology.” Prior to that, if an individual had both inflammatory arthritis and psoriasis, it was thought
to represent RA with coincidental psoriasis. Moll and Wright
introduced the original definition for PsA: presence of psoriasis,
presence of inflammatory arthritis (peripheral arthritis and/or

### Overlap Between Peripheral Spondyloarthritis and Other Subtypes, Particularly Psoriatic Arthritis

The application of pSpA classification criteria is limited by lack of
data exploring their utility, the absence of biomarkers for pSpA
and overlap with other SpA subtypes.
Indeed, pSpA is often
used interchangeably with ReA, PsA, and uSpA.
There is a particular overlap between pSpA and PsA, especially when skin psoriasis is absent in PsA.
However, there are some key differences,
whereby pSpA has a male predominance, a higher prevalence of
HLA-B*27, more enthesitis but less dactylitis, and involvement of
the large joints of the lower limbs compared with those with PsA
without psoriasis.
Further research is required to better characterize pSpA as a disease.

### Reactive Arthritis

ReA is an inflammatory arthritis associated predominantly with
certain infections of gastrointestinal or genitourinary origin (see
Chapter 77
). Several attempts have been made to develop diagnostic or classification criteria for ReA, but none have yet been validated. The earliest known attempt at defining the disease occurred
in 1979,
when the American Rheumatism Association (ARA)
Diagnostic and Therapeutic Committee analyzed 249 case reports
from seven centers, consisting of 83 typical patients and 166 disease controls with seronegative arthritis. They concluded that the
presence of peripheral arthritis with urethritis and/or cervicitis
could be considered clinically acceptable, with a characteristic episode lasting more than 1 month. Specificity of these criteria in this
cohort was 98.2% and sensitivity was 84.3%. Adding eye involvement, HLA-B*27 positivity or other minor criteria (conjunctivitis,
balanitis, mucous membrane, heel pain, fever, weight loss) did not
improve performance of this definition. At that time, the condition
was referred to as Reiter’s syndrome. However, that eponym was
associated with controversy, when it came to light that the German
physician associated with the original description of the condition,
Hans Reiter, was a leader of the Nazi party in World War II,
leading to the retraction of the term
Reiter’s syndrome
.
The term
reactive arthritis
was instead adopted and is now preferred.

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease–Related Spondyloarthritis

IBD describes a chronic relapsing-remitting inflammatory condition of the bowel and includes Crohn’s disease and ulcerative
colitis.
It is well recognized that there is a link between IBD and
arthritis and, more specifically, SpA. In 1964 the ARA included
ulcerative colitis and regional ileitis as diseases with which arthritis is frequently associated.
By 1974, Moll and Wright included
arthritis associated with IBD as one of the SpA subgroups.
However, formal classification criteria for IBD-related SpA don’t yet
exist. Indeed, the spectrum of SpA phenotypes associated with

IBD are varied and include peripheral and axial disease.
IBD
exists as a feature of SpA in the ASAS criteria.
It has long
been recognized that both peripheral arthritis and axial disease
can occur in individuals with IBD-SpA. The Oxford IBD clinic
in 1998 studied individuals attending their clinic with peripheral
joint symptoms only (i.e., excluding those with axial disease) and
proposed classifying individuals with IBD and peripheral arthritis
into two types.
This classification, however, has not been validated prospectively and has fallen out of favor. In the absence of a
specific IBD-associated SpA classification system, the ASAS criteria (axial and peripheral)
are often used. However, these criteria have not been assessed in enough detail in IBD-SpA to know if
they accurately capture the spectrum of the disease.

## Epidemiology of Spondyloarthritis

In 2016, one group systematically summarized the global prevalence of SpA and its subtypes, reporting a worldwide prevalence
of 0.55% (0.37 to 0.77), with the lowest prevalence in South East
Asia (pooled prevalence 0.20%, 95% CI 0.00 to 0.66) and the
highest in the northern Arctic communities (pooled prevalence
1.61%, 95% CI 1.27 to 2.00).
The prevalence of SpA in Europe
was 0.54% (95% confidence interval [CI] 0.36 to 0.78) and
North America 1.35% (95% CI 0.44 to 2.79). The epidemiology
of each subtype of SpA is varied, therefore the remainder of this
section examines each type of SpA separately.

### Axial Spondyloarthritis

Age of Presentation
axSpA typically presents in the second or third decade of life, and
almost exclusively before age 45.
A recent study of 2579 axSpA
patients reported a median age of 26 years at onset of symptoms,
with 92% reporting an age of onset prior to 45 years.
AxSpA
presents earlier in individuals who are HLA-B*27 positive by
approximately 5 years.
Delay to diagnosis of axSpA is a feature
of the disease, with a mean delay of 6.7 years
and median of 2
to 6 years
reported in separate systematic reviews. The delay to
diagnosis has been shown to be longer in women than in men: 8.8
versus 6.5 years.
Lower education levels, younger age at onset
of symptoms, and absence of extramusculoskeletal manifestations
are associated with a longer delay to diagnosis.
Prevalence
Variation in the methodology of studies used to estimate the
prevalence and incidence of axSpA, particularly the change in
classification criteria that occurred in 2009, has led to difficulties
in accurately establishing epidemiologic estimates for axSpA.
The global prevalence of axSpA was estimated as ranging from
0.36% to 0.70% in a systematic review in 2016,
although this
was based on point estimates from only two studies: one from
France and one from the United States. The pooled prevalence of
AS ranged from 0.20% in North America (95% CI 0.10 to 0.34)
to 0.25% in Europe (95% CI 0.18 to 0.33). A systematic review
of population-based studies estimated the prevalence of AS at
23.8 cases per 10,000 in Europe.
A more recent study from
England demonstrated an annual rise in the prevalence of axSpA
until it peaked in 2020 at 0.113% (95% CI 0.773, 0.784).
A
2018 Japanese study reported a very low prevalence: 0.0026%
for AS and 0.0006% for nr-axSpA.
It is feasible that the prevalence figures reported in these reviews underrepresented the
true prevalence figures, due to a lack of true general population
studies and continued underrecognition and underdiagnosis of
axSpA.
Human Leukocyte Antigen-B*27
The wide variation in the global prevalence of axSpA is at least
partly explained by differences in background HLA-B*27 prevalence.
HLA-B*27 is a class I surface antigen encoded by the
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) and is found in 74%
to 89% of individuals with axSpA.
The prevalence of HLAB*27 is highest in the Pawaia tribe in Papua New Guinea (53%)
and the Haida indigenous Americans on Queen Charlotte Islands
in Western Canada (50%) and lowest in Arab countries (2%–
5%) and Japan (1%).
In Europe, HLA-B*27 varies from 4%
to 13% in the west and 15% to 25% in northern Scandinavia (Norway, Sweden, and Finland), with approximately 90%
of individuals with AS HLA-B*27 positive.
HLA-B*27
is exceedingly rare in Japan and Arab countries
and almost
nonexistent in some populations such as the indigenous tribes
of South America.
In Black populations, HLA-B*27 occurs in
less than 60% of cases of AS,
and AS is almost nonexistent in
South American Indians and Australian Aborigines.
The association between HLA-B*27 and AS is weaker in the Middle East
than in Europe.
Sex Differences
Historically, AS has been considered to have a male predominance, with striking male to female ratios of up 10:1 reported in
early studies.
More recently, that ratio has been revised down,
closer to 3:1.
In contrast, the distribution of nr-axSpA is virtually identical between males and females.
Axial symptoms
typically begin earlier in men compared with women (25 vs. 28
years,
p
< 0.001), with definite regional differences; the biggest
gender difference was noticed in Asia (age 23 in men vs. age 28 in
women) and no significant gender differences in age of onset were
noted in Europe or North America.
A systematic review and
meta-analysis published in 2017 examined the diagnostic delay in
SpA and found a mean of 8.8 (7.4–10.1) years for females and 6.5
(5.6–7.4) years for males, a difference that was statistically significant.
This may relate to the differences in presenting symptoms,
with men more likely to report typical IBP symptoms and women
more likely to report widespread pain, which made a delayed diagnosis twice as likely.
Other Genetics of Ankylosing Spondylitis
Most pathogenesis studies to date have focused on AS rather than
axSpA. Studies performed on monozygotic twins and familial
aggregation studies suggest that AS has a heritability of above
90%.
As already outlined, HLA-B*27 positivity is strongly
linked with AS, occurring in only about 5% of the general population but up to 90% of individuals with AS. However, despite
this strong association, HLA-B*27 contributes only 33% of the
total heritability of AS.
Genome-wide association studies based on single-nucleotide
polymorphisms have identified over 100 genetic variants associ
ated with AS.
Twenty percent of the genetic heritability
was explained by MHC variants, which includes HLA-B*27,
and 7% by non-MHC variants.
Two other genetic loci have
been identified as of potential interest: endoplasmic reticulum
aminopeptidase (ERAP) and the interleukin (IL)-23 receptor.
ERAP has a role in preparing peptides before MHC Class I
presentation to immune effector cells.
Also implicated are
genes that affect the IL-23 receptor that cause activation of the

T-helper cells, which leads to the secretion of IL-17 and other
pro-inflammatory cells.

### Psoriatic Arthritis

Prevalence and Incidence
The prevalence and incidence of both PsA and psoriasis vary globally. A systematic review in 2018 reported a global estimate of the
pooled prevalence of PsA as 0.13%.
There was significant heterogeneity between the studies, in part due to differences in how PsA
was defined between studies. When limited to PsA by CASPAR
criteria only, the prevalence was 0.12%.
In an English-based
study, the prevalence of PsA increased every year from 2004 to
2019, peaking at 0.287% (95% CI 0.284, 0.291).
The incidence
of PsA in that same systematic review was 8.3 per 100,000 patient
years.
The highest prevalence rates of PsA are found in North
America (158 every 100,000 subjects) and northern Europe (670
per 100,000 subjects), whereas much lower prevalence rates are
reported in Asia (38 per 100,000 subjects) and southern Europe
(28 per 100,000 subjects).
Although the prevalence of PsA in the general population is
low, it is much higher in those with existing psoriasis. A systematic review analyzing 266 studies reported the prevalence of PsA
in those with psoriasis as 19.7%, with significant global variation,
ranging from 22.7% in Europe to 14.0% in Asia.
Approximately 10% to 15% of individuals develop PsA prior to the onset
of psoriasis.
However, the majority of cases of PsA occur about
a decade after the onset of psoriasis.
Early-onset psoriasis (type
1) refers to an onset of disease before the age of 40 years, and lateonset psoriasis (type 2) occurs after the age of 40 years, with peak
age of onset between 57 and 60 years; there is no clear consensus
on a similar stratification for PsA.
There is mounting evidence
that the peak age of onset is shifting to an older age than was previously found.
PsA is thought to affect men and women almost equally.
However, there may be a sex effect in the expression of the disease.
Women tend to develop psoriasis earlier than men
; unfortunately, there is less literature available regarding the sex differences
in PsA.
Genetics and Psoriatic Arthritis
A positive family history of psoriasis or PsA is strongly associated
with developing PsA. An individual’s risk of developing PsA is 30
to 55 times higher if a first-degree relative is affected.
Many different genes have been implicated in the pathogenesis of PsA, but
the strongest link has been found between HLA and PsA. Approximately 20% of individuals with PsA are HLA-B*27 positive.
HLA-B*39 and B*07 have also been associated with PsA.
HLAB*27 is more common in those with axial disease, whereas HLAB*38 is more frequently found in those with peripheral arthritis.
The association between HLA-B*27 and PsA is weaker than that
in axSpA.
A strong association has been found between psoriasis and
the MHC region, in particular with the HLA-C*06 allele.
Although this gene is also increased in patients with PsA compared with the general population, the association is less strong
than for psoriasis. The presence of the HLA-C*06 allele is associated with a later onset of disease in both psoriasis and PsA.
The presence of HLA-B*27, HLA-B*08, and HLA-B*38 is all
associated with an increased risk of developing PsA in those with
psoriasis.

### Reactive Arthritis

ReA tends to affect individuals between the ages of 20 and 40
years.
ReA typically has onset 1 to 3 weeks after an extra-articular
infection. Microbes from the genitourinary tract
(Chlamydia trachomatis, Neisseria gonorrhea, Mycoplasma genitalium, Ureaplasma
urealyticum)
and gastrointestinal tract (
Yersinia, Salmonella, Campylobacter,
and
Shigella
species) are the most common causes of
triggering ReA.
Both sexes are equally involved when ReA is
secondary to gastrointestinal infections.
The prevalence of ReA
secondary to
Chlamydia trachomatis
, the most common organism
to trigger genitourinary ReA, occurs more commonly in men.
The global prevalence of ReA is estimated to be 1/1000.
However, there is significant global variation. A systematic review
analyzed 17 studies and reported the prevalence of ReA to range
from 0.03 to 0.21% in Europe. No cases were reported in a
single study from India and the prevalence was only 0.001% in
Zimbabwe. In contrast, the prevalence was much higher among
northern Arctic indigenous people, ranging from 0.25% to 1%.
No studies from North America were included in this systematic
review.
The incidence of ReA is estimated to range from 4.6 to
13 per 100,000 for genitourinary ReA and 5 to 14 per 100,000
for gastrointestinal ReA.
There is a perception that ReA may be
decreasing in frequency and severity, based on a study characterizing Canadian rheumatologists’ perspectives.
The variations in global prevalence of ReA may be at least partially explained by the differing prevalence of HLA-B*27. There
is a strong association between ReA and HLA-B*27, with the
prevalence of ReA in HLA-B*27-positive individuals reportedly
5 to 10 times higher than the general population.
A recent
study reported a HLA-B*27 positivity rate of 81.8% in those with
ReA.
HLA-B*27 may have an influence on the phenotype of
the disease, with positive individuals displaying more axial symptoms and those negative for HLA-B*27 more likely to display
peripheral symptoms.
Individuals with HLA-B*27 positivity
are also at higher risk for progression to persistent SpA.

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease-Associated Spondyloarthritis

The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey
(NHANES) estimated the prevalence of IBD to be 1.3% in
2015.
A systematic review estimated the prevalence of IBDSpA from four studies, all European based, to range from 0.0% to
0.09%.
The prevalence of axSpA in IBD is estimated to be 5%
and of peripheral disease to be 16%.
Peripheral arthritis is the
most frequent manifestation of SpA in those with IBD (pooled
prevalence 13%, 95% CI 12%–15%), followed by sacroiliitis
(pooled prevalence 10%, 95% CI 8%–12%) and AS (pooled
prevalence 3%, 95% CI 2%–4%).

### Peripheral Spondyloarthritis

There is scant data available regarding the epidemiology of pSpA.
Limited data investigating the prevalence and incidence of pSpA
suggest a prevalence between 22.8% and 28.5% in those with
SpA.
The ASAS-Peripheral involvement in SpA (PerSpA) study
aimed to characterize the peripheral musculoskeletal manifestations of individuals with subtypes of SpA.
It found that pSpA
was least prevalent in Latin America (8.1%) and most prevalent in
the Middle East and North Africa (36.5%). An ancillary analysis

of the PerSpA study aimed to better characterize pSpA.
Of the
4185 patients included in this ancillary analysis, 5.3% had a pure
pSpA classification (i.e., fulfilled only ASAS pSpA criteria), and
6.4% had a pure diagnosis of pSpA (i.e., diagnosed with pSpA
exclusively according to the treating rheumatologist). When pSpA
was considered as the main disease, it was diagnosed in 10.3% of
individuals. Of those with a combined form, the majority (65.5%)
had an overlap with axSpA, rather than PsA (46.5%).
