## Introduction

Vasculitis affecting the central nervous system (CNS) most commonly occurs as a manifestation of a primary systemic vasculitis or
as a secondary vasculitis in settings such as connective tissue diseases or infections. When the disease is confined only to the CNS
(the brain, meninges, and spinal cord), it is referred to as
CNS vasculitis
(CNSV). Different nomenclatures have been used in the literature to describe CNSV including primary angiitis of the CNS
(PACNS), granulomatous angiitis of the CNS (GACNS), and
isolated CNSV. The 2012 revised International Chapel Hill Consensus Conference nomenclature of systemic vasculitides
defined
CNSV as isolated organ vasculitis when CNSV is not a component of a systemic inflammatory disease or an infectious process.
For the purpose of this chapter, we will use the term CNSV to
reflect primary CNSV.
CNSV is a rare disease that was first reported as a distinct clinical pathologic entity in 1959.
The disease initially was described
as fatal “noninfectious granulomatous angiitis with a predilection
for the nervous system.” Other reports of similar clinicopathologic
phenotypes emerged in the literature, and “granulomatous angiitis of the central nervous system” was proposed to describe this
entity.
Different terms subsequently surfaced in the literature,
such as
isolated angiitis of the CNS
to encompass cases that were
characterized by nongranulomatous pathologic findings.
Currently, CNSV is a well-accepted term for this disease that emphasizes the sole involvement of the CNS.
After the description of
diagnostic criteria for CNSV proposed by Calabrese and Mallek
in 1988
and the potential for effective treatment,
published
cases in the literature increased tremendously, and more than 500
cases have now been described worldwide.

## Epidemiology

Because of its rarity and our evolving understanding of this disease,
the true incidence of CNSV is difficult to calculate.
In the recent
era, the estimated annual incidence rate of CNSV is 2.4 cases/1
million person-years.
Middle-aged men are predominantly
affected by CNSV, with a median age at onset of approximately
50 years and a male-to-female ratio of approximately 2:1.

## Genetics

The pathogenesis of CNSV is not well understood. To date, no
evidence suggests a genetic predisposition, although this possibility remains under active investigation.

## Clinical Features

KEY POINTS

> CNSV is a heterogeneous disease with different clinical and pathologic

> findings.

> Clinical features typically include chronic insidious headaches along

> with diffuse and focal neurologic deficits.

> CNSV should be suspected in patients with typical clinical features

> including multiple CNS ischemic events in different vascular territories

> and vasculopathy without traditional cardiovascular risk factors

Great progress has been made toward understanding the clinical
features of CNSV despite the many challenges, which include the
lack of highly specific diagnostic modalities, the sparse material
for research, and the lack of controlled clinical trials. In the recent

era, specific clinical and pathologic subsets of CNSV have been
identified that have prognostic implications.
The clinical presenting features in CNSV are varied but most
commonly include a subacute onset of insidious headache, cognitive impairment, seizures, and diffuse and focal neurologic
deficits.
Due to the insidious nature of the symptoms, the
diagnosis is often delayed,
and low index of suspicion is needed
to consider and evaluate for this condition in the acute setting.
Less commonly, a subset of patients could present acutely with
symptoms related to the mass effect in patient who have masslike (ML) presentation. Some of the less common presenting features include cranial neuropathies, new onset refractory epilepsy,
and anterior visual pathway involvement.
Because the disease is confined to the brain, meninges, or spinal cord, signs and
symptoms of systemic inflammatory diseases are usually lacking.
CNSV should be suspected in patients with typical clinical features including multiple CNS ischemic events in different vascular
territories and vasculopathy without traditional risk factors.

## CNSV Subsets

### Pathologically Defined Central Nervous System Vasculitis

The original pathologic reports of CNSV as a granulomatous
angiitis imposed a histologic-based nomenclature on the disease
that led to the original name
granulomatous angiitis of the CNS
(GACNS). Until the 1980s, CNSV was largely considered a
homogeneous entity with a uniform clinical picture and a grave
prognosis. This paradigm was challenged with the introduction
of direct vascular imaging as a diagnostic tool and the recognition
that nongranulomatous pathologic findings occur in CNSV.
Initial attempts at subclassification of CNSV described three
broad subsets: GACNS, benign angiopathy of the CNS (BACNS),
and “atypical” CNSV.
It has since become recognized that
BACNS is part of the reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome
(RCVS),
with the other CNSV subsets now being defined by
mode of diagnosis, by either pathologic or radiographic features.
Further, other broad categories of CNSV have emerged in the literature that are based on the affected vessel size; these are referred to
as the small vessel variant (SVV) and the large/medium vessel variant (LMVV). Imaging and histology play important roles in distinguishing these variants. In one study, LMVV had strong/concentric
vessel wall enhancement on vessel wall MR imaging (VW-MR),
while meningeal/parenchymal contrast enhancement was more frequently observed in the SVV group. In comparison, clinical symptoms and outcomes did not show significant differences between
the LMVV and the SVV groups, and although relapse was relatively
higher in the LMVV group than in the SVV group, this did not
meet statistical significance. The majority of SVV was diagnosed
by brain biopsy (SVV: 78.3% vs. LMVV: 30.8%, P = 0.022), with
biopsy having a diagnostic accuracy of 100 % (18 of 18) in SVV
and 57.1% (4 of 7) in LMVV, respectively (P = 0.015).
Three overarching patterns of destructive vasculitis can be
observed in pathologic studies in CNSV
: (1) granulomatous
vasculitis characterized by vasculocentric mononuclear inflammation associated with well-formed granulomas and multinucleated
giant cells, with granuloma seen throughout the vascular wall, typical of GANCS; (2) purely lymphocytic pattern characterized by
predominantly lymphocytic inflammation with occasional plasma
cells, extending through the vascular wall with features of vascular
distortion and destruction; and (3) necrotizing vasculitis, which is
the least frequently seen pattern, characterized by acute necrotizing vasculitis, similar to the pattern seen in polyarteritis nodosa,
with transmural fibrinoid necrosis. Intracranial hemorrhage rarely
occurred among these patients. Deposition of amyloidβ
peptide
can occur in granulomatous and necrotizing pathologic patterns,
and when present, the diagnosis of amyloidβ
–related angiitis
(ABRA) is confirmed. Granulomatous, necrotizing vasculitis
pathologic patterns and ABRA carry a poor prognosis.
GACNS is diagnosed by the pathologic findings of granulomatous angiitis (
Fig. 93.1
). Typically the CSF findings include those
of an aseptic meningitis picture with negative staining for microorganisms. GACNS predominantly affects middle-aged men. The
most common findings on neuroimaging include infarcts, most
often bilateral, as well as high-intensity T2-weighted fluid attenuation inversion recovery (FLAIR) lesions on magnetic resonance
imaging (MRI) in the subcortical white matter and deep gray matter. A cerebral angiogram is not the diagnostic modality of choice,
given its poor spatial resolution in the detection of small vessel
vasculitis, which mainly occurs in GACNS.
ABRA is a rare form of CNSV with overlapping features of
cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) and granulomatous or necrotizing pattern vasculitis on the brain pathology. Amyloidβ
peptide, a fragment of the amyloid precursor peptide, deposits in the
brain, causing disease ranging from Alzheimer’s disease to CAA,
CAA-related inflammation, and ABRA.
Patients with ABRA
tend to be older and more prone to hallucinations and mental
status changes than are other patients with CNSV. MRI cannot
distinguish between ABRA and other forms of CNSV, although
ABRA has a higher incidence of cerebral hemorrhage. ABRA carries a poor outcome, which could be related to older age, recurrent
cerebral bleeding, and comorbidities.
Pathologic findings of lymphocytic infiltration rather than
granulomatous features should prompt thorough evaluation for
secondary causes of CNSV, such as infectious or lymphoproliferative diseases. Adequate staining and immunophenotyping should
always be carefully carried out in this setting.

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests

KEY POINTS

> Spinal fluid, brain and vascular imaging, and brain biopsy are central to

> the diagnosis of CNSV and the process of ruling out other diseases.

> Abnormal spinal fluid is found in 80% to 90% of patients with CNSV.

> Abnormalities on a cerebral angiogram are not specific for CNSV and

> can be seen in a wide range of other settings.

> RCVS is an important mimic of CNSV that is characterized by

> thunderclap headaches, normal CSF, and abnormal findings of a

> cerebral angiogram in which the changes resolve within 12 weeks.

> Other important entities in the differential diagnosis of CNSV include

> infection, lymphoproliferative disease, primary systemic vasculitis,

> connective tissue disease, genetic diseases, and thromboembolic

> disease.

The diagnosis of CNSV is challenging because of the nonspecific clinical presentation, the lack of highly specific laboratory
and radiologic tests, and the difficulty of obtaining pathologic
material.

### Proposed Diagnostic Criteria for Central Nervous System Vasculitis

In 1988, Calabrese and Mallek
proposed diagnostic criteria for
CNSV that emphasized the importance of ruling out mimics
when diagnosing CNSV. These criteria include (1) the presence
of an unexplained neurologic deficit after thorough clinical and
laboratory evaluation, (2) documentation by cerebral angiography
and/or tissue examination of an arteritic process within the CNS,
and (3) no evidence of systemic vasculitides or any other condition to which the angiographic or pathologic features could be
secondary.
In 2009, other investigators
proposed changes to the criteria
described by one study, incorporating levels of diagnostic certainty
in their assessment. They proposed use of the term
definite diagnosis of CNSV
when tissue biopsy findings provide confirmation
of vasculitis and a
probable diagnosis of CNSV
when the diagnosis is based on high-probability findings on an angiogram in the
absence of tissue confirmation but with consideration of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) profiles and neurologic symptoms to discriminate between CNSV and its mimics.
Although the original criteria by Calabrese and Mallek
served
as a platform of literature-based research, their interpretation has
changed fundamentally as advancement of our diagnostic modalities has revealed many unexplained neurologic diseases. The
importance of ruling out mimics of CNSV remains essential in
the diagnostic approach of patients with suspected CNSV.

### Diagnostic Tests

Laboratory Findings
Elevation of acute-phase reactants, anemia, and thrombocytosis
are not typical of CNSV, and if present, they should raise the possibility of a primary systemic vasculitis or another underlying disease. Laboratory testing to rule out connective tissue diseases and
thromboembolic abnormalities should be performed. Infectious
workup with appropriate cultures, serologies, and polymerase
chain reaction testing should be directed by clinical and diagnostic
findings and host risk factors.
Cerebrospinal Fluid Analysis
CSF is an important tool in the evaluation of CNSV. Although
CSF findings are nonspecific in CNSV, their value lies in ruling
out other entities. Obtaining appropriate CSF cultures, microbiologic stains, cytology, and flow cytometry is crucial in ruling out
infectious and neoplastic disease. Elevated protein levels, modest
lymphocytic pleocytosis, and occasionally oligoclonal bands and
elevated IgG synthesis characterize the CSF in 80% to 90% of
patients with pathologically documented CNSV.
The median
CSF white blood cell count is approximately 20 cells/
μ
L, and
the median CSF protein level is approximately 120 mg/dL.
Advanced CSF molecular sequencing is being investigated as an
unbiased approach to identify infectious etiologies in patients
with “idiopathic” neurologic disease.
Radiologic Evaluation
MRI is a sensitive modality for the diagnosis of CNSV, reaching 90% to 100%.
Abnormalities include infarcts in 50% of
patients, commonly affecting the cortex and the subcortex bilaterally. Affected areas include subcortical white matter, followed
by deep gray matter, deep white matter, and the cerebral cortex.
Hyperintense lesions on T2-weighted sequences are common but
not specific for CNSV.
Other abnormalities include spinal cord
presentations, leptomeningeal enhancement in 8% of cases,
and
gadolinium-enhanced intracranial lesions in about one-third of
patients.
Very rarely CNSV can present as an ML lesion occurring in
less than 5% of cases. This presentation gained attention after
the report of a series of 38 patients with histologically confirmed
CNSV who presented with a solitary cerebral mass.
Typically,
the diagnosis is unanticipated and is confirmed after pathologic
examination of either biopsy samples or surgical excision of the
mass. Unfortunately, no specific features on clinical assessment,
neuroimaging, cerebral angiography, or CSF examination can reliably distinguish ML-CNSV from other, more common causes of
a solitary cerebral mass. Use of appropriate stains and cultures to
rule out mycobacterial, fungal, or other infections and immunohistochemistry/gene rearrangement studies to exclude lymphoproliferative disease are essential to secure the diagnosis and exclude
concomitant infectious or malignant processes.
Cerebral vasculature imaging, either by DSA or through MRA
or CTA is an important modality in the diagnosis of CNSV. Alternating areas of dilation and stenosis characterize the angiographic

findings in CNSV and typically involve the vasculature on both
sides but sometimes can involve single vessels.
Other cerebrovascular features include smooth tapering of one or multiple vessels. Although cerebral angiograms may visualize abnormalities
in medium-sized vessels, they have limited sensitivity to detect
abnormalities in small vessels that are less than 500
μ
m in diameter. Although cerebral angiography is valuable, its specificity for
the diagnosis of CNSV can be as low as 25%.
The reported
“typical” angiographic findings for vasculitis are not specific to
CNSV and can be encountered in atherosclerosis, radiation vasculopathy, or vascular spasm. Moreover, a cerebral angiogram
carries a poor positive predictive value in the diagnosis of CNSV
in that the angiographic findings seen in RCVS can be consistent with those found in CNSV. VW-MR has additionally been
used to obtain information regarding the radiographic patterns
and appearance of the vessel wall in large-/medium-sized arteries.
The VW-MR protocol includes black-blood contrast-enhanced
T1-weighted sequences and a time-of-flight MRA of the circle
of Willis using a 3-Tesla MR system for a better signal-to-noise
ratio. Using VW-MR imaging, possible distinction could be made
between atherosclerotic plaques (eccentric nonuniform vessel wall
thickening and contrast enhancement) compared with infectious
or inflammatory vasculitis (usually smooth concentric uniform
vessel wall enhancement likely due to increased permeability of the
endothelium or vasa vasorum
). In one study, using VW-MR features and concentric enhancement on VW-MR imaging, CNSV
could be distinguished from other noninflammatory vasculopathies with a sensitivity of 90.5% and a specificity of 100%.
This
promising technique may have a role in following up with patients
with CNSV. In a recent study of VW-MR, approximately 50% to
75% of patients will show changes in vessel wall contrast enhancement concordant with disease activity.
Vascular studies thus
should be interpreted with caution and should not be considered
the diagnostic “gold standard” in CNSV.
Brain Biopsy
Brain biopsy is thought to carry a low morbidity and mortality in
patients with CNSV
and is an important part of the evaluation to
confirm the diagnosis and rule out mimics. When a brain biopsy
is performed, an alternative diagnosis is identified in 30% to 40%
of cases. The interpretation of the brain biopsy results should take
into consideration the potential for false-negative results because of
the patchy degree of involvement and the small amount of tissue
that often is able to be obtained. The finding of vasculitis on pathologic examination does not exclude the diagnosis of infection or
malignancy, and appropriate stains and markers should be pursued
for accurate diagnosis. The leptomeningeal vessels are more often
involved in comparison to parenchymal vessels, and including leptomeninges in the biopsy increases the diagnostic yield.

### Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstriction Syndromes

Distinguishing RCVS, the leading mimic of CNSV, is important
because it has different implications for treatment and outcome
(
Table 93.2
). RCVS consists of a group of disorders characterized
by acute-onset headaches and reversible cerebrovasoconstriction.
These disorders include Call–Fleming syndrome, drug-induced
angiopathy, migraine angiitis, BACNS, postpartum angiopathy,
and drug-induced vasospasm. The clinical features of RCVS include
acute onset of severe headaches, often thunderclap in nature, with
or without neurologic deficit, with evidence of cerebral vasoconstriction that is reversible. The headaches are usually recurrent
thunderclap headaches and can be precipitated by straining and
coughing. RCVS can be associated with strokes (39%), generalized
tonic–clonic seizures (17%), convexity subarachnoid hemorrhage
(34%), lobar hemorrhage (20%), and brain edema (38%).
RCVS
occurs more frequently in women than men. In contrast to CNSV,
findings of CSF analysis are generally normal in people with RCVS
except when a concomitant disorder or subarachnoid bleeding is
present. The diagnosis of RCVS is usually considered when cerebral
vascular imaging reveals multiple areas of smooth or tapered arterial narrowing followed by segments of normal-caliber or distended
arteries. Typically, arterial narrowing involves multiple intracerebral
arteries and their branches in both hemispheres, resulting in severe
arterial narrowing. The diagnosis of RCVS is secured when repeat
vascular imaging reveals reversibility of the cerebral vascular abnormalities, often occurring in about 6 to 12 weeks, which is in contrast to the fixed angiographic abnormalities encountered in CNSV.
Controlled trials to direct treatment of RCVS are lacking. Calcium
channel blockers are used for symptomatic treatment of the headaches, although no evidence exists that they alter the clinical outcome. Some experts prescribe glucocorticoid therapy, although no
evidence exists that it improves the outcome.

### Systemic Autoimmune Diseases

The brain is a common target in connective tissue diseases. In systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), CNS involvement occurs in
14% to 80% of adults and in 22% to 95% of children.
Multifocal
microinfarcts, cortical atrophy, gross infarcts, hemorrhage, ischemic
demyelination, and patchy multiple-sclerosis–like demyelination
are typical findings in neuropsychiatric lupus. The most common
microscopic brain finding in SLE is microvasculopathy, described
as “healed vasculitis” consistent with hyalinization, thickening, and
thrombus formation.
Rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren’s syndrome,
and mixed connective tissue disease rarely affect the CNS in a vasculitic pattern.
CNSV is typically a late occurrence in these diseases.

### Infections

Infections are one of the most important entities that should be
ruled out when considering CNSV. Vasculitis can occur in the

setting of HIV, often presenting as multifocal cerebral ischemia
with pathologic findings of angiocentric lymphoproliferative
lesions.
In addition, HIV can result in granulomatous arteritis
or can affect the brain as a result of coinfections such as syphilis.
Treponema pallidum
infection can affect vessels in the subarachnoid space and result in thrombosis, ischemia, and infarction that
can mimic CNSV.
Other infectious agents that have predilection to the brain include varicella-zoster virus (VZV). VZV affects
the brain either by involvement of large cerebral vessels, often
affecting the proximal segments of the anterior and middle cerebral arteries in immunocompetent people, or by small vessel disease in immunocompromised patients.
A history of VZV rash
is usually although not always elicited before the CNS involvement. The diagnosis of VZV angiitis is confirmed by finding VZV
DNA in CSF or by the presence of reduced serum/CSF ratios of
VZV IgG.
Neurologic involvement associated with COVID-19,
should always be considered, although the mechanism of neurologic injury in COVID-19 remains speculative.
Hepatitis C,
West Nile virus,
parvovirus B19,
and, rarely, herpes simplex
virus
can cause CNSV. Cytomegalovirus can cause CNSV as
an opportunistic infection in immunocompromised people.
Tuberculosis affecting the CNS is an important cause of CNSV in
endemic areas, and care must be taken to exclude it. Rarely, cysticercosis can involve middle-sized cerebral arteries.
A travel history and history of exposures are important features of the workup
of patients with suspected CNSV.

### Lymphoproliferative Diseases

Lymphoproliferative disease with predilection to the vascular wall,
such as lymphomatoid granulomatosis (LG), can cause CNSV,
leading to multiple small cortical infarcts and pathologic findings
of multifocal angiocentric, angiodestructive lymphoma. Concomitant HIV infection is common with LG and should be ruled out.
Rarely, LG is associated with other systemic autoimmune diseases
such as Sjögren’s syndrome. Other lymphoproliferative diseases
such as CNS lymphoma and intravascular lymphoma can mimic
CNSV.

### Miscellaneous

Atherosclerotic involvement of the intracerebral arteries is common and is often a consideration in the workup of CNSV. The
poor specificity of the cerebral angiogram poses a major limitation in differentiating inflammatory causes from other vasculopathies. However, the lack of inflammatory changes of the CSF
and the presence of multiple atherosclerotic risk factors should
raise suspicion for atherosclerotic disease. Other entities such as
antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, hypercoagulable states,
and thromboembolic causes should be carefully ruled out. The
workup of CNSV should include a transesophageal echocardiogram and a hypercoagulable profile to rule out thromboembolic
causes, especially in patients presenting with recurrent strokes.
Other rare diseases that mimic the angiographic findings of
CNSV include Moyamoya disease, small vessel arterial dissection, cerebral autosomal dominant and recessive arteriopathy
with subcortical infarcts and leukoencephalopathy (CADASIL/CARASIL), retinal vasculopathy with cerebral leukodystrophy from TREX1 gene mutations, adenosine deaminase 2
(ADA2) deficiency, radiation vasculopathy, and thromboangiitis
obliterans.

## Treatment

KEY POINTS

> No standardized trials relating to the treatment of CNSV have been

> conducted.

> Typically, GACNS is initially treated with glucocorticoids and

> cyclophosphamide.

> The treatment of CNSV is based on pathologic findings, the diagnostic

> subset, and severity of neurologic impairment.

The treatment of CNSV is guided by expert opinion and reports
of case series, as well as by extrapolation from controlled trials
relating to primary systemic vasculitis. To date, no controlled trials
have been conducted to investigate the treatment of CNSV. Thus
it is not possible to determine a specific treatment algorithm, and
therapy is based on the subtype and neurologic severity.
The report of successful treatment of GACNS with a combination of cyclophosphamide and glucocorticoids led to the regular
use of this regimen.
Generally, patients are treated with a combination of cyclophosphamide and high-dose glucocorticoids for
3 to 6 months until remission is achieved; we favor 6 months.
Cyclophosphamide is then stopped, and use of a maintenance
agent is initiated, following the same principles used in smallvessel vasculitis.
Maintenance therapy usually consists of either
azathioprine or mycophenolate mofetil and, rarely, methotrexate,
given its low CNS penetration. The duration of maintenance therapy in CNSV is not well defined.
The treatment of other forms of CNSV varies highly. Multiple factors affect the treatment regimen in atypical CNSV, and
the treatment is usually individualized, taking into consideration the degree of the neurologic deficit and the features of the
diagnosis. All forms of CNSV cases are treated with a high dose
of glucocorticoids initially, and the decision to add other immunosuppressive agents is based on each individual case. ABRA
and ML-CNSV are two CNSV subsets in which cyclophosphamide is considered at the outset of the treatment. Cyclophosphamide is the preferred agent in granulomatous angiitis and
necrotizing vasculitis pathologic pattern as these patterns carry a
poor prognosis.
Mycophenolate mofetil can be considered for
the induction of CNSV with non-necrotizing and nongranulomatous pattern on pathology with low disease burden or when
there is lack of high diagnostic confidence despite thorough
workup. Use of TNF inhibitors or rituximab in patients with
CNSV that is resistant to glucocorticoids and other immunosuppressive agents has thus far been confined to case reports that
provide insufficient evidence from which to draw conclusions
regarding efficacy.
Failure to respond to cyclophosphamide
should prompt evaluation for an alternative diagnosis before
additional treatment with an alternative immunosuppressive
agent is instituted.
Assessing disease status and defining remission are crucial steps
in the course of treatment of CNSV. Permanent deficits occurring after the initial event in CNSV should not erroneously be
considered to be lack of remission. Disease is considered in remission when stability or improvement of the clinical and radiologic
features is achieved. Serial MRI should be obtained to help in the
assessment of disease activity. Adjunctive therapy for osteoporosis
prevention and prophylaxis for opportunistic infections should be
incorporated into the treatment plan.

## Outcome

Originally, CNSV was described as a fatal disease,
but an improved
outlook became possible after the description of treatment with
glucocorticoids and cyclophosphamide. According to recent
reports, the estimated mortality rate of CNSV varies between 8%
and 28 %,
with one study finding that survival correlated with
the initial findings of infarcts and gadolinium-enhanced lesions
on MRI.
Some groups
reported that 20% of patients with
CNSV had moderate to severe disability as assessed by the modified Rankin disability scores or the Barthel index, although with
some an improvement in disability scores over time.

## Conclusion

Considerable progress has been made in our understanding of
CNSV. Our ability to recognize disease mimics has substantially
increased our accuracy in diagnosing CNSV. The identification of
RCVS in particular has clarified many of the cases that had been
erroneously diagnosed as CNSV. Advances in molecular diagnostics
have improved our ability to rule out infectious mimics. Effective
diagnosis and management of CNSV requires a multidisciplinary
team to evaluate the clinical features, obtain and interpret diagnostic tests, and determine the most effective regimen that will treat the
underlying disease and minimize therapeutic toxicity.
A great need exists for controlled trials to further guide treatment for patients with CNSV in their initial and maintenance
phases. A multicenter international effort is underway to better understand the pathogenesis of CNSV. Through this effort,
disease definitions will be refined, and consensus approaches to
diagnosis and treatment will be developed. This platform can
then be used to explore and validate new biomarkers of disease,
design clinical trials of new or existing therapies, and improve
our understanding of disease outcomes to inform clinical
decision-making.
