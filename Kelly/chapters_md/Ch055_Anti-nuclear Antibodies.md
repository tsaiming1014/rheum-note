# Anti-nuclear Antibodies

STANFORD L. PENG

KEY POINTS

> Among the rheumatic diseases, anti-nuclear antibodies (ANAs) are

> most characteristic of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE),

> systemic sclerosis (SSc), idiopathic inflammatory myopathies (IIMs),

> and Sjögren’s syndrome (SjS). Their presence is required for the

> diagnosis of some syndromes, such as drug-induced lupus or mixed

> connective tissue disease. ANA testing is an appropriate screening

> step when such diseases are suspected.

> Key ANA specificities in SLE include anti-double-stranded DNA, which

> may correlate with renal disease and overall disease activity;

> anti-Sm and antiribosomal P; and anti-Ro and anti-La, which are

> associated with cutaneous and neonatal lupus.

> Key ANA specificities in SSc include anti-centromere/kinetochore, which

> is associated with CREST (calcinosis, Raynaud’s phenomenon,

> esophageal dysmotility, sclerodactyly, and telangiectasias)

> manifestations, and anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I) and anti-RNA

> polymerase III, which are associated with diffuse cutaneous disease

> and interstitial lung disease.

> Key ANA specificities in IIM include anti-aminoacyl tRNA synthetases

> such as anti-Jo-1 (anti-histidyl-tRNA synthetase), which are

> associated with the poor prognosis anti-synthetase syndrome

> in polymyositis/dermatomyositis; and anti-HMGCR (3-hydroxy-

> 3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A reductase) and anti-SRP (signal

> recognition particle), which are associated with immune-mediated

> necrotizing myopathy.

> Key ANA specificities in SjS include anti-Ro (including Ro52 and Ro60)

> and anti-La, which are also found in mothers of children with

> neonatal lupus and congenital heart block.

> Many ANA specificities are generally considered disease or

> manifestation specific, but exceptions are notoriously common,

> confounded by the presence of many autoantibodies in other

> inflammatory conditions or healthy people.

> Ideally, testing of individual ANA specificities should be performed only

> in the context of clinical signs that correlate with antibody–disease

> associations (e.g., anti-dsDNA in the setting of glomerulonephritis,

> suspecting lupus nephritis), but the growing availability and

> implementation of ANA panels in many clinical laboratories often

> result in the availability of specific ANA tests in settings of uncertain

> clinical significance. As a result, ANA and specific ANA testing are

> generally insufficient to establish or refute diagnoses; rather, they

> add weight to diagnostic considerations that rely heavily on other

> clinical information.

## Introduction

Despite the name, anti-nuclear antibodies (ANAs) include a wide
diversity of autoantibodies directed against multiple cellular antigens, classically consisting of nuclear specificities such as DNA or
small nuclear ribonucleoproteins but also various other cell components including the mitotic spindle apparatus, cytosol, cytoplasmic organelles, and cell membranes.
The “ANA diseases”
(
Table 55.1
) are characterized by a particularly high prevalence
of ANAs, often screened for by the indirect immunofluorescence
(IIF) ANA test: systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), systemic
sclerosis (SSc), and mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD).
The prevalence of ANAs in the idiopathic inflammatory myopathies (IIMs) and in Sjögren’s syndrome (SjS) is somewhat lower
than in the other ANA diseases, but these conditions are often
grouped together because they share several target antigens and
treatment approaches, and therefore presumably also share similar fundamental etiologies. For decades, ANA testing has been an
important diagnostic and prognostic tool for these connective tissue diseases (CTDs), and it has become a routine assay in the
evaluation of patients with suspected autoimmune disease by both
specialists and primary care providers. However, ANAs also arise
in a variety of infectious, inflammatory, and neoplastic diseases,
as well as in healthy people, and thus some knowledge regarding
their intricacies and the limitations of the assays is required for
appropriate clinical utilization.

### History

> sometimes other autoimmune diseases. They have also played a

> significant role in the basic scientific understanding of their target

in SLE bone marrow.

## Methods of Detection

KEY POINTS

> The typical standard screening test for ANAs is the IIF ANA test. Positive

> is generally reported with any titer of 1:40 or higher, although a

> clinical positivity threshold of 1:80, 1:160, or higher may afford

> greater specificity in specific situations and should be guided by

> individual laboratory technique and experience.

> Many antibody tests, including ANA screening in some laboratories, are

> performed via enzyme-linked immunosorbent or other solid-phase

> assays, affording high throughput; however, such techniques may

> result in lower diagnostic and/or prognostic accuracy.

> Optimal clinical interpretation of ANA tests requires knowledge of the

> technique(s) used in each specific case.

### Immunofluorescence

IIF ANA testing provides a rapid yet highly sensitive screening
method for ANA detection and remains an important standard
for initial clinical testing.
With this method, test sera at varying dilutions (typically serially increasing by twofold) are incubated with substrate cells, and bound antibodies are detected by
fluorescein-conjugated anti-human immunoglobulin G, followed
by visualization via a fluorescence microscope. Cultured HEp-2
cells have remained a standard substrate for this test because of
their higher concentration of nuclear and cytoplasmic antigens
and standardization of use, but some laboratories may still use
alternative heterogeneous substrates, such as rodent liver or kidney tissues, which possess the advantage of eliminating interference from blood-group antibodies, heterophile antibodies, or
passenger viruses, but may exhibit lower sensitivity of some cell
cycle–dependent antigens such as Ro60. Some may also utilize
or incorporate alternative HEp-2 substrates such as HEp-2000,
which overexpresses Ro60.
Regardless of the specific substrates,
results are typically reported by two parameters: pattern and titer.
In most laboratories, any pattern of reactivity at a titer of 1:40 or
greater is generally considered positive.
ANA Staining Patterns

presumably reflect the subcellular localization of the respective autoantigen(s) (

(ICAP) working group has identified 30 distinct HEp-2 patterns

all “competent-level” laboratories, including various nuclear (e.g.,
homogeneous, speckled, dense fine speckled, discrete nuclear
dots, nucleolar) and cytoplasmic (e.g., fibrillar, speckled, rods and
rings) patterns.
However, these recommendations have not yet
been widely or formally implemented, resulting in laboratory-tolaboratory variances in the specificity of many pattern descriptions.
Historically, ANA patterns have often been of great clinical
interest, implicating particular ANA specificities with diagnostic
and/or prognostic implications in the absence of specificity testing (e.g., centromere staining in limited SSc or speckled nuclear
or nucleolar in diffuse SSc). However, none are disease specific or
pathognomonic, and in current practice positive screening ANAs
are typically further evaluated and confirmed by additional solidphase assays to identify specific, disease-associated antibodies.
Thus the utility of ANA pattern in routine rheumatological decision making may be waning.
ANA Titer
Titer is generally reported as the last dilution at which an ANA
pattern is detectable; however, controversy remains regarding the
ideal dilution cutoff for positivity. A cutoff of 1:40 was proposed
by a large multicenter study based on the reduced sensitivity of
higher dilutions for SLE, SSc, and SjS; however, 32% of healthy
adults were also positive at this dilution, suggesting a high “falsepositive” rate with that threshold,
and the prevalence of ANA
positivity in healthy persons may only be increasing.
More
recent international expert recommendations suggest that clinical laboratories should identify a screening dilution that produces a positive result in 5% of healthy controls (which would
correspond to 1:160 in the aforementioned study), but this practice has not been widely implemented.

### Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay

Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISAs) are solid-phase
assays that provide highly sensitive and rapid techniques for specific ANA detection. They are commonly employed for the detection of many specificities associated with the ANA diseases—such
as anti-DNA, anti-Sm, anti-U1 RNP, anti-Ro, or anti-La/SS-B—
and are often performed in a “reflex” manner upon detection of a
positive screening IIF ANA test.
With this technique, test sera are incubated in wells pre-coated
with purified target antigen(s) or a mixture thereof, and bound
antibodies are detected via an enzyme-conjugated anti-human
immunoglobulin antibody, followed by color visualization with
the appropriate enzyme substrate. The popularity of this technique has further resulted from the commercial availability of
ELISA kits and the ability to perform these assays on a multiplex platform, enabling large numbers of clinical specimens to
be processed quickly at reasonably low expenditure. These issues
likely also account for the growing use of ELISA instead of IIF
for ANA screening in some laboratory settings. Such an approach
facilitates higher throughput and perhaps greater consistency than
IIF, but may be less sensitive overall due to the inclusion of only
a finite number of target antigens, typically only the 8 to 10 most

### Anti-DNA Antibody Tests

Anti-DNA autoantibodies warrant special consideration because
of their long-recognized assay difficulties.
They can be primarily
divided into antibodies that react with denatured, ssDNA and those
that react with various forms of native, dsDNA, the latter being of
far greater clinical importance for diagnosis and/or management.
ELISA is the most common methodology implemented by clinical laboratories, but its specificity may be limited by the tendency
for dsDNA to at least partially denature to ssDNA in such assays,
resulting in increased sensitivity but reduced specificity.

## Diseases Primarily Associated With Anti-nuclear Antibodies

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

ANAs remain a hallmark of SLE: more than 99% of patients test
positive via current methods at diagnosis, although many may
later seroconvert to ANA negativity, attributed to the natural
history of the disease and/or the effects of immunosuppressive
agents.
SLE often evokes autoantibodies against a wide range of
antigens in many cellular locations, but the majority reside in the
nucleus (
Tables 55.2
and
55.3
;
Fig. 55.1
).
Anti-dsDNA
Anti-DNA antibodies have been described to bind to multiple
forms of DNA, including ssDNA, Z-DNA, or cruciform DNA.
Antibodies against the physiologic nucleosome or chromatin
forms of DNA have been suggested to be most relevant to disease
pathogenesis,
but the vast majority of clinical literature and clinical laboratory capabilities remains focused on classic anti-dsDNA
antibodies, which often associate with glomerulonephritis, may
fluctuate in correlation with disease activity, and also continue
to be included in the 2019 American College of Rheumatology
(ACR)/European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(EULAR) Classification Criteria for SLE.
In such situations, anti-dsDNA is typically initially present in high titer and
reflects a strong disease association with SLE, in contrast to lower
titers that may be observed in other disorders or even healthy
individuals. Anti-ssDNA antibodies are generally not clinically
useful for diagnosis or monitoring because of their much broader
prevalence and lack of correlation with disease activity. Anti-DNA
antibodies have also been observed in drug-induced lupus (DIL);
these are typically anti-ssDNA and associate with anti-histone
antibodies (see below). As a result, the presence of anti-dsDNA
activity should always prompt consideration of lupus and renal
disease, but the presence of anti-dsDNA activities does not always
indicate lupus, lupus nephritis, or vice versa. Indeed, in clinical
settings inconsistent with SLE, anti-dsDNA antibodies likely have
low diagnostic and predictive value.
Anti–Small Nuclear Ribonucleoproteins: Anti-Sm and
anti-U1 snRNP
Small nuclear ribonucleoprotein (snRNP) autoantibodies target
the RNAs or proteins of the spliceosome, a complex of RNP particles involved in pre-messenger (m)RNA splicing.
These particles
include the U1, U2, U4/U6, U5, U7, U11, and U12 snRNPs,
each of which consists of its own uridine-rich (“U”) snRNA and
a set of polypeptides, including a common core of “Sm” polypeptides (B/B
, D1, D2, D3, E, F, and G) and particle-specific
polypeptides.
The most commonly assessed snRNP specificities include antiSm and anti-U1 snRNP (anti-RNP or anti-U1 RNP).
AntiSm antibodies target proteins of the Sm core, particularly the B/B
and D polypeptides and the Sm-like LSm4. They appear in only
a minority of patients and are only inconsistently associated with
disease activity or prognosis but are considered highly specific for
SLE and also continue to be included in the 2019 ACR/EULAR
Classification Criteria for SLE.
In contrast, anti-U1 snRNP
antibodies target the 70K, A, or C polypeptides specific to the
U1 snRNP and are observed more frequently but are not specific for the disease and have also been only variably associated
with specific disease manifestations. Some of these issues may be
confounded by variability in testing format, since some Sm or U1
snRNP ELISAs may not include all possible Sm or U1 snRNPspecific antigens for detection. The presence of anti-Sm can therefore be of particular importance during the diagnosis of SLE, but
testing for both specificities may increase overall sensitivity and
the findings should be interpreted within the context of the specific laboratory’s assay format.
Anti-Ro and Anti-La
This family of autoantibodies includes three distinct autoantigens:
Ro60 (TROVE2; Telomerase, Ro, and Vault 2), which binds misfolded noncoding RNAs, pre-5S rRNA, and several small noncoding cytoplasmic RNAs known as Y RNA
; Ro52 (TRIM21;
tripartite motif containing-21), an E3 ubiquitin ligase that regulates pathogen defense via immunoglobulin detection
; and La/
SS-B, which facilitates the production of RNA polymerase III
transcripts such as tRNAs and the translation of some mRNAs.
Because of the primarily cytoplasmic localization of Ro52 and
the potential loss of Ro60 during preparation of HEp-2 cells for
immunofluorescent staining, the IIF ANA may be falsely negative when anti-Ro are the only autoantibodies present and may
account for more than half of patients with “ANA-negative” SLE.
In contrast, anti-La antibodies are routinely detected by IIF ANA
and rarely occur in the absence of anti-Ro. In most commercial
clinical laboratories today, “anti-Ro/SS-A” testing consists of a
single test combining both Ro60 and Ro52 antigens, a practice
that likely reduces the sensitivity for monospecific sera.
Knowledge of the specific laboratory technique is therefore important for
optimal interpretation of Ro autoantibody results.
Anti-Ro may be found in a wide range of autoimmune disorders, including SLE, SjS, SSc, IIM, MCTD, RA, and other primarily nonrheumatic diseases such as primary biliary cirrhosis and
interstitial lung disease (ILD), and may be the first ANA specificity detected in asymptomatic individuals, preceding the development of SLE or SjS. In contrast, anti-La is generally specific to
SLE and SjS but only when accompanied by anti-Ro; the clinical
significance of anti-La in the absence of anti-Ro remains uncertain.
Both specificities may be observed in mothers of children
with neonatal lupus syndrome and/or congenital heart block.
Anti-Ro52/Ro60 with or without anti-La has been most
strongly associated with SjS and the neonatal lupus syndrome,
whereas anti-Ro52/Ro60 alone has been most strongly associated with cutaneous LE as well as “ANA-negative” or “preclinical”
SLE or SjS. The prevalence of anti-Ro52 versus anti-Ro60 appear
similar in SLE and SjS but appear to differ in other autoimmune
diseases (e.g., with anti-Ro52 more prevalent than anti-Ro60 in
IIM, SSc, and MCTD). In general, anti-Ro52 antibodies have
been suggested to associate with more severe disease and/or interstitial lung disease.
Specific testing for anti-Ro and anti-La is therefore generally
considered as a diagnostic tool in patients in whom SjS is suspected, regardless of ANA status, or in whom SLE is suspected
despite a negative ANA. They are also a consideration for patients
with SLE, SjS, or known ANA positivity who wish to become
pregnant or for patients with a history of birthing a child with
neonatal lupus syndrome or congenital heart block.

ANAs against certain chromatin and nucleolar antigens characterize the autoantibody response in SSc; positive ANAs appear in
approximately 95% of patients. Unlike in SLE, however, SSc sera

Anti-centromere (Kinetochore) and Anti-topoisomerase I
(Scl70)
Anti-centromere/kinetochore antibodies target several components of the centromere/kinetochore macrocomplex.
These antigens are notable in that they require mitotically active cells for
robust detection, possibly accounting for some ANA-negative SSc
findings. Anti-topoisomerase I (Scl70) antibodies predominantly

been extensively studied and are heavily associated with Ray

involvement and interstitial lung disease.

anti-topoisomerase antibodies are included in the 2013 ACR/

although
they are each present in only a minority of patients with SSc

overall and are rarely both found in the same patient. Therefore
they play critical roles in disease classification and prognosis but
may not be used alone for definitive diagnosis.

Anti–RNA polymerase (RNAP) antibodies target the eukaryotic
RNA polymerases 1 to 3. RNAP2 antibodies have been observed
in other diseases, such as SLE or overlap syndromes, but RNAP1
and RNAP3 antibodies appear specific for SSc. RNAP3 antibodies in particular, including those directed against the RP11 and
RP155 subunits, may be helpful for diagnosis and/or prognosis:
they are included in the 2013 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria for Systemic Sclerosis
and may be associated with rapidly
progressive skin disease and joint contractures, renal crisis, gastric
antral vascular ectasia, and malignancy.

### Sjögren’s Syndrome

In individuals with SjS, reported frequencies of positive ANAs
range widely, reflecting differences in study populations, disease criteria applied, and the inclusion/exclusion of secondary,
CTD-related SjS, which increases the likelihood and amplitude
of positive tests.
Thus although ANA positivity as low as 40%
has been reported, many studies report frequencies of greater
than 95%, with a diverse range of autoantibodies, including
both ubiquitous (

and

and muscarinic receptor. Such issues hinder interpretation of
disease associations and disease specificities of ANAs in primary or secondary SjS.
Anti-Ro and Anti-La
In SjS, the most well-characterized and widely employed antibody specificities include Ro52, Ro60, and La (see “Anti-Ro and
Anti-La,” under SLE, above). They are collectively found in the
majority of patients with SjS, and the presence of anti-Ro (with
or without anti-La) is included in the 2016 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria for Primary Sjögren’s Syndrome.
Specific
disease associations with each antibody have not remained consistent across studies, however, perhaps in part due to technical
variability in distinguishing Ro52 from Ro60 in prior studies.
Recent studies, however, suggest that the presence of anti-Ro52,
particularly in the presence of anti-Ro60, may associate with
more severe disease.
Other ANA Specificities in SjS
Several other ANA specificities have been described in SjS. Some
have been proposed as at least somewhat specific to SjS, such as
fodrin, NuMA, and p80 coilin; others have been more characteristically associated with other rheumatic diseases, such as centromere. These have not been consistently associated with diagnostic
or prognostic specificity, limiting their routine use.

### “False-Positive” ANA

A common clinical scenario involves a positive screening ANA test
in a patient with no or undifferentiated rheumatic signs or symptoms. Many such patients will not develop an ANA-related disease
but may harbor another inflammatory or autoimmune disease,
sometimes nonrheumatic, that has been associated with ANA positivity, such as autoimmune thyroid, gastrointestinal or liver disease,
infection, or malignancy. Nonetheless, ANA positivity may predate
a diagnosed rheumatic disease by years or decades,
while many
patients who present with high-titer screening ANAs may continue
for years or decades without diagnosable signs or symptoms. Greater
likelihood of an ANA-related CTD has generally been thought to
correlate with higher titers (e.g., 1:640 or higher), but no specific
cutoff has proven reliable for the exclusion of significant rheumatic
disease.
Therefore in such scenarios, “false positive” may be an erroneous designation; the positive ANA may represent a true autoreactivity but may not necessarily associate with or predict a clinically
significant, specific ANA-related disease (SLE, SSc, IIM, or SjS).
Indeed, there is growing recognition of specificities that comprise a nonpathogenic or natural autoantibody repertoire.
In
particular, the dense fine speckled (DFS70) ANA pattern, which
can sometimes present with a high titer in a screening IIF ANA,
has been found to reflect reactivity with lens epithelium-derived
growth factor (p75;
Fig. 55.3
) and associates with the lack of systemic autoimmune rheumatic disease. In a clinical presentation
otherwise inconsistent with rheumatic disease, a positive DFS70
test may therefore help exclude a significant underlying systemic
rheumatic diagnosis, especially if the IIF ANA reports a dense fine

### Overlap Rheumatic Syndromes

Overlap syndromes of CTD remain matters of nosologic debate,
but virtually all investigators concur that ANAs are universal in these
conditions.
Indeed, the presence of anti-U1 snRNP has been consistently required for classification and/or diagnosis of MCTD, with
associated ANA titers often exceeding 1:10,000.
However, many
such patients experience manifestations, both clinical and serologic,
that allow the diagnosis of a defined CTD, hindering the accuracy
of specific clinical associations of specific ANAs in “overlap” settings. Therefore in such instances, it seems most reasonable to base
the importance of individual autoantibody specificities upon their
primary associations with disease manifestations, despite the lack

predictive of diffuse SSc-like skin disease or ILD or anti-dsDNA as
predictive of lupus-like glomerulonephritis).

### Other Conditions

ANA positivity continues to be described in an ever-growing number of rheumatic and nonrheumatic conditions. In addition to their
role in the diagnosis of the traditional ANA-related diseases, ANA
may contribute to the diagnosis of some nonrheumatic diseases,
particularly autoimmune hepatitis and primary biliary cirrhosis
(
Fig. 55.3
).
Furthermore, ANA may be prognostic in some settings. In Raynaud’s phenomenon, positivity increases the likelihood
of the development of a systemic rheumatic disease, such as SLE,
RA, and SSc,
and in JIA, positivity predicts the development of
uveitis (see
Table 55.1
).
In most other scenarios, the clinical significance of a positive ANA remains indeterminate and may distract
the clinician from a correct diagnosis or prognosis.

## Recommendations for Anti-nuclear Antibody Testing

Fig. 55.4
describes an algorithm for the rheumatologic evaluation
of a patient for ANAs. In most clinical settings, the ANA serves as
a screening test, with a positive result prompting “cascade” testing
by the laboratory and/or prescribing physician for specific autoantibody specificities, such as anti-dsDNA, anti-Ro, anti-La/SS-B,
anti-U1 snRNP, and anti-Sm.
A negative or low-titer ANA in the setting of a low clinical suspicion
of rheumatic disease usually indicates the absence of significant ANAs
and argues against the diagnosis of one of the ANA-related CTDs. In
some situations, though, follow-up anti-DFS70 testing may be helpful to more confidently rule out systemic rheumatic disease, especially
if a dense fine speckled pattern is reported for the ANA and other
testing confirms the absence of other ANA specificities. If the clinical
picture strongly suggests CTD despite a negative or low-titer ANA,
further investigation may involve specific assays for antigens that are
sometimes ANA negative, such as ribosomal P, Ro, tRNA synthetases,
or other myositis-specific antigens. Positive results in such assays may
accordingly suggest further investigation for SLE, SjS, or an IIM.
Positive ANA results in the setting of strong clinical suspicion
usually warrant follow-up with specialized assays. If SLE features are
present, further testing may focus on anti-DNA, anti-Sm, anti-RNP/
U1 snRNP, and anti-Ro antibodies. Similarly, if MCTD, SjS, SSc, or
IIM are suspected, further testing could include, respectively, anti-U1
snRNP; anti-Ro and anti-La; anti-topoisomerase I, anti-centromere,
and anti-RNA polymerase III; or anti-tRNA synthetases and other
myositis-specific antibodies, the latter potentially guided by the presence of specific clinical characteristics (e.g., MDA5 for suspected
CADM). If these tests are negative in the setting of high clinical suspicion, repeat testing at a later date may be warranted because titers of
such autoantibodies can fluctuate over time.
Positive results in these more specialized assays do not alone
signify specific diseases but rather add weight to diagnoses that
should throughout the evaluation rely heavily on other clinical
information. Indeed, many clinical and research studies upon which
antibody–disease associations have been developed often utilized
highly refined reagents or methods unavailable in the routine, highthroughput clinical laboratory setting. Therefore the relevance of
many ANA results in clinical practice continues to require careful,
individualized interpretation by the referring physician.
