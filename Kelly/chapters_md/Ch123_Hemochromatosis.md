## Introduction

Hemochromatosis refers to the presence of excess iron in body
tissues because of increased iron absorption. Primary or hereditary hemochromatosis (HHC) is an autosomal recessive disease,
whereas secondary hemochromatosis refers to iron overload as a
result of increased iron availability, ineffective erythropoiesis, or
inherited abnormalities of iron metabolism.
Hemochromatosis was first recognized in the 1880s in a series
of case reports that described “bronze diabetes” and “pigmented
cirrhosis,” but von Recklinghausen
is credited with the first use
of the term in 1889. In 1935 the familial pattern of HHC was
described by Sheldon,
who suggested that the disease was due
to an inborn error of metabolism. In 1955 Finch and Finch
showed that HHC was caused by abnormal iron absorption in the
presence of a normal diet. At that time, premorbid recognition
of the problem was uncommon, and most cases were diagnosed
at autopsy. In 1972 serum ferritin became available as a measure
of iron stores. Three years later, investigators discovered that the
HHC gene was present on chromosome 6, close to the human
leukocyte antigen (HLA)-A locus.
It took 21 more years for the
mutated gene,
HFE,
to be described, and in the past decade, it
has been recognized that other gene mutations also can cause
iron overload.
Genetic testing has revolutionized the diagnosis of
HHC, although the phenotype of any given mutation may vary
greatly.
Nevertheless, the detection of such genes has greatly
enhanced the overall prognosis of this condition by allowing the
disease to be diagnosed at a pre-clinical stage in high-risk individuals. This discovery, along with improved knowledge at a molecular
level, has further aided our understanding of the pathogenesis and
treatment of HHC and helped many patients achieve a normal
life expectancy.

## Iron Metabolism in Health and Disease

Iron has an essential role in erythropoiesis and in normal cellular
function. Along with anemia, chronic iron deficiency results in
a variety of other clinical manifestations such as changes to the
nails, tongue, esophagus, and muscles.
In normal adults the average total body iron content is 3 to 4 g, mostly contained within
hemoglobin but also present in myoglobin and cytochromes, in
addition to the storage proteins, ferritin and hemosiderin (
Table
123.1
). Of a typical daily Western diet of 10 to 20 mg of iron, 1
to 2 mg is absorbed by duodenal enterocytes each day.
Heme
dietary sources from fish and meat have a higher bioavailability than nonheme sources, such as vegetables. The addition of
ascorbic acid to the meal increases absorption of nonheme iron,
whereas tannins, bran, and phytates inhibit iron absorption.
Iron homeostasis, which is tightly controlled at cellular and
molecular levels, is influenced by regulatory feedback between
body iron requirements and intestinal iron absorption.
Dietary
iron enters the body in the duodenum, where, in the ferrous state,
it gains access to the enterocyte via divalent metal transporter 1.
It is then either stored intra-cellularly and eventually excreted
through the feces or absorbed via ferroportin transfer out of the
enterocyte, aided by ferroxidase activity of the ceruloplasmin
homologue, hephaestin.
Circulating iron is then either used
(mainly by erythroid cells) or stored (in the liver or macrophages).
Communication between sites of iron uptake, utilization, and
storage is tightly controlled. Ferroportin facilitates release of intracellular iron and is a major player in iron homeostasis, modulated
by hepcidin, which plays a central role in the process.
Hepcidin is produced by the liver in response to changes in iron status
and negatively regulates iron levels.
It acts directly on ferroportin by binding to it and causing it to be internalized by the cell and
destroyed.
A signaling pathway for bone morphogenetic protein
(BMP)-6 regulates hepcidin by binding to a specific receptor on
hepatocytes.
Hepcidin expression is normally induced by excess
iron or inflammation and downregulated by iron deficiency, ineffective erythropoiesis, or hypoxia.

### Other Inherited Iron-Overload Syndromes

Several other inherited iron-overload syndromes have been
described. In people of African descent, a common polymorphism in the ferroportin 1 gene is associated with mild microcytic
anemia and a tendency to iron loading.
Aceruloplasminemia
is caused by homozygous mutation of the ceruloplasmin gene
and results in anemia, evidence of iron excess, glucose intolerance, and neurologic symptoms.
Familial atransferrinemia is
an autosomal recessive condition associated with severe anemia in the presence of tissue iron deposition.
A number of
ferritin mutations have been described that result in hyperferritinemia, some with specific organ damage. Patients with hereditary hyperferritinemia-cataract syndrome have elevated ferritin
levels and bilateral congenital cataracts.
Neuroferritinopathy
is an autosomal dominant movement disorder in which iron
preferentially deposits in the brain despite variable serum ferritin levels.
In contrast, the entity of benign hyperferritinemia
has also been reported in which, despite a mutation in the coding region of l-ferritin, no clinical manifestations develop.
A
congenital form of iron overload is neonatal hemochromatosis,
in which excess iron deposition results from immune-mediated
liver injury and is ameliorated by administration of intravenous
immunoglobulin during pregnancy.

## Epidemiology

Although HHC was previously thought to be a rare condition,
genetic testing has revealed that it is one of the most common
heritable disorders. Although 5 out of every 1000 individuals of Northern European origin are homozygous for the
HFE
mutation, phenotypic expression varies, and clinical cases are
much fewer in number.
In a study of nearly 100,000 individuals from primary care practices in the United States, the
prevalence of C282Y homozygosity was as follows: white,
0.44%; Native American, 0.11%; Hispanic, 0.027%; African American, 0.014%; Pacific Islander, 0.012%; and Asian,
0.0004%.
Peak age at the time of diagnosis is 40 to 60 years
for classic HHC.

## Influence of Other Genes and Environment

Clinical manifestations of HHC vary greatly between people with
similar mutations, suggesting that other factors or genes influence
disease expression. One study demonstrated that among C282Y
homozygotes, up to 82% have hyperferritinemia, whereas approximately 28% of homozygous males and 1% of homozygous females
ultimately experience clinical manifestations of HHC—defined as
the presence of liver disease, hepatocellular carcinoma, and arthritis of the second and third metacarpophalangeal joints—by age 65
years.
In contrast, compound heterozygotes with C282Y/H63D
mutations exhibit higher serum ferritin and transferrin saturation
levels compared with normal control subjects but are at very low
risk of clinical HHC.
Studies have found that hyperferritinemic
patients with relatives who have suffered the consequences of iron
overload are at increased risk of organ damage compared with
those with no family history of such clinical problems.
Other genes may play a role in modifying the phenotypic
expression of iron overload. The presence of the gene
CYBRD1,
which encodes duodenal reductase DCYTB, is associated with
lower serum ferritin levels than in C282Y homozygotes.
Furthermore, mutations of other iron-related genes, such as hepcidin,
hemojuvelin, haptoglobin, and BMP, may influence disease manifestations.
In addition, increased expression of profibrotic
genes (e.g., transforming growth factor) may accelerate the onset
of cirrhosis in susceptible people.
The reason that HHC disease penetrance is more evident in
C282Y homozygote males than females may be explained by
recurrent menstrual blood loss and consequent slower accumulation of iron stores in women. Nevertheless, genetically determined
sex differences in ferritin levels may occur because distinct HLA
A*03B*07 and A*03B*14 haplotypes have been reported in men
and women with clinical evidence of HHC.
Environmental factors, including diet, smoking, alcohol
intake, and comorbid diseases, also influence clinical expression
of HHC. Metabolic syndrome is associated with insulin resistance–associated iron overload, which, in the presence of HHC,
may have a synergistic effect on liver damage.
Concurrent liver
disease due to hepatitis or steatosis may exacerbate the process

of fibrogenesis.
In addition to the known toxic effect of alcohol on hepatocytes, it also downregulates hepcidin expression and
increases iron absorption, contributing to end organ damage in
patients with innate iron overload.
Furthermore, meat consumption and high citrus fruit intake also contribute to increased
iron loading. In addition, ingestion of noncitrus fruits may have
a protective effect.

## Pathogenesis

The clinical expression of HHC may be influenced by several
pathophysiologic mechanisms, including increased duodenal
absorption of dietary iron; decreased expression of the key regulatory peptide, hepcidin; altered function of the HFE protein; and
the direct and indirect toxic effects of iron deposition in target
organs.
Recent insight has been gained into the function of hepcidin. Human mutations and murine knockout models of genes
for hepcidin, hemojuvelin,
TfRs
, or
HFE
result in reduced hepcidin levels and increased duodenal iron absorption.
The HFE
protein also plays a role as a hepatocyte membrane-based iron sensor, interacting with HFE and TfR-1 and -2, thereby influencing
hepcidin expression.
Chronic iron overload is thought to cause tissue damage by
several mechanisms, including impairment of mitochondrial
function and weakening of lysosomal membranes with consequent discharge of enzymes into the cytoplasm.
Increased free
radical formation contributes to lipid peroxidation of cell membranes, and profibrogenic cytokines are released.
The extent and
duration of iron deposition correlate with the development of
fibrosis, and it is thought that substantial hepatocyte and Kupffer
cell iron accumulation precedes organ damage.
In HHC, iron
first accumulates in parenchymal cells, with reticuloendothelial
(RE) involvement a late feature, in contrast to transfusional iron
overload, in which RE cells are primarily targeted. Values of serum
ferritin exceeding 1000
μ
g/L are associated with significantly
increased risks of liver fibrosis and cirrhosis.

## Clinical Features

### Extra-articular Manifestations

HHC presents more commonly in men than in women and typically manifests in middle-aged adults when iron stores gradually
accumulate, often reaching 20 to 30 g.
The classic findings
of hepatic abnormalities, skin pigmentation, arthritis, DM, erectile dysfunction, heart enlargement, and/or conduction defects
indicate late disease.
Organ involvement varies and is unpredictable, although the liver, as the major site of iron storage, is
usually affected. Not infrequently, abnormalities of liver enzymes,
checked as part of a routine health review, identify the problem.
With the recent availability of genetic testing, however, presentation by asymptomatic relatives of probands is now more common.
In the liver, increasing iron deposition results in eventual fibrosis and cirrhosis. It is worth noting that some patients with HHC
and iron overload may progress to fibrosis even in the presence of
normal levels of serum transaminases.
Therapeutic phlebotomy
can cause regression of some of these changes, particularly in early
disease.
Additional causes of significant liver disease are common in patients with HHC and may contribute to hepatocellular
damage.
In people with established cirrhosis, the risk of malignant hepatoma is 20-fold greater than the general population,
with a higher risk in affected men than in women.
Iron deposition in the heart can result in conduction system
abnormalities and heart failure. Several large population studies
of HHC and atherosclerosis have failed to find a link. Nevertheless, elevated ferritin levels, particularly in the setting of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, may be associated with vascular damage
via hepcidin upregulation.
Glucose intolerance tends to be a
late finding in HHC and is due to progressive iron accumulation in pancreatic
β
cells, causing low C-peptide and insulin levels.
Usually
α
cell function is preserved, and serum glucagon
levels are normal or increased. In patients with HHC who pre
sent symptomatically, DM has been noted in approximately 50%.
For patients with HHC who have early insulin resistance and no
evidence of cirrhosis, however, venesection may reverse iron deposition–induced
β
cell dysfunction.
The risk of DM is higher
in C282Y heterozygotes with no clinical evidence of HHC compared with control subjects.
Pituitary involvement in HHC can result in reduced serum
levels of secreted hormones, mainly from the anterior aspect of
this gland, resulting in loss of libido and erectile dysfunction.
Primary hypogonadism from testicular iron overload is much less
common.
Therapeutic phlebotomy may reduce these symptoms, particularly in younger men.
Hypothyroidism in HHC is
thought to be due to a direct toxic effect of iron on thyroid cells
and is associated with low thyroxine and elevated thyroid-stimulating hormone.
Such endocrine abnormalities may contribute
to the development of osteoporosis in these individuals.
Skin discoloration occurs as a result of extra melanin and iron
in the epidermis. It is a late finding, and the development of
bronze diabetes represents the end stage of years of iron accumulation in the tissues.
Patients with HHC have increased susceptibility to certain
infections. High serum iron concentrations may increase bacterial
virulence, and excess iron in macrophages is thought to reduce
phagocytosis.
Particular caution is advised with uncooked seafood because of the risk of septicemia from
Vibrio vulnificus.
In
addition,
Yersinia enterocolitica, Listeria monocytogenes, Salmonella
enteritidis
serotype
typhimurium, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Escherichia coli, Rhizopus arrhizus,
and
Mucor
species all cause severe
illness in patients with iron overload.
Apart from the increased risk of malignant hepatoma in
patients with established cirrhosis, an independent association
of nonhepatic cancers, particularly breast and colorectal tumors,
in HFE C282Y homozygotes has been reported.
In addition,
H63D homozygosity increases susceptibility to colorectal cancer
in people with pre-existing hereditary risks for this malignancy.
Iron is potentially carcinogenic via several mechanisms, including its immunosuppressive properties and its role as an essential
cofactor for tumor cell growth and in catalyzing the formation of
hydroxyl radicals. Furthermore, cancer risk is lower with reduced
iron stores.

## Differential Diagnosis

Because of the lack of a physiologic mechanism to enhance iron
excretion, the inevitable result of increased iron absorption is
iron overload, which may be due to inherited defects in iron
metabolism, as previously described, or secondary to other
causes (
Table 123.3
). A full history of medication use, including
over-the-counter iron tablets, and a review of any blood transfusions should be elicited. Ineffective erythropoiesis in conditions
such as the thalassemias or sideroblastic anemia can also result
in the buildup of iron stores. Porphyria cutanea tarda is associated with hyperferritinemia, high transferrin saturation levels,
and an increased incidence of
HFE
mutations.
Nevertheless,
the clinical findings, particularly dermatologic findings, distinguish this disease from HHC. Elevated ferritin levels may result
from severe inflammation. Interleukin (IL)-6 is a potent stimulator of hepcidin via the STAT3 pathway, resulting in reduced
iron absorption and elevation of tissue and serum ferritin levels,
although these changes are accompanied by low transferrin saturation.
Chronic liver disease resulting from hepatitis, alcohol
excess, or fatty infiltration is associated with hyperferritinemia
and normal or elevated transferrin saturation values.
Alcohol,
independent of liver damage, induces ferritin synthesis and iron
overload in people who consume more than two alcoholic drinks
per day.

## Diagnostic Tests

A high index of suspicion is helpful when a patient presents with
joint pain and abnormal liver enzymes. Although the differential
diagnosis is wide, the presence of elevated ferritin and transferrin
saturation levels (serum iron × 100/total iron-binding capacity)
strongly points to the diagnosis of HHC. Serum iron should be
measured with the patient fasting because concentrations may be
increased after a meal. High ferritin levels also may be caused by
systemic inflammation or malignancy, but these conditions tend
to be associated with reduced transferrin saturation. Other reasons
for elevated transferrin saturation include high serum iron as a
result of hepatic cytolysis or low transferrin levels due to liver failure, and these possibilities should be excluded. If ferritin measures
greater than 200
μ
g/L and transferrin saturation is greater than
45%, genetic screening is recommended. The finding of homozygosity for the C282Y mutation or compound heterozygosity
for C282Y/H63D confirms the diagnosis. If these mutations
are not present and evidence of iron overload exists, investigation for more rare genetic defects, such as alterations of the genes
that encode hemojuvelin, hepcidin, TfR2, or ferroportin, can be
considered in the context of the pertinent clinical features (
Table
123.2
). Although hepcidin levels may be helpful, they are currently not routinely available.
A liver biopsy may be considered for prognostic purposes in
established cases. The patients most likely to show evidence of
cirrhosis are those who have the combination of a ferritin level
greater than 1000
μ
g/L, elevated transaminases, and thrombocytopenia.
In contrast, patients with a serum ferritin level less
than 1000
μ
g/L and no additional risk factors for liver disease are
unlikely to have cirrhosis. A liver biopsy is therefore considered
for those with advanced disease or if the diagnosis is in question,
particularly in people with evidence of iron overload who are HFE
negative. HHC can be distinguished histologically from alcoholic
cirrhosis by the preferential distribution of iron in the hepatocytes
in the former and in the Kupffer cells in the latter.
Other indications of liver damage include aspartate aminotransferase-to-platelet ratio index (APRI) and Fibrosis-4 Index
(Fib4), which have been validated against liver biopsy for detecting advanced hepatic fibrosis in HFE-related HHC. However,
ultrasound-based elastography underdiagnoses advanced fibrosis
in these patients, particularly in those with ferritin levels <1000
μ
g/L.
MRI of the abdomen also can be used to determine iron
overload in the internal organs. Gradient T2-weighted sequences
show decreased signal intensity and correlate highly with liver iron
concentrations.
This imaging method also can identify other
locations of iron deposition (e.g., in the spleen, pancreas, lymph
nodes, and heart). Because HHC is a systemic condition, other
investigations should include a search for diabetes, thyroid disease,
hypogonadism, osteoporosis, and cardiomyopathy. Disease mimickers, such as porphyria cutanea tarda, ineffective erythropoiesis,
chronic alcohol excess, and the metabolic syndrome, should be

### Population Screening

HHC is an attractive target for population screening because of
its high prevalence, potential disease severity, availability of costeffective treatment, and impact of early diagnosis on the morbidity and mortality of affected patients. Nevertheless, the clinical
penetrance of HHC is low, and the negative consequences (e.g.,
economical and psychological) of finding an associated gene mutation in an asymptomatic person without any evidence of disease
can be considerable. General screening for HHC is therefore not
recommended.

### Screening People With a Positive Family History

Individuals with a first-degree relative who has been diagnosed
with HHC should be tested for
HFE
mutations and evidence of
iron overload.
For children of a patient with HHC, it is recommended that the other parent be screened first, because offspring
will be affected only if both parents carry a relevant mutation. In
first-degree relatives of C282Y homozygotes, the degree of iron
overload is associated with disease severity in the proband.
The risk of clinically relevant iron toxicity is extremely low for
people who are found to have C282Y or H63D heterozygosity.
Infrequently, H63D homozygosity is associated with mild iron
excess.

### Screening in Patients With Related Diseases

The European Association for the Study of Liver Diseases has
recommended
HFE
testing in patients with unexplained chronic
liver disease and raised transferrin saturation levels (
Fig. 123.2
).
Furthermore,
HFE
testing should be considered in people with
porphyria cutanea tarda, well-defined chondrocalcinosis, and
hepatocellular carcinoma and those with late-onset type 1 DM
because C282Y homozygosity has a higher prevalence in these
conditions.
HHC-related genotypes are a risk factor for the
development of liver disease in the presence of significant comorbid illness, particularly chronic infectious hepatitis, porphyria
cutanea tarda, or steatosis.

## Treatment

Removal of iron from the body can be undertaken by phlebotomy, erythrocytapheresis, or the use of iron chelators. Treatment
is divided into the “depletion” stage, which aims to reduce ferritin levels to normal, and the “maintenance” stage, in which
the frequency of iron removal is decreased and ferritin levels are
monitored. Some, but not all, consequences of iron deposition
are reversed by removing iron. Most patients note an improvement in well-being and skin pigmentation; furthermore, amelioration of cardiac function and glucose control, in addition
to hepatic fibrosis, has been documented.
Nevertheless,
symptoms of arthritis do not change, and established cirrhosis
is irreversible.
Phlebotomy is the mainstay of treatment for iron overload in
the setting of HHC. Every 500 mL of whole blood contains up
to 250 mg of iron, and thus it can take more than 1 year for iron
stores to normalize with weekly venesection. It is recommended
that treatment be started when serum ferritin levels are consistently and progressively above normal local reference ranges.
Weekly phlebotomy of 500 mL of blood should be guided by ferritin and hemoglobin/hematocrit levels, aiming for serum ferritin
of 50
μ
g/L in the absence of anemia. Transferrin saturation levels
are not an accurate measure of therapeutic efficacy because they
are relatively resistant to change in iron stores in C282Y homozygotes.
In the maintenance phase, the frequency of venesection depends on the response to treatment. Anemia should be
avoided because it may trigger further lowering of hepcidin levels
and encourage intestinal iron absorption.
Although generally
safe and well tolerated, adverse effects of blood removal in HHC
include cardiac arrhythmias that may develop during rapid mobilization of iron stores.
Vitamin C supplementation may exacerbate such problems by facilitating iron release and increasing
pro-oxidant and free radical activity.
In many countries,
therapeutic phlebotomy from patients with HHC may contribute
to the blood donation pool.
Erythrocytapheresis removes more red blood cells per session
than phlebotomy and preserves platelets, plasma proteins, and
coagulation factors.
The target of normal iron levels is achieved
more rapidly with this technique in comparison with phlebotomy, but the financial costs are greater. The additional technical

expertise means that erythrocytapheresis is not commonly used to
treat iron overload in individuals with HHC.
Iron chelators may be administered orally (e.g., deferasirox)
or parenterally (e.g., deferoxamine); however, these drugs are
associated with more adverse effects than phlebotomy and thus
are reserved for patients who are intolerant of regular venesection.
Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) can potentially contribute to reduced iron absorption because reduced gastric acid
secretion lowers the solubility of inorganic iron, thereby decreasing its absorption. Furthermore, they may influence iron uptake
into red blood cells, a process that requires proton cotransport.
Currently, however, there is no evidence to show that
chronic use of PPIs significantly affects iron levels in patients
with HHC.
High doses of vitamin C facilitate iron release and increase prooxidant and free radical activity.
Therefore patients with
HHC should not take supplemental vitamin C but can continue
to eat fresh produce containing this substance. Other dietary recommendations include the reduction or avoidance of food containing high doses of iron, such as red meat and internal organs.
Uncooked shellfish is a particular hazard because of the risk of
contamination with
V. vulnificus.
Some alcoholic drinks contain iron, and all are potentially hepatotoxic. Alcohol should be
consumed only occasionally because it seems to have a synergistic
effect in the presence of iron overload on the development of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.
Maintenance of normal
body weight is important to avoid the hepatic damage associated
with steatosis.
Just as the pathogenesis of joint pain in HHC is unclear, treatment of arthritis in this condition is unsatisfactory, and joint
symptoms may progress despite effective phlebotomy. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, colchicine, and intra-articular corticosteroids may be helpful in some cases. Osteoporosis is a potential
disease complication, particularly in the setting of hypogonadism
or reduced thyroid function. Hormone replacement, if indicated,
should be instituted, although some patients may require additional treatment with vitamin D and bisphosphonates.
Although animal studies suggest that iron overload contributes
to an overall risk of carcinogenesis, little epidemiologic information to date suggests that this phenomenon applies to humans with
HHC. Nevertheless, age-appropriate screening for malignancy
may be considered in the holistic approach to such patients.
Note should be made of an unexpected reduction in the need for
maintenance phlebotomy in patients with HHC as this may be an
indicator of occult blood loss (e.g., from the gastrointestinal tract),
particularly in older individuals, necessitating further investigations to rule out malignancy.

## Outcomes

The earlier HHC is diagnosed, the better the prognosis, because
morbidity and mortality are directly related to the extent of iron
overload and consequent organ compromise. The absolute risk of
liver damage is approximately 5% in homozygous males and 1%
in females.
The development of cirrhosis, however, is a robust
indicator of reduced longevity. For patients with HHC-related
hepatic failure who undergo liver transplantation, survival rates
are lower compared with people who receive liver transplants for
other indications. Post-operative death in these circumstances is
typically due to cardiac complications or infection.
In the absence of cirrhosis or diabetes, patients with HHC
have a normal life expectancy.
Given the importance of timely
recognition of this common metabolic problem, a vigilant physician can make an enormous difference in the lives of patients
who present with early symptoms of this disease. In this context,
the rheumatologist has a particularly relevant role in keeping a
high index of suspicion for the diagnosis of HHC in patients with
atypical osteoarthritis or chondrocalcinosis.

## Conclusion

Cost-effective screening focused on people at greatest risk of
clinical disease, in association with a high level of diagnostic
suspicion in patients with relevant symptoms and signs, should
reduce the number of patients presenting with irreversible
organ damage from HHC. In addition, greater understanding
of the genetic and environmental modifiers of clinical disease
expression may help delay or mitigate the consequences of
iron overload in susceptible individuals. How comorbid disease influences the onset and extent of disease is particularly
relevant in the context of the growing numbers of patients with
obesity, metabolic syndrome, and hepatic steatosis. Although
phlebotomy has been the mainstay of treatment for many years,
exact targets for iron stores and ferritin levels warrant further
investigation. New chelators are under review, and the development of hepcidin peptides/agonists, which may be able to
correct the physiologic deficit linked to increased iron absorption and deposition, hold promise for the future and may contribute to our understanding of clinical penetrance in patients
with genetic susceptibility to iron overload.
In the meantime, the predictive value of symptom combinations in relevant
people may influence screening guidelines and facilitate a costeffective way of reducing the disease burden in populations at
risk of HHC.
