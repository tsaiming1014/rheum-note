## Introduction

Glucocorticoids have been a vital treatment for rheumatic diseases
since their discovery by Drs. Philip Hench and Edward Kendall.
Dr. Hench had observed an improvement in rheumatoid arthritis
(RA) symptoms among some patients with jaundice and during
pregnancy and, although these effects were likely not related to
glucocorticoids, his observation led to a search for an intrinsic
substance that could treat RA with a focus on the adrenal cortex.
Fortuitously, his colleague Dr. Kendall had isolated “Compound
E,” a glucocorticoid later named cortisol, from the adrenal glands.
In 1948 Dr. Hench’s treatment of patients with RA with “Compound E” led to dramatic effects
—a finding that led the pair to
be awarded the Nobel Prize in medicine.
Since that time, the anti-inflammatory and immunoregulatory effects of glucocorticoids have made them a mainstay of
treatment for a wide range of autoimmune conditions, in addition to use in the treatment of certain malignancies, asthma
and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, infections, and
other inflammatory processes.
While other chapters detail the
treatment of individual autoimmune rheumatic conditions,
including the use of glucocorticoids, this chapter provides an
in-depth discussion of glucocorticoids as a therapeutic. First, the
mechanisms and pharmacology of glucocorticoids are discussed,
including recent discoveries of the cell-type-specific nature of
glucocorticoid effects. An overview of the use of glucocorticoids
in the treatment of autoimmune rheumatic diseases includes
evidence guiding appropriate doses of high-dose glucocorticoids
as induction therapy, the emergence of glucocorticoid-sparing
regimens, and evidence guiding long-term use of low-dose glucocorticoids. Finally, the chapter includes a detailed examination
of glucocorticoid adverse effects.

## Structure and Classification of Steroid Hormones

Glucocorticoids are a type of steroid hormone, which are compounds derived from cholesterol that share a common backbone
of 17 carbon atoms arranged in four rings (the cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene backbone) (
Fig. 60.1
). In humans, cortisol
(called hydrocortisone when used as a medication) is the primary
endogenous glucocorticoid and is produced in the adrenal cortex.
The adrenal cortex also produces additional steroid hormones:
dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), which can subsequently be
converted to the sex hormones testosterone and estrogen, and the
mineralocorticoid aldosterone. The word
steroid
, in fact, refers to
a large class of hormones that includes glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids (collectively termed “corticosteroids”), sex hormones, and other hormones. While patients may be more familiar
with the word
steroids
than
glucocorticoids
, steroid may sometimes
lead to confusion with anabolic-androgenic steroids (synthetic
versions of the sex hormone testosterone), which are misused by
some weight lifters. The therapeutics used to treat autoimmune
rheumatic diseases are most precisely termed
glucocorticoids
.
A number of synthetic glucocorticoids are used in the treatment of rheumatic diseases, with prednisone, prednisolone, methylprednisolone, and dexamethasone the most commonly used oral
glucocorticoids. These compounds have small structural modifications compared with cortisol, as shown in
Fig. 60.1
, which affect
solubility, half-life, glucocorticoid-receptor binding, and mineralocorticoid activity (see the “Pharmacology” section).

## Glucocorticoid Mechanisms of Action

### Genomic Mechanisms of Action

Endogenous cortisol and synthetic glucocorticoids act by binding
the glucocorticoid receptor (GR) in the cytosol and translocating to
the nucleus. The GR–glucocorticoid complex then regulates transcription by directly binding to DNA at multiple glucocorticoid
response elements (GREs), leading to transactivation or transrepression (
Fig. 60.3
).
Effects are dose-dependent, with different
glucocorticoid concentrations needed for GR binding for different GREs.
In addition, the GR–glucocorticoid complex further
affects transcription through interference with other transcription
factors such as nuclear factorκ
B (NFκ
B).
Glucocorticoids have
effects on the transcription of thousands of genes—an estimated
20% of the genome—affecting metabolism, inflammation, development, and other processes.
Recent work has revealed that
actions of glucocorticoids are highly cell-type specific, with the
majority of glucocorticoid responsive genes differing across cell
types.
This cell-type-specific response is driven by differences in
chromatin accessibility, expression of GR cofactors, and possibly
by cell-type-specific GR isoforms and post-translational modiThrough regulation of genes in different cell types,
fication.
glucocorticoids have wide-ranging physiologic effects, including
effects not only on hematopoetic cells of the innate and adaptive
immune system but also on osteoblasts, chondrocytes, myoblasts,
fibroblasts, endothelial cells, adipocytes, hepatocytes, and other
cell types.
Effects on these other cell types may contribute to
both therapeutic and adverse effects of glucocorticoids. The fact
that glucocorticoids have effects on thousands of genes across
multiple cellular targets has made it particularly challenging to
identify and isolate the specific mechanisms driving therapeutic
response in different rheumatic diseases.

### Immunologic and Anti-inflammatory Effects of Glucocorticoids

Glucocorticoids are most commonly employed for their immunomodulatory and anti-inflammatory effects. These effects are
thought to be primarily due to effects on immune cells and,
indeed, glucocorticoids have varying effects on a wide range of
adaptive and innate immune cells including T helper and cytotoxic T cells, B cells, mast cells, macrophages, neutrophils, basophils, eosinophils, natural killer cells, and dendritic cells (
Table
60.1
).
In many but not all cases these effects involve downregulation of gene transcription (transrepression) within these cell types.
For example, glucocorticoid effects have been shown to lead to an
impairment in B cell receptor and Toll-like receptor signaling. Yet
glucocorticoids have also been found to lead to upregulation of
interleukin-10 expression in B cells and monocytes, an inhibitory
cytokine.
In addition, there is evidence from mouse models that
some anti-inflammatory effects of glucocorticoids may also be
mediated by effects on mesenchymal cells such as chondrocytes.

### Other Physiologic Effects of Glucocorticoids and Mechanisms Driving Adverse Effects

As discussed in the “Adverse Effects of Glucocorticoids” section,
glucocorticoids are associated with a wide range of adverse effects.
While the increased risk of infection is directly related to the
immunologic effects of glucocorticoids, other adverse effects are
driven by different mechanisms. The most studied mechanisms
are glucocorticoid effects on glucose metabolism, bone, and muscle. Effects are complicated and again are mediated by effects on
multiple cell types.
Glucocorticoid effects on glucose metabolism involve effects
on adipocytes, hepatocytes, myocytes, and pancreatic beta cells.
Glucocorticoids lead to insulin resistance in the liver, adipose tissue, and skeletal muscle, with glucocorticoid-induced diabetes
having a mechanism similar to that of type 2 diabetes mellitus.
Effects on hepatocytes also lead to increased gluconeogenesis in
the liver.
In addition, glucocorticoids may cause pancreatic beta
cell dysfunction, limiting compensatory increases in insulin production to counteract insulin resistance.
In addition to notable effects on glucose metabolism in muscle,
glucocorticoids can exert catabolic and anti-anabolic effects that
can promote breakdown of muscle proteins and induce myocyte
apoptosis, resulting in adverse changes to body composition.
These effects are primarily observed at moderate to high doses.
Glucocorticoids also inhibit amino acid transport and disrupt the
insulin-like growth factor-1 pathways, which can reduce the ability
to maintain muscle integrity and promote muscle growth.
Glucocorticoids thus can lead to muscle wasting by reducing anabolic
protein synthesis and increasing protein degradation.
Muscle loss
can be mirrored by changes in fat distribution, as glucocorticoids
also have direct effects on adipose tissue metabolism and can promote fat redistribution toward greater accumulation in the visceral

osteocytes. Glucocorticoids inhibit osteoblast proliferation,
increase osteoblast apoptosis and autophagy, and reduce the production of bone matrix proteins. Suppression of osteoprotegerin
and an increase in the receptor activator of NFκ

proteolytic enzymes such as collagenase 3 in osteoclasts. In osteocytes, glucocorticoids can cause apoptosis and increase the release

cardiovascular adverse effects. The mechanisms underlying these
adverse effects are less well understood and remain active areas of
investigation.

The therapeutic potency of glucocorticoids depends on their relative glucocorticoid activity. This difference in potency can also
be stated in terms of equivalent glucocorticoid doses of different

∼

hypoalbuminemia might be hypothesized to benefit from reduced
dosing of glucocorticoids in order to avoid toxicity. Differences
in plasma protein binding may also affect central nervous system
penetration, although whether these differences are relevant in
the treatment of autoimmune rheumatic disease is unclear.
The

Some

this is unclear. Other traits have been associated with variability
in free-form glucocorticoid levels including biologic sex, body

and a number of mechanisms have been described that include
variability in pharmacokinetics, genetic variants, local variation in

dosing guidelines based on these factors do not currently exist.

including alternative-day dosing regimens, the goal of which is
to preserve normal pituitary-adrenal responsiveness. While some
small studies have suggested benefits, there are no large trials to
routinely support this approach.

### Repository Corticotropin Injection

Repository corticotropin injection is an intramuscular injection
that includes ACTH analogs and other pituitary peptides. This
therapy increases endogenous cortisol release from the adrenal
glands and also has other nonglucocorticoid effects that have not
been fully elucidated.
Although studied in several autoimmune
rheumatic conditions,
the expense of this therapy and a lack
of studies directly comparing its effectiveness and safety to glucocorticoids limits widespread use at this time.

## Glucocorticoid Use in the Treatment of Autoimmune Rheumatic Diseases

Glucocorticoids are commonly used for the treatment of a wide
range of autoimmune and inflammatory rheumatic conditions.
In the interest of having consistent terminology, definitions for
what is considered low, moderate, and higher doses have previously been published, although there are substantial differences
in therapeutic and adverse effects even within these categories
(
Table 60.3
).
Details on the use of glucocorticoids for individual autoimmune rheumatic diseases are discussed within chapters focusing
on the treatment of these conditions. This section focuses on
overall themes related to use of glucocorticoids, using data from
several different autoimmune conditions as examples. Specifically, the continued debate around the role of glucocorticoids as
a disease-modifying therapy and as bridging therapy in RA is discussed, as is the role of long-term use of low-dose glucocorticoids
across autoimmune rheumatic diseases. The use of glucocorticoids
as induction therapy in severe autoimmune rheumatic diseases
and emerging data guiding specific glucocorticoid tapering regimens and movement toward glucocorticoid sparing regimens are
also covered.

### Glucocorticoid Use in Rheumatoid Arthritis

RA was the first autoimmune rheumatic condition in which
glucocorticoids were tested as a therapeutic,
and more than 70
years later glucocorticoids continue to be commonly used.
While more data exist evaluating the use of glucocorticoids in
RA compared with other diseases, the specific indications for

glucocorticoid use remain controversial, with conflicting recommendations from professional societies and significant variability
in use across providers.
Glucocorticoids as Short-Term Bridging Therapy and
Effects on Radiographic Progression in Patients With
Rheumatoid Arthritis
Several clinical trials have examined the use of glucocorticoids as
part of initial therapy of RA. The Combinatietherapie Bij Reumatoide Artritis (COBRA) trial found that combination therapy
with sulfasalazine, low-dose methotrexate (7.5 mg/week, stopped
at week 40), and prednisolone (60 mg tapered down to 7.5 mg
over 6 weeks and stopped at week 28) led to improvements in
disease activity over the first 28 weeks of the trial, although after
discontinuation of prednisolone there were no significant differences in disease activity between groups.
The combination
therapy group, however, had less radiographic damage over the
course of the trial and lower rates of radiographic progression in
long-term follow-up over the next 5 years.
The COBRA strategy
was also compared with more commonly used current regimens
in the BeSt trial.
Starting with the COBRA regimen of sulfasalazine, low-dose methotrexate, and prednisolone led to similar
clinical outcomes and similar rates of radiographic progression
compared with initial combination methotrexate and infliximab.
Compared with initial use of methotrexate with step-up combination therapy, starting with the COBRA regimen led to improved
clinical outcomes at 6 months and lower rates of radiographic
progression, although clinical differences were not apparent at 12
months and differences in radiographic progression were small.
Lower doses of glucocorticoids have also been studied, with similar clinical outcomes with a “COBRA-light” strategy including
weekly methotrexate titrated up to 25 mg/week in combination
with prednisolone starting at 30 mg, tapered to 7.5 mg at week 9,
and continued through week 28.
A recent trial of low- or highdose glucocorticoids (10 mg or 60 mg) tapered over 12 weeks versus placebo as bridging therapy in patients initiating methotrexate
found differences at disease activity at 26 weeks but not at 52
weeks, with no differences in radiographic progression.
Taken together, these and other studies demonstrate that initial use of glucocorticoids can lead to more rapid improvements
in disease activity than conventional disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) alone, although clinical improvements are not sustained once glucocorticoids are discontinued.
While these studies suggest that glucocorticoids indeed have small
impacts as disease-modifying therapies by reducing radiographic
damage, short courses of glucocorticoids (e.g., 12 weeks) seem to
have minimal effects on radiographic progression. Furthermore,
the role of glucocorticoids as disease-modifying therapies may be
less relevant in the current era of highly effective therapies, which
limit radiographic progression in the majority of patients. The
clinical benefits of glucocorticoids have justified their use as shortterm bridging therapy for some patients starting or switching
DMARDs, especially those with high disease activity and substantial functional limitations. It is notable, however, that the onset
of action of biologic and targeted synthetic DMARDs (i.e., Janus
kinase [JAK] inhibitors) is more rapid than that of conventional
therapies. For example, in the BeST trial there were no differences
in disease activity at 3 months among those that received methotrexate and a TNF biologic compared with those that received high
doses of prednisone.
The use of glucocorticoids as a bridging
therapy to provide short-term clinical disease activity benefits is
endorsed by European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(EULAR) guidelines,
although current guidelines from the
American College of Rheumatology (ACR) conditionally recommend against using glucocorticoids as bridging therapy because of
the difficulty tapering off of glucocorticoids for many patients.
As with many therapies, a consideration of individual patient factors and patient preference is important when making decisions
about glucocorticoid use as bridging therapy.
Long-Term Use of Low-Dose Glucocorticoids in Patients
With Rheumatoid Arthritis
Although both EULAR and ACR guidelines recommend against
the use of glucocorticoids as a long-term treatment in most
patients, 30% to 40% of patients with RA remain on low-dose
glucocorticoids long term.
It is clear from a number of trials
that low-dose glucocorticoids at doses described by some as “physiologic” (i.e., prednisone <10 mg/day) can provide clinical benefit
for patients with RA. In the CAMERA-II (Computer Assisted
Management in Early Rheumatoid Arthritis trial-II), the addition
of prednisone 10 mg to methotrexate led to lower disease activity
and disability and less need for additional therapies over a 2-year
period.
Similarly, in the BARFOT (Better Anti-Rheumatic
PharmacOTherapy) trial, the addition of prednisolone 7.5 mg/
day to DMARD therapy increased the rate of disease remission
at 2 years.
More recently the 2-year GLORIA (Glucocorticoid
LOw-dose in RheumatoId Arthritis) trial found that prednisolone
5 mg/day led to modestly lower disease activity compared with
placebo.
Additionally, the Steroid EliMination in Rheumatoid
Arthritis (SEMIRA) trial demonstrated that low-dose glucocorticoids provide benefit to some patients receiving biologic therapy.
In this trial, patients with low disease activity who were receiving tocilizumab and prednisone 5 mg/day were randomized to
taper or continue prednisone; disease flares were more common
in patients who discontinued prednisone, although two-thirds of
patients were able to taper off of glucocorticoids without experiencing a disease flare.
Based on the SEMIRA trial, patients who have achieved low
disease activity but remain on glucocorticoids should attempt
glucocorticoid tapering, as many of these patients are able to discontinue them altogether. For patients who have worsening disease activity with glucocorticoid tapering, however, decisions on
whether to change DMARD therapy or continue glucocorticoids
requires weighing risks and benefits and considering patient preferences. Existing clinical trials are not powered to assess adverse
effects, although the GLORIA trial did find an increase in the risk
of nonserious infections in patients receiving glucocorticoids and
a numerically higher rate of serious infections.
These findings are
in line with data from observational studies using claims databases
demonstrating a modest increase in infection risk even with prednisone
≤
5 mg/day, similar in magnitude to the risk of infection
with biologic therapies.
Impacts of low-dose glucocorticoids on
long-term metabolic outcomes, osteoporosis, and cardiovascular
disease remain uncertain. Some but not all studies have suggested
cardiovascular risk even with doses
≤
5 mg/day.
These outcomes are discussed in more detail in the section “Adverse Effects
of Glucocorticoids.”

### Long-Term Use of Low-Dose Glucocorticoids in Other Autoimmune Rheumatic Diseases

Glucocorticoids are also commonly used at low doses in numerous
other autoimmune rheumatic conditions. As has been observed in
RA, low doses of glucocorticoids have demonstrated effectiveness

in maintaining disease control in many of these conditions. Longterm glucocorticoid use is even more frequent in patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) than in patients with RA, with
more than half receiving them long term.
EULAR guidelines
for the treatment of SLE recommend that glucocorticoids should
minimized to <7.5 mg/day and discontinued when possible.
An
open-label, randomized trial of patients with SLE with clinical
quiescent disease for 1 year on a regimen including prednisone 5
mg found that patients were more likely to have a disease flare if
they discontinued versus continued prednisone 5 mg, although, as
in the SEMIRA trial for RA, about two-thirds of patients were able
to discontinue prednisone without experiencing a flare.
These
results demonstrate the effectiveness of low-dose glucocorticoids
for maintaining remission but also support EULAR recommendations to attempt glucocorticoid tapering in patients who are doing
well.
A similar glucocorticoid tapering trial (The Assessment of
Prednisone In Remission Trial [TAPIR]) is currently underway for
patients with granulomatosis with polyangiitis.

### How to Taper Low-Dose Glucocorticoids

As discussed previously, mounting evidence in different diseases
suggests that a substantial number of patients on long-term lowdose glucocorticoids who have achieved disease control are able
to discontinue them and maintain disease control, although some
will have disease flares. Direct evidence on how to best taper glucocorticoids is limited.
In the SEMIRA trial, patients on prednisone 5 mg reduced their dose by 1 mg every 4 weeks, with no
episodes of adrenal insufficiency reported.
This approach is commonly employed in practice and has the advantages of limiting the
risk of adrenal insufficiency and allowing patients and clinicians
to identify the lowest needed dose of glucocorticoids if disease
activity increases during tapering. Patients who have been on glucocorticoids for shorter periods of time may be able to discontinue
them more quickly.

### Glucocorticoids as Induction Therapy in Life- Threatening or Organ-Threatening Autoimmune Rheumatic Diseases

Glucocorticoids are used at high doses (e.g., prednisone
≥
30 mg/
day) in a number of autoimmune rheumatic conditions with lifethreatening or organ-threatening manifestations, with the goal
of rapidly controlling inflammation. Specific evidence to guide
the optimal glucocorticoid doses in these conditions is limited,
although an increasing number of studies have begun to compare glucocorticoid regimens or evaluate glucocorticoid sparing
regimens.
High-dose IV glucocorticoids at doses 250 to 1000 mg/day are
commonly used in patients with severe, organ-threatening manifestations of SLE, although the optimal initial glucocorticoid doses
remain uncertain and it is possible that lower doses could achieve
similar outcomes for many patients.
After IV glucocorticoids

A recent trial examining the

In this study, use

with use of a more conventional 20-week glucocorticoid taper.
Together, these studies demonstrate accumulating data to
guide initial glucocorticoid dosing and glucocorticoid tapers in
severe autoimmune rheumatic diseases. Over time it is likely that
an increasing number of reduced-dose or glucocorticoid-free
induction regimens will be evaluated, assessing comparative effectiveness and just as critically comparative safety.

### Cardiovascular Risk With Glucocorticoids

As already discussed, high doses of glucocorticoids have a number
of metabolic effects that are likely to contribute to a greater risk of
premature heart disease. In addition to adverse effects on metabolism, glucocorticoids may have direct effects on the heart and the
vasculature.
It is perhaps not surprising, then, that high doses of
glucocorticoids have been linked to a higher risk of cardiovascular
events in several studies.
The risks of low doses of prednisone
are less clear, with some studies demonstrating a significant risk
even for doses <5 mg/day and others showing no increase in risk
with low doses.
Because glucocorticoids are used in greater
amounts during periods of more active and severe illness, there
is a significant concern for confounding in epidemiologic studies. Clinical trials of low-dose prednisone have not demonstrated
higher rates of cardiovascular events, although these studies are
underpowered to detect small differences in risk.

### Effects of Glucocorticoids on Bone Health and Fracture Risk

The direct effects of glucocorticoids on bone have been well defined.
Glucocorticoids have direct effects on osteoblasts, osteocytes, and
osteoclast precursors and act to increase bone resorption and
inhibit bone formation.
Glucocorticoid use and resulting bone
loss has also been defined as a clinical risk factor for osteoporotic

fracture in prior studies, even at low doses.
These well-defined
risks have led to guidelines aimed at prevention of osteoporosis
and fracture among patients using glucocorticoids over the long
term.
In these guidelines, bone mineral density screening is recommended for patients over 40 years of age within 6 months of the
initiation of glucocorticoids in order to facilitate more accurate risk
prediction. These guidelines recommend using the FRAX™ calculator to determine 10-year risks of osteoporotic fractures. These
estimates should be increased by 15 to 20% for prednisone doses
>7.5 mg daily. FRAX thresholds for prophylaxis among glucocorticoid users are quite low compared with standard thresholds (10%
for major osteoporotic fracture and 1% for hip fracture) such that
older patients are often observed to meet the threshold for prophylaxis. Delays in initiating bisphosphonate therapy should be
avoided since the greatest bone loss may occur early, particularly
with the initiation of high doses of glucocorticoids.
Unfortunately rates of adherence to these guidelines are low.
High-dose and prolonged use of glucocorticoids is also associated with osteonecrosis, although the mechanism has not been
defined.
Patients experiencing osteonecrosis often have additional risk factors.
Osteonecrosis may be asymptomatic or can
present with significant pain, primarily with weight bearing.
Patients are at risk for progression and continued use of glucocorticoids may increase the risk of new lesions.

### Glucocorticoids and the Risk of Myopathy

Myopathy is underrecognized as a side effect of chronic glucocorticoid use.
While myopathy is unusual at low doses (<10 mg/day
prednisone equivalents), glucocorticoids can induce muscle weakness at high doses within weeks (although onset is highly variable).
Myopathy is more common among older, physically inactive patients.
Muscle weakness typically occurs gradually and preferentially in large,
proximal muscle groups, sometimes resulting in functional limitations. There is no specific diagnostic testing, but symptoms generally
begin to improve after 2 to 4 weeks with appropriate dose reduction.

### Other Glucocorticoid Side Effects

Glucocorticoids are associated with numerous other side effects
that are dose-dependent, including sleep disturbance, psychiatric
effects (irritability, insomnia, mood swings, and, in some cases,
psychosis), skin changes (striae, ecchymoses, acne), glaucoma,
and cataracts (
Fig. 60.4
). Poor wound healing is also observed in
patients that have received chronic systemic glucocorticoids, particularly at high doses.
The association between glucocorticoids
and peptic ulcer disease is less clear based on conflicting reports,
although some studies suggest up to a twofold increase in risk.
Routine gastrointestinal (GI) prophylaxis with glucocorticoids,
however, is not recommended. Given the higher risk among those
that use both glucocorticoids and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs, this combination should be used with caution and prompt
consideration of the addition of a proton-pump inhibitor.
Among those taking lower doses of glucocorticoids, the most
commonly described side effects include acne, skin ecchymoses,
cataracts, and weight gain.
Of these common side effects, weight
gain was the most important to patients in one survey study.
The GLORIA trial, evaluating the benefits and risks of low-dose
prednisone in patients with RA, demonstrated a numerically
higher risk of infection (28.9 vs. 21.9%), GI disorders (20.8 vs.
14.6%), and metabolic disorders (7.9 vs. 2.8%) and twice the risk
of skin and subcutaneous disorders (17.4 vs. 8.4%).

### Capture and Measurement of Glucocorticoid Adverse Effects

A specific glucocorticoid adverse event measurement instrument
called the Glucocorticoid Toxicity Index was developed by a committee of experts across a range of medical specialties for use in clinical trials (
Table 60.5
).
This instrument includes 31 items across
9 domains, with weighting to account for differences in severity.
Domains include weight (body mass index), glucose tolerance,
blood pressure, lipids, bone density, steroid myopathy, skin toxicity,
neuropsychiatric toxicity, and infection. A separate “specific list” of
less common but severe adverse effects was also included for capture
in trials. This tool is designed to allow quantification of multiple
adverse effects in a single instrument, and not all adverse effects of
glucocorticoids are included. A particularly notable omission is cardiovascular events, which were excluded because of the potential for
confounding by comorbidities or disease severity.
The Outcome Measures in Rheumatology (OMERACT) Glucocorticoid Impact Working Group subsequently has identified a
core domain set that they recommend should be captured in clinical
trials to evaluate the adverse effects and benefits of glucocorticoids
(
Fig. 60.5
).
This core domain set was developed by incorporating
data from literature review and patient surveys and through a Delphi process that included patients, clinicians, and researchers—the
consideration of which glucocorticoid effects are most important to
patients is a particularly important aspect of this work. In addition
to effects of glucocorticoids on disease control, mandatory domains
recommended to measure in all trials include infection, bone fragility, mood disturbance, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, weight,
fatigue, and death. Additional domains that were considered
important but were noted as optional because they are either rare
or usually occur with high doses or long-term follow-up included
osteonecrosis, eye problems, physical appearance, and sleep disturbance. Domains overall are similar to that in the Glucocorticoid
Toxicity Index, although myopathy, lipid changes, and skin changes
are not included in the OMERACT core domain set, and effects on
fatigue and mortality are not included in the GTI. While these measures are intended to guide capture in clinical trials, they may also
serve as a reminder of some of the most important adverse effects of
glucocorticoids, although not all are included. Future work should
include the identification of instruments that best characterize and
quantify effects in each of the OMERACT domains both for use in
trials and for use in clinical practice.

## Conclusion

More than 70 years after their discovery, glucocorticoids remain
a key therapy for the treatment of rheumatic diseases. The wideranging effects of glucocorticoids contribute to both their therapeutic benefit and adverse effects, and extricating beneficial
effects from harms has thus far proved elusive. Consequently, clinicians are frequently faced with the challenge of balancing the
risks and benefits of glucocorticoids, requiring an understanding of the dose-dependent risks of glucocorticoid therapy. Fortunately, accumulating evidence is providing more data-driven
approaches for the use of glucocorticoids. While glucocorticoids
will remain an important therapeutic tool for the foreseeable
future, there are an increasing number of reduced-glucocorticoid

or glucocorticoid-free treatment regimens under study which aim
to improve outcomes and reduce side effects for patients with
rheumatic diseases.
