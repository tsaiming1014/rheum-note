## Epidemiology

### Incidence and Prevalence

Scleroderma is a rare disease with an estimated incidence of approximately 18 to 20 cases per million population per year and a prevalence
of 100 to 300 cases per million population. Reported rates vary based
on the method of ascertainment, the population under study, and
the definition of disease. Scleroderma is found in all races and in various geographic areas, but the prevalence and severity of disease vary
among different racial-ethnic groups. The prevalence of scleroderma
appears to be greater in the United States, where it has been estimated
at 24.2 per 100,000, than in Europe. Studies conducted in Iceland,
England, France, Poland, Norway, and Greece have estimated rates
ranging from 7.1 to 15.8 per 100,000.
In Australia, estimates are
similar to those in the United States, whereas in Asia (i.e., Japan
and Taiwan), a lower prevalence (3.8 to 5.6 per 100,000) has been
reported.
The highest scleroderma prevalence has been reported
among Choctaw Native Americans (469 per 100,000), in whom the
disease appears to be more severe than in other populations.
Several surveys in the United States show that African Americans have
a higher age-specific incidence rate and experience more severe disease compared with subjects of European ancestry.
Disease occurrence does not differ in urban and rural areas. Occasional reports have
described unusually large numbers of cases of scleroderma clustered
in restricted geographic regions, suggesting nonrandom distribution
of the disease. For example, in a rural area in the province of Rome,
a geographic cluster of scleroderma was reported with a prevalence
1000 times greater than expected.
Likewise, a study in the United
Kingdom found a higher scleroderma prevalence in three geographic
locations close to airports compared with other areas.
The average age at onset is between 35 and 50 years, and the
disease is more common among women (3 to 7:1 female-to-male
ratio). Disease onset in older adults is well described; it is uncommon for the disease to become manifest before the age of 25 years.
Older age at onset is associated with increased risk for multisystem
disease and, in particular, for development of pulmonary arterial
hypertension (PAH). During the period from 1963 to 1982, in
Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, the highest rates of scleroderma were
observed between the ages of 45 and 54 years in Black women
and between the ages of 55 and 64 years in White women.
Younger age at disease diagnosis among Black women compared
with White women was reported in Detroit, Michigan.
Interest
in defining pre-clinical or early forms of scleroderma has led to the
identification of patients with minimal manifestations and different risk of progression. The evolution to definite systemic sclerosis
(SSc) is more frequent in patients presenting minimal manifestations of disease such as Raynaud’s phenomenon (RP), nail-fold
capillary changes, and scleroderma-related autoantibodies.

### Survival

Mortality among patients with scleroderma is higher than in ageand sex-matched healthy individuals, with most deaths (up to
75%) being attributed directly to disease manifestations.
The
standardized mortality rate (SMR) measures the ratio of observed
death rate in a specific disease group to the death rate expected
in the general population. A comprehensive review of 29 scleroderma cohort studies published between 1988 and 2019 reported
pooled SMRs ranging from 2.72 to 3.64.
While overall survival
rates have improved in scleroderma patients over time, several
studies indicate that the SMR has not changed.

Survival in scleroderma is significantly influenced by disease
subtype, degree of internal organ involvement, and comorbid
conditions. Factors associated with poor prognosis include diffuse
skin disease, the presence of pulmonary disease (particularly PAH),
renal or cardiac involvement, severe gastrointestinal (GI) failure,
multisystem disease, older age at disease onset, and the presence
of anemia. Studies conducted on inception cohorts indicate that
early mortality is predicted by male sex, older age at disease onset,
diffuse disease subtype, development of PAH, renal crisis, and
anti-SCL70 positivity. In particular, lung and cardiac involvement
have been identified as the leading causes of scleroderma-related
mortality. A population-based study conducted in Canada found
1-, 5-, and 10-year overall survival rates in scleroderma patients of
85.0, 64.5, and 44.9%, respectively, compared with 77.1, 44.4,
and 22.0% in subjects affected by interstitial lung disease (ILD).
Both presence of diastolic dysfunction and pulmonary hypertension (PH) secondary to heart failure with preserved ejection
fraction (PH-HFpEF) have been independently associated with
increased risk of death.
Anti-Scl70 is associated with higher
overall mortality as well as increased SMR. Anti-centromere antibody (ACA) predicts a normal expected mortality. Anti-RNA
polymerase III antibody positivity is associated with diffuse skin
disease and scleroderma renal crisis (SRC), but also with a lower
frequency of ILD and survival rates, similar to those detected in
patients with ACA. A more severe disease course with increased
mortality has been reported in African American patients.
A
recent public database survey found the highest age-adjusted mortality rate among Blacks or African Americans at 5.703 per million
(95% confidence interval [CI], 5.521–5.885), followed by Native
Americans or Alaska Natives at 5.047 (95% CI, 4.428–5.667) and
Whites at 3.766 (95% CI, 3.713–3.819).
Infection, malignancy,
and cardiovascular events are expected causes of death in scleroderma patients not related to the underlying disease. However,
recent studies indicate that scleroderma is independently associated with a higher risk of developing complications from macroscopic coronary artery or cerebrovascular involvement beyond
what is expected in the general population.
Improved survival in recent years results from more assertive
management and adoption of more effective treatments for specific organ-based complications. In particular, the natural history of SRC has changed with the use of angiotensin-converting
enzyme (ACE) inhibitors. Historically, patients with this complication had a 1-year survival rate of less than 15%.
Recent
case-control studies conducted after the introduction of ACE
inhibitors suggest a 1-year survival rate greater than an 85%.
Decreased mortality over the past few decades has been confirmed
in several cohorts. In a large U.S. scleroderma center, the 10-year
survival rate from the time of disease diagnosis improved from
54% in the 1972 to 1981 patient group to 66% in the 1982 to
1991 group.

### Environmental Influences

Geographic clustering, low concordance rates in identical twins,
and case reports following exposures to toxins suggest that environmental factors may be responsible for triggering scleroderma,
but definitive proof is still lacking. Evidence of geographic clustering has been reported in London boroughs located near international airports, in a rural community near Rome, in an area of
high-density industry in Canada, in areas of mining in Montana,
and in locations with increased density of industry and waste sites
in Massachusetts.
The most compelling evidence for an environmental factor
driving the disease has been the detection of typical manifestations of scleroderma among coal miners and workers exposed
to silica. A meta-analysis including 15 case-control studies and
four cohort studies of silica exposure showed an overall odds ratio
(OR) of 2.81 (95% CI, 1.86–4.23;
p
< 0.001) and relative risk of
17.52 (95% CI, 5.98–51.37;
p
< 0.001), respectively.
Cases are
more likely to be males manifesting diffuse skin involvement and
ILD.
Although occupational exposure to solvents (e.g., paint
thinner or removers, mineral spirits, trichloroethylene, trichloroethane, perchloroethane, gasoline, aliphatic hydrocarbons, halogenated hydrocarbons, and solvents containing benzene, toluene,
or xylene), heavy metals, or polyvinyl chloride is associated with
scleroderma, the role of these agents in causing disease is controversial and remains unproven.
Case reports have suggested that the silicone present in certain
breast implants may play a role in triggering scleroderma. A study
evaluating long-term implant-related outcomes in women who
received silicone implants indicates a scleroderma standardized
incidence ratio (SIR) of 7.00 compared with the general population.
In contrast, many other epidemiologic surveys have not
found a greater incidence of scleroderma in women with breast
implants or in those exposed to silicone. Epidemics of sclerodermalike illness after exposure to dietary (l-tryptophan or adulterated
rapeseed oil), occupational (silica), or pharmacologic (e.g., bleomycin and gadolinium-based contrast media) toxins and exaggerated reactions to radiation support the idea that certain chemical
and various agents can trigger a fibrotic disease. Drugs implicated
as potentially causative for SSc-like illnesses include bleomycin,
pentazocine, and cocaine. Interestingly, cigarette smoking is not
associated with an increased risk for scleroderma.
A survey of
3319 patients (23% ex-smokers, 11% current smokers) did not
identify any major adverse effect of smoking on progression of

scleroderma-specific pulmonary or cutaneous manifestations.
A semiquantitative analysis assessing cumulative exposure to toxic
compounds in the work environment (i.e., crystalline silica,
chlorinated solvents, aromatic solvents, hydrocarbons, epoxy resins, and welding fumes) and in nonwork lifestyle settings (i.e.,
smoking, gardening, painting, woodwork) established a dosedependent association between such exposures and decline over
time of the forced vital capacity (FVC), independent of sex.
A retrospective analysis conducted in Brazilian scleroderma
patients found that myopathy was independently associated with
previous exposure to organic solvents, silica, silicone, and pesticides (OR = 2.09; 95% CI, 1.15–3.82), especially silica exposure
(OR = 3.09; 95% CI, 1.67–5.73.

## Clinical Features

### Diagnostic Criteria

A subcommittee of the American College of Rheumatology
(ACR) established the “preliminary” diagnostic criteria in 1980
based on expert consensus.
The purpose of these criteria was
to provide diagnostic certainty and consistency for research and
other documentary exercises. Consequently, despite a high specificity, their sensitivity was poor, and patients with early-stage
scleroderma and 20% of those with limited cutaneous disease
did not meet these criteria.
Since then, knowledge about the
prognostic relevance of scleroderma-specific autoantibodies has
significantly improved, and the use of nail-fold capillaroscopy has
become a widely accepted, reliable diagnostic tool, supporting the
recommendation by some experts to revise the 1980 classification criteria to be more inclusive of patients with early disease

### Classification and Clinical Subsets

The scleroderma disease process is complex, and its clinical expression is heterogeneous, such that the disease is expressed in several
distinct clinical phenotypes. Classifying patients into subtypes is
useful both for investigative purposes and for clinical practice.
A specific subtype can define increased risk for internal organ
involvement and overall prognosis. The traditional way to classify patients is by the degree of skin thickening as determined by
physical examination. The skin is scored by pinching a fold and
deciding whether it is abnormally thickened because of the scleroderma process. A committee of experts decided by consensus that
two major groups of patients can be identified based on the distribution of skin changes and associated clinical and laboratory
outcomes (see
Fig. 85.1
).
Patients are considered to have diffuse
skin disease if skin changes are found proximal to the elbows and/
or knees or on the trunk, excluding the face. These patients tend
to have higher risk of multisystem disease and poorer survival.

## General Principles of Disease Evaluation

### Natural History of Disease

The disease course and the ultimate clinical outcome vary a great
deal among patients meeting diagnostic criteria for scleroderma.
The clinical expression of the disease changes over time, with features of vascular disease such as RP presenting during early phases
followed by an inflammatory process triggering varying degrees
of skin fibrosis and internal organ involvement. Patients fall into
very distinct subtypes of scleroderma for which the disease severity
and type of internal organ involvement generally can be predicted
by the extent and pace of affected skin, race, age of onset, and
associated autoantibodies. Internal organ disease tends to be more
severe in patients with the diffuse skin type than in those with
limited scleroderma. A survey of 695 patients who had a baseline
visit within 1 year after RP onset found that incident non-RP
manifestations were skin sclerosis (75%), GI symptoms (71%),
impaired diffusing capacity for monoxide less than 80% predicted
(65%), digital ulcers (34%), cardiac involvement (32%), FVC
less than 80% predicted (31%), increased pulmonary arterial
systolic pressure more than 40 mm Hg (14%), and renal crisis
(3%).
Approximately half of these incident organ manifestations
occurred within 2 years from presentation. While patients presenting with sclerodactyly alone have an overall better long-term
prognosis, in a subset of them long-term complications such as
severe GI dysfunction, ILD, or PH can develop. Subjects with diffuse skin disease are more likely to develop internal organ disease
early in the course. A rapid rate of skin progression is associated
with early internal organ involvement and increased mortality.
Progression of skin thickening in most cases stops after several
months reaching different degrees of severity and then gradually
improves as skin recovers. In a subset of patients, severe skin and
deep tissue involvement involving most of the body surface persist
over years. (see “Skin Involvement” section).

### Measuring Disease Activity and Severity

One of the challenges in studying or caring for patients with
scleroderma consists of determining and measuring disease activity, severity, and irreversible damage. These measures are not
independent of each other and thus cannot be considered in isolation. Activity is a measure of ongoing disease that is dynamic and
reversible; it is a direct reflection of ongoing biologic processes
such as autoimmune-mediated inflammation or progressive tissue fibrosis. Severity is a measure of the impact of damage on the
whole person or a specific organ at any given time. In the extreme,
severity is a measure of irreversible end-stage damage. It is important to distinguish reversible disease activity from irreversible
damage because, although both can cause clinical distress, intervention differs for the two. Patients with scleroderma can accumulate irreversible damage over time, and thus measuring disease

activity is more relevant early on, when disease has the potential
of being reversed.
Serial bedside measurement of changes in skin thickness (modified Rodnan skin score [mRSS]) is used as a surrogate measure
of disease activity. Active skin disease is associated with tissue
edema, stiffness, and intense pruritus. Patients will report involvement of new areas of the body during the active phase. The overall
degree of involvement is the level of severity, which is a predictor of the extent of organ involvement and overall prognosis (see
“Skin Involvement” section).
The Rodnan skin score is used as
a quantifiable, easily obtainable, and valid score that changes over
time. Thus the skin score has been used in clinical trials as a major
outcome measure. In patients with diffuse skin disease, improvement of the skin score is associated with a better clinical outcome.
More detailed scales for specific organs are also in use, as well as
other composite measures that include functional testing and selfreported clinical outcomes. For example, a skin assessment tool has
been developed that combines the mRSS with a visual analogue
scale (VAS) score of the patient’s assessment of skin activity, a VAS
score of the physician’s assessment of skin activity, and measures
obtained by a durometer.
A validated questionnaire is in use
to define mild to severe GI disease.
Tools to assess severity and
outcomes in PAH as related to scleroderma include pulmonary
arterial pressures and cardiac function as measured by right heart
catheterization (RHC) and echocardiography (ECHO), exercise
testing as measured by the 6-minute walk and oxygen saturation,
New York Heart Association functional class, severity of dyspnea
on a VAS, and measures of quality of life and function obtained
by the Short Form 36 and the Health Assessment Questionnaire
and Disability Index (HAQ-DI) and physician global scale. Outcome measures considered valid in scleroderma patients include
the Raynaud’s condition score (RCS) for severity of RP, FVC, and
Mahler’s dyspnea index to monitor ILD, serum creatinine, blood
pressure, and complete blood cell count during a SRC, and serum
creatine phosphokinase (CPK) to monitor muscle disease.
A scleroderma severity scale has been developed by investigators to assess disease severity status in individual patients both at
a given time and longitudinally.
This scale is based on scoring
that ranges from 0 (normal) to 4 (end stage) for each organ system involved in scleroderma, including a general measure, along
with measures of the peripheral vascular system, skin, joints and
tendons, GI tract, lung, heart, and kidney. This severity scale has
the potential to measure disease activity if it is used in studies with
serial observations, when individual organ scales can be combined
into a composite score.
The HAQ-DI has been validated in patients with scleroderma
with evidence of correlation with objective physical signs and the
course of the disease. Higher degrees of impairment are found
by HAQ scores in patients who have diffuse skin disease, higher
skin scores, abnormal hand function, presence of myopathy or
friction rubs, and joint pain.
Change in the HAQ score occurs
with changes in skin involvement and progressive organ disease.
The HAQ is useful in evaluating patients with RP and correlates
with the severity and presence of digital ulcers. VAS instruments
that assess disease in various domains specific for scleroderma are
added to the HAQ to focus on scleroderma-specific issues, including measures of RP and digital ulcers. The Short Form 36 survey,
a simple self-report tool composed of 36 items in eight domains,
is also used to assess health-related quality of life (HRQoL). A
patient-reported outcome measure, the Cochin 17-scale, that
includes measures of mobility and complex tasks execution
and items related to cognitive function, has been evaluated in
scleroderma patients showing good reliability in capturing higher
levels of global activity limitation in patients with more severe
disease.
Developing reliable tools to assess disease activity and response
to treatment in clinical care and clinical trials has been challenging. In 2001 the European Scleroderma Study Group (EscSG) has
developed a preliminary activity index (AI) based on 11 core activity variables identified in a large multicenter cohort of scleroderma
patients.
This index provides more of an organ-specific assessment than a measure of global activity. The EscSG-AI has been
subsequently revised and tested in a non-European scleroderma
cohort, showing a moderate association with physician-rated disease activity.
Using an expert-consensus approach, a combined response
index for use in clinical trials in early diffuse scleroderma has been
developed. The ACR Composite Response Index in Systemic Sclerosis (ACR-CRISS) uses a single weighted score to measure the
likelihood of response to treatment integrating worsening or incident cases of cardiopulmonary-renal involvement with changes in
five core set measures (mRSS, FVC percentage predicted, HAQDI, and patient and clinician global assessments).
Analyses of
ACR-CRISS scores in past clinical trials has raised some concern
about its responsiveness to change due to evidence of both ceiling and floor effects. For this reason, a new “revised CRISS” has
recently been proposed to assess the proportion of patients who
achieve a predefined percentage of improvement in
≥
1 of the core
set measures.
A significant body of research has been devoted to defining
more precise measures of disease activity and prognosis, including quantification of serologic markers of inflammation, immune
activation, and circulating cellular subsets, but none has developed into a widely accepted, clinically useful tool and currently no
“gold standard” to measure disease activity in scleroderma exist.

## General Guidelines for Management

Several basic principles can be of help in managing patients with
scleroderma. The first principle is that scleroderma encompasses
different subsets of disease, each with a unique clinical phenotype;
thus carefully determining the specific subtype of the patient sets
the scene for deciding the most appropriate therapeutic approach.
Even within a given subtype, the clinical manifestations and disease activity can be highly variable, likely reflecting different susceptibility to, or risk for, systemic complications and a dynamic
biologic process that vary with time and that can shift in nature at
different stages of the disease. The initial clinical presentation of
RP suggests that vascular involvement targeting peripheral arteries
and small tissue vessels is underlying the entire disease process.
This vascular disease unique to scleroderma manifests as a multisystem fibrotic vasculopathy characterized by intimal fibrosis and
occlusion of small peripheral arteries, arterioles, and capillaries,
resulting in tissue hypoxia and injury. This generalized vasculopathy is associated with the second seemingly independent biologic
process of tissue fibrosis, which results from injury and maladaptive repair and can lead to severe organ dysfunction. Early intervention is key to prevent irreversible tissue damage.
Signs of classic inflammation can often be present during the
initial phases of the disease, particularly in the skin and soft tissues where features of edema or erythema may be visible, mimicking states of fluid overload. However, later on the fibrotic process
becomes more dominant and, even if subclinical, it can gradually drive multiorgan damage. Although the skin may be the site

of the most dramatic physical findings, scleroderma is a multisystem disease, and the physician must always search for early
organ-based complications. While a disease-modifying agent that
controls overall the clinical manifestations of scleroderma would
be ideal, most success in managing this condition has focused on
an organ-specific approach. Occult organ disease should be identified early to allow a more customized and targeted therapy. Early
intervention is most important for success; once irreversible tissue
or organ damage is established, supportive care becomes the main
and often only option. It is important to perform a full evaluation
of every scleroderma patient, even in those with very early disease
manifestations. Examination should include a baseline skin score
and careful assessment of nail-fold capillaries in that these features
can predict disease course. Autoantibodies, pulmonary function
tests (PFTs), high-resolution computed tomography (HRCT)
scan of the lungs, and echocardiogram should be included as well.
Appreciation of age of onset, gender, and race also provides relevant insight. Careful assessment of comorbid conditions including standard screening for cancer is recommended. Scleroderma
is a chronic disease that can be disfiguring, causes disabilities, and
in some cases is life-threatening. It is important to educate the
patient about the disease and the spectrum of its manifestations
and provide continued effective emotional support.
Defining the subtype of scleroderma and the evidence for existing specific organ involvement determine the intervention needed.
When a patient presents with rapidly progressive inflammatory
skin disease without early signs of other vital organ involvement,
it is reasonable to implement combination or sequential immunosuppression, followed by maintenance for an extended time. The
anti-inflammatory program can then be coupled with necessary
organ-specific therapeutic interventions based on the unfolding
clinical phenotype. The missing component for a truly comprehensive approach is the availability of a uniformly effective direct
anti-fibrotic agent that is able to prevent the progression of fibrosis
once the inflammatory phase is under control. In addition, while
scleroderma-related vascular manifestations are currently managed
with effective vasoactive drugs (e.g., RP, PH, and SRC), no clear
insight exists regarding the actual role of immunosuppression in
preventing onset and progression of the systemic vasculopathy.
Although no one agent has been shown to control the disease,
a variety of biologic drugs targeting specific inflammatory pathways and agents with some anti-fibrotic effects are used alone or in
combination to attempt control of aggressive manifestations and
prevent progressive organ disease (e.g., ILD). Patients with severe,
nonresponsive disease can also be treated with myelo- or immuneablative therapeutic approaches with or without autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation or alternatively entered into
clinical trials testing novel therapies.

## Raynaud’s Phenomenon

RP is an exaggerated vascular response of the digital arterial circulation triggered by cold ambient temperature and emotional stress.
The diagnosis of RP is based on a history of excessive cold sensitivity and recurrent events of sharply demarcated pallor and/or
cyanosis of the skin of the digits (see
Fig. 85.2
). Blanching reflects
digital arterial vasospasm, and cyanosis occurs as a result of the
deoxygenation of sluggish venous blood flow. Some skin blushing
(redness) may follow due to reactive hyperemia after regular blood
flow has been restored. Various surveys throughout the world estimate a prevalence of RP ranging from about 3% to 15% of the
general population, more commonly among females (3 to 4:1).

Cutaneous involvement in scleroderma begins with clinical
signs of inflammation, which is called the
edematous phase
because
it is characterized by edema of affected body areas. In patients with
limited scleroderma, this event is mild and is restricted to the digits; however, in the diffuse form of the disease, cutaneous swelling
and edema can be widespread and so impressive in the limbs that
it mimics a fluid overload state such as congestive heart failure.
Edema can also cause local tissue compression. For example, upon
involvement of the wrist area, patients with scleroderma are not
infrequently diagnosed with carpal tunnel syndrome (especially at
disease onset) to explain hand and wrist discomfort. In association
with edema, signs and symptoms of inflammation are common.
Erythema of the skin, intense pruritus, and pain (see
Fig. 85.7C
)
are characteristic of advancing active diffuse skin disease. This pain
has a neuropathic quality with a reported “pins-and-needles” sensation. The disease process leads to loss of skin appendages and
decreased hair growth and loss of sweat and exocrine glands; thus
the skin surface becomes dry and uncomfortable. Small papules
can be seen in areas of trauma as the result of scratching, giving
the surface a cobblestone texture. The edematous phase continues
for several weeks but eventually gives way to a fibrotic stage, with
protracted activity that may last months or years.
During the fibrotic phase, acute inflammation is clinically less
obvious, and excessive collagen deposition in the dermis thickens the skin, making it inflexible and causing further loss of skin
appendages. Fibrosis extends beyond the dermis into the deeper
layers with loss of subcutaneous adipose tissue (lipodystrophy).

elbows often appear as a result of dermal sclerosis and fibrosis with
atrophy of underlying tissues. Skin ulcers can develop as a complication of avascular fibrotic or damaged thinned skin and are
very common at sites of trauma, such as over the digital MCP or

Although highly variable, active skin involvement might persist
for the first 12 to 18 months of the disease, with no further clinical
signs of inflammation or progressive skin fibrosis seen after this
interval. During this later stage, the skin begins to repair and can
return to normal texture or, in areas most severely affected (e.g.,
the fingers and hands), it can become thin and atrophic. During
this recovery phase, new robust hair growth is seen, particularly
on the forearms, and itching and pain disappear, consistent with
spontaneous resolution of disease activity. After years from disease
onset, the skin rarely relapses again into an active phase and gradually can recover normal texture and color. Patients who, over time,
experience the establishment of a phenotype that is limited to the
fingers (plus or minus facial changes) do not undergo conversion
to the diffuse form of the disease.

### Management of Skin Disease

Patients with only mild skin thickening (as is often the case in
limited scleroderma) or those diagnosed at later stages (i.e., several
years into their disease course) typically do not require any immunosuppression for their skin disease (though this may be considered for other organ-specific manifestations, such as ILD or
musculoskeletal inflammation, discussed below).
Telangiectasias can be shrunk with laser therapy (typically performed by a dermatologist) if they are cosmetically bothersome to
the patient. However, they typically tend to redevelop over time
on the same sites. There is currently no effective medication to
treat calcinosis. Patients should be counseled to avoid repetitive
local trauma and friction over affected areas as much as possible
(e.g., wear soft elbow pads or silicone cushions on their fingertips)
and to optimize tissue blood perfusion as hypoxia appears to be a
significant trigger for calcinosis, particularly in the fingers.
Treatment of scleroderma skin involvement should be considered during the early active inflammatory phase, particularly in
patients with evidence of diffuse and progressive cutaneous disease. It is important to recognize that while skin disease is the
most clinically obvious, associated systemic manifestations and
other organ involvement need to be addressed at the same time
(see particular sections).
In the early active stage of diffuse scleroderma, pruritus can be
one of the most distressing symptoms. Antihistamines, analgesics,
or tricyclic anti-depressants (e.g., doxepin) are often used but usually provide only partial benefit. Phototherapy, low-dose naltrexone, and gabapentin can be helpful. Dryness of the skin surface
results from damage to the exocrine structures caused by decreased
or absent natural oil (sebum) production. This dryness can worsen
pruritus, leading to skin trauma as a result of repetitive scratching. Ulceration and secondary tissue infection may also result. The
best approach to treatment is characterized by frequent topical
application of an emollient preparation, periodic cleansing with
soap and water, and use of topical antibiotics for any traumatic
skin ulceration. Physical therapy is important to prevent severe
skin and joint contractures and to help patients with activities of
daily living.
It is now recognized that the early edematous phase is secondary to an acute immune-mediated inflammatory process that triggers the fibrotic disease process. A variety of immunomodulatory
drugs and newer agents with anti-fibrotic properties have been
used to attempt control of skin disease. However, the U.S. Food
and Drug Administration (FDA) to date has not approved any
therapy for cutaneous involvement in scleroderma as no randomized controlled clinical trial has showed clear superiority of one
treatment over placebo. The use of the mRSS as the major outcome measure in these studies may have contributed in part to the
failure of proving these treatments effective, as the identification
of ideal cases with truly reversible disease has been challenging.
Nonselective immunosuppressive or anti-inflammatory treatments are used in early progressing skin disease. Corticosteroids
can dampen inflammatory features such as associated arthritis or
myositis, but they have not shown efficacy in stopping skin disease progression. In addition, there is evidence that steroids can
increase the risk of SRC (see “Renal Involvement” section). Steroid use should be avoided or only used in low doses (less than 15
mg/day of prednisone).
Cyclophosphamide (CYC), methotrexate (MTX), and mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) are agents with anti-proliferative effects
on inflammatory cells, in particular on activated T and B lymphocytes, that have been used for active skin disease.
A prospective, observational cohort comparing use of MTX, CYC,
MMF, or no immunosuppression therapy to treat early scleroderma skin disease found a modest trend favoring treatment with

the immunosuppressants in terms of mean change in skin score
after 12 months, although the difference was not significant.
MTX efficacy in treating skin disease and lung function has been
investigated in two randomized placebo-controlled trials, showing
only a small benefit on skin scores after 6 months (with weekly
intramuscular injections) or after 12 months (with weekly oral
therapy), respectively.
Based on the findings of these studies, some experts use MTX for active scleroderma skin disease.
Both MMF and CYC have shown evidence of benefit in the management of active scleroderma skin disease.
MMF is typically
preferred as the initial treatment based on the evidence gathered
from uncontrolled case series and post-hoc analyses of ILD clinical trials showing favorable benefit on skin disease, good tolerance, and less toxicity compared with CYC. Further support for
MMF comes from reports of flares occurring after dose reduction
or discontinuation of MMF and the subsequent recovered disease
control after restarting the medication.
MMF is usually started
at low doses such as 500 mg orally twice daily and titrated to up
to 1500 mg twice daily. Benefit manifests with clinical evidence
of reduced skin inflammation (e.g., decreased pruritis, less edema)
and reduced or stable bedside mRSS score. Improvement generally
begins within 6 to 8 weeks after reaching fully tolerated dosing.
If the patient does not stabilize or improve on MMF with evidence of further progression into the fibrotic phase of skin disease,
then the use of other agents should be considered, although in
many cases their benefit has been shown only in small studies or
case series.
Intravenous immunoglobulins (IVIGs) exhibit immunomodulatory and anti-fibrotic properties. Administration of IVIG to
patients with scleroderma has been evaluated in small open-label
studies with some evidence that it can improve skin fibrosis, joint
disease, muscle, and digestive tract symptoms.
One trial in
Japan found that repeated administration of IVIG was required
for an improved outcome for diffuse skin disease.
Some experts
will add IVIG to MMF in cases that are not improving, particularly in the presence of associated musculoskeletal inflammatory
manifestations.
The use of rituximab (anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody) has
shown some promising results, with improvement of skin fibrosis and stabilization of lung function.
A prospective study
conducted within the EUSTAR network showed that treatment
of scleroderma patients with rituximab compared with matched
untreated subjects had a good safety profile and favorable changes
on skin fibrosis.
Cell-based immunotherapy using autologous hematopoietic
stem cell transplantation (HSCT) preceded by myeloablative
or nonmyeloablative conditioning regimens has also been studied to treat scleroderma patients with severe disease.
In the
Autologous Stem Cell Transplantation International Scleroderma
(ASTIS) trial and the American Scleroderma Stem Cell versus
Immune Suppression Trial (ASSIST), patients were randomized to receive HSCT or pulsed IV doses of CYC administered
monthly for 12 or 6 months, respectively.
Overall, these investigations confirmed previous observations that HSCT is effective
in rapidly controlling skin disease, stabilizing lung function, and
improving quality of life as assessed by patient-reported outcome
measures.
The ASTIS trial has also shown a better long-term
event-free and overall survival for HSCT compared with monthly
IV CYC. A third study, the Scleroderma Cyclophosphamide
Or Transplantation (SCOT) trial, randomly assigned patients
to undergo myeloablative autologous stem cell transplantation
(36 participants) or to receive CYC (39 participants).
Unlike
ASTIS and ASSIST, the SCOT protocol added total body irradiation to the conditioning regimen and did not use CYC for
stem cell mobilization. The primary end point was a global rank
composite score (GRCS) comparing participants with each other
based on a hierarchy of multiple disease features assessed: death,
event-free survival (survival without respiratory, renal, or cardiac
failure), FVC, the score on the HAQ-DI, and the mRSS. In the
intention-to-treat population, the GRCS at 54 months showed
that 67% of 1404 pairwise comparisons favored HSCT compared
with 33% favoring CYC (
p
= 0.01). At 72 months, Kaplan-Meier
estimates of event-free survival (74% vs. 47%) and overall survival
(86% vs. 51%) also favored transplantation (
p
= 0.03 and 0.02,
respectively). Treatment-related mortality in the transplantation
group was 3% at 54 months and 6% at 72 months, compared
with 0% in the CYC group. Nonmyeloablative (immunoablative)
conditioning regimens without HSCT have demonstrated similar
results.
Despite these positive findings, improvement of multiple organ
function (i.e., skin, lungs), and sustained drug-free remission, this
approach is not considered the standard of care for scleroderma,
particularly for the risk of treatment-related morbidity and mortality (3% in SCOT; 10.1% in ASTIS) associated with high-dose
immunosuppression and for the evidence that the disease can
relapse in time. Myeloablation with or without autologous HSCT
is an option for severe progressive scleroderma that should be performed in experienced centers with care to select patients who
have failed low-dose immunosuppression or other novel investigative therapies and who have associated visceral organ disease that
has not progressed to a stage of significant dysfunction.
Selective immunotherapy including T lymphocyte–directed
treatments such as cyclosporin A, sirolimus (rapamycin), and
anti-thymocyte globulin have some benefit.
However, these
studies were generally small and limited by drug toxicity. The use
of extracorporeal photoimmunotherapy or photopheresis has been
reported with modest improvement in skin disease but lacks any
efficacy on internal organ manifestations.
Several early-phase clinical trials (randomized and open label)
have tested the efficacy of novel therapies to treat scleroderma
skin disease, including agents directed against cytokines such as
TNF (infliximab, etanercept), interleukin (IL)-6 (tocilizumab),
IL-1 (rilonacept), transforming growth factor (TGF)-beta (fresolimumab, CAT-192), and IL-4/IL-13 (romilkimab); blocking
costimulation between antigen-presenting cells and T lymphocytes (abatacept); or inhibiting Janus kinase (JAK) 1/3 singling
(tofacitinib), soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) (riociguat), LPA1
receptor (SAR100842), and B cell function by depletion (inebilizumab) or survival antagonism (belimumab).
In general, none of these early studies have provided significant
evidence of benefit to control skin disease. IL-6 inhibition was
further tested in a phase 3 double-blind study, again comparing
tocilizumab to placebo.
While no significant reduction of the
skin tightness was observed, the favorable effect on the lung function with stabilization of the FVC over 48 weeks has led to the
FDA approval of this treatment for ILD (see “Lung” section). JAK
inhibitors have been shown to be efficacious in treating cutaneous
thickening in both mouse models of scleroderma and in patients
with refractory morphea.
In a small unblinded randomized
trial, tofacitinib 5 mg twice daily showed greater efficacy than
MTX 10 mg orally weekly in reducing skin thickness and showed
a satisfactory safety profile.
Small uncontrolled trials testing use of the tyrosine kinase
inhibitor imatinib showed no benefit and frequent adverse events

including fluid retention, nausea, fatigue, and elevation of creatine
kinase.
While nintedanib was effective in slowing down the
progression of ILD, no clear benefit for scleroderma skin disease
was detected.
d-Penicillamine, a chelating agent that blocks collagen crosslinking, was a popular therapy focused on the idea that scleroderma was mainly secondary to progressive fibrosis. However, a
double-blind, randomized clinical trial comparing the efficacy of
low-dose versus high-dose d-penicillamine in early diffuse scleroderma found no significant difference between skin scores of the
two groups at 24 months.

## Interstitial Lung Disease

Pulmonary involvement is found in most patients with scleroderma, but severe lung disease occurs in a relatively small subset
of patients. ILD and PH are recognized as the most common lung
complications and are now regarded as the major cause of death in
scleroderma. A survey of the EUSTAR database, a cohort of over
5800 patients, found that 35% of all scleroderma-related deaths
were directly attributable to ILD, with 26% of deaths due to PAH
and 4% due to renal disease.
In combination, it is estimated
that lung disease is responsible for 60% of scleroderma-related
deaths.
While ILD and PH can both occur at the same time,
one process usually dominates over the other. Lung disease can
manifest without symptoms, or it can cause a significant limitation of quality of life or progress to respiratory failure. Chronic
aspiration, pleural disease, spontaneous pneumothorax, neuromuscular weakness, drug-induced pneumonitis, and lung cancer
need to be considered in the differential diagnosis of a patient with
scleroderma who presents with respiratory symptoms.
ILD becomes clinically significant in up to 60% of patients
with diffuse cutaneous scleroderma and in 20% of patients with
limited skin disease. Higher risk of developing severe progressive
ILD has been observed in patients with diffuse skin involvement,
African Americans, Native Americans, and those positive for antitopoisomerase 1 (Scl-70), anti-U3-RNP, or anti-Th/To antibodies. Anti-topoisomerase positivity has been strongly linked to the
carriage of the human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-DRB1*11 and
HLA-DPB*1301 alleles, suggesting that a genetic predisposition
to ILD exists.
Data from a study of patients with active lung
disease show that those with limited and diffuse scleroderma were
indistinguishable with regard to their baseline pulmonary functions, although patients with limited skin disease presented with
more extensive pulmonary fibrosis, perhaps reflecting a delay in
diagnosis and more advanced progression of lung involvement
before study entry.
ILD typically manifests with declining lung volumes and
increased parenchymal fibrosis with reticular interstitial thickening that is greatest at the lung bases. The most common histologic pattern of fibrosing alveolitis in scleroderma is nonspecific
interstitial pneumonia (NSIP), as opposed to usual interstitial
pneumonia (UIP), which is the typical presentation in idiopathic
pulmonary fibrosis. A mixed pattern of NSIP and UIP can also
be found. The histopathologic classification does not appear to
predict outcome, but a greater degree of disease severity and functional respiratory measures at initial presentation are associated
with poor prognosis.
A surgical lung biopsy is not required in
the setting of patients experiencing a typical disease course because
the histopathology tends to be repetitive (NSIP) and not a useful
predictor of subsequent disease course. ILD is usually characterized by a functional restrictive ventilatory defect with reduced gas
exchange. In patients with severe involvement, honeycombing of
lung parenchyma and bronchiectasis and some modest degree of
airway obstruction can be found. Pleural reactions are not common in scleroderma. Rare cases of spontaneous pneumothorax,
adult respiratory distress syndrome, and pulmonary hemorrhage
can occur. Aspiration resulting from oropharyngeal dysfunction
or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), a secondary infection, or heart failure can all complicate the course of scleroderma
lung disease. Some investigators consider GERD associated with
chronic aspiration to be a potential inciting factor for the development of ILD in scleroderma.
During earlier stages of ILD, the underlying active fibrosing
alveolitis may be totally asymptomatic and may not be detected
by conventional chest radiographs. The most common symptoms
of scleroderma lung disease are dyspnea (initially upon exertion)
and fatigue. Chest pain is not typical, and nonproductive cough
is usually a late complication, particularly in association with the
presence of traction bronchiectasis. Coughing in patients with
scleroderma often is not the result of a primary lung problem but
rather is a manifestation of GERD with associated laryngeal irritation. The most characteristic finding of ILD on physical examination is bilateral fine inspiratory crackles (i.e., “Velcro” rales) at
the lung bases. The PFT is the method most used to detect ILD
(see
Fig. 85.10
). The earliest functional abnormality is a reduction in the single breath diffusion capacity of carbon monoxide
(DLCO). Although a decline in DLCO correlates with the severity of pulmonary fibrosis as measured by HRCT and predicts
worse outcome, it is not a very specific measure and can also be
abnormal in the context of pulmonary vascular disease and underlying chronic obstructive pulmonary disease/emphysema. Studies
find that detection of ground-glass changes or fibrosis by HRCT
scan may occur before a significant decline in lung function and
thus may be a more sensitive test to diagnose early lung disease.
A restrictive ventilatory defect is identified by a depression of the
FVC and the total lung capacity. The most reliable tool for monitoring ILD over time is serial measurements of FVC. A decline in
FVC of more than 10% from baseline is often indicative of disease
activity and is associated with increased mortality. The course of
lung disease may remain stable or exhibit worsening symptoms
after variable intervals (months or years) of unchanged lung function. In some cases, progression is invariable and leading rapidly
to end-stage lung disease. Longitudinal studies of large patient
populations find that subgroups following a unique course of lung
disease exist and are associated with specific serologic and clinical features. Patients with ACA and limited skin disease are less
likely to have ILD, whereas patients with anti-topoisomerase have
more commonly progressive disease. Patients presenting with a
normal FVC during early stages of scleroderma have a better lung
prognosis; in contrast, a reduced FVC at the initial encounter is
associated with further progression of lung involvement, usually
within the first 2 to 4 years from disease onset. The presence of
ILD predicts increased mortality. One study found that patients
with severe ILD have a 9-year survival rate of approximately 30%,
whereas patients with no severe organ involvement have a 72%
survival rate.
The most rapid decline in FVC is thought to
occur within the initial 3 years from disease onset, but progression
of ILD can also occur later on, so it is recommended to monitor
pulmonary function regularly at 6-month intervals in the early
years and then on a yearly basis.
HRCT is more accurate than chest radiography in evaluating
diffuse lung disease; it is a well-established, sensitive, and noninvasive method of detecting and characterizing ILD in scleroderma

Pathologic studies of patients with scleroderma with early lung
disease have shown that lung fibrosis is preceded by the presence of a mixed interstitial inflammatory infiltrate spilling into
the alveolar spaces (alveolitis). It has been assumed that measuring abnormal levels of inflammatory cells in the bronchoalveolar
lavage (BAL) would allow identification of patients with active
lung disease at risk for progression and who would potentially
benefit from immunosuppressive therapy. The value of BAL cytology in predicting lung disease progression or response to treatment has not been uniformly confirmed and the routine clinical
use of BAL cytology is not recommended. Other biomarkers have
been investigated to monitor the activity and progression of ILD
more accurately, but they are not yet available for routine clinical
use. Markers of alveolar cell damage such as KL-6 or surfactant
proteins A (SP-A) and D (SP-D) are increased in the serum of
patients with scleroderma who have active ILD and predict survival.
Increased levels of matrix metalloproteinases (i.e.,
MMP-7) and proinflammatory molecules such as CCL-18 or
IL-6 are also associated with declining lung function and worse

## Pulmonary Hypertension

Pulmonary arterial vascular disease represents a difficult clinical
challenge with life-threatening consequences.
It is now appreciated that small- and medium-sized arteries are a major target of
the disease process in scleroderma. This affects the systemic vascular beds including the heart and lungs, causing defects in vascular reactivity with abnormal vasoconstriction, and also leading
to abnormal vascular remodeling. An imbalance of local vascular
mediators can be detected in the blood vessels affected by scleroderma. This includes enhanced secretion of endothelin, a vasoconstrictor and fibro-proliferative mediator, and defective synthesis
of nitric oxide and loss of normal activity of prostacyclin, both
potent vasodilator molecules. These changes lead to both vasoconstriction and progressive neointima formation, luminal narrowing, and obliteration of medium and pre-capillary arteries.
Given the high capacitance of pulmonary arteries, these lesions
impair blood flow, accounting for increased resistance and the
eventual progression to PAH and its hemodynamic consequences.
The injury to the pulmonary vascular system can be mainly “precapillary” in the setting of “pure” PAH or can unfold via either or
both pre- and/or post-capillary mechanisms, manifesting as PH.
Cardiac etiologies such as left-ventricular (LV) failure can lead to
right heart dysfunction and should always be assessed carefully.
RHC is required to confirm a diagnosis of PAH (see below).
The pulmonary vascular process is indolent and can remain
clinically silent for a variable time. When it progresses to clinically
significant PH or PAH it becomes a major cause of respiratory
distress and mortality. Typical symptoms associated with clinically
manifested PAH include dyspnea upon exertion, fatigue, and, less
commonly, chest pain. Physical examination may be normal during the early stages of PAH, but as the disease progresses, a systolic
murmur of tricuspid regurgitation; a loud pulmonic component,
the S2; an S3 gallop; and signs of right heart failure (e.g., rightsided parasternal heave, prominent and elevated jugular venous
pulse, hepatomegaly, and signs of fluid overload with peripheral
edema) are appreciated. Later in the course of the disease, patients
become dyspneic with mild physical activity, have a resting tachycardia, and demonstrate hypoxia with exercise. Sudden syncope
or death can occur because of severe hypoxia and congestive heart
failure.
PAH is usually a late complication of scleroderma, typically
manifesting after 10 years from disease onset, particularly in
patients with limited cutaneous involvement. However, every
patient should be considered at risk for PH or PAH and yearly
evaluation is indicated. It is becoming more evident that in some
patients this condition may develop early and remain undetected.
Bedside examination is not very sensitive in detecting PH, particularly during early stages, and thus it is recommended that every

patient with scleroderma be screened using objective testing such
as electrocardiography (ECG), ECHO, and PFTs (see
Fig. 85.12
).
Proper initial workup also should aim at excluding non-scleroderma-related complications such as chronic thromboembolic
disease and underlying obstructive sleep apnea. The ECG can be
normal but, as disease progresses, signs of right-sided heart strain
with right-ventricular (RV) hypertrophy and right axis deviation
can be observed. Detection by ECHO of increased right-ventricular systolic pressure (RVSP) is useful, but abnormal values need
to be confirmed by RHC, given the possibility of false-positive
or false-negative results. When the ECHO estimates an RVSP
greater than 45 mm Hg, about 95% of the time this finding
will be confirmed by RHC.
Some clinicians use the tricuspid
gradient as a measure of pulmonary artery pressure. Additional
noninvasive screening includes PFTs. A low diffusing capacity
(DLCO) without evidence of obstructive or restrictive lung disease
(i.e., low FVC) suggests a defect in gas exchange that can result
from pulmonary vascular disease. DLCO may progressively decline
for years before the diagnosis of PAH is established.
Increased
serum N-terminal (NT)-pro hormone brain natriuretic peptide
(NT-proBNP) may indicate presence of PAH and right heart
strain, even when levels are modestly elevated (>395 pg/mL).

The definition of PAH has been changed to allow early intervention. It is now defined as resting mean pulmonary arterial pressure equal to or greater than 20 mm Hg, with normal pulmonary
capillary wedge pressure equal to or less than 15 mm Hg and pulmonary vascular resistance >3 wood units, although this threshold
has been recently lowered to >2 wood units in the 2022 European Society of Cardiology/European Respiratory Society guidelines for the diagnosis and treatment of PH.
In addition to
confirming abnormal hemodynamic values, the RHC can help in
differentiating PAH from pulmonary venous hypertension resulting from left heart dysfunction or pulmonary vascular occlusive
disease. The 6-minute walk test can provide baseline functional
information but does not accurately assess the severity of PAH.
Because baseline hemodynamic parameters do not correlate well
with the clinical course of PAH in patients with scleroderma, new
cardiopulmonary dynamic testing such as exercise stress ECHO
and exercise during RHC have been introduced, but their role

right heart disease can also be driven by underlying myocardial

under exercise conditions, with decreased capacity to compensate
for elevated right heart pressures generated by progressing pulmonary vascular disease. Associated myocardial right heart disease
accounts for the poor prognosis observed in scleroderma-related
PAH compared with idiopathic PAH.
Estimates of the prevalence of PAH in patients with scleroderma vary. Using ECHO as a diagnostic tool, the prevalence of
PAH has been estimated at between 30% and 50% of all studied patients, while investigations using confirmatory RHC have
detected a prevalence of 8 to 12%.
A meta-analysis reports
an overall PAH prevalence in scleroderma of 6.4% (95% CI,
5–8.3) and PAH incidence of 18.2 cases per 1000 person-years
(95% CI, 12–27.4).
Clinically, scleroderma-related pulmonary vascular disease can present and progress in some patients
as severe isolated PAH; in others, it can be associated with mild
or severe pulmonary fibrosis that is believed to contribute to lung
injury and hypoxia causing vascular disease. Association of ILD
and PAH usually entails a worse prognosis. Pulmonary vascular
disease can also develop more slowly, manifesting with dyspnea
upon exertion, isolated decline of the DLCO with no significant
signs of ILD (i.e., low lung volumes), and a normal resting ECHO
and/or RHC. These patients are considered to have microvascular
lung disease and may be at high risk of developing overt PAH.
Factors increasing the risk of developing PAH include limited skin
disease, late age at onset of scleroderma, male sex, the presence of
numerous telangiectasias, reduced capillary nail-fold density, low
DLCO, increasing estimated RVSP on ECHO, or high serum
NT-proBNP. PAH can occur in association with several scleroderma-related autoantibodies including anti-centromere, anti-U3
RNP, anti-Th/To, anti-Ku, anti-Ro52/TRIM21, and anti-U1
RNP. Patients with anti-centromere tend to develop PAH later
in the course of the disease, although not all case-control studies have identified anti-centromere as an independent risk factor
for PAH.
Patients with anti-topoisomerase I rarely develop
PAH but can manifest PH secondary to ILD and chronic hypoxia.
Pulmonary vascular disease has a major impact on quality of
life and survival of affected patients. While there is no pharmacologic cure for PAH, early intervention aims to slow or prevent
disease progression, reduce morbidity and mortality, and improve
quality of life. The natural course of the disease is characterized
by progression of hemodynamic impairment, resulting in right
heart failure and leading to a poor outcome. Despite the availability of newer targeted therapies, the median survival in patients
with scleroderma have PAH ranges between 1 and 3 years.
A
progressive rapid increase in pulmonary pressure is more likely to
occur in patients with limited skin disease and late age at the onset
of scleroderma. This more aggressive course is associated with
higher mortality. Survival in patients with ILD-associated PAH
is significantly worse (a 3-year survival of 46%) than in patients
with PAH alone (a 3-year survival of 64%).
This difference
is possibly explained by the presence of underlying comorbidities in scleroderma, in particular biventricular heart (myocardial)
involvement. One survey found that scleroderma patients with

patients with PAH.

and higher New York Heart Association functional class also have
a negative impact on prognosis.

### Management of Pulmonary Hypertension

All patients with PAH are encouraged to continue an active
lifestyle as tolerated. Formal educational and exercise programs
can improve their quality of life. Conventional therapy includes
diuretic therapy (loop and potassium-sparing diuretics) and
supplemental oxygen therapy, if needed. Anti-coagulation is not
recommended in that studies suggest a worse outcome in scleroderma patients who are anti-coagulated. CCBs play no role in
the treatment of scleroderma-related PAH. In recent years, several targeted agents have become available for the treatment of
PAH. Many clinical trials testing these drugs in idiopathic PAH
have included about 20% of patients with CTD, primarily scleroderma. Specific therapies include prostaglandin derivatives (e.g.,
epoprostenol, treprostinil, beraprost, and iloprost), endothelin
receptor antagonists (ERAs) (e.g., bosentan, sitaxsentan, ambrisentan, and macitentan), PDE5is (e.g., sildenafil, tadalafil, and
vardenafil), a selective prostacyclin receptor (IP receptor) agonist
(selexipag), and a soluble guanylate-cyclase stimulator (riociguat).
Riociguat is primarily indicated to treat inoperable and persistent/recurrent chronic thromboembolic PH and PAH. Inhaled
prostaglandins can be an option in severe cases and have shown
benefit in a large double-blind, placebo-controlled trial to treat
PH associated with ILD.
All these drugs can improve hemodynamics, exercise tolerance, and quality of life. Clinical trials do
not suggest that any one of these agents is superior to another, but
evidence indicates that prostacyclin therapy is the most effective
and should be considered immediately in patients presenting with
severe PAH.

Most clinicians believe that early intervention is most important
because it leads to better outcomes (see
Fig. 85.12
). For patients
with mild-to-moderate disease (functional class I or II), treatment
with a single oral agent is usually considered (i.e., sildenafil, bosentan, or ambrisentan). However, data from recent clinical trials
indicated a favorable survival benefit for ambrisentan and tadalafil
upfront combination therapy compared with monotherapy in
patients with newly diagnosed mild-to-moderate PAH.
Sildenafil and bosentan can also be used in combination, even
if their interaction and increased hepatotoxicity is of some concern.
Patients presenting with more severe disease (functional
class III or IV) and those who do not respond to initial therapy are
usually treated with IV or inhaled prostaglandins in combination
with oral agents. Several nonvasodilator drugs targeting inflammation, cell proliferation, hormones, growth factors, and vascular
remodeling are under investigation for PAH.
An RCT of B cell
depletion with rituximab conducted in scleroderma PAH patients
reported an adjusted mean change in 6-minute walk distance at
24 weeks favoring the treatment over placebo, although results did
not reach statistical significance.
New studies will investigate
the impact of immunosuppressive therapy on pulmonary vascular
disease. Mutations in the bone morphogenic protein receptor type
2 (BMPR2), a member of the TGF beta family, have been found
in 70%–80% of patients with familial PAH and 10%–20% of
patients with idiopathic PAH.
Sotatercept is a fusion protein
binding activins and growth differentiation factors and exhibiting the ability to promote pulmonary vascular remodeling by
impacting BMPR2 activity. In phase 2 clinical trials conducted
on patients receiving background therapy for PAH, sotatercep
improved hemodynamic and functional outcome measures at 24
weeks compared with placebo.
Lung or heart-lung transplantation for PAH remains the
treatment of last resort when medical therapy fails. Patients with
scleroderma who are carefully selected for transplantation have
an outcome that is similar to patients with other causes of lung
disease. PAH in scleroderma is a progressive disease for which no
specific or single therapy may suffice. Therefore careful longitudinal monitoring is important. Noninvasive testing with periodic
clinical assessment with the 6-minute walk distance, repeat PFTs,
and ECHO studies are helpful. When a major therapeutic decision is needed, such as switching to or adding a new therapy, a
repeat RHC may be indicated. Further improvement in survival
of patients with scleroderma who have PAH may result from
future therapeutic interventions targeting the underlying inflammatory process and the dysregulated endothelial proliferation that
mediates pulmonary vascular remodeling and consequent failure
of the right heart.

## Renal Involvement

Renal involvement in scleroderma is classically characterized by
the abrupt onset of very high blood pressure (malignant hypertension), elevated plasma renin, and rising serum creatinine reflective of acute renal failure, along with a constellation of symptoms
and clinical manifestations such as headaches, malaise, hypertensive retinopathy, encephalopathy, and pulmonary edema,
usually referred to as
scleroderma renal crisis (SRC)
. Subclinical
renal disease in scleroderma can also be present in the absence
of symptoms. Despite the frequent detection of histopathologic
renovascular lesions typical of scleroderma vasculopathy, clinically
important renal impairment, independent of SRC, is seen in only
a minority of patients.
Although SRC is the most recognized
renal complication, abnormal renal function can be explained by
factors other than intrinsic scleroderma renal disease such as medication adverse effects, comorbid conditions, or associated heart,
GI, or lung disease. Cases of biopsy-proven oxalate nephropathy
have been identified among scleroderma patients and are thought
to be related to underlying GI dysfunction.
One survey suggests that mild unexplained proteinuria without loss of renal function or evidence of glomerular disease is a common sign of renal
disease in scleroderma.
A superimposed inflammatory kidney
disease can also occur. Several cases of pauci-immune necrotizing
crescentic glomerulonephritis associated with myeloperoxidasespecific ANCAs have been reported in patients with scleroderma
who have a presentation mimicking SRC.
SRC needs to be considered in patients with scleroderma who
have sudden and severe elevation of blood pressure, with or without renal failure; in patients with sudden renal failure, with or
without hypertension; and in patients with sudden-onset microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, with or without hypertension or
renal failure. Nonmalignant hypertension alone without signs of
renal dysfunction or mild urine abnormalities is unlikely to be due
to SRC. SRC occurs in 5% to 10% of patients, mostly in those with
diffuse scleroderma and in up to 20% to 25% anti-RNA pol III
SSc patients. It is usually encountered within the first 2 to 4 years
from disease onset. The estimated median duration of scleroderma
at the time of SRC diagnosis is 8 months.
Occurrence in
later disease is uncommon.
Patients at greatest risk of developing SRC are those with diffuse cutaneous scleroderma, particularly
during phases of rapidly progressive skin disease. SRC is a rare
complication in patients with limited scleroderma (approximately
1% to 2%). African Americans and patients with diffuse skin disease who are treated with a high dose of corticosteroid (>40 mg/
day of prednisone) or low-dose cyclosporine are at increased risk
of developing SRC. A long-term use of low-dose prednisone may
also increase the risk for SRC. For this reason, it is recommended
to avoid corticosteroids or to use doses less than 15 mg daily, if
necessary. Another survey has identified additional clinical features at the time of scleroderma diagnosis that are associated with
future development of SRC: proteinuria, anemia, hypertension,
chronic kidney disease, elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate,
thrombocytopenia, hypothyroidism, and anti-Ro antibody positivity.
Antibodies to RNA polymerase III were found in about
60% of patients with SRC in one survey.
Renal crisis is also
associated with a positive ANA (speckled pattern) anti-U3-RNP
and anti-topoisomerase I antibodies, but usually is not seen in
patients with anti-centromere antibodies.
SRC presents with typical signs of malignant hypertension,
including headache, altered vision, signs of congestive heart failure,
florid pulmonary edema, confusion, or neurologic signs as a result
of hypertensive encephalopathy in the setting of an abnormally
high blood pressure, usually greater than 150/90 mm Hg. Hypertensive encephalopathy is characterized by acute or subacute onset
of lethargy, fatigue, confusion, headaches, visual disturbances
(including blindness from hypertensive retinopathy), seizures, or
cerebral hemorrhage. In about 10% of cases, SRC occurs with
normal blood pressure. New-onset anemia, asymptomatic pericardial effusion, and cardiac disease may precede an SRC. Laboratory
testing at onset may show normal or high creatinine, modest to
absent proteinuria, and/or microscopic hematuria. Renal insufficiency is usually not the presenting problem in an SRC but rather
is a late complication of untreated disease. However, a rapid rise in
serum creatinine can occur over several days with a benign urinalysis such as less than 2 g of protein excretion in 24 hours or mild

hematuria with some granular casts. Microangiopathic hemolytic
anemia and thrombocytopenia can accompany or precede an SRC
and may be the only finding in a normotensive patient. This presentation with normotensive SRC usually entails a worse prognosis. Distinguishing SRC-associated thrombotic microangiopathy
from idiopathic thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura may
be difficult but is important because treatments differ. Accurate
assays for ADAMTS (a disintegrin and metalloproteinase [ADAM
family] with thrombospondin-1 domains)-13 activity can be helpful because its level should be normal in scleroderma.
Poorer outcome is more frequent among males and among
patients presenting with creatinine greater than 3 mg/dL (265
mmol/L). Approximately two-thirds of patients with SRC will
require renal support with dialysis, but about half will recover
function and discontinue therapy, usually within several weeks
and most before 6 months.
In some patients, dialysis may
be required for longer periods up to 24 months before discontinuation, distinguishing SRC from other causes of renal failure.
Patients with SRC may continue to recover renal function up to
3 years after the crisis, most often within 12 to 18 months. Interestingly, while patients with anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies
are at higher risk of developing SRC and to require dialysis, they
are also more likely to discontinue dialysis (53 vs. 26%,

= 0.01)
after SRC and have better long-term survival (

= 0.003) than subjects who are anti-RNA polymerase III negative.
The incidence
of end-stage kidney disease from scleroderma appears to have
declined in the United States since 1996. After initiating therapy
for SRC, patients with scleroderma had a greater likelihood of
kidney function recovery and a lower likelihood of transplantation
than demography-matched patients without scleroderma.
The renal crisis truly mimics malignant hypertension, with rapidly progressive renal failure driven by microvascular disease, vasospasm, and tissue ischemia. The characteristic lesion of scleroderma
vascular disease such as intimal hyperplasia or luminal narrowing,
involving in particular the renal arcuate arteries, is detected even
in patients without SRC. Although the histopathologic features of
renal disease are well described, the pathogenesis of SRC remains
poorly understood. It is assumed that intrinsic renal vessel disease
is complicated by an intense vasospasm triggering high levels of
plasma renin, which results in malignant hypertension. A study
demonstrated that a significant decrease in complement hemolytic
activity is present in SRC versus non-SRC patients, indicating that
complement consumption does occur during a renal crisis.
Vascular thrombosis, glomerular ischemic collapse, and peritubular
capillary C4d deposits in patients with SRC are associated with
a worse outcome (e.g., requiring dialysis or transplant or death)
compared with normotensive controls and hypertensive non-SRC
control subjects.

from multiple case reports suggesting benefit from early administration of eculizumab, a monoclonal antibody directed against

formation.

### Management of Scleroderma Renal Crisis

Before the discovery of ACE inhibitors, hypertensive renal crisis almost uniformly had a fatal outcome. In contrast, patients
treated with ACE inhibitors have a good outcome 60% of the
time, and death or irreversible end-stage renal disease is now much
less common. The mortality rate at 1 year associated with SRC has

## Gastrointestinal Involvement

Almost every patient with scleroderma has symptoms of GI
involvement, ranging from mild GERD to severe bowel dysfunction, which can be life threatening. Virtually any segment of the
GI tract can be affected (see
Table 85.3
). While there is some data
to suggest IVIG can improve GI motility in a subset of patients,
there is no clear evidence that immunosuppressive therapy is useful to prevent or treat GI dysfunction.

### Oropharynx

Patients may report that chewing is difficult because of decreased
facial flexibility caused by skin and deeper tissue fibrosis. Perioral
skin tightening can result in a decreased oral aperture, affect speech,
and alter the ability to open the mouth wide enough for proper

and oral augmentation exercises can improve oral function and
mouth opening.

### Stomach

The degree of delayed gastric emptying (gastroparesis) is underappreciated in scleroderma, and gastroparesis often causes early
satiety, aggravation of GERD symptoms, anorexia, abdominal
pain, a sensation of bloating, or nausea. Frequently, weight loss in
patients with scleroderma is a consequence of poor caloric intake
as a result of lack of appetite from poor gastric function. Prokinetics (e.g., erythromycin, metoclopramide, domperidone) are used
to improve gastric emptying and related symptoms, but results are
generally disappointing and several small meals with good caloric
content is often the most effective therapy. Gastritis or gastric ulcers
can occur. Dilation of the microvasculature in the gastric mucosa
is found in a subset of patients. This manifestation, also called
gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE)
, is thought to be caused by an
abnormal angiogenesis, leading to bizarre dilation of microvessels
and arterial-venous (A-V) malformations similar to the telangiectasias seen in the skin, lips, and oral mucous membranes. One
survey reports a prevalence of GAVE in 10.6% of patients, with
an increased risk associated with older age of onset, diffuse disease subtype, and anti-RNA polymerase III positivity.
In general, GAVE lesions are asymptomatic, but they can be responsible
for occult GI bleeding. Extensive clusters of A-V malformations
lead to the presence of longitudinal red stripes in the inner lining of the stomach, converging to the pylorus and described on
endoscopy as “watermelon stomach” based on their appearance
(see
Fig. 85.15B
). Endoscopic therapy with laser photocoagulation or cryotherapy can be used to ablate bleeding vessels. Rarely,
bleeding associated with GAVE is refractory and requires multiple
transfusions, repeated laser or cryotherapy, or even gastric surgery
(e.g., antral or gastric resection). While small case series suggest
CYC may improve GAVE, there is no solid evidence to show a
benefit with immunosuppressive therapy.
While the beneficial
use of thalidomide to treat refractory GI bleeding secondary to
GI vascular malformations, including GAVE and GI angiodysplasia, has been suggested in a small case series and some anecdotal reports, formal use in scleroderma has not yet been properly
investigated.

### Lower Gastrointestinal Tract

Bloating, abdominal distention, diarrhea, and constipation are
common complaints caused by dysmotility of the small and large
bowel in scleroderma patients. Functional and pathologic studies suggest that microvascular disease, neural dysfunction, smooth
muscle atrophy, and later tissue fibrosis similar to that seen in the
esophagus cause intestinal disease. Most cases present in similar
fashion to irritable bowel syndrome. Constipation due to sluggish
or atonic bowel function can be followed by repeated bouts of
diarrhea with malabsorption, progressive loss of weight, and severe
malnutrition. Delayed intestinal transit generates an environment conducive to small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO),
which can manifest with abdominal discomfort, bloating, and
recurrent bouts of diarrhea and eventually lead to malabsorption
and malnutrition if left untreated.
SIBO is estimated to affect
between 30% and 60% of scleroderma patients and can be associated with severe disruption of quality of life.
Recurrent episodes of pseudoobstruction constitute one of the
most serious intestinal problems in scleroderma. These episodes
are sometimes mistaken for surgical emergencies. Pseudoobstruction is a manifestation of profound loss of bowel smooth muscle
function, causing severe dysmotility and segments of luminal dilation. Pneumatosis cystoides intestinalis sometimes complicates
scleroderma of the bowel when gas leaks into the diseased intestinal wall and tracks into the mesentery of the gut or the peritoneal cavity, mimicking a bowel perforation (see
Fig. 85.15C and
D
). Asymptomatic wide-mouthed diverticula, also resulting from
fibrosis and atrophy of the bowel wall, are pathognomonic of
scleroderma. Volvulus, stricture, and perforation are uncommon
complications of severe bowel involvement. A scintigraphy-based
whole gut transit study in scleroderma found there was no significant association between slow colonic transit and delayed transit
in other regions of the GI tract.
Management of the lower GI tract includes preventing a constipation–diarrhea cycle through adequate fiber ingestion, use of
stool softeners (docusate), or, if constipation is severe, periodic
doses of osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol). The low fermentable, oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols diet is thought to benefit scleroderma patients with altered
lower GI motility by decreasing the osmotic load and the rapid fermentation by colonic microbes. Fiber supplementation has been
used but often causes bloating and may worsen gastroparesis and
colonic emptying. Lubiprostone is helpful if chronic constipation
predominates. Novel compounds are now available for chronic,
refractory constipation such as prucalopride and linaclotide, with
reports indicating benefit in scleroderma patients.
Some
experts also support the use of pyridostigmine and octreotide
in the presence of severe, persistent lower GI hypomotility and
pseudoobstruction. Intermittent-use antibiotics such as rifaximin,
amoxicillin/clavulanic acid, ciprofloxacin, and metronidazole has
shown efficacy in controlling cases with overt SIBO or with recurrent episodes of pseudoobstruction. In some cases of intractable
diarrhea, the use of low doses of cholestyramine can be beneficial.
Abnormal gut microbiota may be playing a role in GI dysfunction.
While probiotics are often used, their role in managing symptoms
of presumed GI dysbiosis is still under study. Novel therapeutic
strategies with fecal microbiota transplantation are being explored
but preliminary results need to be validated with larger trials. Total
parenteral nutrition (TPN) may become necessary in patients
with severe scleroderma-related bowel disease and malnutrition
irresponsive to medical therapy. The presence of intestinal failure
requiring TPN is associated with an overall poor prognosis.
Fecal incontinence can manifest in scleroderma caused by
dysfunction of both internal (atrophy and fibrosis) and external
(weakness) anal sphincters as demonstrated by endoanal ultrasound and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Treatment options
for scleroderma-related anorectal dysfunction and fecal incontinence are mainly symptomatic, as surgical interventions are generally associated with unsatisfactory outcomes. Beneficial treatments
include firming stool texture, performing exercises to strengthen
the pelvic muscles, implementing biofeedback methods, and conducting surgical repair when aggravating factors such as rectal prolapse or severe hemorrhoids are present. Sacral neuromodulation,
involving a direct, continuous, and low-voltage electrical stimulation of the sacral nerve roots, has been used with favorable results
in a subgroup of patients, but more studies are needed.

## Primary Cardiac Involvement

The clinical manifestations of heart disease in scleroderma are
highly variable, ranging from clinically silent cardiac involvement
to frank heart failure. The reported prevalence of heart disease varies from 10% to more than 50%, depending on the diagnostic
method used, but in general it tends to be occult and underestimated. Symptoms such as dyspnea, chest pain, or palpitations
are commonly thought to be the result of more obvious organ
involvement such as the lungs or the upper GI tract, and the contribution of heart disease often is not appreciated until the later
stages. Cardiac disease can occur in both diffuse and limited subtypes of scleroderma and can manifest as a primary heart problem
or in association with other organ failure. When clinically evident,
heart disease entails an overall poor prognosis and predicts shortened survival.
Along with pulmonary fibrosis and PAH,
cardiac disease accounts for the majority of deaths in scleroderma.
One study found that 25% of deaths could be directly related to
heart disease (mostly heart failure and arrhythmias).
A survey
from Spain reports that mortality from heart disease has increased
when comparing the 1990 to 1999 to the 2000 to 2009 decades.
While scleroderma is overall more common in women, severe
cardiovascular involvement is found in men, in association with a
poorer prognosis.
In a large meta-analysis, cardiac involvement
was associated with increased mortality (hazard ratio, 2.8; 95%
CI, 2.1–3.8) after adjustments for age and sex.
A study comparing 129 patients with scleroderma who had a left ventricle ejection
fraction (LVEF) of less than 55% with 256 subjects who had a
normal LVEF demonstrated that male sex, age, digital ulcerations,
myositis, and no use of CCBs were independent factors associated
with LV dysfunction.
A survey of published data found evidence that cardiomyopathy in scleroderma patients is associated
with several indicators of poor prognosis such as diffuse cutaneous
disease, positive scleroderma-specific serology, Black race, older
age at disease onset, tendon friction rubs, and worse quality-of-life
scores.
Scleroderma cardiac disease can be characterized by involvement of the endocardium, myocardium, and pericardium, separately or concomitantly. Pericardial effusion, auricular and/or
ventricular arrhythmias, conduction disease, valvular regurgitation, myocardial ischemia, myocardial hypertrophy, and heart
failure are all reported. Clinically overt pericarditis is uncommon,
but asymptomatic and hemodynamically benign pericardial effusions are frequently detected by ECHO. In a controlled study,
significant pericardial effusion was found in about 15% of patients
compared with 4% of control subjects.
Pathologic studies have
shown that some degree of pericardial involvement is detectable in
33% to 77% of patients with scleroderma, usually with evidence
of a fibrinous pericarditis with adhesions and chronic inflammatory cell infiltrates.
Hemodynamic compromise resulting from
tamponade physiology is rare but may require acute intervention
with anti-inflammatory medication, pericardiocentesis, or creation of a pericardial window for slow decompression. The presence of a large pericardial effusion, even if it is asymptomatic, is a
poor prognostic sign, particularly if it is associated with PAH or
renal disease. Colchicine is recommended by experts as a first-line
treatment for both acute and recurrent pericarditis; however, its
role in scleroderma pericardial disease is not studied.
Focal myocardial fibrosis is the hallmark of established primary heart involvement in scleroderma. The fibrotic lesions
are patchy, involve the myocardium of both ventricles, and are
usually accompanied by evidence of microvascular disease with
concentric intimal hypertrophy associated with fibrinoid necroThis
sis of the intramural coronary arteries and arterioles.
phenomenon results in reduced coronary flow reserve even with
normal epicardial coronary arteries and in the absence of clinically
manifested cardiac dysfunction.
Vasospasm of coronary vessels
and associated myocardial perfusion defects are demonstrated to
occur at rest, with exercise, and after exposure to cold temperatures.
These findings suggest that myocardial fibrosis may be
associated with reversible vasospasm of the microvascular coronary circulation and that vasodilating agents such as CCBs may
have the capacity to improve coronary flow and prevent further
cardiac damage. Patients with angina-like chest pain may need
to undergo angiographic studies because nuclear perfusion studies are likely to be abnormal as a result of microvascular disease.
Studies have provided controversial data regarding increased risk
for atherosclerotic coronary artery disease. Although pathologic
studies and an analysis of coronary angiograms have found little
involvement of coronary arteries in patients with scleroderma,
epidemiologic surveys seem to indicate an increased risk of atherosclerosis in this disease, like in other rheumatic conditions such as
RA or SLE.
Therefore, typical angina symptoms should make
one consider atherosclerosis of the coronary vessels or another
process. Atypical chest pain can also be caused by musculoskeletal
problems, esophageal reflux disease, or PAH mimicking cardiac
disease. While microvascular disease and focal ischemia leading
to myocardial fibrosis is considered a leading pathogenetic process
in scleroderma primary cardiac disease, occurrence of myocardial
inflammation (myocarditis) can also be detected. This is associated
more frequently with heart failure and more extensive myocardial
fibrosis compared with virus-negative myocarditis secondary to
other systemic autoimmune disorders.
ECG and Holter monitoring often reveal some heart conduction defect or asymptomatic arrhythmia, which are thought to be
consequences of myocardial fibrosis. Premature ventricular contractions are the most common abnormality, but sinus node dysfunction, first-degree heart block, supraventricular arrhythmias
(supraventricular tachycardia and atrial fibrillation), and ventricular arrhythmias (ventricular tachycardia) are also observed. Complete heart block is uncommon. Scleroderma-related syncope is
an ominous manifestation of late-stage PAH or an important
arrhythmia. When an arrhythmia is suspected, prolonged assessment of the heart rhythm with Holter monitoring or loop recorders should be promptly pursued. More extensive evaluation with
electrophysiologic studies is indicated in serious cases because a
pacemaker or ablative therapy can help in the management of
these life-threatening complications. In subjects with documented
advanced cardiomyopathy and significant ejection fraction
depression, placement of an automatic implantable cardioverter
defibrillator device should be considered to prevent sudden cardiac death.
Transthoracic ECHO (TTE), a widely used type of noninvasive
cardiac testing, is a sensitive tool to detect the presence of cardiac
disease. TTE can provide estimates of elevated RV and pulmonary artery pressures, as well as evidence of right heart dysfunction such as atrial and ventricular dilation or septal wall motion
abnormalities. A depressed LV contractility is not frequently
detected in patients with scleroderma with the use of conventional
TTE, but relaxation abnormalities can be found in up to 40% of
cases. LV diastolic dysfunction is detected especially in patients
with diffuse scleroderma, often occurring in the absence of systemic hypertension (independent of renal disease) and in association with pulmonary vascular disease. The presence of diastolic

dysfunction is related to a poor prognosis.
Heart failure with
preserved ejection function (HFpEF) is not infrequent in scleroderma. When present, HFpEF can pose a significant challenge
with management and entails a poor prognosis, particularly when
it becomes associated with significant diastolic dysfunction or secondary pulmonary hypertension (PH-HFpEF).
Severe impairment of contractility and diastolic dysfunction is also found in the
RV and this can result from primary heart disease or be secondary
to pulmonary vascular disease and associated PH.
Although TTE is very helpful in monitoring underlying cardiac
abnormalities later in the disease process, it lacks some sensitivity
during pre-clinical stages of heart involvement. Novel diagnostic
methods such as tissue Doppler ECHO, with or without strain
imaging, are being used to detect subtle cardiac dysfunction.
Analysis of speckled-derived strain of the RV by two-dimensional
(2D)-ECHO has revealed a heterogeneous pattern of heart strain
with significant regional myocardial dysfunction in scleroderma
patients compared with controls.
A recent study conducted
by 2D speckle tracking echocardiology found early LV and RV
regional impairment in 22% to 24% of patients with scleroderma,
while only 2.1% showed reduced LVEF.
Exercise testing during
ECHO can also be used to detect underlying diastolic dysfunction and PH not previously recognized by resting studies. Exercise
provocation in conjunction with RV longitudinal systolic strain
identified differential regional contractile response to exercise in
patients with scleroderma, suggesting subclinical impairments in
RV reserve that may be missed by resting noninvasive 2D-ECHO
assessments alone.
Patients with regional and global LV longitudinal systolic strain (LVLSS) are more likely to have elevated
CRP values, suggesting greater inflammation. LVLSS also appears
to worsen in SSc patients over time.
Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR) is a noninvasive, nonradiation diagnostic imaging technique that can provide functional and
structural characterization of scleroderma heart involvement earlier
than conventional ECHO and with greater accuracy.
CMR
detects subclinical myocardial fibrosis early in the disease course of
patients with VEDOSS or in subjects with limited skin involvement and no significant internal organ involvement, a subset usually expected to have a more benign disease course.
One of
these studies did not identify any association between focal or diffuse myocardial fibrosis and ventricular arrhythmias, making the
exact prognostic implications of myocardial fibrosis by CMR in
asymptomatic subjects unclear.
While CMR has great sensitivity,
its specificity remains suboptimal, and further studies are needed.
The systematic measurement of BNP or NT-proBNP is an
integral part of cardiac evaluation in scleroderma patients. Blood
levels of these molecules reflect both RV and LV volume/pressure
overload and has proven to be a reliable prognostic marker for
right or left heart failure in patients with scleroderma, independently predicting mortality.
Elevated serum levels of troponin I can also be detected in association with echocardiographic
abnormalities such as low ejection fraction and regional hypokinesia and may represent a useful marker to identify ongoing myocardial injury in scleroderma patients.
In one survey, patients
with elevated troponin had a 2.16-fold (95% CI, 1.01–4.63,
p
=
0.046) increased risk of death compared with those without elevated troponin even after adjusting for age, sex, disease duration,
and cardiopulmonary risk factors.
Thus persistently elevated
troponin levels may warrant closer clinical investigation to assess
for presence of scleroderma-related cardiac disease.
Early detection of scleroderma cardiac involvement is crucial to prevent life-threatening complications. All patients with
scleroderma should receive a routine screening ECHO, regardless of the presence or absence of arrhythmia. This should be performed with Doppler and STE when possible. Provocation with
exercise should be considered when unexplained dyspnea is present without obvious findings at rest. Given the high prevalence
of subclinical cardiac disease in scleroderma, it is reasonable to
perform ECG, BNP, or NT-proBNP annually, as these are sensitive tests with low risk and relatively low cost.
An isolated cardiomyopathy without evidence of an inflammatory process is managed with conventional medications. Effective
treatment of arrythmias, diuretic therapy for HFpEF, and appropriate management of comorbid conditions (e.g., hypertension,
diabetes, hypercholesterolemia) that can affect heart function is
key. Heart disease often manifests in the context of the multisystem manifestations of scleroderma (see sections), which require
immunosuppressive or anti-fibrotic agents. There is no clear evidence that use of these drugs for asymptomatic findings of occult
heart disease prevents subsequent complications. Novel therapeutic options to dampen early on in the inflammatory-fibrotic process in scleroderma heart disease (i.e., IL-1 inhibition) are under
consideration.
Patients with diffuse cutaneous scleroderma and
skeletal muscle inflammation (polymyositis) are particularly prone
to develop severe cardiomyopathy and tend to have a particularly
poor prognosis. Acute myocarditis can in fact occur in association with inflammatory muscle disease and can present as sudden-onset heart failure. Prompt institution of immunosuppressive
treatment is indicated in these cases. A CMR and/or an endomyocardial biopsy may be helpful tools to differentiate overt myocarditis from the myocardial fibrosis commonly present in patients
with scleroderma. While therapy with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or colchicine may be helpful to treat
active pericarditis, it is not clear whether anti-inflammatory treatment is indicated for small, stable, and asymptomatic pericardial
effusions. A predesigned approach involving a multidisciplinary
team including a cardiologist, pulmonologist, and rheumatologist
is recommended.

## Musculoskeletal Involvement

Musculoskeletal symptoms are often the earliest sign of the disease, with estimates of up to 20 to 30% of scleroderma patients
presenting muscle or joint symptoms within the first year of
diagnosis. Usually, these are nonspecific and include arthralgia,
myalgias with diffuse muscular pain, and musculoskeletal stiffness
and/or weakness that mimics a flulike syndrome, making a diagnosis of scleroderma challenging. It is common for patients to be
diagnosed with carpal tunnel syndrome resulting from soft tissue
swelling and inflammation in the hand and wrist area prior to
recognizing the underlying diagnosis of scleroderma. The degree
and type of musculoskeletal disease vary a great deal, influenced
by the duration of disease, the level of overall disease activity, and
the serologic and clinical subtype of scleroderma, for example,
in early diffuse scleroderma, pain that is often widespread in
areas of skin inflammation and advancing fibrosis. As the disease
progresses, these patients can develop deep tissue disease with
involvement of joint structures, tendons, subcutaneous tissues,
and underlying muscles. Impaired hand function, with decreased
hand mobility, reduced dexterity, and decreased grip force, is the
most common musculoskeletal manifestation and is often a major
cause of impairment to perform activities of daily living. In later
stages, diffuse disease can result in irreversible joint contractures
and a fibrosing myopathy, which are associated with pain, loss of

function, and significant disability. In patients with limited scleroderma, puffy fingers and loss of hand function and grip may be
the only musculoskeletal symptoms throughout the disease course.
Musculoskeletal involvement occurs in conjunction with active
multisystem disease and is an independent cause of disability and
impaired quality of life. Loss of muscle or sarcopenia is seen in
about 20% of patients with scleroderma and is associated with
physical impairment affecting everyday life activities and ability to
participate in work.
Although histologic evidence suggests that
synovitis occurs, arthralgias, pain, and stiffness without signs of
frank signs of synovitis constitute the usual clinical picture.
Erosive polyarthritis, seemingly specific for scleroderma, or
seropositive RA can occur in patients with limited or diffuse
skin disease. The prevalence of scleroderma and RA overlap is
low (1% to 2%), with an estimated incidence of 5%.
Anticitrullinated protein antibodies are rarely detected in patients with
scleroderma and arthritis. Erosive arthritis is also commonly associated with periarticular calcinosis (see
Fig. 85.16A
) and can be
seen with significant bone loss or osteolysis. A radiologic survey
of 120 patients found abnormalities including erosion (21%),
joint space narrowing (28%), arthritis (defined by concomitant
erosion and joint space narrowing; 18%), radiologic demineralization (23%), acro-osteolysis (22%), flexion contracture (27%), and
calcinosis (23%).
Radiologic studies using ultrasonography and
MRI confirm that hand involvement is usually advanced, with
striking articular, bone, and soft tissue changes.
Resorption
of distal phalanges is significantly associated with digital ulcers
and extraarticular calcification. While loss of bone in the fingers

Contractures of the PIP and MCP joints are most common

Patients with diffuse skin disease can develop contractures of large
joints, including wrists, elbows, shoulders, hips, knees, and ankles.
These are the hallmark of severe scleroderma and are associated
with fibrosis and ankylosis of the joint, resulting from disease in
the overlying skin, fascia, joint capsule, and tendon structures.
Tendon friction rubs can be felt as a coarse crepitus over joints
or over the forearm or lower leg with adjacent joint movement.
These rubs are thought to be a result of low-grade tenosynovitis,
local edema, and fibrosis of the tendon sheath, fascia, and joint
structures. Friction rubs are seen primarily in patients with diffuse
skin disease; when present, they are an indicator of a poor overall
prognosis. They are found in about 15% to 30% of cases, more
commonly in patients with diffuse skin disease and those with
positive anti-topoisomerase, anti-RNA polymerase, or anti-U3-
RNP antibodies.
A large multicenter survey reported the frequencies of synovitis, tendon friction rubs, and joint contractures to be 16, 11, and
31%, respectively.
Synovitis, tendon friction rubs, and joint
contractures are more likely to occur together and are more prevalent in patients with the diffuse cutaneous subset. These manifestations also associate with systemic disease including severe vascular,
muscular, renal, and interstitial lung involvement. The presence of
myopathy associates with cardiac disease and poor outcome.

Calcinosis is most often seen in patients with digital ulcers and
is similarly observed in patients with the diffuse or limited cutaneous subtype. Calcium deposits often develop in the subcutaneous
tissue of areas subjected to trauma such as the extensor surfaces
of the forearms, elbows, or patellae. Unlike the more extensive
calcinosis seen in patients with myositis, calcium deposits in
scleroderma tend to be found in clusters around joints and sites of
trauma. These deposits can restrict joint motion and can be associated with local acute inflammation similar to a gouty arthritis.
They can also rupture through the skin, weeping a thick, white
material. Once the skin is broken, secondary infection can occur.
Calcinosis pathogenesis is not fully understood but it occurs in
sites of poor blood flow and trauma, manifesting as a post-inflammatory defective repair process. The degree of calcinosis and some
measure of activity help guide intervention. X-rays and ultrasound
are considered sensitive modalities for assessing calcinosis.
Weakness in the muscles of the hands, arms, and legs is very
common (80%); this can be subtle or profound. One study found
measurable weakness in 30% of patients, with proximal more
than distal strength being compromised. Myopathy in a person
with scleroderma is often multifactorial, but patients with muscle
involvement are at increased risk for poor outcome and decreased
survival. A survey noted that the presence of muscle weakness
is associated with several features of worse disease burden and
independently predicts disability as measured by the HAQ-DI
score.
Weakness is often caused by muscle atrophy as a result of
the inflexibility due to joint disease and fibrotic skin with lack of
usual mobility and exercise. It can also occur because of malnutrition resulting from scleroderma-related bowel disease. Frequently,
muscle weakness in scleroderma can be a result of direct muscle
disease. In a survey, 40.6% of scleroderma patients undergoing
MRI showed evidence of skeletal muscle edema consistent with
myopathy, even in subjects without symptoms or other signs of
muscle involvement.
A pathologic study of 42 subjects with
muscle disease (79% female and 64% diffuse scleroderma) found
multiple types, including muscle necrosis (67%), inflammatory
myopathy (48%), acute neurogenic atrophy (48%), and fibrosis
(33%).
In people with diffuse scleroderma, fibrosis can extend into
the striated muscle, causing muscle atrophy and clinical weakness.
This phenomenon called
fibrosing myopathy
presents in the setting of severe diffuse skin disease with joint contractures and mild
elevation of CPK. Electromyography (EMG) demonstrates nonirritable myopathy, and a muscle biopsy shows little inflammation,
fibrosis, and type 2 muscle atrophy. Fibrosing myopathy does not
respond to traditional anti-inflammatory medications, including
corticosteroids, and is associated with myocardial involvement,
heart failure, and arrhythmias that can be severe and irreversible.
This unique histologic subtype of myopathy carries a significantly
higher mortality compared with scleroderma muscle disease characterized by inflammation and/or necrosis.
Approximately 5% to 10% of patients with scleroderma have
a more defined inflammatory muscle involvement that can follow a disease course like in other forms of idiopathic inflammatory myopathy. These patients develop a more rapidly progressing
proximal muscle weakness associated with high CPK levels, EMG
significant for an irritable myopathy, and a muscle biopsy with
typical inflammatory muscle infiltrates. In severe polymyositis
cases, myocarditis can occur with heart failure. High levels of antiPM/Scl-100 antibodies are found in about 4% of patients with
scleroderma, often in association with acute inflammatory muscle
disease. Patients with muscle weakness should be fully evaluated
for underlying causes with the use of laboratory measures of muscle enzymes, EMG studies, MRI, and muscle biopsy. These studies will help differentiate inflammatory muscle disease from other
forms of myopathy.

### Management of Musculoskeletal Disease

Active symptoms and signs of musculoskeletal disease are frequently associated with overall disease activity, and these may
improve as systemic treatment to manage other organ involvement
is implemented. The management of muscle weakness begins with
a careful evaluation to identify underlying causes. A myopathy due
to disuse, malnutrition, medications, or late irreversible disease
is best managed with direct intervention with improved dietary
intake, discontinuation of toxic drugs (e.g., statins, steroids), and
physical therapy–directed exercise. Muscle enzymes, EMG, and
MRI of major muscle groups should help further define causes of
underlying weakness. A muscle biopsy is recommended to characterize the disease process in that each histologic subtype can
guide therapy and predict outcome. Inflammatory muscle disease
is treated in the same manner as other cases of myositis. Steroids
can be effective for musculoskeletal inflammation but should be
used only at low doses (less than 15 mg/day) due to concerns of
triggering an SRC. For severe myositis, IVIG can be initiated with
steroids and MTX or MMF added for long-term control. Rituximab is also effective in a case series of scleroderma patients positive for anti-PM/Scl.
The management of fibrosing myopathy
is not established but again this can benefit from effective control
of the systemic disease with immunosuppression and anti-fibrotic
agents.
NSAIDs are helpful to control pain and joint inflammation but
must be monitored and used with caution due to GI side effects and
possible renal toxicity. Immunosuppressive agents may also dampen
active joint and soft tissue inflammation. MMF used by most experts
as the initial therapy for skin and ILD is not very effective for active
joint disease but may treat an inflammatory muscle process. When
inflammatory arthritis is present, traditional medications used in
RA can be helpful. MTX is an effective agent to treat polyarthritis
using the same approach as for RA. If MTX is not effective alone,
then a biologic can be added or used as an alternative. One case
series demonstrated that etanercept can be efficacious in improving active inflammatory joint disease in scleroderma patients and is
generally safe and well tolerated.
While tocilizumab did not show
benefit to treat scleroderma skin disease, a case series reports benefit
for inflammatory joint disease.
Disappearance of tendon friction
rubs is a sign of systemic and articular disease control.
Physical and occupational therapy should be started early to
decrease the risk of contractures and support patients with conditioning and activities of daily living. Exercise and good nutrition while controlling the disease process are also important to
prevent loss of muscle mass and strength even in the absence of
inflammation.
Evidence of bone mineral deficit should always be assessed
with baseline bone density scan, particularly in post-menopausal
patients and those with significant nutritional difficulties. Presence
of osteoporosis is treated in the same manner as nonscleroderma
patients. As the pathogenesis of osteolysis is not fully understood,
most experts approach this complication by optimizing control of
RP with the idea that hypoxia and aberrant repair may be driving
mechanisms.
Many agents exert some efficacy in treating calcinosis, but the
medical management of this scleroderma complication is in general

very disappointing. Vasodilators (e.g., diltiazem), antiresorptive
agents (e.g., bisphosphonates), calcium binding agents such as
sodium thiosulfate (topical or systemic), vitamin K, colchicine, the
IL-1 inhibitor anakinra, rituximab, and minocycline are among the
agents that are helpful.
Controlled studies of therapeutic agents
for SSc-related calcinosis are needed, and outcome measures should
be based on objective measures of disease activity. Reducing trauma,
cushioning high-risk sites, and controlling the underlying inflammatory disease are the most effective strategies for treatment. Calcinosis accumulating around articular structures can drive events
similar to acute crystal arthropathy (i.e., goutlike) and are best
treated with NSAIDs and rest. Open wounds can occur at calcinosis sites that may get secondarily infected needing antibiotic therapy
and/or debridement. Surgical removal of painful calcium deposits
is an option but reoccurrence in the same area is very common and
expected.

## Associated Disorders

The most common endocrine problem associated with scleroderma is thyroid disease, while the prevalence of other endocrinopathies is similar to that of the general population. Thyroid
fibrosis, autoimmune thyroiditis resulting in hypothyroidism,
and hyperthyroidism from Graves’ disease are frequent and often
unsuspected findings in scleroderma. Thyroid disease is reported
in 10% to 15% of patients with scleroderma.
An autoimmune basis for thyroid involvement is suggested by one series,
which found Hashimoto’s thyroiditis in 6% and Graves’ disease in 3% of scleroderma-affected individuals.
In a survey of
719 scleroderma patients, 273 (38%) subjects presented at least
another autoimmune condition, most frequently autoimmune
thyroid disease.
Another cohort study reported an increased risk
of thyroid disease among female scleroderma patients compared
with controls, including subclinical hypothyroidism (OR, 3.2;
95% CI, 1.8, 5.7), clinical hypothyroidism (OR, 14.5; 95% CI,
2.3, 90.9), anti-thyroid peroxidase antibody positivity (OR, 2.7;
95% CI, 1.8, 4.1), and thyroid ultrasound hypoechoic pattern
(OR, 3.2; 95% CI, 2.2, 4.7).
Another survey found that presence of hyperthyroidism in scleroderma patients carries a 1.54-
fold increase of mortality rates during a mean follow-up of 17
years.
Autoimmune thyroid disease should especially be sought
in scleroderma patients with PBC, CREST features, and Sjögren’s
syndrome, as there are reports of overlap with all these conditions.
Thyroid disease may not be recognized, as many symptoms of
hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism can overlap with the clinical
manifestations of scleroderma and can be incorrectly attributed to
the systemic disease. Treatments for thyroid disease are effective
and should be implemented as persistent thyroid dysfunction may
negatively impact the overall management of scleroderma.
Other disease-associated manifestations that often go unrecognized and yet cause significant morbidity include neurologic
and audio-vestibular disease, oral complications, sicca syndrome,
liver disease, bone disease (osteoporosis, osteolysis, avascular
necrosis), lower-extremity skin lesions, and erectile dysfunction
(ED). Awareness of these associated problems can improve quality of life and prevent undue distress. Involvement of the peripheral and autonomic nervous systems are the most commonly
appreciated neurologic complications. Although the true prevalence has not been thoroughly investigated, a systematic review
including reports on 182 cases indicates that myopathy (51.8%),
trigeminal neuropathy (16.52%), peripheral sensorimotor polyneuropathy (14.25%), and carpal tunnel syndrome (6.56%) are
the most frequent manifestations affecting the peripheral nervous
system.
Peripheral neuropathy in scleroderma patients can
result from multiple causes, including nutritional deficiency from
GI disease, entrapment neuropathy, vasculitis, or vasculopathy
of small vessels typical of scleroderma vascular disease. A survey
found a total of 17 in 60 (28%) patients with scleroderma had
evidence of a peripheral neuropathy as defined by the presence of
neuropathic symptoms and/or electrophysiologic evidence of neuropathy. However, the cause was attributed in 82.3% to a comorbid nonscleroderma-related condition.
Autonomic neuropathy
involving cardiovascular and GI systems is also reported.
Central nervous system (CNS) disease is generally clinically
absent, but smoldering cerebrovascular disease is suggested by more
frequent detection in scleroderma patients compared with controls
of asymptomatic white-matter lesions by MRI. A survey found
that CNS lesions correlate with presence of clinical headaches,
depression, and peripheral vascular disease, and they are thought
to be due to involvement of smaller arterial branches and perforating arteries.
In a study, carotid artery Doppler ultrasound
and brain MRI with brain fluid-attenuated inversion recovery and
diffusion-weighted sequences were conducted on 72 patients with
scleroderma and no CNS symptoms and 42 controls.
Scleroderma patients had more common carotid artery intima-media
thickness, higher CRP, and more brain MRI hyperintense lesions
than controls (
p
< 0.05), suggesting an increased risk of vascular
and CNS disease. Results from a study measuring carotid intima
media thickness and pulse wave velocity showed that the presence
of subclinical atherosclerosis is comparable between scleroderma
and RA, a systemic disease with well-established high atherosclerotic burden.
Subclinical atherosclerotic disease affecting the
coronary arteries in asymptomatic scleroderma patients has also
been confirmed by detection of increased cardiac calcium scores
compared with matched healthy controls.
Data also suggest
that cardiovascular disease risk and atherosclerosis measured by
macrovascular parameters correlate with the presence and severity of microvascular disease assessed by abnormalities on nail-fold
video capillaroscopy.
A cognitive function measurement study
conducted in 20 scleroderma patients using Mini-Mental State
Examination, Wechsler Adult Intelligence scale, and the P300 (P3)
wave component of event-related potentials found that subclinical
cognitive impairment is present in 65% of cases, suggesting that
this is an unappreciated problem needing more dedicated investigation.
A survey of anti-centromere-positive patients with limited scleroderma showed presence of subjective hearing loss 54%
of the time compared with 0% in controls, with objective hearing
loss detected by audiogram in 77% of patients compared with
26% of controls (
p
< 0.001).
Hearing loss was bilateral in most
cases and was attributed to sensory neural hearing loss.
In older patients with scleroderma, altered urinary tract function is a common comorbid condition. Evidence from urodynamic
studies suggest that autonomic dysfunction can also affect bladder
function in scleroderma patients presenting with increased urinary frequency, nocturia, hesitancy, and decreased stream.
Dry eyes (keratoconjunctivitis sicca) and/or oral mucous membranes (xerostomia) occur in about 25% of patients. Dryness in
the eyes may result from associated Sjögren’s syndrome or from
severe facial periorbital skin fibrosis, which limits full closure of
the eyelids. Topical artificial tears should be used frequently in
these cases.
Although the liver is generally spared as a primary target
organ in scleroderma, abnormal liver function tests are often
encountered either due to a comorbid condition or associated

autoimmune disease. Both PBC and autoimmune hepatitis
may develop in scleroderma. The prevalence of antimitochondrial antibody, anti–smooth muscle antibodies, and liver-kidney
microsomal-1 autoantibody is increased in scleroderma patients
compared with healthy controls. The prevalence of PBC among
patients with scleroderma is about 2% to 2.5%, usually in patients
with limited scleroderma who are anti-centromere positive.
Bone disease can occur for a variety of reasons. Most patients
with scleroderma are women who are near or in menopause and
therefore are at risk for osteoporosis and its complications. This
risk is increased by the chronic inflammatory nature of the disease, disuse, and several commonly used medications, such as
corticosteroids or PPIs. Malabsorption and lack of sun exposure
may lead to vitamin D deficiency, and bowel dysfunction can limit
calcium intake. Avascular necrosis of the carpal bones of the wrist
is reported and is thought to be a result of scleroderma peripheral
vascular disease. In addition, osteolysis or bony resorption of the
tips or tufts of the fingers, middle phalanges, distal radius, and
ulna bones (less commonly, the distal clavicle, ribs, mandible, and
distal toes) may occur (see
Fig. 85.16B and C
). Acro-osteolysis
is common, presenting with shortening of the finger or pain of
the fingertip. The pathogenesis of osteolysis is thought to be a
manifestation of peripheral vascular disease and aberrant remodeling with increased osteoclast formation and higher VEGF levels
affecting bones.
Lower-extremity cutaneous lesions can occur due to several
causes, including skin fibrosis, stasis dermatitis, lipodermatosclerosis, panniculitis, subcutaneous calcinosis, and leg ulcers. Leg
ulcers may result from traumatic breakdown of atrophic fibrotic
skin, ischemic ulcers as a result of scleroderma vascular disease,
vasculitis, and occlusive vascular events resulting from an associated hypercoagulable state. Livedoid vasculopathy is a rare complication causing skin ulcerations on the lower leg and foot as the
result of small-vessel vasculitis or small-vessel thrombosis aggravated by venous insufficiency or states of fluid overload.
The presence of ED is often not investigated. However, studies
find that ED and associated impotence are present in more than
80% of men with scleroderma due to local fibrosis of the corporeal
smooth muscle and scleroderma microvascular disease. The management of ED is challenging and includes exclusion of comorbid
conditions, the use of phosphodiesterase inhibitors, and consideration of the surgical placement of penile prostheses.

## Psychosocial Aspects

Scleroderma is a disease that can alter virtually every aspect of
the patient’s life. Although scleroderma can often be mild in its
expression, a newly diagnosed patient is faced with a life crisis
and must deal with a rare chronic disease that is complex and
not completely understood by the medical community. A review
of 26 studies identified major themes of psychosocial distress
among patients with scleroderma, such as distressing appearance
transformation, palpable physical limitations, social impairment,
navigating uncertainty, and feeling alone and misunderstood.
Patients learn that scleroderma is potentially a life-threatening
disease that alters physical capacity and can be disfiguring. At its
onset, patients are often confused, anxious, and frightened. Fear
and misunderstanding become a major source of distress. Studies of psychosocial adjustment support the conclusion that a substantial proportion of patients with scleroderma have difficulty
adapting to the disease and have reduced quality of life measures.
In addition, several medications used to treat the disease and its
complications may also have some influence on mood and the
sense of well-being. The presence of functional disability significantly impacts HRQoL assessed by physical function, physical
role, bodily pain, general health, social functional, emotional role,
and mental health domains.
A high prevalence of sleep disturbance in patients with scleroderma was found to be associated
with esophageal involvement, severity of disease, depression, and
worse quality of life.
Psychosocial aspects of the disease do not appear to be consistently influenced by age, sex, race/ethnicity, education, or marital status. However, some data suggest that being disabled with
low income leads to higher psychological distress. Depression
in patients with scleroderma is common and is more related to
the patient’s personality, degree of pain, and level of social support than to actual disease severity. A large epidemiologic survey
found that the proportion rate of depression among 2500 patients
was significantly higher than healthy controls (16.2% vs. 10.9%)
and that this proportion was even higher in females and subjects
with low socioeconomic status.
Pain and fatigue are common
manifestations and, together with depressive symptoms, the most
significant determinants of physical functioning and social adjustment—two important components of HRQoL—in patients with
both limited and diffuse disease subtypes. Expert interviews with
patients with scleroderma-ILD found that the disease manifestations have a profound impact on quality of life, working ability,
psychological well-being, and social interactions of both patients
and caregivers.
Likewise, the severity of GI dysfunction has
shown significant association with emotional distress and quality
of life.
Personality traits influence the degree of psychological adjustment. Patients with scleroderma who describe themselves as
anxious, worried, tense, or detail oriented are more likely to be
depressed than are patients who describe themselves as agreeable
or extraverted and outgoing. Disease unpredictability and lack of
control cause increased distress. High self-reported uncertainty is
related to poor adjustment. Thus improving the patient’s understanding of the disease and defining expectations through education may reduce uncertainty and improve quality of life. Body
image dissatisfaction is a significant concern. Patients are distressed
by disease-related changes in visible parts of their body, especially
of the face, fingers, and hands. A survey found that tight skin and
facial changes were not as distressful as hand deformity with finger
contractions and hand dysfunction. A scleroderma cohort study
reported that concerns about appearance were very common in
these patients, particularly in men across all age groups and in
younger women.
The impact of scleroderma on sexual function is not well studied and often not addressed by the caring physician. However,
sexual dysfunction affects both men and women with scleroderma
and has a major impact on their quality of life. Attention should
be dedicated to this problem as part of a comprehensive patient
evaluation and included in the delivery of care. While ED is the
most common cause of sexual problems in males, genital tract and
general physical changes are major contributors to sexual function
disturbances in females. Women with scleroderma, particularly
those with diffuse skin disease, have high levels of sexual impairment compared with women who have other chronic diseases for
which sexual function has received greater attention. One study
reported the prevalence of female sexual dysfunction to be 73%
in scleroderma compared with 31% in healthy controls, showing that impaired sexual function was associated with higher disease activity, the presence of dyspnea and ILD, reduced physical

activity, functional disability, more severe depression, more pronounced fatigue, and impaired quality of life.
Psychological factors with demonstrated relevance to scleroderma include pain, depression, and distress about disfigurement,
physical function, and social function. Although these dimensions
of quality of life are interrelated, pain, depression, and distress
about disfigurement are common and may respond to psychological intervention. Such intervention begins with providing compassionate support beyond just ordering medications. It is important
to spend time in a comfortable setting educating patients about
scleroderma, providing a clear understanding of the degree of their
disease, and explaining to them what they need to do and what
can be done for relief. Having insight into social support and life
circumstances, including financial distress, work environment,
and family structure, provides a framework for helping patients to
decrease external distress. For example, a family conference held to
explain the reality of a patient’s health status or relevant needs can
reduce home tension and can clarify the actual capacity in their
family role. Support in obtaining adjustments to the work environment (e.g., permission to use a space heater) can make a difference. Follow-up visits that provide time to discuss issues of coping
and social support are most important. Recognizing the personality of patients while addressing their specific concerns is helpful.
Treating underlying depression and especially providing effective
management of disease-related pain help improve quality of life
and reduce social and psychological distress. Body image dissatisfaction is a significant concern in women with scleroderma and
should be assessed routinely. Early identification and treatment of
body image dissatisfaction may help prevent depression and psychosocial impairment in this population. Focus group interviews
found that receiving emotional, informational, and instrumental
support was important, with emotional support being the priority.
To cope with the challenges of scleroderma, patients try to
develop strategies to better manage the physical and emotional
disease burden and improve their own quality of life. Easier access
to disease-specific educational resources, to knowledgeable health
care providers, to patients’ groups and to supportive interventions, including tailored scleroderma self-management programs,
can make a difference.
The possibility of premature death from
the disease is a major cause of fear and needs to be addressed with
patients and their family. Most often, life expectancy is not influenced by the disease, yet patients fear death because they have
scleroderma. When a patient is facing death from a severe disease,
appropriate honest and sensitive support must be provided.

## Conclusion and Practical Recommendations

The first principle in managing scleroderma is recognizing that the
disease expression is very heterogeneous. While the name “scleroderma” focuses attention to the skin, it is not just a fibrotic skin
disorder, but rather a systemic disease with the potential of mild
to severe multiorgan involvement. Defining the patient’s specific
clinical and serologic phenotype provides insight into risk for
disease manifestations and guides intervention. Not all patients
develop progressive or severe disease or develop specific organ
involvement (see particular sections), yet the potential for scleroderma to become a life-threatening illness is present in every case.
Despite the variable disease expression, there are common clinical
features and biologic processes that link disease subtypes together.
There is compelling evidence that scleroderma is driven by an
autoimmune process and targeting the inflammatory/immune
system early on makes sense. Autoantibodies are present years
before the onset of clinical symptoms, suggesting that a chronic
indolent immune process underlies the disease at early stages. In
fact, evidence has been provided that in very early scleroderma
presenting with RP, puffy fingers, and evidence of microvascular
changes, the earliest insult is a unique nonimmune complex process that leads to a systemic proliferative occlusive microvascular
disease. This vascular insult triggers tissue injury via a cascade of
secondary immune, inflammatory, and profibrotic mediators, yet
a single pathway has not been identified as critical to the disease
activity. This dynamic and uniquely complex biology can sometimes present with such subtle manifestations that a diagnosis is
challenging. In contrast, it can unfold as an explosive disease with
rapid progressive skin fibrosis and obvious systemic organ malfunction. Every patient with early clinical disease needs a comprehensive evaluation to define organ involvement, stage of disease
activity, and level of severity of organ disease. It is recommended
that every patient, in addition to standard examination, have a
baseline PFT, ECG, ECHO, HRCT scan of lungs, assessment of
nail-fold capillaries, and well-documented mRSS. In addition to
standard laboratory testing, serologic studies (see “Autoantibodies” section), CPK, pro-BNP, thyroid-stimulating hormone, and
inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) are appropriate. These data
along with the clinical assessment will help in defining the subtype of scleroderma, the expected risk, and the evidence of specific
organ disease.
Progressive disease may not invariably occur in every patient,
but early intervention may be appropriate if organ disease is
detected in a high-risk individual. For example, evidence of
early ILD may be treated promptly in a young African American
woman who is anti-topoisomerase positive, especially in the presence of active skin disease and friction rubs. Serial evaluations and
repeated assessments are key. In patients who are otherwise stable,
these can be done every 6 months. Of course, more frequent studies should be considered based on the level of disease activity. The
rheumatologist may not see patients in the early phase of the disease. Scoring the time from first onset of RP, which sometimes
occurs years before the first non-RP clinical symptom, helps in
appreciating the degree of occult disease progression before the
diagnosis is made. It is common for scleroderma specialty centers
to evaluate patients referred for consultation after 1 to 4 years from
disease onset. This could imply that the pathobiology underlying
the disease may have transitioned from an early active immunedriven process to a more dominant chronic profibrotic network
associated with a decreased responsiveness to immunosuppression
alone.
Recognizing the various expressions of the disease, the different
stages and its variable course, and the degree of organ involvement is very helpful to define current management plans (see

). Major advances have been made with interventions
directed to specific organs or systems including RP, SRC, ILD,
PAH, GI dysfunction, and musculoskeletal disease (see specific
sections). However, no single drug has shown yet the ability to
control the overall disease process. This is likely due in part to
the challenges encountered by previous clinical trials, which have
mostly focused on progressive skin disease and used the mRSS
as their main outcome. Studies investigating novel therapies for
scleroderma are now attempting to enroll patients with more
homogeneous clinical phenotypes, who have early active and/or
potentially reversible disease, and test composite scores of disease
burden as primary outcome.

All patients need education, emotional, and physical support.
Scleroderma is a painful disease, and managing pain is essential
for reducing depression and improving quality of life. Optimization of nighttime rest may require the use of sleep aids (e.g.,
