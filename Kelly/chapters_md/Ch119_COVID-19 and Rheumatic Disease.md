## Introduction

Considering the complete absence of data about the impact coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) had on patients with rheumatic
disease in early 2020, one must consider the rapid acquisition of
knowledge about this vulnerable population as remarkable. The
creation of multiple global research consortiums early in the
pandemic has generated numerous, important observations that
helped guide the management of patients with rheumatic disease
receiving immunosuppression. Later, focused industry efforts to
develop mitigation strategies against COVID-19 such as vaccination and neutralizing monoclonal antibodies were critical for
the global attenuation of poor COVID-19 clinical outcomes,
although many of these studies either had no or very few participants on immunosuppression. Thus this led to another concerted effort by the academic research community to determine
whether the benefits observed in the immunocompetent population were also observed in the immunosuppressed. Through these
efforts, strategies have been identified for immunosuppressed
patients to navigate through the challenges of the COVID-19
pandemic. Nevertheless, subsets of these patients continue to have
an elevated risk of poor COVID-19 outcomes such as those on
B cell depletion therapies or mycophenolate mofetil. Here, we
describe the risk of severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) infection, the impact of immunosuppression
on COVID-19 outcomes, the development of de novo inflammatory conditions resembling systemic autoimmune diseases, and
the current strategies to prevent and manage these patients with
COVID-19.

## Risk of SARS-CoV-2 Infection

Potential differences in SARS-CoV-2 infection rates between individuals with rheumatic disease compared with the general population have been examined in multiple studies, including several
population-based studies. In a Korean cohort study of 133,609
people tested for SARS-CoV-2 infection in early 2020, the risk
of this infection in patients with rheumatic disease was slightly
higher compared with matched individuals without rheumatic
disease (adjusted odds ratio [aOR] 1.19; 95% confidence interval
[CI], 1.03–1.40).
Conversely, a population-based cohort from
Italy did not demonstrate a higher risk of SARS-CoV-2 infection
for people with rheumatic disease on anti-malarials compared
with the general population (OR 0.94; 95% CI, 0.66–1.34)
despite a slightly higher probability of being tested (OR 1.09;
95% CI, 0.94–1.28).
A population-based matched cohort study
from Ontario, Canada, analyzing data from January to December 2020 also found that the standardized cumulative incidence
of SARS-CoV-2 infection was the same in those with immunemediated inflammatory diseases (IMIDs) compared with matched
comparators (90 cases per 10,000 population in both groups),
despite testing rates that were 20% higher in people with IMIDs.
Yet a meta-analysis found an increased prevalence of SARS-CoV-2
infection in those with rheumatic diseases versus those without
(relative risk [RR] 1.53; 95% CI, 1.16–2.01).
It appears that a more consistent increased risk of infection is
observed when considering specific rheumatic diseases. For example, an Italian case-control study observed an increase in patients
with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) (aOR 1.64; 95% CI, 1.32–2.05)
but not in the whole connective tissue disease cohort (aOR 1.09;
95% CI, 0.72–1.66).
A 34% increased risk of infection in
patients with RA compared with those without was also observed
in the UK Biobank, but not in patients with gout.
Similarly, the

U.S. Veterans Affairs data set also found elevated infection risk
(25%) in those with RA versus without.
Likewise, in those with
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), an elevated risk was observed
compared with those without (adjusted hazard ratio [aHR] 1.28;
95% CI, 1.03–1.59),
consistent with other studies that demonstrated an increased prevalence of COVID-19 in patients with
SLE versus without SLE.
Overall, the interpretation of these data has been complicated
by the heterogeneity of study design and confounders such as age,
sex, and comorbid conditions. This is in addition to significant
unmeasured confounding that could explain differences in infection rates, such as immunosuppressive use, circulating variants,
rheumatic disease severity and activity, availability of testing and
testing rates, case definition, local public health measures, and differences in individual behaviors related to COVID-19 risk mitigation
​
.

## Impact of Immunosuppression on COVID-19 Outcomes

Systemic immunosuppression may affect COVID-19 risk and
severity for people with systemic autoimmune rheumatic diseases
who require these medications to control their underlying disease.
This potential risk resulted in a relative increase in shielding
behaviors such as social distancing and masking for people with
rheumatic diseases,
particularly early in the pandemic.
While
glucocorticoids and disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs
(DMARDs) have long been recognized to generally increase infection risk and severity, studies in the pandemic have provided more
specific guidance about how this risk may vary by mechanism of
action. Since glucocorticoids and several specific DMARDs have
been repurposed to show efficacy in treating COVID-19, this
adds to the complexity of how immunosuppression may affect
COVID-19 risk during different phases of viral infection. Finally,
immunosuppressants may blunt vaccine immunogenicity, which
may result in less protection from COVID-19 infection and
increased severity. An overview of the effects of immunosuppression on COVID-19 outcomes is shown in
Table 119.1
.

early treatment should be encouraged.

### Conventional Synthetic DMARDs

Baseline use of conventional synthetic (csDMARDs) for rheumatic diseases has also been evaluated throughout the pandemic.
Despite some early enthusiasm,
hydroxychloroquine is not
effective in clinical trials to treat COVID-19,
and patients with
SLE on baseline hydroxychloroquine are not protected from
being infected with COVID-19.
Despite its common use in
rheumatology, methotrexate has not emerged as a consistent risk
factor for COVID-19 severity,
although studies show it does
blunt vaccine humoral responses.
Mycophenolate mofetil has
also emerged as one of the csDMARDs that may severely impact
vaccine immunogenicity.
Among patients with SLE, mycophenolate mofetil, along with cyclophosphamide and calcineurin
inhibitors (e.g., cyclosporin), has also been associated with severe
COVID-19 compared with hydroxychloroquine.
While not
traditionally viewed as a potent immunosuppressive, sulfasalazine
has also been shown to increase COVID-19-related mortality.
While this may be explained by confounding factors, it is also
plausible that sulfasalazine may impact COVID-19 severity by
inhibiting type I interferon (IFN) production by plasmacytoid
dendritic cells.
There are relatively less data focusing on leflunomide and azathioprine.

### B Cell–Depleting Therapies

B cell–depleting therapies such as rituximab have shown the most
consistent association with severe COVID-19 outcomes across
many immune-mediated inflammatory diseases. For example, an
early study in a French cohort showed that rituximab-treated rheumatic patients had three-fold increased risk compared with those
treated with other immunosuppressants.
A GRA study focusing
on patients with RA also found strong associations of rituximab
use with severe COVID-19 compared with TNF inhibitor use.
Rituximab severely reduces humoral response to vaccines,
although patients may still generate sufficient cellular responses.
Thus rituximab-treated patients are at increased risk of breakthrough infection after vaccination and are the group within the
rheumatic disease population still at highest risk for severe outcomes.
Mitigation strategies should strongly be encouraged for

rheumatic patients on B cell–depleting therapy (see the section
“Strategies to Mitigate Poor COVID-19 Outcomes in Immunosuppressed Patients With Rheumatic Disease”).

### Other Biologic DMARDs

Despite their widespread use, TNF inhibitors have not emerged
as having a consistent signal for an association with COVID-19
severity. Using pooled data from three international registries of
patients with COVID-19, TNF inhibition was associated with
lower risk for severe COVID-19 compared with alternative therapies for immune-mediated inflammatory diseases,
although this
data should be interpreted with caution given the high risk of bias
in this study such as reporting bias due to the use of registries,
lack of a control group, and regional differences in hospitalization
thresholds. Interleukin (IL)-6 inhibitors such as tocilizumab have
proven efficacy in treating a subgroup of hospitalized patients with
severe COVID-19,
but baseline use of these medications has not
been associated with COVID-19 severity.
Similarly, IL-1 inhibitors such as anakinra may have efficacy to treat COVID-19,
although less research has focused on baseline use in rheumatic
patients. Abatacept has not shown any consistent association with
COVID-19 severity.
Despite its mechanism related to inhibiting
B cell signaling, belimumab has not emerged as a risk factor for
COVID-19 severity.
There is currently a paucity of data related
to COVID-19 risk of other biologic DMARD mechanisms of
action, including IL-17A, IL-12/23, IL-23, type 1 IFN, and terminal C5 inhibitors.

### Targeted Synthetic DMARDs

Baricitinib, a Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitor, has efficacy to treat
hospitalized patients with severe COVID-19.
However, a GRA
study found that baseline JAK inhibitor use was associated with
severe COVID-19 compared with TNF inhibitor use in RA.
As noted with glucocorticoids, this apparent paradox may be
explained by differential biologic effects of JAK inhibitors based
on the timing of use in the different phases of viral infection (i.e.,
viral replication vs. hyperinflammatory phases). Also, baricitinib
is less commonly used in RA, and there may be differences in
infection risk based on JAK selectivity. Likewise, baracitinib
has been identified using artificial intelligence algorithms for its
potential to inhibit endocytosis of SARS-CoV-2 via inhibition of
G-associated kinase and adaptor-associated kinase. Whether alternative JAK inhibitors such as tofacitinib or upadacitinib share this
potential property is not well understood.
Compared with
many other DMARDs, the half-life of JAK inhibitors is short, so
discontinuation of these medications may also impact associations
in observational studies with COVID-19 outcomes. However, the
association of baseline use of JAK inhibitors with worse COVID19 severity has not been consistent
and may be explained by
confounding factors such as line of therapy and rheumatic disease
severity. There is currently a paucity of data related to COVID-19
risk of targeted synthetic DMARDs that include apremilast and
avacopan.

## Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children and Adults

In the initial global wave of SARS-CoV-2 infection, pediatricians noted that in general COVID-19 led to much less severe
disease in children than seen in adults.
However, in the spring of
2020 reports emerged of children hospitalized with critical illness
including hyperinflammatory shock, heart failure, and features
resembling Kawasaki disease (KD).
The majority of these children had known prior COVID-19 or close contact with an individual with COVID-19 in the preceding weeks, or had antibodies
detected against SARS-CoV-2. This disorder was initially termed
pediatric multisystem inflammatory syndrome temporally associated with COVID-19 in the United Kingdom and multisystem
inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) by the U.S. Centers
for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the World Health
Organization. By the end of 2022, more than 9000 children in
the United States had been hospitalized with MIS-C and was estimated to occur in up to 3 per 10,000 SARS-CoV-2 infections in
children.

It was recognized early that patients with MIS-C demonstrate
significant variability in presentation.
Two studies utilized latent
class analysis in large cohorts of patients to define common disease phenotypes of MIS-C.
Broadly this work highlights
three groups of patients: one with the most severe disease including shock and myocardial dysfunction; one with moderate disease, numerous organ systems involved, and overlap with acute
COVID-19; and one with more mild disease. The distribution of
these phenotypes appeared to change over time, with fewer severe
cases in more recent MIS-C waves.
There are no broadly accepted diagnostic criteria for MIS-C,
and diagnosis is made by physician judgment. However, the MIS-C
case definition generated by the CDC
provides a framework for
the evaluation of suspected cases as well as features consistent with
its diagnosis (
Table 119.3
). Given the wide clinical spectrum of
MIS-C and need to promptly diagnose and treat this condition
while excluding other common mimics, a stepwise approach to
evaluation is warranted. One approach endorsed by the American
College of Rheumatology (ACR) suggests using routine laboratory tests to screen patients followed by a comprehensive evaluation for those with suggestive findings including lymphopenia,
thrombocytopenia, hyponatremia, and hypoalbuminemia.

Pathogenesis
The pathogenesis of MIS-C is incompletely understood. Initial
immunologic investigations identified that children with MIS-C
showed high circulating cytokine levels with low SARS-CoV-2
viral loads, supporting the notion of this as a post-infectious complication of COVID-19.
This cytokine profile is distinct from
acute COVID-19 and KD and is accompanied by activation of
innate immune effector cells and lymphocytes.
Notably, the
elevation of IFN-gamma-induced pathways seen in severe MIS-C
is a common feature of other cytokine storm syndromes including
macrophage activation syndrome.
This cytokine-rich environment is accompanied by production of autoreactive antibodies,
which have strong specificity for proteins expressed in endothelial,
cardiac, and intestinal tissue,
which may relate to the unique
clinical features of MIS-C.
An unresolved question is why MIS-C occurs in a small fraction of children after an otherwise unremarkable primary SARSCoV-2 infection. Several studies have reported that some patients
with MIS-C have mutations linked to autoinflammation, cytokine storm, or other inborn errors of immunity.
Some reports
have also found persistent leakage of SARS-CoV-2 viral proteins
from the gut,
although other work has failed to confirm this.
More broadly, increasing evidence supports the existence of a
superantigen effect in MIS-C, wherein an immunostimulatory
molecule binds major histocompatibility complex class II outside
the antigen-binding domain and stimulates large families of T
cells in a Vbeta-specific manner. Patients with severe MIS-C have
profound expansion of T cell receptor (TCR) beta variable gene
11-2 (TRBV11-2), which can represent up to 24% of clonal T
cells.
Modeling studies suggest that SARS-CoV-2 spike glycoprotein may resemble known superantigens and have a high-affinity interaction with TCR and specifically with TRBV11-2,
further supporting this hypothesis (
Fig. 119.1
).

Prevention and Vaccination
A critical question related to the pathogenesis of MIS-C is whether
it can be prevented. Since early 2022, the occurrence of MIS-C
has dramatically reduced, including after the spread of new variants and periods of more substantial community transmission of
SARS-CoV-2. This may be related to more widespread immunity
to the virus, both through vaccination and prior infection. There is
accumulating evidence to suggest that COVID-19 vaccination is
highly protective (>90%) against the development of MIS-C.
The reduction in MIS-C could also be linked to changes in viral
pathogenicity, as a small study of unvaccinated Danish children
found a substantially lower risk of MIS-C during the Omicron
wave than from prior variants.

## Autoantibodies and De Novo Autoimmune Disease in COVID-19

A concerning manifestation observed in some patients post-acute
COVID-19 is the acquisition of new-onset autoimmune syndromes, as described through numerous case reports.
Two
retrospective large database analyses have demonstrated increased
risk in the development of an autoimmune disease following the
acute phase of COVID-19. Using a test-negative design, data
from the TriNetX U.S. Collaborative Network with propensitymatched patients with COVID-19 and controls, several systemic
autoimmune and autoinflammatory diseases were found in higher
rates in those with COVID-19, such as RA (aHR 2.98; 95% CI,
2.78–3.20), SLE (aHR 2.99; 95% CI, 2.68–3.34), and ankylosing spondylitis (aHR 3.21; 95% CI, 2.50–4.13) (
Table 119.5
).
Similar findings were reported in a pre-print using routine data
from German statutory health insurance organizations totaling
over 640,000 people, where an incidence rate of an autoimmune
disease 3 to 15 months post-COVID-19 was 15.05% (95% CI,
14.69–15.42) per 1000 person-years compared with 10.55%
(95% CI, 10.25–10.86) in those who never had COVID-19.
It
remains unclear currently, however, which risk factors are important, whether they are previously associated with autoimmune
diseases (i.e., genetic predisposition, environmental triggers, hormonal milieu) or new risk factors specific for COVID-19-associated autoimmune disease.
A hallmark of robust immune activation, including the immune
response to SARS-CoV-2 infection, is the generation of de novo
autoantibodies. Autoantibodies against a broad range of protein
classes, including immunomodulatory proteins and nuclear antigens classically associated with connective tissue diseases and other

with connective tissue diseases.
Importantly, no such autoantibody development has been observed following SARS-CoV-2
messenger (mRNA) vaccination, including those with mRNA
vaccine-associated myocarditis,
preserving this as a critical
strategy to reduce the occurrence and severity of COVID-19.
The mechanism by which broadly reactive autoantibodies are
generated in COVID-19 has been elucidated. Infection by SARSCoV-2 drives a potent B cell response through the extrafollicular
(EF) pathway, particularly in those with severe COVID-19.
As opposed to B cell responses generated through germinal
centers (GCs), which may require weeks to fully manifest, EF
responses occur early (within days) after pathogen infection or
vaccination.
EF responses serve to provide an initial source of
antibodies, a response that is perpetuated in the absence of tolerance checkpoints present in the GCs to limit autoreactivity.
In those with COVID-19, IgG1 antibody-secreting cells (ASCs)
with autoreactivity to nuclear antigens, glomerular basement
membrane, and carbamylated proteins were expanded from B
cells possessing characteristics of EF pathway activation (i.e., double-negative B cells, little to no somatic hypermutation) within

2 weeks of infection.
Patients who died from COVID-19
lacked GCs, also suggesting an EF-dominant response.
Notably, this is the same mechanism by which autoreactive B cells drive
autoantibody production in SLE.
Because EF responses are typically transient, this leads to the
possibility that autoreactivity may also be transient in those recovering from COVID-19. Indeed, there was a rapid loss of these
IgG1 ASCs (both autoreactive and SARS-CoV-2-specific) along
with a slower contraction of autoantibody titers in those who
recovered from COVID-19.
In this same cohort, a subset of
patients with persistently elevated autoantibody titers continued
to exhibit symptoms following the acute phase of COVID-19,
consistent with PASC.
Similar observations have been reported,
with persistently elevated ANA titers noted 12 months following the acute phase in those affected by PASC.
It remains
unclear currently whether those with COVID-19-induced autoimmune disease will also experience a resolution of B cell autoreactivity and regression of their disease or possess a more durable
autoreactive response similar to those with SLE.

## Strategies to Mitigate Poor COVID-19 Outcomes in Immunosuppressed Patients With Rheumatic Disease

While vaccination remains the most durable strategy to effectively reduce the likelihood of poor clinical outcomes due to
COVID-19 in patients with rheumatic disease, those on potent
immunosuppressive regimens such as B cell–depleting agents,
anti-metabolites such as mycophenolate mofetil, and high-dose
glucocorticoids remain at risk of breakthrough infections including severe COVID-19.
Patients who are immunocompromised
have prolonged shedding of SARS-CoV-2,
which can contribute to prolonged infections that can contribute to the emergence
of novel variants.
For these susceptible individuals, additional layers of protection are needed to overcome the poor immunogenicity generated following SARS-CoV-2 vaccination due to
these immunosuppressive therapies (
Table 119.6
). Two main
strategies have emerged as tools for these patients: (1) monoclonal antibodies against the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2, delivered
either as pre- or post-exposure to SARS-CoV-2, and (2) anti-viral
medications.

### Pre-exposure Prophylaxis Using Monoclonal Antibodies

Monoclonal antibody therapies have recently emerged as an
important tool for patients on immunosuppression. Its use as
either pre-exposure prophylaxis (i.e., tixagevimab-cilgavimab) or
post-exposure therapy against asymptomatic or mild-to-moderate COVID-19 (i.e., bamlanivimab-etesevimab, casirivimabimdevimab, sotrovimab, bebtelovimab) have improved clinical
outcomes in mostly immunocompetent recipients.
These
antibodies possess highly neutralizing properties to pre-Omicron variants of concern
and, at least in the case of tixagevimab-cilgavimab, led to higher neutralizing antibody titer at
day 270 post-administration compared with nonrecipients.
Tixagevimab-cilgavimab uniquely harbor the YTE modification
(M252Y/S254T/T256E)
that increases neonatal Fc receptor
antibody affinity by nearly nine-fold compared with wild-type
IgG. This subsequently increases its half-life to nearly 90 days by
protecting against endosomal degradation and thereby enhancing
antibody recycling.
This abrogates Fc
γ
R binding, eliminating any nonneutralizing effector functions that have been observed
in mouse models of SARS-CoV-2 with sotrovimab.
Data later emerged from several observational studies in the
immunosuppressed population, particularly for tixagevimabcilgavimab, where its administration associated with dramatic
reductions in both breakthrough infection rates and severe
COVID-19 outcomes compared with nonrecipients.
The
use of casirivimab-imdevimab as post-SARS-CoV-2 exposure in
patients on B cell–depleting therapies also led to substantially
improved COVID-19 outcomes compared with nonrecipients.
Both examples clearly demonstrate the efficacy of highly neutralizing antibodies against SARS-CoV-2 spike protein in clinical
protection.
A major limitation of monoclonal antibody therapy is its
static nature, the inability to adapt to new mutational threats the
way infection can by triggering either new immune responses
to novel variants or by vaccination that can generate long-lived
germinal center responses that diversify antibody repertoires.
This limitation already has been realized with monoclonal antibodies, which had previously received early use authorizations
(EUAs) from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA),
authorizations that were subsequently revoked following the
emergence of highly immune evasive subvariants of Omicron
harboring the R346X mutation (i.e., BA.4.6, BQ.1.1, XBB) in
late 2022 that rendered these agents ineffective.
Interestingly,
in a small observational study, the emergence of mutations in
the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein was associated with reductions in
viral susceptibility to tixagevimab-cilgavimab or bebtelovimab,
although the clinical impact of such mutations remains unclear.
Nevertheless, this highlights the need to continuously generate
novel monoclonal antibodies that highly neutralize current SARSCoV-2 variants, or possibly use high-titer COVID-19 convalescent plasma, although insufficient data existed at the time of
this writing for the COVID-19 Treatment Guidelines Panel to
provide recommendations (
https://www.covid19treatmentguidelines.nih.gov/therapies/antivirals-including-antibody-products/
covid-19-convalescent-plasma/
).

### Anti-viral Therapy

Ritonavir-boosted nirmatrelvir represents the only highly effective
oral anti-viral medication for the treatment of COVID-19. Nirmatrelivir targets the SARS-CoV-2 3-chymotrypsin-like cysteine protease (M
),
an essential enzyme in cleavage of viral polyproteins
into functional proteins,
and ritonavir is a CYP3A4 inhibitor
that boosts nirmatrelvir pharmacokinetics.
A reduction in the
risk of hospitalization or death in unvaccinated adults with high risk
of progressing to COVID-19 with laboratory-confirmed SARSCoV-2 infection was observed in those taking ritonavir-boosted
nirmatrelvir, although <1% of the participants in the treatment
group were immunosuppressed.
Data for patients with rheumatic disease emerged from the MassGenBrigham effort, where in a
retrospective analysis, 1.3% of those who received ritonavir-boosted
nirmatrelvir required hospitalization due to COVID-19 compared
with 17.6% of those who received no outpatient treatment.
After
adjusting for age, estimated glomerular filtration rate, race/ethnicity, comorbidities, and zip code and median household income,
the OR for severe COVID-19 in those who received anti-virals was
0.13 compared with no outpatient treatment (95% CI, 0.06–0.28).
Notably, most of the study participants had been vaccinated against
SARS-CoV-2, further supporting the use of anti-viral therapy in
this population.

## Conclusion

Taken together, the data generated thus far have provided reassuring results for most immunosuppressed patients with rheumatic
disease. While there appears to be increased risk of SARS-CoV-2
infection, most patients requiring immunosuppression for their
rheumatic disease appear to have acceptable clinical outcomes
from COVID-19. Anti-virals continue to be an important mitigating strategy for those in the early phase of COVID-19, while
monoclonal antibodies that can neutralize the circulating Omicron variants are under development.
Important questions remain, however. Will SARS-CoV-2 continue to evolve with more immune-evasive variants, requiring
updated vaccines and monoclonal antibodies? If so, will those at
the highest risk of both poor COVID-19 outcomes and immunogenicity responses both to SARS-CoV-2 infection or vaccination
face a continuous threat that cannot be fully mitigated? Should we
be withholding immunosuppressives following infection or prior
to vaccination, and for how long? What will be the long-term fate

of those who have acquired new autoimmune syndromes following COVID-19? Finally, a formal discussion of PASC, outside of
acquired de novo autoimmune disease described previously will
require formal diagnostic or classification criteria, as over 200
symptoms have been attributed to this entity.
We anticipate
having a clearer idea of the impact PASC has on patients with
rheumatic disease in the future.
