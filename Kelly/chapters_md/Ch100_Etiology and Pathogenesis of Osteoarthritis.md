> joint homeostasis, resulting in inflammation, pain, tissue damage,

> and other features of joint organ failure.

> Derangements in joint physiology in OA are caused by cell stress in all

> joint tissues, which suppresses endogenous tissue repair processes

> and impairs joint healing.

> A key feature of OA pathophysiology is induction of innate immune

> inflammatory mechanisms that accelerate tissue damage and

> stimulate nociceptive signaling and pain experiences.

> Key OA risk factors include age, sex, obesity and metabolic syndrome,

> abnormal biomechanics, and genetics.

## Introduction

Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common joint disease in the
world and yet one of the least well-understood forms of arthritis.
It affects more than 500 million people worldwide and is one of
the world’s major causes of disability.
According to the World
Health Organization, OA causes a greater number of years lived
with disability with a greater impact on disability than either heart
disease or tuberculosis. OA is one of the greatest unmet need in
rheumatology and currently lacks any approved disease modifying
therapy, largely due to a limited understanding of the pathophysiology of the disease.
The cardinal sign of OA is joint pain, especially pain that
occurs or increases during physical activity. Other symptoms
including joint stiffness, “gelling” phenomenon, aching, and
other distressing experiences occur at different stages of the disease. Notably, the development of constant pain (the experience
of pain that never goes away, even though it may fluctuate) is a
sign of progression from early to later stages of OA.
Underlying
these symptoms is a complex pathophysiology within the joint
environment, leading to derangements in homeostatic joint
physiology and damage to essential joint tissues. OA pathogenesis progresses over years, fluctuates over time, and ultimately
produces the clinically recognizable features of tissue damage,
pain, and loss of joint and physical function, which is best
described as joint failure.
Decades of important basic biomedical and clinical translational research have improved our understanding of the pathophysiologic mechanisms that occur during OA. However, it is
increasingly recognized that it is the integration of these pathophysiologic processes through cross-talk mechanisms within and
between joint tissues that ultimately leads to disease sequelae.
Ongoing work aims to understand the full extent of integrated
OA joint pathophysiology and marks an exciting new phase
in the field, with opportunities to identify disease-modifying
treatments.

## The Synovial Joint Is an Organ

Fundamental biologic mechanisms are required for maintaining
the homeostasis of the synovial joint organ system. As a group
of tissues with interdependent functions, the synovial joint is
an organ like any other. The functional integration of the joint
capsule, ligaments, synovium, synovial fat pads, articular cartilage, subchondral bone, and accessory tissues such as menisci (in
some joints) permits movement while accommodating and normalizing mechanical loads. When synovial joints fail, movement
is significantly impaired or completely lost. Maintaining joint

homeostasis is therefore critical to joint function. This simple
concept relies on complex physiology at the local joint level and
is modulated through systemic physiologic interactions with the
rest of the body.

### Synovial Joints Maintain Homeostasis by Interfacing With the Rest of the Body

All organs require perfusion, lymphatic drainage, and innervation to sustain tissue homeostasis. Synovial joints require
the same inputs and egresses along with mechanical supports
provided by bones and their ligament and musculotendinous
junctions. Nearly all perfusion of the joint is handled by the
vascular and lymphatic supply to/from the synovium and joint
capsule. Oxygen and nutrients dissolved in blood plasma are
perfused through these tissues and into the joint cavity to supply
the avascular, alymphatic, and aneural cartilage and the inner
fibrocartilaginous two-thirds of menisci when present,
although
the outer fibrous third of the meniscus has its own limited vascular–lymphatic and sensory nerve supply.
Oxygen tension
and glucose concentration are therefore highest at the surface
of healthy articular cartilage (cartilage–synovial fluid interface)
with declining gradients of each factor established between the
surface and deeper zones.
Conversely, inverted gradients are
established due to blood flow in subchondral bone, which helps
to supply oxygen and glucose to cartilage from below the cartilage–subchondral bone interface.
Although synovium-mediated perfusion is vital for cartilage and
other tissue homeostasis, it has been demonstrated that additional
inputs and egresses exist at the junction between articular cartilage
and subchondral bone in both healthy and diseased states. The
presence of small channels emanating from the subchondral bone
and extending into the calcified articular cartilage zone
provides
additional sources for nutrient delivery,
which are important for
chondrocyte survival.
This interface also provides opportunities
for articular chondrocytes to communicate with osteocytes within
the underlying supportive bony structure, for example through
prostaglandin signaling,
with osteoblasts, osteoclasts, and
other bone marrow stromal cells. Although it is likely that the
osteochondral junction plays an even more important role in joint
diseases such as OA,
more detailed investigation is needed to
understand the nature and mechanisms of molecular communication between cartilage and bone in homeostasis.
Due to the fundamental relationship between synovial joints
and movement, sensory and sympathetic innervation to the joint
is critical. Sensory fibers innervating the synovium and joint capsule, the outer third of the meniscus, subchondral bone, tendons,
and ligaments provide real-time and constant proprioceptive and
nociceptive feedback to the central nervous system for optimal
control and protection of the joint during movement and rest.
Underscoring the vital importance of joint sensory innervation,
catastrophic joint failure can rapidly occur due to severe sensory
nerve dysfunction, also known as a neuropathic joint. Impaired
proprioceptive and nociceptive function in sensory nerves can
occur due to primary (e.g., Charcot–Marie–Tooth disease
) or
secondary (e.g. long-standing diabetes
) peripheral neuropathies.
Additionally, sympathetic fibers accompanying the vascular supply to synovial joint tissues regulate blood flow to these structures, which is critical for joint homeostasis as described. Control
of these sympathetic inputs is regulated both locally (e.g., due to
release of purinergic signals by local joint tissues) and centrally by
the autonomic nervous system.

### Joints Bear Mechanical Loads and Permit Movement

Synovial fluid starts out as blood perfusate, which synovial joints
further modify to support normal joint functioning during movement. These modifications also support communication between
peripheral joint tissues such as the capsule and synovium with
articular cartilage. The synovium, and to a lesser extent the superficial layer of articular cartilage and menisci,
secrete multiple
factors including the lubricating protein lubricin, encoded by
PRG4
in humans.
Lubricin dramatically lowers the coefficient
of friction of synovial fluid.
This provides the healthy joint
with easy, near-frictionless movement at the surfaces of cartilage
and tendons, whereas loss of lubricin due to joint diseases likely
plays a key role in loss of joint function. Lubricin has additional
functions in joint homeostasis such as the prevention of synovial
fibroblast hyperplasia.
Synovium also secretes large amounts of
hyaluronan into the healthy joint. At higher concentrations, hyaluronan can form a gel due to its physical properties as a polymer,
which helps cartilage to resist compressive loads. In joint diseases
when hyaluronan concentration is much lower, these properties
are reduced or lost,
which subjects the joint tissues to abnormal
mechanical loading. However, hyaluronan on its own is a poor
lubricant.
By interacting with lubricin, the hyaluronan–lubricin
complex provides the joint with excellent boundary lubrication (at
tissue surfaces) and high load-carrying capacity, including during
movement.
Muscular contractions exert forces through tendinous insertions into bones to generate movement. Muscle-tendon functions are therefore closely related to synovial joints and have direct
impacts on joint physiology. Through gravity and muscle-generated forces, the joint bears several different types and magnitudes of
mechanical loading with wide ranging effects on synovial joint tissue physiology. Synovial joint structures have evolved accordingly
in order to withstand and respond adaptively to these mechanical
loads. Although historical medical advice about managing jointrelated problems often included limitations on movement and
activity, important advances through arthritis and musculoskeletal research have transformed our understanding of the important
clinical and physiologic benefits of nearly all forms of movement
(sometimes meeting definitions of exercise).
The cumulative
effects of movement on synovial joint tissues provide an excellent
model to understand how normal joint physiology is dependent
on normal joint loading and movement.
Fibrous tissues such as the joint capsule provide mechanical
stability to the joint during movement and prevents disarticulation. Strain forces on the joint capsule and synovium during acute
exercise produce anti-inflammatory responses including the production of interleukin-10 (IL-10),
which is a major cytokine
involved in resolution of inflammation and tissue healing. Conversely, joint immobilization caused joint capsule atrophy and
collagen bundle narrowing within 2 weeks in rats.
In a similar experiment, immobilization of joint tissues caused increased
expression of type I, II, and III collagen and matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)-1 and -13, with lower levels of tissue inhibitor of
metalloproteinase (TIMP)-1, -2, and -3 expression in rabbit joint
capsules.
Although overloading any tissue can clearly cause traumatic injury, normal loading is required for normal joint tissue
physiologic functions. Lack of normal tissue loading can quickly
lead to the activation of catabolic mechanisms including the secretion of inflammatory cytokines and MMPs that can weaken tissues through enzymatic digestion.

### Osteoarthritis Causes Joint Organ Dysfunction

Due to the large number of risk factors and associations of OA
with other diseases, OA cases result from a range of multidimensional processes that disrupt healthy joint physiology (
Fig. 100.1
).
Regardless of the contributing factors, OA results from failure
to restore and maintain joint homeostasis. As described above,
synovial joints are designed to withstand normal environmental
stresses and maintain joint homeostasis under these conditions.
Therefore OA can develop when joints are subjected to abnormal
environmental stresses that overwhelm endogenous repair and
homeostatic mechanisms and/or when risk factors reduce the ability of the joint to withstand normal environmental stresses.
The abundance of evidence in the field currently suggests that
pathogenic OA mechanisms probably initiate in structural joint
tissues such as cartilage and bone, with emerging evidence that
pathogenic mechanisms in synovium, tendons, ligaments, and
meniscus propagate the disease. At the tissue level, it is often difficult to differentiate the pathophysiologic effects of risk factors
on joint tissues from OA-specific mechanisms. Together, these
mechanisms lead to the failure of joint tissues to withstand various normal and abnormal sources of physiologic stress. The resulting pathophysiology is analogous to organ failure in other diseases
such as cardiac or renal failure.

usually a mechanical injury. Articular cartilage, subchondral
bone, and synovium all have and contribute to the regenerative
capabilities of the joint in response to injury. These processes

### Articular Cartilage

Normal, Physiologic Mechanical Loading and
Homeostasis
Articular cartilage is subjected to a wide range of mechanical loads
during routine daily activities. For example, stair climbing can
generate peak compressive forces between 10 to 20 Megapascals
(MPa) that send signals to the articular chondrocytes embedded within the dense extra-cellular matrix (ECM).
In the short
term, this leads to small degree of cartilage compression but when
sustained can lead to compression of up to 45% of the cartilage
thickness.
This unique ability of cartilage to withstand different degrees and durations of mechanical compressive, tensile, and
shear forces makes it a specialized tissue that requires a unique set
of homeostatic programs to maintain its integrity. The durability
of articular cartilage over time is related to the chondrocytes and
cartilage ECM components, especially the major proteoglycans
(e.g., aggrecan) and collagens (type II collagen). Mechanical forces
that are transmitted through the cartilage ECM are transduced
by articular chondrocytes. Numerous mechanisms allow chondrocytes to detect and respond to the mechanical stimuli.
Normal mechanical loading of joint tissues produces protective and adaptive biologic responses that promote homeostasis and
maintain the health of the joint. For example, running produces
knee cartilage strains (compression) of 10% to 30%, varying by
joint region, to which chondrocytes respond with increased synthesis of ECM components including collagens type II and IX,
cartilage oligomeric protein (COMP),
glycosaminoglycans,
and aggrecan.
In contrast, both insufficient or excessive tissue

loading inhibits these anabolic effects.
Too little activity (e.g.,
among sedentary populations) is therefore an important biomechanical risk factor for OA. Physically or metabolically injured
joint tissues are also more susceptible to mechanical loads, such
that loading magnitudes that approach the upper limit of normal
might produce abnormal/injurious responses.
Normal loading of intact/healthy cartilage can provide counterbalancing effects including suppression of ECM disruption
by MMPs and aggrecanases, which are produced by chondrocytes in response to stimulation with IL-1
β
.
Mechanical loading in healthy and mouse models of OA (e.g., experimental OA
induced in mouse knee joints by surgical joint destabilization) also
decreases endoplasmic reticulum stress and increases autophagy
in chondrocytes of noncalcified and calcified cartilage and in
synovial cells.
However, normal biomechanical loading is not
well-defined, despite several decades of research into the effects
of static and dynamic compression, shear forces, physiochemical
stimuli, hydrostatic pressure, and even electric currents in articular chondrocytes and cartilage. In general, static compression is
deleterious, whereas dynamic compression is generally protective
within the range of normal (physiologic) loading. Static compression of articular cartilage and chondrocytes inhibits the synthesis of aggrecan and other proteoglycans and increases in cytokine
and chemokine expression that paradigmatically lead to induction of MMPs and other degradative enzymes.
On the
other hand, dynamic compression stimulates synthesis of aggrecans and matrix components and their assembly to restore tissue
matrix.
The frequency and amplitude of dynamic compression determines whether loading produces protective or deleterious physiologic effects, which has important considerations for
clinicians and rehabilitation. For example, patients with hip or
knee OA who must stand for long periods of time due to occupational requirements are at risk of OA exacerbation from prolonged
static loading.
Responses to dynamic compression are conserved across mammalian species, although most studies have been completed in
small and large nonhuman mammals. Human cartilage likely has
the same properties, although experimental testing of live, healthy
human cartilage in studies is uncommon, and most experimentation has been done in diseased samples with OA,
usually at a late
stage of disease. Dynamic compressive loading in chondrocytes
also stimulates the production of chondrogenic growth factors
and cytokines, including transforming growth factor
β
(TGFβ
).
It has also been shown that prolonged dynamic compression
up to 12 hours per day in tissue culture is able to stimulate proteoglycan synthesis in mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs) and can
increase differentiation along a chondrogenic program, even in the
absence of chondrogenic cytokines and growth factors. Therefore
the combination of both physiologic dynamic compression and
exposure to chondrogenic factors is likely most effective to induce
chondrogenesis and/or maintain homeostasis under conditions
of physiologic stress. Subsequent responses to normal loading
include changes in chondrocyte gene expression including at the
type II collagen promoter and synthesis and deposition of glycosaminoglycan (GAG) in the ECM over a period of days to a few
weeks.
Reflecting similar patterns in articular cartilage in vivo,
the increased expression/synthesis is typically concentrated in
the central region of the loaded area, with decreased expression/
synthesis in the annular region.
Importantly, stimulation of
aggrecan production by dynamic compression appears to be localized to regions with high interstitial fluid flow.
Taken together,
these in  vitro and in  vivo animal model studies indicate that
appropriate biomechanical loading of articular cartilage is essential for the maintenance of chondrocytes and cartilage homeostasis. This concept has bolstered the rehabilitation and orthopedics
fields to prioritize correction and maintenance of normal biomechanical loading in patients, but it remains unclear how to define
appropriate biomechanical loading in the clinical context, and
work in this area is ongoing.
Cartilage Responses to Acute Injury
Responses to articular cartilage tissue injury have been well characterized. Articular cartilage has limited capacity for intrinsic
repair due to its avascular nature and therefore the inability to
mount a de facto wound-healing response. However, contrary to
earlier descriptions of cartilage as having insufficient endogenous
repair,
articular cartilage does have the ability to autonomously
repair superficial lesions, whereas deeper lesions may be repaired
by fibrocartilaginous tissue that is different from healthy articular cartilage in both cellular and ECM composition, leading to
altered mechanical properties of the repair tissue.
As a result, it
is commonly thought that this repair tissue is more susceptible to
ongoing damage and disruption and therefore increases the risk of
developing OA.
In response to injury, chondrocytes within the articular cartilage proliferate and synthesize ECM components such as collagen and proteoglycans to repair the damaged tissue. In addition,
progenitor cells located in the bone marrow and synovium can
migrate and differentiate into chondrocytes to help repair the
damaged cartilage. In the setting of blunt or acute trauma, chondrocyte death leads to hypocellularity within the cartilage matrix.
Interestingly, it has been shown that chondrogenic progenitor cells
respond to this cartilage injury and repopulate the areas where
mature chondrocytes have been lost within 7 to 14 days after the
injury.
These articular cartilage–derived chondrogenic progenitor cells (CPCs) migrate from the surrounding matrix and were
first discovered in calf cartilage in 2004.
CPCs reside mostly in
the superficial zone of articular cartilage
and are defined by
their co-expression of CD105 and CD166
or expression of CD9/
CD90/CD166,
clonogenicity, multipotency, and expression of
chondrogenic genes.
There is no single marker that defines
CPC cell type, but numerous studies have reported that NOTCH
expression is key for the progenitor phenotype of CPCs in addition to the markers mentioned above.
CPCs comprise up to
∼
5% of the total articular chondrocyte population but expand in
number under OA conditions.
Interestingly, additional sources
of progenitor cells have also been reported, including cells originating in the bone marrow and migrating from below the tidemark and calcified cartilage.
Articular cartilage-derived CPCs
do not undergo hypertrophic chondrocyte differentiation, unlike
bone marrow–derived MSCs with chondrogenic potential.
Suppression of the osteogenic transcription factor RUNX2 in
CPCs increases the expression of SOX9 (a key chondrogenic transcription factor) and enhance the capability of CPCs to produce
and secrete ECM components that are key for articular cartilage repair.
In vivo, CPCs migrate toward the site of injury to
participate in endogenous repair of the cartilage lesion.
CPCs
respond to and migrate toward chemotactic signals including
advanced glycation end products, damage-associated molecular
patterns (DAMPs), and alarmins such as mitochondrial DNA and
HMGB-1.
Such factors are released after injury by chondrocytes
undergoing necrotic cell death.
Taken together, these features underscore that articular cartilage possesses intrinsic repair capability. Further, these studies

highlight that OA can result from the failure of intrinsic cartilage repair mechanisms to meet increased demand on the joint
organ system leading to joint failure. Clearly, the endogenous
repair potential of articular cartilage is an effective contributor to
maintaining joint homeostasis in the absence of other OA-precipitating risk factors. The last two decades of work to characterize
and understand these repair mechanisms has led to excitement in
the OA field that it may be possible to harness the intrinsic repair
capability of cartilage for therapeutic benefit.
Fibroblast Growth Factor Signaling and Acute Cartilage
Injury
Although most of our early knowledge about the role of fibroblast
growth factor (FGF) signaling came from developmental biology,
much has been learned about the role of FGF signaling in articular
cartilage homeostasis and response to injury. In particular, FGF2
is produced endogenously in articular cartilage.
It is sequestered within the ECM by proteoglycans, including perlecan and
heparan sulfate proteoglycan. When articular cartilage is acutely
injured, FGF2 is released from the ECM to activate FGF receptors (FGFRs) on the surface of articular chondrocytes. During
OA, FGF2 expression is nearly twice as high as that of healthy
normal joints in the synovial fluid. Because of the effects of FGF2
on driving vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) expression and neovascularization, a role for FGF2 in driving catabolism, impaired cartilage homeostasis, and hyperalgesia has been
proposed.
An important dichotomy exists between humans and murine
models of OA that confounded the field for some time. We now
understand that human articular chondrocytes predominantly
express FGFR1 and FGFR3, whereas mice and other murine species express FGFR2 and FGFR4.
The dominant FGF receptor
expression profile (with or without FGFR1) changes the role of
FGF2 depending on the species context and has thereby limited the translatability of FGF treatments from pre-clinical OA
models into patients. FGF2 activates both FGFR1 and FGFR3,
so the expression profile of the receptor on chondrocytes determines the most likely biologic outcome. FGF2 in humans will
activate FGFR1 signaling, which leads to Ras/Raf/MEK/ERK
(mitogen-activated protein kinase [MAPK]) signaling, activation of RUNX2-mediated expression of matrix metalloprotease
13 (MMP-13), a disintegrin and metalloprotease with thrombospondin motif-4 and -5 (ADAMTS-4, ADAMTS-5), type X collagen, and VEGF, stimulating ECM degradation and inhibition
of anabolic repair processes.
In contrast, when FGFR3
expression is higher, FGF2 signaling leads to anabolic effects and
maintenance of chondrocyte phenotype, which sustains homeostasis and cartilage repair. Overall, FGF2 (via FGFR1) leads to
catabolic loss of ECM in human cartilage and thus is not a viable
therapy. On the other hand, FGF18 is specific for FGFR3 signaling and does not bind to FGFR1. Thus FGF18 is an anabolic
growth factor that is involved in chondrogenesis and endochondral development
and is also important for maintenance of articular cartilage phenotype and repair. Although both FGFR1 and
FGFR3 are capable of activating MAPK signaling, FGFR3 leads
to suppression of the transcription factor noggin,
which otherwise suppresses the protective effects of bone morphogenetic protein 7 (BMP7) signaling. Thus FGFR3 signals stimulate cartilage
repair. It has been shown in acute cartilage impact injury models
that FGFR3 signaling stimulated by recombinant human FGF18
(rhFGF-18; also known as sprifermin) leads to increased synthesis of aggrecan, increased type II collagen synthesis biomarkers
(CPII), and decreased type II collagen turnover biomarker C2C.
Moreover, a large randomized, double-blind placebo-controlled
trial of rhFGF-18 led to increases in articular cartilage thickness
in patients with established radiographic knee OA.
This study
validated the chondrogenic potential of FGFR3 signaling that was
seen in pre-clinical studies. These recent examples demonstrate the
importance of pre-clinical research and underpin the enthusiasm
of harnessing endogenous repair processes to sustain homeostasis
and repair articular cartilage that has been damaged due to injury.

### Bone Marrow–Derived Mesenchymal Stem Cells and Endogenous Joint Tissue Repair

Endogenous joint tissue repair mechanisms also lead to the
recruitment of MSCs from other tissues, which originate from
a bone marrow lineage. Largely, these reach the joint through
the synovium either by perfusion or expansion of synovial tissuederived MSCs
or via the subchondral bone/bone marrow
through the osteochondral junction.
Surface marker expression
of bone marrow–derived MSCs is similar to that of articular cartilage derived CPCs, with the main difference being the expression
of hematopoietic markers.
Numerous chemoattractant–receptor pairs have been studied

FGF2 via FGFR-1, FGFR-2, FGFR-3, or FGFR-4; Nerve growth

among other factors.
These factors are induced and released
by injured chondrocytes and other joint tissues and contribute to
the initial recruitment of a pool of MSCs to participate in joint
healing responses.
The inflammatory joint environment in OA leads to breakdown
of hyaluronic acid (HA), and low molecular weight HA will bind
to TLR-4, thereby increasing MSC migration.
Indeed, MSCs are
found in high abundance in synovial fluid of patients with OA
and may adhere to articular cartilage. However, it has been shown
that this adherence is inhibited by HA through interactions with
the CD44 hyaluronic acid receptor expressed on MSCs.
Highermolecular-weight HA creates a pericellular HA coat that physically
impairs MSC adherence to articular cartilage.
It is thought that
the intrinsic repair mechanisms mediated by MSCs may be ineffective an OA because of the inhibitory effect of hyaluronic acid.
The synovium remains an important reservoir of adult MSCs
with joint regenerative capability. Synovial-derived MSCs arise
during joint patterning and formation of the joint interzone during embryonic development and can be defined as mesenchymal
in origin by marker expression of growth and differentiation factor
5 (GDF5) and persist throughout adult life.
The joint regenerative capability of these synovial-derived MSCs is dependent on
Yes-associated protein (YAP). These cells contribute to synovial
lining hyperplasia and migrate to sides of cartilage injury to participate in cartilage repair. Notwithstanding the discussion above on
the inhibitory effects of HA and other factors that may interfere
with MSC binding to articular cartilage, synovial-derived MSCs
at least have the potential to participate directly in cartilage repair
processes.

### Synovial Tissue

Synovium is a highly regenerative tissue that lines the inner surface of the joint capsule and produces synovial fluid. In response
to injury, synovial cells can proliferate and produce ECM components such as hyaluronan and proteoglycans to help repair the
damaged tissue. As a connective tissue, synovium has its own vasculature, lymphatic, and neural structures. As such, synovial tissue
is able to undergo canonical wound-healing processes. With a simple mechanical injury, the synovial membrane does not undergo
an extensive hypertrophic reaction but can demonstrate vasomotor changes with capillary dilatation and exudative effusion developing in the joint.
A small cellular infiltrate will follow, and the
synovial lining (intima) will increase from 3 to 10 cell layers thick.
This morphologic change will resolve over days to weeks as long
as the injury is not sustained. Accordingly, surgical models of OA
that rely on joint arthrotomy will demonstrate complete healing
of synovial tissue with minimal or no residual scar. This woundhealing property can be used as a “sham” or control condition,
which is then compared with the experimental OA condition in
which additional joint structures are transected, leading to joint
destabilization.
If synovial injury is repeated, post-traumatic
synovitis will develop. This includes further thickening of the
synovial intima, further infiltration of perivascular lymphocytes
and monocytes, and infiltration of myeloid (mostly macrophages)
and regenerative cells (MSCs) into the synovial fluid.
The synovial membrane also contains a population of progenitor cells that can differentiate into various cell types including
synovial fibroblasts and macrophages.
Synovial tissue–derived
MSCs were first identified in human synovial tissue in 2001.
Numerous subsequent studies have investigated the regenerative
potential of these cells noting important properties including
clonality, migration, and multipotency but also some important
limitations. Several studies have demonstrated the ability of synovial tissue–derived MSCs to migrate first to synovial fluid
and
then to the damaged areas of the joint and differentiate into chondrocytes and osteoblasts, leading to regeneration of both articular
cartilage and subchondral bone.
One example of a study
on synovial joint tissue regeneration involved the injection of
MSCs into the joints of rabbits with induced OA.
It was determined that the regenerative potential of synovial tissue– and bone
marrow–derived MSCs is greater than that of MSCs derived from
muscle or adipose tissues.
Although in vitro conditions may be ideal for chondrogenesis
and maximize the regenerative potential of MSCs, the in  vivo
OA joint milieu is more complex and less amenable to sustaining chondrogenic programs. There are likely numerous inhibitory
factors that reduce the ability and regenerative capacity of MSCs
to participate in joint repair regardless of their site of origin. However, secreted anabolic factors from synovial cells also play a role in
determining cell fates. Several researchers have found that synovial
fluid contains high levels of growth factors such as TGFβ
, which
can promote the proliferation and differentiation of synovial cells
to repair damaged tissue. For example, in a surgically induced
rat model of OA, it was shown that synovial tissues stimulate a
transient anabolic response in articular chondrocytes and that this
effect was mediated at least partly by TGFβ
.

### Subchondral Bone

Mechanobiological responses occur in subchondral bone,
including early after acute traumatic or cyclic loading events.
Thinning of the subchondral plate within 1 to 2 weeks after joint
injury is attributed to increased osteoclast activity causing early
bone resorption and is associated with the onset and advancement
These observations from mouse OA models
of cartilage lesions.
demonstrate the mechanical interrelationship between cartilage
and bone, which may also be partially mediated by signaling at
the bone–cartilage interface.
The biologic interactions occurring between cartilage and synovium or between cartilage and
bone underscore the importance of appropriate tissue loading to
restore joint homeostasis when the joint experiences stressors such
as injury. Bone remodeling in response to mechanical loading
was also seen in a rabbit model of hindlimb loading.
Increased
osteoblast-mediated bone mineral deposition was rapidly followed by newly mineralized bone tissue formation. The sequential
activation of bone resorption, followed by bone formation and
remodeling, indicates the dynamic nature in early responses of
subchondral bone to mechanical loads. Similar changes appear
to occur in humans based on analogous imaging studies.
However, more work is needed to understand the importance
and regional differences in subchondral bone microenvironment
changes in response to acute and chronic injuries. Conflicting
studies have shown that subchondral cortical plate and trabecular
thickness will decrease shortly after injury such as in the medial
femoral groove model of OA in dogs,
while thickness increased
in rabbit and mouse OA models after medial meniscectomy.
Some differences in these observations may be explained by altered
weight bearing on the affected limb after the induced injury.
Similar to the cartilage context, cyclic compressive loading also
activates regenerative responses in subchondral bone including
adaptive increases in subchondral cortical bone plate thickness in
the posterior tibial plateau, corresponding to regions of increased
load transfer.
This tends to line up with the regions of cartilage
pathology as well, underscoring the linkage between the osteochondral components.
In multiple studies, trabecular bone
mass will decrease in opposition to the increasing subchondral
plate cortical bone thickness.
The induction and resolution of bone marrow lesions (BMLs)
under areas of articular cartilage impact also show signs of regenerative response to injury. Bone marrow edema is detected using
water-sensitive sequences on MRI, revealing areas of microfracture.
Histopathological studies of BML have demonstrated
perivascular edema and features of tissue healing in humans and
animal models of injury.
Active bone remodeling is typically
seen and osteocytes play a key role in mediating the bone remodeling process through the release of ATP, membrane-derived lipids such as sphingosine-1-phosphate, numerous chemokines, and
VEGF species including VEGFA and RANKL (receptor activator
of NFκ
B ligand).
RANKL contributes to the recruitment
and activation of osteoclasts in the subchondral microenvironment
to stimulate bone remodeling and repair of the microfractures
associated with BMLs. Although it is clear that bone responds
rapidly to normal and injurious mechanical loading, it is not clear
whether the early changes affect longer-term clinical outcomes.
Osteophyte Formation
Although osteophytes are clearly a radiographic hallmark of OA,
they also occur in people with no history of active symptomatic
OA.
Further, osteophytes are formed through regenerative biologic processes in the joint environment. As such, osteophytes are
considered an epiphenomenon associated with OA rather than a
pathogenic driver of OA. Osteophytes may be formed as a compensatory attempt to improve joint stabilization, since surgical

removal of osteophytes increases joint instability.
The size of
osteophytes is not associated with an increased risk of progression
in OA,
underscoring that osteophytes are mostly in keeping
with a sign of active or prior joint tissue repair. The lack of association of osteophyte size with OA progression may be because some
patients with fewer or smaller osteophytes have less severe disease,
whereas others have more severe disease but mount a less robust
anabolic response to OA (“atrophic OA”). Thus interpreting the
presence, features, or changes in osteophytes in the clinic is contextual and challenging.
Osteophytes arise from cell populations at the intersecting
cartilage and periosteal margins and at the entheses where they
are known as enthesophytes. They arise due to recapitulation of
the endochondral bone formation process. Multipotent periosteal
cells are recruited to the joint margin and activated and proliferate in response to an inflammatory or mechanical stimulus.
Periosteal cells will then differentiate along a chondrogenic lineage until hypertrophic chondrocyte formation occurs.
This
initially progresses centrally within the developing osteophyte and
then expands outwardly as chondrocytes/cartilage undergo mineralization and eventual replacement by osseous tissues mediated
by TGFβ
and BMP2.
The origin of osteophytes is also
clearly linked to mechanobiological responses. It has been shown
that osteophytes can be induced with a single mechanical impact
applied to the periosteum in a rat model.
Underscoring the concept that osteophytes develop in the absence of OA-related joint
pathophysiology, this model confirmed that mechanical manipulation of the periosteum itself is sufficient to develop osteophytes
in the absence of OA.

### Cross-Talk Mechanisms and Tissue Repair

Cartilage–Bone Cross-Talk
Proper functioning of the osteochondral unit is essential for joint
homeostasis. In the 1950s, it was believed that the calcified cartilage was an impermeable barrier and that transfer of molecules
in solution was not possible between the subchondral bone and
overlying cartilage. In 1962, it was shown that cationic dyes could
permeate the subchondral bone to the calcified cartilage interface.
Numerous studies have since demonstrated that crosstalk between subchondral bone and articular cartilage supports
cartilage oxygenation, nutrient delivery, and the biomechanical
properties of both tissues.
Most of the interactions between
chondrocytes and osteocytes in the osteochondral junction
is indirect via soluble mediators. However, there may be some
regions where cells are close enough to permit cell–cell contact.
Survival factors are made by subchondral bone cells including
osteocytes and osteoblasts. These factors increase the viability
of chondrocytes, which has been observed in explant co-culture
models.
Healthy osteoblasts can stimulate the expression of
aggrecan, type II collagen, SOX9, and decrease the expression of
MMP-3 and MMP-13 in chondrocytes. Cell culture of chondrocytes together with healthy osteoblasts can blunt the induction
of inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1
β
and IL-6,
whereas
osteoblasts from diseased bone in OA abrogate most of these
effects.
Taken together, these studies indicate that interactions between subchondral bone and deep zone/calcified zone
articular cartilage support joint homeostasis.
Cartilage Cross-Talk With Synovium and Adipose Tissue
Cross-talk between synovium and other tissues in the joint is
an emerging field. Co-culture of synovial tissue with articular
chondrocytes leads to induced anabolic responses in articular
chondrocytes, mediated in part by TGFβ
signaling.
Similarly,
co-culture of cartilage with synovium enhanced the effect of interleukin-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1Ra), including less GAG release
and nitric oxide generation and induction of anti-catabolic factors including IL-14, matrilin-3, and carbonic anhydrase.
On
the other hand, single-cell RNA sequencing studies of human OA
synovial and cartilage tissues have generated prediction models,
suggesting that procatabolic cross-talk occurs between synovial
cell subpopulations and articular chondrocytes.
In particular,
inflammatory synovial macrophages expressing high amounts of
IL-1
β
and dendritic cells (DCs) may contribute to pro-inflammatory responses in chondrocytes. Supporting these findings,
co-culture of bovine cartilage with synovium increases the activity
of MMPs and aggrecanases to carry out cartilage degradation.
This suggests that targeted depletion or inhibition of pathologic
synovial cell subpopulations could be a therapeutic strategy in
OA. Similarly, procatabolic signaling between adipose tissue and
cartilage has been suggested by surgically inducing experimental
OA in a lipodystrophy mouse model.
The absence of adipose
tissue protected against the development of joint damage and joint
pain, whereas reconstitution of systemic adipose stores abrogated
the protective effects on joint disease development.
Modeling experiments using whole transcriptome gene expression databases have demonstrated the potential for cross-talk
signaling between all joint tissues including articular cartilage,
synovium, subchondral bone, meniscus, and adipose tissues.
However, follow-up studies to confirm the biologic and clinical
importance of these modeled ligand–receptor interactions are
needed to better understand how their changes affect the joint
milieu. Together, these studies provide important insights into
the critical role played by regenerative and pathogenic cross-talk
mechanisms between joint tissues on the maintenance of joint
homeostasis (
Fig. 100.2
).

## Osteoarthritis Risk Factors and Their Effects on Joint Physiology

Classical OA risk factors exert their negative effects on OA risk
by overwhelming and inhibiting joint regenerative responses and/
or promoting pro-inflammatory mechanisms, leading to pain and
joint tissue damage.

### Aging

Aging is associated with loss of physiologic homeostasis, which
leads to compromised tissue function and increased susceptibility
to tissue injury and damage.
OA is not an inevitable consequence of aging, but age is a major OA risk factor.
Age-related
changes are apparent in joint tissues. In cartilage, a loss of physiologically appropriate mechanical properties, decreased cellularity, and thinning (atrophy) are all features of natural aging. While
these changes do not cause OA directly, aging processes increase
the susceptibility to OA onset by reducing the capacity (organ
reserve) of joint tissues to compensate and adapt to normal environmental stresses. Other age-related changes include increased
infrapatellar fat pad size and secretion of adipokines into the
joint.
Many of the hallmarks of aging
such as increased cellular senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, telomere attrition,
and altered cell–cell communication have been described in joint
tissues.

Age-related mitochondrial dysfunction causes impaired respiratory chain function, decreased ATP generation, and increased
pro-inflammatory mediators
similar to those released by

Indeed, mitochondrial DNA haplogroups can increase
risk and severity of OA or conversely protect against OA development.
Interestingly, when one group compared proteomic

signatures related to mitochondrial function.
Redox homeostasis is maintained through the balance of oxidizing and reducing
reactions.
An inverse relationship between reactive oxygen species (ROS) and antioxidants can be seen in aging. While ROSs
increase with age,
decreases in antioxidants occur including
glutathione and superoxide dismutase-2 (SOD2), rendering the
cells more vulnerable to the effects of ROS.
Increasing ROS
concentrations can cause mitochondrial DNA damage
and
exacerbate respiratory chain dysfunction. Ultimately, alterations
in redox homeostasis increase intra-cellular ROS and cause oxidative stress and production of inflammatory mediators, which can
stimulate MMP-mediated tissue damage.
Oxidative stress
and age-associated reductions in antioxidant protein function accelerates telomere shortening and cellular aging, promoting chondrocyte senescence and apoptosis.

During oxidative stress, excess ROS promote oxidative post-translational modification and cleavage of cartilage collagen and aggrecan.
These modifications ultimately result in disorganization of
the collagen fibers and bundles compromising the biomechanical properties of the ECM,
Oxidative stress can also promote
the formation of advanced glycation end products, which in
turn promote formation of new ROS by activation of receptor
for advanced glycation end products (RAGE).
Increased ROSs
reduce chondrocyte responsiveness to the growth factor IGF-1,
resulting in less activation of phosphoinositide (PI) 3-kinase–AKT
signaling and suppressing matrix synthesis.
In a mouse model
of experimental OA, recurring administration of
N
-acetylcysteine
(an antioxidant) prevented chondrocyte senescence and OA development,
highlighting the importance of redox homeostasis in
cartilage health.
Nuclear factor erythroid 2–related factors 2 (Nrf2) is a major
regulator of antioxidant and detoxifying enzymes including superoxide dismutases and heme-oxygenase 1 (HO-1) and is important in maintaining redox homeostasis. Aged chondrocytes show
decreased levels of Nrf2
and HO-1 protein.
Interestingly,
when researchers knocked out
Bach1
, an HO-1 transcriptional
repressor, they were able to reduce the severity of age-related OA
in a SOD2-dependent manner.
Further, antioxidants such as
resveratrol,
licochalcone,
diallyl disulfide,
pterostibene,
wogonin,
prontandim,
and 6-gingerol
exert anti-inflammatory and chondroprotective effects through Nrf2-dependent
pathways. Finally, decreased Nrf2 indirectly increases NFκ
B
activity, causing inflammation
and perpetuating mitochondrial
dysfunction, oxidative stress, and senescence.
Adenosine monophosphate–activated protein kinase (AMPK)
responsiveness is reduced in aged chondrocytes,
compromising its ability to function as an energy sensor and regulate redox
homeostasis. In addition, inflammatory cytokines including
some SASPs hinder the phosphorylation of AMPK,
reducing
its function. Chondrocyte-specific deletion of AMPK resulted in
accelerated age-related OA. Further, systemic administration of
berberine chloride (an AMPK agonist) limited aging-associated
OA development in mice through activation of AMPK-dependent
antioxidant pathways.
Interestingly, AMPK activates sirtuin
1 (SIRT1), another energy-sensing molecule, which is required for
chondrocyte expression of aggrecan and type II collagen alpha 1
chain.
While less studied, AMPK may also play a role in limiting synovial inflammation. When one group stretched rabbit synovial fibroblast in vitro, AMPK was activated and led to reduced
phosphorylation of NFκ
B.
Further, systemic administration
of chitosan oligosaccharide, an AMPK activator, suppressed synovial inflammation in an experimental model of OA.
Further,
a reduction in AMPK activity in macrophages increases aerobic
glycolysis, which is associated with pro-inflammatory activation
and ROS generation.
Taken together, these studies suggest that
AMPK may suppress age-associated synovial inflammation and
play a critical role in maintaining redox homeostasis in cartilage
ECM.
Another consequence of aging is a reduction in autophagy.
Autophagy maintains cell homeostasis by clearing expired and
damaged intra- and extra-cellular components to lysosomes for
degradation and recycling.
Due to the limited rate of cell
turnover in cartilage, autophagy is essential for maintaining normal function.
Importantly, autophagy prevents accumulation
of damaged organelles and misfolded proteins,
preventing cellular stress and apoptosis. Age-related decreases in chondrocyte
autophagy contribute to cell death and reduced anabolic capacity
of the tissue.
Longevity factors including SIRT1, mammalian
target of rapamycin (mTOR), FOXO3, and NFκ
B all regulate
autophagy. Inflammation and stress repress autophagy through
NFκ
B and mTOR, while SIRT1 and FOXO3, another regulator
of cell metabolism, enhance autophagy.
Autophagy is required
for the beneficial effects of calorie restriction on longevity,
highlighting its intersection with cellular metabolism and mitochondrial function. Indeed, autophagy of mitochondria (mitophagy)
is able to prevent accumulation of ROS by removing damaged
mitochondria. Not surprisingly, stimulating mitophagy leads to
improved joint structural damage outcomes in pre-clinical OA
models,
highlighting the importance of this pathway in OA risk
and disease progression.
Aging cells exhibit an altered ability to respond to growth factors, independent of ROS accumulation. In aging chondrocytes,
the relative expression of ALK1 to ALK5 (TGFβ
receptors) is
altered, resulting in decreased matrix synthesis and increased
expression of MMP-13,
further accentuating structural dysfunction of articular cartilage. Interestingly, with age both bone
and bone marrow stromal cells show impaired IGF-1 responsiveness.
IGF-1 is an anabolic factor that stimulates bone
formation. Reduced bone formation can lead to alterations in
mechanical force transmission increasing the risk of OA.
Collectively, these processes can be considered aspects of
inflammaging.
Increases in cellular SASPs and adipokines
drive inflammation, cellular stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction. Reduced autophagy and cellular homeostasis lead to
decreased tissue cellularity and viability, anabolic capacity, and
mechanical quality. These pathogenic mechanisms demonstrate
how aging increases the risk of OA.

### Female Sex

Despite a lower prevalence of OA in women under 50, women are
still two times more likely to develop OA. The dramatic increase
in OA prevalence in post-menopausal women suggests that sex
hormones participate in joint homeostasis.
For instance,
estrogen replacement therapy decreases risk of knee OA and
reduces incidence of total knee arthroplasty.
While it was
traditionally believed that joint tissue did not respond to estrogen,
it is now clear that bone, cartilage, and synovial cells express estrogen receptors.
Estrogens and progesterone both appear to promote anabolic
processes in articular chondrocytes, but systemic concentrations of
estrogens and progesterone decline during menopause. Research
has primarily focused on in vitro experiments to understand the
role of estrogens and progesterone on chondrocytes. When adult
female chondrocytes were treated with 17
β
-estradiol, anabolic
processes including proliferation, production type II collagen and
aggrecan, and increased proteoglycan sulfation were detected.
Further 17
β
-estradiol treatment reduced production of MMP-1,
-3, and -13 in female chondrocytes, stressing the role of estrogen
in cartilage homeostasis.
Interestingly, a dimorphic response to
17
β
-estradiol was seen, where two groups identified these effects
only in female chondrocytes, suggesting that sex defines cellular
responses in chondrocytes.
Another group highlighted the
role of 17
β
-estradiol in preventing telomere shortening where premenopausal concentrations prevented shortening but post-menopausal concentrations did not, suggesting that there is an overlap
between estrogens and cellular senescence.
Progesterone may
play a role in maintenance of cartilage thickness by suppressing
MMP activity.
Indeed, serum progesterone concentration is

positively associated with cartilage volume in women.
Progesterone and estrogen supplementation in a rat model of OA suppressed cartilage turnover and reduced signs of pathologic changes
associated with OA; however, no clinical association between
combined progesterone and estrogen supplementation and the
need for total knee arthroplasty was seen.
Although less
studied, estrogen is able to modulate bone turnover and remodeling by regulating osteoblast and osteoclast activity, with declining
estrogen levels in post-menopausal women increasing the rate of
bone remodeling in the subchondral plate.
In the synovium,
estrogen stimulates fibroblast-like synoviocyte production of
MMPs, while progesterone inhibits production.
In addition,
progesterone appears to inhibit secretion of pro-inflammatory
mediators by DC.
Women have more robust immune responses, and post-menopausal reductions in estrogens are associated with increased production of inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6.
Further,
low serum levels of estradiol and progesterone are associated with
increased knee effusion synovitis in women.
Estrogens appear
to inhibit IL-6 production, and there is likely a synergistic relationship between reduced estrogens and IL-6 production.
Metabolic syndrome is more prevalent in women, especially
post-menopausal women,
and the relationship with increased
systemic inflammation associated with metabolic syndrome may
drive OA risk. For example, a higher proportion of new knee
OA cases were attributed to a body mass index of 27 or greater
in women, further solidifying an interaction between female sex
and MetS.
On the other hand, it is thought that testosterone
is protective against OA development, as the lack of testosterone
in animal models and male patients with testosterone deficiency
is associated with worse OA symptoms. Whether these effects are
direct or indirect requires further investigation.

### Obesity and Metabolic Syndrome

Metabolic syndrome (MetS) is a group of metabolic derangements including central obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia,
and hypertension.
An association between metabolic syndrome
and low grade inflammation exists,
and metabolic syndrome
increases risk of OA.
Knee OA is most strongly associated
with MetS,
in which increased joint load is hypothesized as the
mechanism of action. However, one group also discovered that
obesity increased the risk of OA in non–weight-bearing joints,
suggesting that mechanical load is not the only cause of MetSassociated OA. Interestingly, a direct role for adipose tissue was
recently demonstrated in experimental models of OA. In a mouse
model of lipodystrophy, the absence of adipose tissue protected
animals from both spontaneous and post-traumatic OA, regardless of a lean or high-fat diet. Further, reintroduction of systemic
adipose tissue restored the susceptibility to OA, highlighting a
critical role for adipose tissue and adipose tissue–derived signaling
molecules in OA risk and pathogenesis.
Adipose tissue is the major producer of adipokines produced in
the joint. Adiponectin, leptin, IL-6, and TNF are the best studied mediators in OA. Adipokine secretion changes with increasing
adiposity, with which leptin, IL-6, and TNF increase and adiponectin tends to decrease. Interestingly, adiponectin appears to
slow the progression of hand OA.
Further, when chondrocytes
are treated with adiponectin in vitro, they increase expression of
TIMPs (specifically TIMP2), decrease expression of IL-1
β
, and
are protected against ROS-induced apoptosis via AMPK/mTOR
regulation of autophagy.
Outside of cartilage, adiponectin
is important for bone homeostasis. It promotes osteogenic differentiation and promotes osteoblast commitment but also inhibits
osteoblast activity.
In the synovial membrane, adiponectin has dual roles in macrophage activity. Adiponectin induced
production of IL-10 in alternatively activated macrophages and
induced expression of TNF, IL-6, and IL-12 in pro-inflammatory
macrophages. Adiponectin also repolarizes pro-inflammatory
macrophages toward a proresolving phenotype, suggesting a role
for adiponectin in resolving inflammation.
With these beneficial effects of adiponectin in mind, MetS decreases the systemic
concentration of adiponectin, increasing OA risk.
Models of leptin and leptin receptor deficiency have demonstrated the protective effects of removing leptin.
In  vitro,
leptin induces chondrocyte production of IL-8, MMP-1, MMP3, MMP-9, MMP-13, and expression of
Col10a1,
with leptin
deficiency reducing type X collage levels in growth plates.
Further, leptin may cause chondrocyte progenitor senescence,
perpetuating chondrocyte inflammation and catabolic activity.
Leptin also regulates bone remodeling through
β
-adrenergic signaling, inhibiting osteoblast proliferation and enhancing osteoclast
resorption while simultaneously binding leptin receptors on MSCs
and enhancing proliferation and osteoblast differentiation.
Resistin and chemerin are less studied in OA but represent an
interesting avenue for future study. Resistin, produced by white
adipose tissue, can increase chondrocyte expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and MMP genes while also promoting osteoblast proliferation and osteoclast formation.
Chemerin was originally described as a leukocyte chemoattractant,
but it is now recognized that chemerin receptors are also expressed
by chondrocytes and bone cells.
Interestingly, chemerin can
promote chondrocyte production of MMPs and mediate bone
metabolism through testosterone-mediated osteoblast and osteoclast formation.
Interestingly, the adipokines IL-6 and TNF are both described
as true SASPs, and IL-6 levels are associated with increased risk of
knee OA.
The infrapatellar fat pad produces IL-6 and TNF and
secretes it directly into the synovial fluid contributing to the intraarticular milieu.
As described earlier, IL-6 promotes cartilage
and bone catabolism and suppresses anabolism,
while TNF
promotes catabolism, inhibits anabolism, induces ROS generation, and promotes osteoclast formation.
Altered cellular metabolism is a major consequence of metabolic disease. As discussed above, AMPK is a major regulator of metabolic reprograming via energy sensing and redox
homeostasis. Interestingly, insulin resistance and hyperglycemia
reduce AMPK activity causing inflammation and ROS generation, leading to oxidative stress.
Further, glucose-6-phosphate
dehydrogenase stimulates inflammation and oxidative stress and
is found in higher quantities in obese patients.
Interestingly,
obesity and nutrient excess induces hyperactivation of mTOR
complex 1 (mTORC1), a critical nutrient sensor responsible for
anabolic processes. Increased mTORC1 activity impairs macrophage polarization toward proresolving phenotype.
In addition, myeloid-specific deletion of tuberous sclerosis complex 1
led to hyperactivity of mTORC1, which also increased inflammatory factor production and worsening OA progression.
Chronic nutrient excess overwhelms adipocytes’ capacity to store
fatty acids, resulting in excess circulating low-density-lipoprotein
(LDL) cholesterol (dyslipidemia) and free fatty acids. Mice fed
diets consisting of lower ratios of omega-6 (pro-inflammatory) to
omega-3 (anti-inflammatory) polyunsaturated fatty acids resulted
in lower blood levels of IL-1B, TNF, leptin, and resistin.

At the joint level, lower ratio omega-6 to omega-3 also reduced
joint tissue ossification, synovial inflammation, and macrophage
infiltration in this model.
Changes in lipid metabolism can also affect joint homeostasis
through abnormal accumulation of lipids in chondrocytes.
Interestingly, chondrocyte cholesterol accumulation and highfat diet both aggravate disease in mouse models of OA.
Further when researchers explored activation of nuclear receptors
involved in lipid metabolism, activation of peroxisome-proliferated activated receptor
δ
, a fatty acid binding transcription factor, upregulated expression of
Txnip. Txnip
inhibits thioredoxin
and causes endoplasmic reticulum stress, inflammation, and accumulation of ROS, suggesting a role for free fatty acids in ROS
generation and oxidative stress.
Further, when chondrocytes,
bone cells, or macrophages are cultured with free fatty acids, they
increase production of inflammatory cytokines, chemokines,
and ROS.
Finally, dyslipidemia also plays a role in activating synovial macrophages in OA joints via cholesterol. Binding
of oxidized LDL to the LDL receptor stimulates the release of
growth factors, MMPs, and pro-inflammatory cytokines such as
IL-6 and IL-8, which promote OA changes in joint tissues.
Taken together, metabolic disease and derangements in nutrient
profiles lead to reduced anabolic responses, and increased catabolism, inflammation, ROS generation and oxidative stress, all of
which are deleterious for joint health.

### Abnormal Biomechanics (Alignment, Repetitive Microtrauma, and Joint Shape) and Injury

Mechanical loading is an important and well-known risk factor for OA. Historically, it was thought that mechanical load in
OA resulted simply in “wear and tear”; however, research in the
field has strongly demonstrated that mechanical load activates
mechanosensitive signaling pathways that drive damaging pathophysiology leading to tissue damage. Biomechanical loads that are
within the normal physiologic range (physiologic loading) stimulate restorative and homeostatic processes in the healthy tissue
context. However, biomechanical loading can become abnormal
(pathogenic) when the load magnitudes either exceed the range
of physiologic loading for healthy tissues (e.g., with abnormal
joint alignment, joint trauma, increased adiposity, and occupational risk) or when physiologically normal loads are transmitted
through a joint that has reduced capacity for mechanoprotective
mechanisms via loss of joint stability, altered articular cartilage or
subchondral bone integrity and mechanical properties, and muscular atrophy.
Joint biomechanics are pivotal to OA pathogenesis and can differ across stages of disease. Measures of knee joint loading during
walking are associated with pain
and measures of structural
progression in OA.
Additionally, less dynamic and sustained
knee loading patterns are seen in patients with OA compared with
controls.
Further, alterations in muscle activation patterns during joint movement are associated with OA progression.
Most biomechanics studies have been conducted in large, weightbearing joints such as the knee or the hip; however, developments
in the field are enabling more work to be conducted to understand
the role of mechanics in hand OA.
Cartilage and bone are not the only joint tissues that experience mechanical load, nor are they the only joint tissues that are
mechanoresponsive. Tensile strain loading of synovial cells in vitro
induces changes in gene expression related to processes such as
inflammation, ECM integrity, and oxidative stress,
all of
which can affect joint homeostasis. One group found that the tensile strain loading of fibroblasts resulted in oxidative stress-induced
pro-inflammatory gene expression, which was suppressed by overexpression of HO-1 (a key mediator of redox homeostasis).
This suggests that mechanical stresses may disrupt the homeostatic redox handling capacity of synovial fibroblasts. In line with
this concept, OA pathogenesis may cause similar derangements in
synovial tissue physiology and lower the tolerance to mechanical
loads; this is similar to what has been described in cartilage and
bone. A recent study found that physiologic responses to tensile
loading in late-stage OA synovium included inflammatory signaling mechanisms, alterations in cell metabolism, and redox-related
cell stress, all of which were frequency dependent.
Similarly, in
the temporomandibular joint, overloading shifted the homeostatic
balance of anabolic and catabolic processes.
Together, these
facts suggest that OA may disturb synovial homeostasis, lower
tolerance to mechanical stress, and lead to aberrant responses to
mechanical load such as mechanoinflammatory and oxidative
stress processes, ultimately perpetuating OA pathogenesis.
Mechanoinflammation is defined as the inflammatory response
to mechanical injury and/or stimulation, and it influences OA
pathogenesis.
Processes indicative of mechanoinflammation
have been investigated in cartilage and synovial tissues in the context of OA. For instance, mechanical stresses on articular cartilage evoke inflammatory, redox, and metabolic changes in cellular
physiology.
Inflammatory responses to mechanical injury can
activate TAK1, which drives aggrecan degradation in vitro and
in vivo.
TAK1 also activates NGF, a key mediator of OA
pain.
In patients with late-stage knee OA, synovial perivascular edema, a sign of tissue stress, was associated with aberrant knee loading patterns throughout gait, supporting the link
between mechanical stress and synovial inflammation.
Interestingly, immobilization of the joint after OA induction
prevents the development of OA and also inhibits inflammatory
gene and protease regulation.
Similarly, changes in the composition of synovial fluid biomarkers were observed after joint
unloading with distraction surgery.
In another study, suppression of mechanoinflammation in OA inhibited pain and matrix
turnover and promoted intrinsic repair signaling.
It has been hypothesized that mechanoinflammation may be
part of normal joint repair response; however, appropriate control of inflammatory signaling is required for proper homeostatic
repair outcomes.
Previous work suggests that the type of load
can influence the balance between pro-degenerative and prorepair pathways.
For example, shear stress at the articular surface activates mechanoinflammatory responses, while compressive
load tends to drive chondroprotection,
as long as it is in the
physiologic range.

### Genetics

Over the past decade, comprehensive analysis of genetics has given
fundamental insights in disease pathophysiology and allowed
identification of targets for therapeutic intervention. Genomewide association scans (GWAS), comparing OA patients to controls, has enabled the discovery of single nuclear polymorphisms
(SNPs) associated with disease. These studies have focused on the
most common form of OA, age-associated OA, and used multiple
diagnostic tools such as joint space narrowing, pain or need for
total joint replacement to select cases. Accounting for the effects of
sex and genetic ancestry, researchers can rigorously interrogate disease-associated genetic variants using GWAS analysis. Numerous

large GWAS studies in OA have determined that the vast majority
of OA is caused by permutations of polygenic risks, most often
through the additive effects of numerous SNPs that contribute
to risk of developing pathophysiological changes to one or more
joint tissues.
One review indicates that 124 SNPs are significantly associated with OA risk.
More recently, a meta-analysis
on GWAS data encompassing 826,690 individuals identified 223
independent SNPs, with 84 of them previously unreported.
Like other common diseases, OA-related SNPs are primarily
located in noncoding regions of the genome, further solidifying
OA as a polygenic disease.
As discussed earlier, endogenous regenerative mechanisms
and tolerance to physiologic stresses are critical for the maintenance of homeostasis. SNPs in ECM proteins such as
COL2A1,
COL11A1, COL27A1, CHST11,
and
CHST3
are all associated with OA risk and may compromise the tissues ability to
withstand mechanical stress.
Further, dysregulation of
growth factors including TGFβ
, FGF18, and
GDF5
are all associated with increased OA risk.
As discussed earlier, TGFβ
is a critical anabolic growth factor for chondrocytes. The OA
risk variant rs6060369 is associated with a reduction in
GDF5
expression, which results in changes in bone morphology and
increased risk of spontaneous OA; this increased risk may be
associated with altered biomechanics and increased mechanical
stress.
Further, GWAS has identified risk variants associated
with fibrillin 2,
LTBP1,
and
LTBP3,
which function together
to regulate availability of TGFβ
in cartilage highlighting the
connectedness of OA risk variants. Interestingly, FGF18 promotes an antiarthritic effect in a rat model of post-traumatic
OA through anabolism and maintenance of redox homeostasis,
suggesting a preventative role in oxidative damage.
Several OA risk variants colocalize with genes encoding histone modifying proteins, suggesting an epigenetic component of
OA genetic risk factors. Inhibition of histone deacetylase enzymes
reduced expression of MMPs and IL-1 in OA chondrocytes and
OA mouse models.
DNA methylation, another form of
epigenetic regulation of gene expression, is also associated with
OA risk variants. Specifically, researchers have identified a differentially methylated region upstream of
RUNX2
that has reduced
methylation in the OA risk variant and increased expression of
RUNX2,
a promotor of chondrocyte hypertrophy.
Age remains
the strongest risk factor for OA. However, since polygenic risks
may exert their effects on joint tissues slowly over time, advancing
age may interact with genetic and epigenetic risk factors leading to
the emergence of OA phenotypes in later life.
Individual cases of OA are not usually explained by a single genetic risk allele. However, there are rare, extreme cases of
OA-like phenotypes with early-life onset that can be caused by
major mutations in key risk-associated genes. Such conditions
provide us with an important view of how the affected genes
function and their importance to maintenance of joint homeostasis. For example, camptodactyly arthropathy–coxa vara–pericarditis syndrome (CACP) is caused by an autosomal recessive
mutation in
PRG4
, encoding lubricin.
Most synovial joints
are affected, causing joint dysfunction due to loss of the lubricating, anti-inflammatory, and homeostatic effects of lubricin.
Studies in animal models of OA have shown that intra-articular
injection of lubricin protects against OA-related joint damage.
Acromesomelic chondrodysplasias are a group of rare, heterogeneous skeletal conditions caused by mutations in
GDF5
, including in Hunter–Thompson and Grebe chondrodysplasia.
Observation of the neonatal-onset limb-shortening effects of
these conditions caused by autosomal recessive mutations has
uncovered the pivotal role played by
GDF5
in joint patterning and development. Pseudoachondroplasia is caused by an
autosomal dominant mutation in cartilage oligomeric protein
(COMP), which increases the risk of developing OA due to the
critical role of COMP function in maintaining the biomechanical and physiologic properties of healthy articular cartilage.
Autosomal dominant mutations in important components
of cartilage ECM cause multiple epiphyseal dysplasia, including collagen type IX
α
-1
(COL9A1),
collagen type IX
α
-2
(COL9A2),
collagen type IX
α
-3
(COL9A3), COMP,
and matrilin-3
(MATN3).
Interestingly, an autosomal recessive mutation in the sulfate transporter gene
SLC26A2
can also cause
multiple epiphyseal dysplasia. Together, these mutations contribute to derangements in joint homeostasis due to ECM protein misfolding in the affected gene products. The accumulation
of misfolded ECM proteins causes endoplasmic reticulum (ER)
stress. A similar phenomenon of protein-misfolding/ER stress
occurs in metaphyseal chondrodysplasia type Schmid, which is
caused by a mutation in type X collagen.
Mutations that lead
to the accumulation of calcium crystals also increase the risk
of developing early-life onset of OA. These include autosomal
dominant mutations in the osteoprotegrin gene
TNFRSF11B
(chondrocalcinosis 1) or the
ANKH
gene, controlling inorganic
phosphate production and deposition in the ECM (chondrocalcinosis 2).
The accumulation of CPP crystals in the ECM of
cartilage, synovium, and other joint tissues leads to activation
of the inflammasome (discussed below). Finally, alkaptonuria,
caused by genetic deficiency of homogentisate 1,2-dioxygenase
(HGD),
causes accumulation of homogentisic acid in cartilage
and blue-black discoloration (ochronosis).
This leads to cartilage dysfunction and altered biomechanical properties of the
tissue, increasing chondrocyte stress, and therefore increased risk
of OA development.

### Interaction Between OA Risk Factors

Combinations of risk factors (stressors) such as tissue aging and
metabolic derangements (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, and dyslipidemia) can produce aberrant biologic responses in joint tissues.
Age-related changes in joint tissues include tissue atrophy (e.g.,
thinning of articular cartilage relative to younger ages), oxidative stresses resulting from mitochondrial dysfunction and the
resulting release of ROS,
and accumulation of senescent cells.
Senescent cells are suspected to perpetuate local tissue damage due
to SASP factors, including cytokines, chemokines, and MMPs
associated with cartilage damage.
Current research efforts are
examining the potential efficacy of removing senescent cells using
senolytic treatments in joints with OA.
Age-related increases in
myeloid cells and circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines
are
accompanied by increases in visceral and intra-articular adipose
stores.
Adipocytes are linked to OA through the production
of adipokines including adiponectin, leptin, resistin, and visfatin,
which can increase the production of MMPs and aggrecanases by
chondrocytes
and induce senescence in joint-lining cells.
Despite being independent OA risk factors, age and metabolic
derangements frequently co-exist and are closely overlapping in
pathophysiologic mechanisms. Aging and metabolic derangement likely act synergistically by contributing to aberrant innate
immune activation in synovial joints. Overall, these processes

likely contribute to OA pathology in particular by reducing the
joint’s organ reserves and thus the ability to withstand both normal and abnormal biomechanical stresses that occur throughout
the lifespan.

## Pain Experiences and Nociceptive Signaling in OA

OA-related pain originates due to pathophysiologic changes at the
level of joint tissues and persists, progresses, spreads, and evolves
due to failure of those early pathophysiologic changes to resolve. As
such, OA is a major cause of chronic pain worldwide.
OA pain
pathophysiology is complex, and the experience of pain among
patients is multifactorial and progresses over the course of disease.
The most common type of OA pain is mechanical; patients experience pain when walking on a flat surface, stair climbing, and other
activities of daily living. Although activity-induced pain is the
most common pain experience, many other types of pain experiences have been described in OA such as inflammatory pain,
neuropathic-like pain,
and widespread pain.
There are key pain characteristics that align with OA disease
stage.
In early OA, patients describe pain as sharp and typically brought on by a predictable trigger. At the midpoint of disease, pain becomes more constant with a greater impact on daily
activities and can be associated with other joint symptoms such
as locking, some of which may be unpredictable. In the advanced
stages of OA damage, the pain is described as a dull, achy constant
pain that is accompanied by short, rather intense, and unpredictable intermittent pain.
In advanced OA, the pain significantly
affects daily activities and leads to avoidance behaviors and modification of activities.
In addition to intermittent and constant
type pain, people with OA show signs of both peripheral and
central sensitization, which involves altered pain processing at the
joint and at sites away from the affected joint, respectively. Due to
the heterogeneous nature of OA pain over the course of disease,
it is imperative to increase our understanding of pain in OA to
provide better management for patients.

### Synovial Joint Innervation

OA is a disease of the whole joint
and affects all joint tissues.
Therefore understanding the relevant subsets of neurons and their
innervation is key for the management of OA pain.
Healthy Joint Innervation
In healthy joints, the synovium, capsule, menisci, ligaments, subchondral bone, and periosteum are all innervated by sensory and
sympathetic neurons,
while the articular cartilage remains
aneural and avascular. Normal processing of pain is a protective
mechanism that involves the detection of stimuli that are harmful
or potentially harmful to the tissue. There are specialized sensory
and sympathetic neurons that innervate the joint and contribute
to normal joint function.
Sensory nerves of the joint perform nociception and are either
thinly myelinated, fast conducting A
δ
-fibers or unmyelinated,
slow conducting C-fibers. These innervate synovium, capsule, ligaments, menisci, subchondral bone, and periosteum and are polymodal, which means they can be activated by noxious mechanical,
chemical, and thermal stimuli.
Sympathetic nerve fibers of the joint are involved in vasomotor
control, light touch, and proprioception and are thick myelinated
A
α
and A
β
fibers. These innervate synovial membrane, joint capsule, periarticular bursae, fat pad, ligaments, menisci, and bone and
are typically non-noxious and activated by joint movement.
Osteoarthritis-Induced Changes in Joint Innervation
Over the disease course of OA, joint innervation changes. In
human and rodent OA knee joints, there is nerve growth and
vascular penetration into the subchondral bone, osteochondral
junction, menisci, and osteophytes.
Articular cartilage
is aneural and avascular under normal physiologic conditions.
However, sensory and sympathetic nerves can infiltrate the
articular cartilage in both mild and severe OA via vascular
channels.
Several studies have shown increased PGP9.5+
nerves (pan-neuronal marker),
increased presence of
neuropeptide-immunoreactive nociceptors,
and an increased
presence of Na
1.8+ nociceptors in OA joints,
all of which
contribute to increases in pain.
The synovium has many homeostatic functions for the joint but
is an important source of pain in OA. However, there is mixed evidence surrounding OA-induced changes in synovial innervation.
One group found a decrease in nerve fibers in the synovial lining
that was dependent on the degree of synovial inflammation.
In concordance, another study found there was decreased synovial lining innervation with more advanced cartilage degeneration
in animals,
Whereas other groups have identified increases in
synovial sensory nerve fibers in human
and animal models
of experimental arthritis.
Taken together, synovial innervation in OA is likely heterogeneous and context specific to the joint
environment. Synovial neuroplasticity in OA should be investigated further.
Importantly, the plastic changes in joint innervation induced
by OA may contribute to the recruitment of previously silent
nociceptors. Studies assessing joint afferents at the level of the dorsal root ganglia (DRG), using Ca
imaging, showed increased signaling from a population of newly sensitized neurons in response
to noxious joint stimulation.
However, further characterization of this population of neurons is required.
The purpose of joint nociceptor remodeling in OA is unknown;
however, it has been hypothesized that it may serve as a protective
mechanism for preventing joint usage via enhanced pain signaling
and therefore promoting healing. It has also been hypothesized
that nociceptor remodeling may represent neurogenic repair of
the joint to combat the progressive degeneration of cartilage and
bone.

### Joint–Spine–Brain Connection

Pain signaling begins when joint nociceptors are activated by various stimuli and transmit pain signals via the DRG to the dorsal
horn of the spinal cord, where they synapse with second order
neurons. The signal crosses over and continues up the spinothalamic tract to the brain stem and thalamus, and signals get relayed
to the hypothalamus, periaqueductal gray, amygdala, basal ganglia, limbic system, and somatosensory cortex.
The descending
pathway synapses in the dorsal horn to prevent release of neuropeptides and depolarization of neurons, thus dampening the pain
signal. Loss of inhibition from the descending pathway along the
neuroaxis can amplify central processing of pain.
Although there is much evidence to support that peripheral
sources are the main driver of pain in OA, there is increasing literature suggesting the involvement of brain centers in OA pain.
Remodeling of pain centers in the brain is seen in patients with

OA, and it has been hypothesized that there is a misinterpretation
of peripheral source of pain by the central centers.
Functional imaging of patients with hip OA showed significantly greater brain stem activation compared with healthy
patients in response to the same noxious stimuli. The magnitude
of brain stem activation was positively correlated with neuropathic-like elements reported using the painDETECT score.
The same group found that thalamic atrophy associated with painful hip OA was reversed at 9 months after total hip arthroplasty.
Conversely, in a study of patients with knee OA, increased neural
activity and connectivity between the cingulate cortex and rostral
ventromedial medulla pre-operatively was associated with moderate to severe long-term pain after total knee arthroplasty.
One
study found that, relative to controls, functional connectivity
between the secondary somatosensory cortex and left posterior
insula was increased, while the functional connectivity between
the bilateral posterior insula and motor cortices was decreased in
patients with hip OA.
Further, in response to painful physical
activity, functional connectivity increased between the thalamus,
periaqueductal gray matter, and brain stem, indicating that some
connections are modulated by painful activity in OA.
Changes
in functional connectivity within the brain have also been shown
in OA of the small joints. For instance, in patients with hand OA,
functional MRI analysis showed increased activity in the thalamus, cingulate, frontal, and somatosensory cortex compared with
controls.
Interestingly, exercise interventions such as tai chi
and stationary cycling reduce pain and functional connectivity in
periaqueductal gray matter and ventral tegmental area for patients
with knee OA over 12 weeks.
From the current literature, it appears there are distinct connectivity signatures and brain region activation that are dependent
on the joints involved, stage of disease, and intervention applied
(i.e., exercise versus surgery). This area of research in the OA field
remains largely understudied, with small sample sizes, compared
with other areas and requires further research to gain a complete
understanding of brain circuitry in OA pain.

### Nociceptive Mechanisms in Osteoarthritis

Types of Osteoarthritis Pain
Nociceptive, inflammatory, and neuropathic pain have all been
described in OA, and each have distinct neurobiological mechanisms. Nociceptive pain is caused by tissue damage or stimuli that
is potentially damaging to the tissue. This type of pain is what
is classically associated with OA due to degenerative changes in
the joint tissues. Aberrant joint biomechanics due to malalignment, osteophyte formation, subchondral bone sclerosis, muscle
weakness or atrophy, or traumatic joint injury will open mechanogated ion channels on nociceptive nerves and generate nociceptive
pain.
Inflammatory pain arises from several sources within the OA
joint. As structures within the joint degenerate, sensory nerves in
the subchondral bone, menisci, and newly innervated articular cartilage become exposed to the intra-articular environment, which
is concentrated with inflammatory mediators.
In addition, sensory nerves in the synovium are also exposed to the intra-articular
environment, and pathologic changes within the synovium such
as increased immune cell infiltration and increased production of
a localized source of proalgesic molecules contribute to inflammatory pain signaling.
Neuropathic pain is present in approximately 23% of people
with hip and knee OA, is caused by damage to the nervous system
itself, and includes nerves at the level of the joint and in the dorsal root ganglia, spinal cord, and brain.
The exact mediators of
nerve damage in OA have not been fully elucidated to date; however, molecules such as lysophosphatidic acid,
known to have
demyelinating effects on peripheral nerves, have been found to be
upregulated in OA synovial fluid. It stands to reason that other
molecules concentrated within the intra-articular environment in
OA could also contribute to demyelination of joint afferents; however, this remains to be explored.
Pain Sensitization in Osteoarthritis
Increased and/or constant nociceptive mediator input from
mechanical or chemical stimulation can alter the properties of
nociceptive neurons at the level of the joint by activating intracellular signal transduction and increasing the production and
transport of transducer channels and voltage-gated ion channels.
The threshold for activation becomes reduced, and membrane excitability increases, making the nerves more likely to fire
in response to normal and non-noxious stimuli,
which is
called peripheral sensitization.
Continuous nociceptor input locally at the joint can lead to
increased excitability of second order neurons in the dorsal horn
of the spinal cord. This is called central sensitization, a form of
synaptic plasticity, and typically results in augmentation of the
nociceptive signal and intensification of pain sensations.
When there is long-term potentiation of nociceptive input to
the spinal cord, lower-threshold afferents convert to pain drivers, and sensory input from outside the injury site is recruited.
The expanded area of signaling allows second order neurons to
receive input from tissues outside the affected joint and therefore
leads to the phenomenon of referred pain.
These synaptic
changes have been observed in patients with established OA
and
are linked to high levels of pain and disability,
diffuse pain
around the joint,
increased pro-inflammatory cytokines,
and
poor outcomes after joint replacement surgery.
Recently the International Association for the Study of Pain
(IASP) introduced the term “nociplastic pain,” which is defined
as “pain that arises from altered nociception despite no clear evidence of actual or threatened tissue damage causing activation
of peripheral nociceptors or evidence for disease or lesion of the
somatosensory system causing the pain.”
Although sensitization is not included in the definition of nociplastic pain, it is a
major underlying mechanism.
Clinical criteria for nociplastic
pain in patients with chronic musculoskeletal conditions has been
released by the IASP
and should be considered when assessing pain phenotypes of patients with OA.

### Neuroimmune Interactions in OA Pain

Interactions between the immune system and the nervous system
play an important role in OA pain and have been characterized
as bidirectional, meaning each can act on the other.
Macrophages are the most abundant immune cell in the OA joint
and can release inflammatory mediators such as TNF, IL-1, and
NGF or other neurotrophins that signal via TrkA and increase
nociceptor excitability.
Macrophages also produce reactive
oxygen species that can activate nociceptors and can contribute to pain signaling in OA.
Several studies have found that
increased synovial fluid levels of macrophage markers
and
macrophage-associated chemokines
are associated with worse
pain in patients with OA. In synovial tissue, NGF expression
and an increase in activated macrophages
were associated with

pain and other clinical symptoms. However, many studies to date
use pan-macrophage makers for identification, which fails to convey the complex spectrum of macrophage activation and function
within the OA joint.
Locally within the damaged joint, nociceptors can produce and
respond to pro-inflammatory chemokines that lead to production
of CCL2, a strong chemotactic signal for macrophages and monocytes.
Neuropeptides released by nociceptors modulate the
macrophage phenotype.
Substance P released by activated
nociceptors can result in increased NFκ
B–dependent production of pro-inflammatory cytokines by macrophages.
Interestingly, in a pro-inflammatory environment, neuropeptides such as
CGRP can act on TLR4-stimulated macrophages to increase production of IL-10,
which is an important molecule for immune
regulatory mechanisms within the joint.
In addition to acting locally within the joint, macrophages in
the central nervous system, called microglia, are known to play
an important role in chronic OA pain. Activation of microglia is
associated with pain behaviors in both surgical
and chemical
models of OA. Further, pain behavior was attenuated
when microglia activation was inhibited in experimental OA,
suggesting that spinal mechanisms are an important aspect of the
pain experience in OA. The changes in microglia were also identified in the contralateral dorsal horn,
indicating that these
effector cells may be important in complex pain states including
widespread pain and referred pain.
Immune cells such as T cells and mast cells and nonimmune
cells such as fibroblasts are also contributors to OA pain experiences.
One study found that increased Th1 and Th2 cell infiltration in the
synovial membrane was associated with higher pain scores on the
numeric pain rating scale (NRS).
Levels of circulating peripheral
T helper and Th17 cells correlate with worse Western Ontario and
McMaster Universities Arthritis Index (WOMAC) pain scores.
In a rodent model of experimental OA, mast cells signaling via TrkA
were involved in pain behavior outcomes and when exposed to NGF
upregulated other pro-inflammatory mediators such as COX2 and
PGD2.
Synovial fibroblasts within painful joint regions from
patients with early- and late-stage OA were found to have different
gene expression profiles that promoted signaling related to fibrosis,
inflammation, and nerve growth.
As pain is strongly associated
with the degree of inflammation (synovitis) in OA, understanding
the pathogenesis of pain requires further elucidation of the immune
and nonimmune signaling networks within the OA joint.
Cross-Talk Between Inflammation and Sensory Nerve
Fibers in Osteoarthritis
DAMPs are molecules resulting from the degradation of joint tissues that can activate receptors expressed by nociceptors. Breakdown
products from extra-cellular matrix proteins, leaking of plasma proteins from joint vasculature, and alarmins released from stressed or
apoptotic cells are all sources of DAMPs in the OA joint.
DAMPs including TLR4 ligand,
α
-macroglobulin, S100A8,
and S100A9
and for a 32-amino-acid aggrecan fragment
have been detected in OA joints and can excite nociceptors via
TLR signaling. Activation of TLRs by 32-mer increased production of CCL2 by cultured DRG neurons,
which is a proalgesic molecule and chemokine for immune cell recruitment.
The 32-mer aggrecan can activate nociceptors through TLR2
and produce hyperalgesia. Interestingly, TLR2 and TLR4 null
mice developed OA-related joint degeneration and were protected
from knee hyperalgesia after destabilization of the medial meniscus (DMM) surgery
but were not protected from developing
mechanical allodynia, suggesting that DAMPs may contribute to
OA pain via mechanisms other than TLR signaling. Thus DAMPs
are an important source of nociceptive activation within the
degenerating OA joint and highlight cross-talk between nociceptive and non-nociceptive tissues in the joint.

### Nerve Growth Factor and Pain in Osteoarthritis

NGF was identified as a key molecule that regulates the growth
and survival of sensory and sympathetic neurons
and plays
a major role in producing pain.
NGF has high affinity for the
receptor TrkA on a subset of nociceptors, and this activation
increases expression and activation of ion channels and receptors, voltage-gated sodium and calcium channels, mechanosensitive channels, and acid-sensing ion channels. NGF signaling
also modifies nociceptor ion channels leading to sensitization.
In the OA joint, synovial fluid levels of NGF are significantly elevated compared with non-OA joints
and are correlated with poorer
knee joint function in OA patients.
In human joints with advanced
OA, NGF expression in synovial fibroblasts and macrophages was
associated with pain.
Additionally, NGF has increased expression
in osteochondral channels in subchondral bone from patents undergoing total knee arthroplasty.
In rodent models in which OA is
surgically induced, NGF gene expression is upregulated in the joint
at both early (mild OA) and later (chronic OA) time points in the
model.
Given the important role NGF plays in pain signaling
and its identification within the OA joint, NGF has been explored
as a potential target in the management of OA pain, and anti-NGF
biologic therapies have shown efficacy in clinical trials. However, joint
safety concerns have prevented the approval of anti-NGF therapies
to date. Further research is needed to understand the role of NGF in
joint homeostasis beyond its effects on pain.

## Joint Tissue Damage

As outlined above, numerous mechanisms are activated in response
to mechanical stimuli and other day-to-day physiologic stressors
experienced by all joint tissues. Endogenous repair and homeostatic
mechanisms aim to maintain tissue integrity, prevent the development of joint disease symptoms, and ultimately prevent the progressive loss and damage of joint tissues from leading to joint organ
failure. Although OA is a single disease, it presents with remarkable phenotypic heterogeneity due to numerous permutations and
interactions between extrinsic and intrinsic OA risk factors, which
vary widely among patients. OA risk factors inhibit and reduce the
effectiveness of endogenous repair and homeostatic mechanisms to
protect the joint from OA pathogenesis. Further, when biomechanical and physiologic stressors experienced by joint tissues exceed the
normal physiologic range (greater or lesser), OA pathogenic mechanisms are induced and overwhelm the joint’s endogenous repair and
homeostatic mechanisms, leading to the clinical manifestation of
OA symptoms, functional loss, and structural damage.

### Cartilage Damage

Cartilage health and damage is central to OA. The ECM that is
synthesized by chondrocytes is made up of type II collagen fibers,
which are formed during development and largely remain stable
through life, along with a rich intercellular composition of GAGs,
hyaluronan, aggrecan, and other proteoglycans. Distinction
should be made between the interterritorial matrix that extends
between chondrocytes and the territorial matrix immediately

surrounding chondrocytes (the pericellular matrix [PCM]). The
chondrocyte interacts extensively with the territorial matrix and
leverages this microenvironment to receive mechanical and other
signals that are transmitted through the ECM. Early responses
to mechanical injury in articular cartilage include changes to the
PCM, which leads to weakening of the PCM.
Breakdown and
loss of key proteoglycans including decorin, perlecan, and heparan
sulfate proteoglycan in the PCM result in increased exposure of
chondrocytes to compressive and shear forces being experienced
within their lacunae,
thereby triggering exaggerated responses
of the chondrocytes to increased biomechanical stresses.
These changes lead to the release and activation of matrix-bound
factors such as FGF2 in the PCM, as discussed above.
Damage to articular cartilage manifests initially as superficial
zone erosion and loss of the superficial zone cells. This is particularly critical as vital regenerative cell populations (CPCs) reside
in the superficial zone and upper cartilage layers. As the surface
becomes more fibrillated, proteoglycan loss and altered proteoglycan distributions develop. Initially, proteoglycan loss occurs in the
interterritorial matrix accompanied by increases in GAG production in the territorial matrix due to increased chondrocyte synthesis of proteoglycans as they attempt to repair the damaged ECM.
Over time, proteoglycan losses lead to disruption of the interterritorial matrix as well. As cartilage damage progresses, deeper fissures form. Additional signs include proliferation of chondrocytes
within lacunae, enlargement of the chondrocytes to take on more
of a hypertrophic chondrocyte phenotype and eventually hypocellularity of large regions of the cartilage ECM (
Fig. 100.3
). In its
worst state, superficial clefts extend down through the mid zone
and into the calcified zone of cartilage, progressed to total cartilage
erosion and exposure of the underlying subchondral bone plate
(denudation).

Recapitulation of Endochondral Ossification-like
Processes
OA pathogenesis is not identical to endochondral ossification, but
the two processes have overlapping features. Many molecules that
are silenced in mature adult chondrocytes such as NOTCH-1,
Jagged-1, SYND4, VEGF, and MMP-13 become reactivated in
chondrocytes during OA development and progression.
Supporting the pathogenic role of each of these molecules, targeted
deletion of each gene individually has led to protection against
surgical induction of OA in most models. For example, cartilagespecific deletion of a NOTCH signaling factor (Jk recombination
signal-binding protein) leads to impaired endochondral ossification at the latest stages and completely inhibits the development
of OA in a mouse model.
Similarly, genetic loss of
SDC4

models.

to the development and progression of OA cartilage damage.
Inflammatory Mechanisms Activated by Chondrocytes
One of the key differences between endochondral ossification
during development and OA-specific chondrocyte phenotypic
changes is the induction of inflammatory mechanisms in articular chondrocytes. Although the dichotomization of inflammatory
versus noninflammatory arthritis has persisted in the clinical literature, it is well-established that inflammation is a key mediator of pathogenesis and disease progression in OA. OA does not
demonstrate loss of immune tolerance and other facets of adaptive/autoimmune pathophysiology that are seen in rheumatoid
arthritis and other forms of “inflammatory arthritis,” but innate
immune-driven inflammatory mechanisms have long been overlooked as a key aspect of OA, including for cartilage damage.
Secreted inflammatory factors contribute to all aspects of OA,
including altered chondrocyte metabolism, amplification of catabolic cascades, shifts of chondrocytes toward hypertrophy, bone
remodeling, and nociceptive signals released by chondrocytes to
act on sensory nociceptors in the synovium and other tissues.
Major cytokines including IL-1 and IL-6 promote cartilage damage. Chondrocytes become sensitized to inflammatory cytokine
signaling when exposed to chondrocyte phenotype destabilizing
factors such as SDC4.
This results in exaggerated gene expression and other physiologic responses when OA-transformed chondrocytes are exposed to relatively small levels of inflammatory
cytokines. Other aspects of innate immunity are also implicated
in OA. Chondrocyte activation leads to triggering of the alternative pathway of complement activation and terminal complex
formation. Blockade or genetic deletion of membrane attack complex components including C5a is protective against the development and progression of OA in mouse models of experimental
knee OA.
Similarly, TLR2 and TLR4 expression is increased in
OA chondrocytes.
Numerous TLR ligands are endogenously
produced and increased in the OA joint environment, including
low-molecular-weight HA, fibronectin, tenascin-C, and biglycan. These mediators can activate inflammatory mechanisms by
binding to TLR2/4 and other DAMP receptors expressed on the
surface of chondrocytes.
These mechanisms lead to further
shifts in chondrocyte phenotype, amplification of local inflammation by the release of chemokines from chondrocytes, and perpetuation of local matrix damage in the PCM and territorial matrix
and microenvironment due to the induction of aggrecanases,
MMPs, and other enzymatic factors in chondrocytes. This creates
a vicious cycle of damage, begetting further cartilage damage and
loss of chondrocyte phenotypic stability and viability.

further compromise joint health and function. Further, synovial

associated with increased synovial inflammation.

### Crystal Formation and Dystrophic Tissue Calcification (Basic Calcium Phosphate, Calcium Pyrophosphate Dihydrate)

Dystrophic and pathologic calcification of cartilage and other joint
tissues including synovium is a hallmark feature of OA. The most
common crystal present in OA tissues is basic calcium phosphate
(BCP), although calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate (CPP) crystals can also be present either instead of or co-existing with BCP
(see
Chapter 98
). BCP crystals include hydroxyapatite, octacalcium phosphate, tricalcium phosphate, and carbonate apatite.
Although the crystals can be detected in synovial fluid, they are
commonly present in cartilage presenting as chondrocalcinosis on
radiographs. Nearly all patients have BCP crystals in knee cartilage and approximately one in five have CPP crystals at the time
of arthroplasty.
Therefore cartilage calcification is common
and strongly associated with aging although the degree of cartilage
damage, and worse OA symptoms are also related to the presence
of BCP and CPP crystals.
Overall, BCP is more strongly associated than CPP with OA. Interestingly, vitamin K is an important regulator of bone and soft tissue mineralization. Vitamin K
deficiency, including with the use of vitamin K antagonists such
as warfarin, is associated with radiographic joint damage in OA.
It is thought that hypertrophic chondrocytes mediate much
of the crystal nucleation and crystal formation for both BCP
and CPP crystals. However, mitophagy and apoptosis are most
strongly associated with BCP crystal formation. As described
above in chondrocytes, high levels of RUNX2 and type X collagen
underpin the hypertrophic chondrocyte phenotype and may also
push chondrocyte transdifferentiation into osteoblast-like phenotypes capable of synthesizing and secreting different forms of
crystals into cartilage ECM and the synovial joint environment.
Therefore factors that promote chondrocyte hypertrophy-like
states in the pathologic context of OA cartilage also engage the
cellular machinery that is used by chondrocytes to calcify ECM
in development and in the calcified zone of healthy articular cartilage.
In chondrocytes and other cell types including synovial
cells, mitochondrial autophagy is an important crystal nucleation
mechanism. Inflammation, hypoxia, and other cells stress factors
lead to calcium and inorganic phosphate sequestration in mitochondria, which causes the formation of amorphous calcium
phosphate precursors (ACP).
As autophagy progresses through
sequential steps toward autophagosome formation, digested components of mitochondria containing ACP must be expelled by the
cells, which leads to aggregation in the ECM and further calcification of the tissues.
Just as important as the mechanisms of formation, the effects
of crystal precipitation and signaling in the ECM and in synovial
joint environment contribute to the elaboration and propagation
of innate immune dysregulation. The major effect of BCP and
CPP crystal signaling through DAMP receptors is the stimulation of transcription, synthesis, and release of IL-6. Importantly,
treatment of human cartilage explants with the tocilizumab to
block IL-6 signaling–protected cartilage against BCP crystal–
induced cartilage damage.
Similarly, in a mouse model of OA,
blocking IL-6 signaling or the downstream mediator STAT3

was protective against joint damage induced by BCP crystals.
BCP crystals also activate chondrocyte hypertrophy by inducing
type X collagen expression through canonical Wnt signaling and
inhibition of SOX9 expression in cartilage.
Key matrix–
degrading enzymes including MMP-3, MMP-13, ADAMTS-4,
and ADAMTS-5 are also stimulated downstream of BCP crystal signaling. Together, physiologic stresses in the joint environment contribute to the generation of BCP and CPP crystals, and
reciprocally, the downstream effects of crystal-mediated innate
immunity contributes to cartilage damage and progression of joint
destruction pathways.
Adding further to crystalline complexity in OA, it should also
be noted that a growing body of literature is demonstrating associations between worse patient and joint structural outcomes and
the presence of hyperuricemia, gout, or intra-articular monosodium urate crystals; uric acid levels in the synovial fluid of OA
patients without gout is associated with OA severity.
The mechanism of action is not clear, but it is likely that increased innate
immunity is one of the key components as uric acid is itself a
danger signal (i.e., DAMP).

## Conclusion

The synovial joint is an organ system that is optimized for ambulation while responding restoratively to the normal biomechanical and physiologic stresses experienced by joints throughout the
lifespan. However, when physiologic and biomechanical stresses
exceed the intrinsic repair properties of joint tissues, cellular stress
mechanisms are triggered. If sustained, pathogenic cell stress processes lead to suppression of endogenous repair mechanisms and
the genesis of damaging and pro-inflammatory responses. In the
clinical context, multiple stresses (OA risk factors) often co-exist
in the same individual and coalesce to overwhelm joint homeostatic mechanisms and produce OA pathophysiology. These events
underlie the clinical manifestations of joint pain experiences, nonresolving inflammation, and the progression of structural joint
damage in patients with OA, which can ultimately lead to joint
organ failure if left unaddressed.
Our understanding of the biologic mechanisms leading to
the loss of joint regenerative capacity combined with cell stress is
expanding rapidly. Ongoing research efforts will need to inform
how these mechanisms are integrated and engage in cross-talk
within and between joint tissues to identify treatment strategies to
sustain joint health. Excitingly, tools are beginning to emerge that
either restore necessary anabolic aspects of joint physiology and/or
suppress pathogenic mechanisms of nonresolving innate immune
inflammation. As clinicians, we should be targeting all modifiable sources of abnormal joint stresses while promoting the use of
strategies aimed at improving the whole joint’s and the individual
tissues’ ability to withstand normal stresses to reduce suffering,
improve patient outcomes, and preserve the lifespan of the joint
organ system.
