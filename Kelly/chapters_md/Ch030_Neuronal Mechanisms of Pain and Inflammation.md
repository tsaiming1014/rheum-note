DAVID ANDREW WALSH AND ANNE-MARIE MALFAIT

> Peripheral mechanisms are major drivers of pain in rheumatic

> diseases.

## Introduction

Pain is often the most troublesome problem of rheumatic diseases. Up to one-third of the global population have some form
of chronic musculoskeletal symptoms, with an economic impact
greater than any other health condition.
Pain is “an unpleasant
sensory and emotional experience associated with, or resembling
that associated with, actual or potential tissue damage.”
Acute
pain has a protective function, for example by triggering a withdrawal reflex on contact with a hot plate. Loss of nociception, the
ability to detect injury, can lead to augmented tissue damage, for
example in Charcot’s arthropathy. Chronic pain, however, is often
dissociated from any useful protective function.
Inflammation or damage to joints, muscles, ligaments, or
tendons can cause nociceptive pain in rheumatic diseases. Sometimes disease or damage to nerves can cause neuropathic pain, for
example in carpal tunnel syndrome or following surgical trauma.
Musculoskeletal pain may occur even in the absence of overt tissue
damage or inflammation, and pain that is inadequately explained
by tissue pathology is referred to as nociplastic pain.
Painful
rheumatic diseases such as fibromyalgia, in which tissue damage
might not be evident at all, are now classified as
primary pain conditions
in the International Classification of Diseases (ICD) 11.
Each of these diverse manifestations is underpinned by multiple
pain mechanisms, both in peripheral tissues such as the joint and
within the peripheral and central nervous systems. Mechanisms
within the musculoskeletal system that cause, exacerbate, or prolong pain and how interrupting these peripheral pain mechanisms
may relieve these symptoms in rheumatic diseases will be reviewed
in this chapter.
Almost all pain in rheumatic diseases depends on activation
of peripheral nerves, even where the stimuli that activate those
peripheral nerves might not necessarily be damaging. People with
rheumatoid arthritis (RA), osteoarthritis (OA), or fibromyalgia
report pain on movement or weight bearing, which may decrease
again once mechanical stimulation ceases. Rest pain, or otherwise
pain without mechanical stimulation, might be associated with
tonic activity within primary nociceptors.
Pain is commonly attributed by both patients and clinicians
to inflammation or damage within the peripheral musculoskeletal
system. Pain is a cardinal sign of acute inflammation. Prostaglandins and bradykinins are released upon tissue injury. Tissue pH
can result from the increased metabolic demand of inflammation.
Each chemically activates sensory nerves through interaction with
specific cell surface receptors or activation of ion channels such as
transient receptor potential cation channel subfamily V member
1 (TRPV1). Alongside these immediate effects of inflammation,
peripheral nerves are sensitized, and therefore what would normally be a subthreshold mechanical, thermal, or chemical stimulus can trigger potential generation and nociceptive transmission.
These acute inflammatory mechanisms continue to operate in
chronic inflammatory diseases and recur during acute inflammatory flares. Chronic inflammation also changes tissue structure
and triggers diverse immune responses and growth factor expression that might specifically contribute to chronic pain.
Addressing peripheral nociception can be very effective at
reducing pain. Where inflammation drives pain, for example
during inflammatory flares of RA, systemic or locally injected
glucocorticosteroids can rapidly improve symptoms, and effective disease modifying or immunomodulatory treatment can produce sustained improvements in pain. Where articular cartilage is
severely damaged, joint replacement surgery can have life-changing benefits. However, removal of peripheral nociceptive drive
does not always abolish pain, particularly when it has been prolonged and severe. Up to 20% of people may continue to suffer
knee pain after technically successful knee replacement surgery in

Nociplastic pain often has qualities that overlap with neuropathic pain (e.g., burning, shooting), and is associated with other

symptoms of CNS dysfunction, including anxiety, depressed

The complex mechanisms that contribute to nociplastic
pain can involve both peripheral and central nervous systems.
They might include persistent sensitization of primary nociceptive
neurons in the joint or dorsal root ganglia (DRG). Central sensitization denotes increased activity in nociceptive pathways within
the CNS in response to a standard primary afferent input. This
might be due to facilitation or deficient descending inhibition of
nociceptive signalling.
Nociplastic mechanisms might themselves
involve inflammatory mediators. Macrophages and satellite cells
within DRG and glial cells within the CNS may produce inflammatory mediators that cause peripheral or central sensitization.

Pain is both a sensory and an emotional experience and needs
to be understood within its biopsychosocial context. Aspects of
brain function, including anxiety, depression, catastrophizing,
cognitive impact, sleep disturbance, and fatigue, each contribute
to the experience of pain and are reciprocally affected by pain.
The
meaning that is attributed to pain contributes to how it is experienced. For example, a patient might believe their biologic treatment must have stopped working if their joints now hurt. They
might believe that they are damaging their joint if it hurts when
they walk. These cognitions can drive behaviors such as seeking
treatment escalation or decreasing physical activity. Understanding pain mechanisms by people with rheumatic diseases is important to help them to navigate their symptoms and adopt optimal
management.
Pain mechanisms need to be considered within contemporary
contexts of nociceptive, neuropathic, and nociplastic pain. Recognizing the specific mechanisms that contribute to a patient’s pain
can help indicate what treatments are most likely to help them
and also explain why not all treatments are effective for all people.
Where there is inflammation, molecular nociceptive mechanisms
might overlap with, but not necessarily replicate, those mechanisms that lead to joint damage. Where there is joint damage, secondary inflammatory mechanisms might contribute to peripheral
sensitization and pain. Interventions aim to reduce current symptoms and prevent future end organ damage. Pain is paramount
among the symptoms of rheumatic diseases.

patterns of ion channels, calcium-binding proteins, peptides, and
receptors.

stimuli.

### The Nociceptive Pathway

Understanding the mechanisms underlying pain associated with
rheumatic diseases requires a precise description of the properties of the neuronal pathways that mediate nociceptive signaling
from musculoskeletal tissues. Activation of peripheral nociceptor
terminals occurs in the tissues they innervate and results in depolarization and firing of action potentials that invade the DRG,
where the cell bodies of afferent neurons are located. The signal is
transmitted through centrally directed axons to the first synapse
in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord (
Fig. 30.1
).
Propagation
of these electric signals requires the activation of specific types of
voltage-gated sodium channels, Na
1.7, Na
1.8, and Na
1.9.
Loss-of-function mutations in genes encoding these channels
result in congenital insensitivity to pain, whereas gain-of-function
mutations may cause erythromelalgia, a rare syndrome characterized by episodes of severe, burning pain, redness, and increased
skin temperature.
Neurotransmitters such as glutamate, released from primary
nociceptor terminals in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, activate
second-order neurons, which in turn transmit to higher centers
in the thalamus. Supraspinal synaptic transmission activates primary sensory cortex, somatotopically associated with the localization of nociceptive stimuli. Other brain regions are also activated,
such as the anterior cingulate cortex, involved in the emotional
components of pain. Neuromodulators, such as substance P and
brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), released alongside
glutamate from central terminals of primary nociceptive neurons,
modulate the excitability of second-order nociceptive neurons.

### Peripheral Sensitization

Hyperalgesia and Allodynia Are Behavioral
Manifestations of Sensitization of the Nociceptive
Pathway
A fundamental process that underlies clinical pain is that nociceptors become hyperexcitable during inflammation and injury of the
tissues they innervate. Under normal conditions, nociceptors are
excited by noxious stimuli. In pathologic conditions, mediators
present in an inflammatory milieu lower the activation threshold. These direct effects can lead to activation (firing of action
potentials) of an otherwise resting nociceptor, and sensitization
(lowering the threshold at which a nociceptor will respond to a
stimulus). The process of sensitization of pain pathways can be
clinically inferred indirectly from augmented pain responses, such
as allodynia and hyperalgesia.
Allodynia
means that innocuous
stimuli such as light touches are perceived as painful, and
hyperalgesia
means that a painful stimulus elicits an exaggerated response.
Sensitization also occurs in the CNS, but the importance of
peripheral sensitization in arthritis is highlighted by the analgesic
effects of interventions such as neutralizing antibodies to NGF,
whose actions are restricted to outside the blood–brain barrier.
Hyperalgesia and allodynia can be detected in people by quantitative sensory testing (QST) and have been described in many rheumatic diseases, including OA,
RA, and spondyloarthritis (SpA).
Patients might display sensitization to mechanical stimuli and show
hyperalgesia, manifesting as lowered pain pressure thresholds when
a force is applied to the joint, as well as allodynia distant from the
joint. Sensitization measured by QST might result from changes in
either or both peripheral and central nervous systems. Sensitization
to mechanical stimuli is also a consistent feature of animal models

of OA and RA, and sensitization to thermal cutaneous stimuli is
also described. Distinguishing peripheral from central sensitization
is challenging in humans and in animal models is best addressed by
electrophysiological recordings.

### Neuroimmune Interactions and Neuroinflammation as Drivers of Pain

Peripheral Musculoskeletal Tissues
Inflammation drives response to tissue injury, and the association
of pain with injury and structural damage is in part explained by
inflammation. Inflamed synovium may be an important source of

joint pain. Synovitis detected by imaging is associated with knee
pain in OA,
but even in RA, such associations might only be
weak.
In the OA joint, synovitis is characterized by influx of
leukocytes, primarily macrophages and T-lymphocytes, but also
mast cells, B-lymphocytes, and plasma cells. Synovial macrophage
infiltration is associated with OA pain severity.
Histologic evidence of inflammation is also present in subchondral bone, both
in inflammatory arthritides and in OA.
Subchondral inflammatory cell infiltrates are associated with high signal on T2-weighted
or STIR magnetic resonance images and described as BMLs.
BMLs are associated with pain.
The innate and adaptive immune systems contribute to peripheral sensitization, because nociceptors can interact with resident or
infiltrating immune cells. Neuroimmune cross-talk has emerged
as a key process in initiating and modulating pain processing. In
the arthritic joint, immune cells can release mediators that either
directly activate or sensitize nociceptors or act on other resident
cells (e.g., fibroblasts, chondrocytes) to further modulate nociceptor activity. Depending on their polarization state, macrophages
are a source of a variety of mediators that can directly act on nociceptors. In experimental models, macrophage depletion can have
analgesic effects.
Synovial inflammation, with hyperplasia and
immune cell infiltration, is a key characteristic of RA that drives
autoimmunity and results in the production of factors that promote pain through acting directly on free nerve endings in the
joint. Specific immune responses may also contribute to OA pain,
and positive correlations between Th1/Th2 cells and OA-related
knee pain have been reported.
Mast cells might contribute by
producing NGF,
prostaglandin D
, or other activators of primary nociceptors in response to NGF.
In addition, autoantibodies can form immune complexes with type II collagen in arthritic
mice, and these complexes act on Fc
γ
-receptor I expressed by neurons, resulting in pain.
Thus a picture has emerged whereby cross-talk between joint
immune cells, resident cells (fibroblasts, chondrocytes), and nociceptor terminals that innervate the arthritic joint results in sensitization and amplified pain.
The precise molecular pathways
by which inflammation causes pain and causes structural damage
might diverge. Analgesic effects in RA may differ between different biologic or targeted immunomodulatory agents, despite
apparently similar benefits in reducing inflammation or structural
progression.
Radiographic progression continues in spondylitis
despite excellent analgesia from biologic agents.
Further research
is required to identify targets that optimally reduce both pain and
tissue injury.
Recent -omics approaches and sophisticated bioinformatics
analyses and techniques to interrogate cell–cell communication,
such as scRNAseq and spatial transcriptomic approaches, have
led to detailed mapping of the cellular environment in synovial
tissues of different forms of arthritis. A direct link between the
synovial microenvironment and pain has not yet been reported,
but these approaches will likely enable identification of key elements in the communication process between neurons and the
joint environment.
Neuroimmune Interactions Within the Nervous System
The contribution of inflammatory processes in the nervous system, including macrophage infiltration of DRGs and activation of dorsal horn microglial cells (the resident macrophages of
the CNS) is increasingly recognized as an important regulator
of pain in chronic diseases. In experimental models of OA and
RA, knee-innervating DRGs become infiltrated by macrophages,
while microglia and astrocytes in the dorsal horn of the spinal
cord become activated.
Neuroimmune processes may facilitate
central sensitization through increased neuronal excitability and
synaptic efficacy.
TLRs play a critical role at the neuroimmune
interface, and their activation has been implicated in maintenance
of pain, including in arthritis.
Neuroimmune interactions may
also occur in the brain, where it has been proposed that microglia
may play a role in fibromyalgia pain.
Neuroinflammation in arthritis is an emerging field, but it is
clear that joint inflammation and joint damage can have a profound impact on the nervous system, and pain is a consequence of
this. It can be expected that unraveling the precise nature of these
interactions in the joint and at different levels of the neuraxis may
result in targeted therapies that may well be disease-specific.

## Pain-Resolving Pathways in the Periphery

Opioids are endogenous analgesics with predominant actions
within the CNS, and although some peripheral analgesic activity
has been reported, its biologic significance is unclear.
Pain might
be initiated by tissue injury, but successful tissue repair does not
necessarily result in resolution of pain. Pain resolution can be an
active process, rather than simply due to the removal of the initiating stimulus. Proresolution pathways are now well-recognized
for inflammation, and indeed, mediators of inflammatory resolution might also contribute to pain resolution, both by removing
an inflammatory stimulus and by direct actions on peripheral or
central nervous systems. Two lipid pathways have been linked to
arthritis pain: specialized proresolving mediators (SPMs) and the
soluble epoxide hydrolase (sEH) pathway.
Maresins, SPMs
produced by macrophages, have analgesic properties in a model
of inflammatory arthritis,
and synovial fluid levels of SPMs
were negatively associated with pain.
The SPM intermediate
17-hydroxydocosahexaenoic acid (17-HDHA) is associated with
lower pain scores in people with OA
and can reduce pain behavior in rodents with OA
or inflammatory arthritis.
The sEH
pathway regulates epoxyeicosatrienoic acid (EET) levels, which
may also modulate OA knee pain.
Exploitation of peripheral
antinociceptive lipid pathways for therapeutic benefit is currently
in its infancy.
A recent study in people with LBP performed transcriptomic
analysis in immune cells and found that neutrophil activation–
dependent inflammatory genes were upregulated in subjects
whose pain resolved, in contrast to patients with persistent pain.
This suggests that neutrophil activation in the acute inflammatory response may protect from chronic pain development. It was
confirmed in rodent models that anti-inflammatory treatments
prolonged pain duration. In the UK Biobank, clinical data suggested that the use of anti-inflammatory drugs was associated with
increased risk of persistent LBP.

## Interactions Between Peripheral Pain Pathways and the CNS

The pain experience is highly dependent on CNS function. Reciprocally, persistent nociception alters how the CNS modulates pain
and influences mood, cognition, sleep, and other higher functions. Pain is both a sensory and emotional experience, and its
emotions are a part of the pain rather than merely a consequence.
Pain in people with rheumatic diseases is understood in terms of
events and pathology within peripheral tissues, typically triggered

by biomechanical factors and augmented by musculoskeletal
pathology. Truly central pain (pain originating from within the
CNS, without peripheral inputs) is experienced by some people
after cerebrovascular accident. Central pain is rarely confirmed
in people with rheumatic diseases. CNS mechanisms most often
modulate rather than cause musculoskeletal pain, changing its
intensity, localization, or sensory and emotional qualities.
Pain modulation can occur at all levels within CNS nociceptive pathways.
Within the spinal cord, second-order nociceptive
neurons display increased tonic activity, increased responsiveness to primary nociceptor inputs, and increased receptive fields
responding to inputs from innervations in adjacent tissues
(including skin). This increased responsiveness might represent
facilitated activity or a loss of endogenous inhibition from interneurons and descending spinal pathways originating in the brain
stem. Noninvasive neuroimaging in healthy individuals and people with chronic pain has identified multiple regions in the CNS
that undergo alterations with chronic pain,
including changes
in gray matter density and volume, as well as cortical thickness, in
conditions such as LBP or fibromyalgia. Within the brain, fMRI
has shown increased connectivity of regions that in healthy people
are activated by painful stimuli, including thalamus and primary
sensory cortex, with brain regions that are associated with affective
state, such as the anterior cingulate cortex.
The specificity of neuroimaging findings for pain, rather than
attention or saliency, has been difficult to prove, but brain signatures might point to specific mechanisms contributing to pain
in individuals and eventually support stratified or personalized
analgesic approaches.
Increased activation of affective pathways
in response to a standard ascending nociceptive stimulus might
contribute the prominent affective dimensions of chronic musculoskeletal pain. Increased functional activity in brain stem regions
such as the periaqueductal gray might represent activation of
descending antinociceptive signaling, and failure of these endogenous analgesic pathways to adequately suppress nociceptive
inputs can contribute to central pain hypersensitivity. Descending
analgesic pathways can be activated by ectopic painful stimuli, as
demonstrated by the QST modality of conditioned pain modulation (CPM), whereby sensitivity to a painful pressure or thermal
stimulus is normally reduced when pain is induced at another
body site.
Furthermore, endogenous descending analgesic
pathways might be activated by placebos, contributing to placebo
analgesia.
Inefficient CPM in people with musculoskeletal pain
might either represent deficient endogenous analgesic pathways
or indicate that endogenous analgesic pathways are already maximally activated in people with chronic pain.
Cellular and molecular mechanisms of central pain hypersensitivity are only now becoming understood. Musculoskeletal
inflammation might lead to central sensitization through sustained nociceptive inputs, increased production of centrally sensitizing factors such as BDNF by primary nociceptor terminals in
the spinal cord, and systemic factors that might cross the blood–
brain barrier.
An understanding of mechanisms within musculoskeletal tissues and the peripheral nervous system can help people to accept
and manage their pain. However, pain is a subjective experience
that depends on CNS, as well as peripheral mechanisms. Pain
cannot be “seen” by an observer, through electrophysiology, molecules. or cells. The gold standard for measuring musculoskeletal
pain must always remain self-report, and self-reported aspects of
pain inevitably depend on its psychosocial context. It depends
on the patient’s beliefs about what pain means. An increase in
reported pain severity might indicate increased sensitization rather
than increased damage or inflammation or might represent a cry
for help beyond that which has been offered. Each of these factors contributes to, rather than confounding, the measurement of
musculoskeletal pain.

## Systemic Factors Contributing to Pain Heterogeneity in Rheumatic Diseases

Sensitivity to pain, the experience of pain, and the response to
pain therapy vary greatly between individuals. Contributions to
this heterogeneity may come from interacting biologic and environmental factors, including genetic traits, sex, ethnicity, age,
and psychosocial variables. An extensive literature discusses these
factors and how their interactions contribute to the pain experience.
Below, we discuss the most salient determinants that
affect the pain experience in rheumatic diseases, experimentally
and clinically, with a focus on mechanisms within peripheral tissues. The extent to which the CNS modulates pain can also vary
between people, between diseases, and over time between different stages of disease, creating a varying disparity between tissue
injury and pain severity. Animal models and clinical research are
each increasingly taking variables such as age, sex, and ethnicity/
genetic background into consideration, and this will continue
to refine our understanding of the pathophysiological pathways
underlying the observed heterogeneity. A detailed understanding
of heterogeneity should enable the development of more targeted
pain management strategies.

### Sex as a Modifier of Pain

Chronic pain is more prevalent in women than in men, and women
are at greater risk for pain associated with rheumatic diseases,
including LBP, fibromyalgia, temporomandibular disease (TMD),
and OA.
Analgesic responses to opioids may also differ between
men and women.
While there are many cultural and biosocial factors that may contribute to this observation, there is also increasing
evidence that pathophysiological pain processing mechanisms differ
between the sexes and may underlie the observed differences. The
clearest sex dimorphism has been documented in the contribution
of neuroimmune pathways to pain processing in animal models of
chronic pain, including joint pain.
Microglia appear to dominate in establishing pain in male mouse models but not in females,
which in contrast rely on adaptive immune cells in the spinal cord.
The dominant role of the innate immune system in male mice is
also illustrated by findings that TLR4 signaling in the spinal cord is
more critical for maintaining mechanical allodynia in males than in
female mice. Clinical trials usually recruit participants of either sex,
and an understanding of differences in pain mechanisms between
sexes indicates a need for preclinical research to also investigate both
males and females.

### Genetics of Pain

Pain-associated genotypes may contribute to geographic, ethnic,
and sex differences in musculoskeletal pain. Rare mutations in pain
genes can cause insensitivity to pain.
For example, mutations in
the gene encoding TrkA,
NTRK1,
cause congenital pain insensitivity associated with anhidrosis.
In addition, common variations
with small effect sizes may confer genetic risk for chronic pain
in general and for chronic pain in rheumatic diseases.
Genetic

influences might operate through mechanisms in musculoskeletal
tissues, peripheral nociceptors, or within the CNS. Genetic influences on CNS pain pathways might lead to heterogeneity in central pain processing
and psychological traits such as anxiety
and depression
across a wide range of rheumatic and nonrheumatic conditions. Genetic background can influence joint structure, altering biomechanical factors that can activate nociceptors,
as well as influencing inflammation and tissue repair.
In addition, genetic factors associated with musculoskeletal pain encode
nociceptor-specific proteins,
may affect interactions between
nociceptor terminals and musculoskeletal tissue matrix,
and also
can influence immune responses and therefore potentially neuroimmune interactions within pain pathways.
Many single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) have been
reported that modulate susceptibility to pain. Specific genes associated with chronic musculoskeletal pain may hint at underlying
pathophysiological mechanisms. In OA, SNPs that confer protection from pain or increased susceptibility to pain in the presence
of radiographic knee OA have been reported.
For example, a
TRPV1
variant associated with decreased thermal pain sensitivity confers a decreased risk of painful knee OA.
Genetic variation in
TACR1,
the gene encoding the receptor for substance P, is
associated with pain in individuals with radiographic knee OA.
Of interest, variants in
COMT,
the gene that encodes catecholO
-methyltransferase, has been associated with several forms of
musculoskeletal pain including temporomandibular joint pain,
LBP, fibromyalgia, and OA,
implicating the catecholaminergic
system in chronic musculoskeletal pain. LBP has an estimated
heritability up to 46% and has been associated with SNPs in
genes that encode matrisome proteins, matrix metalloproteinase
(MMP)1, as well as with genes encoding IL-1
α
or IL-1 receptor antagonist,
IL1RN
.
Genetic variants might point to specific
mechanisms underlying musculoskeletal pain or indicate barriers
to treatment and therefore infer risk of pain persistence.

### Obesity

Obesity has been associated with increased prevalence and severity
of pain attributed to a range of rheumatic and nonrheumatological
conditions. Being obese or overweight increases risk of future pain
in people with OA,
while OA pain can be a barrier to exercise and
weight loss. Obesity predicts an increased need for arthroplasty
but also predicts worse pain outcomes after arthroplasty,
although
any effect of obesity on outcome is small compared with the likely
benefit from primary
or even revision
arthroplasty, suggesting
that obesity might make only a small contribution to advanced OA
or postarthroplasty pain. Correspondingly, weight reduction strategies result in significant but overall only small improvements in
OA knee pain in people with obesity.
Benefit might be limited
by low effectiveness of weight reduction strategies in treating obesity. Biomechanical mechanisms are often assumed for the impact
of obesity on knee pain, but obesity is also associated with nodal
OA of the hands, and other factors may be important. The relationship between obesity and pain is multifactorial,
including genetic
associations, effects of obesity-associated adipocytokines on pain
pathways,
lifestyle factors such as exercise engagement, dietary or
psychological factors, and comorbidities.

## Efferent Actions of Nociceptive Pathways

Sensory neurons are by definition afferent; they carry information
from the innervated tissues in the periphery to the CNS. However,
all sensory neurons have bidirectional functions. Nociception primarily alerts the organism to actual or potential tissue damage in
order that corrective or avoidance actions can be adopted. While
conscious actions can result from the sensation of pain, nociception may more directly influence motor function and tissue repair.
Furthermore, the efferent sympathetic nervous system moderates
nociception.
Acute pain triggers muscle contraction, for example through
spinal nociceptive reflexes,
whereby the hand might withdraw
from a hot plate, or a knee might “give way” in response to abrupt
pain, potentially causing falls. Chronic joint pain is associated
with reduced voluntary muscle strength,
and impaired neuromuscular function in painful OA contributes to this weakness.
Pain also is a barrier to engaging in physical activity.
Muscles
around a painful joint can therefore quickly atrophy. Chronic
pain is associated with increased functional connectivity between
motor and sensory brain regions, for example in fibromyalgia.
Such augmented connectivity might contribute to alterations in
central pain modulation but also in motor function.
Neurogenic inflammation, the initiation, exacerbation, or
maintenance of inflammation by sensory nerves, was originally
characterized in skin, where it contributes to wheal and flare
responses to acute injury, and was subsequently recognized in
joints.
Peptidergic primary nociceptors release neuropeptides
such as substance P and CGRP in the spinal cord, where they
modulate activity in second-order neurons. Electrical activity in
peptidergic neurons also releases neuropeptides from nerve terminals in peripheral tissues, where they bind receptors on multiple
cell types. Substance P activates NK1 receptors on endothelial
cells to increase plasma extravasation,
and CGRP activates
CGRPR1 on microvascular smooth muscle to cause vasodilation and increased blood flow.
This acute inflammation can be
accompanied by increased macrophage infiltration. Antagonists of
neuropeptide receptors can reduce physiologic and histologic evidence of inflammation, although any contribution of neurogenic
inflammation to pain or tissue damage in human arthritis remains
unproven. Neuropeptides may also modulate the function of specific immune cells, mast cells, and osteoblasts. Sensory denervation can lead to dystrophic changes in joint tissues and impair
bone healing,
suggesting a contribution of efferent actions
of nociceptors to tissue homeostasis and repair. Complete sensory
denervation involving large myelinated neurons may be associated
with Charcot’s arthropathy, for example in diabetic neuropathy.
Sympathetic nerves richly innervate joint tissues including
synovium, periosteum, and ligaments, often in close proximity to
blood vessels and adjacent to peptidergic sensory nerves.
Sympathetic neurotransmitters such as norepinephrine can contribute
to the sensitization of peripheral nociceptive neurons,
and sympathetic hyperactivity is associated with rheumatic diseases and
pain. Sympathetic cotransmitters are also generated and active
within the CNS, where they might contribute to descending analgesic pathways.
However, sympathetic blockade has not yet
shown consistent analgesic benefit in human rheumatic diseases.

## Clinical Scenarios

### Inflammatory Arthritis

People with inflammatory arthritis, such as RA, PsA, or ankylosing spondylitis (AS), experience chronic inflammation often with
acute flares. Chronic inflammation may be associated with unremitting pain, associated with cytokine and growth factor release

and structural changes in musculoskeletal tissues. Inflammatory
symptoms, including pain, often display diurnal variation.
Worsening symptoms each morning is in part attributable to
circadian rhythm of endogenous anti-inflammatory mechanisms
involving the hypothalamopituitary axis.
People with inflammatory arthritis can also experience troublesome and often unpredictable inflammatory flares, lasting days or weeks, even on the
background of otherwise stable disease. Inflammation might be
recognized by increased pain and morning stiffness, joint swelling
and tenderness, elevated serum C-reactive protein levels or erythrocyte sedimentation rate, ultrasound evidence of increased blood
flow on Power Doppler imaging, or MRI evidence of increased
water content as high signal on T2-weighted or STIR imaging in
synovium or adjacent bone. Biomarkers may support a diagnosis
of active inflammation but might sometimes persist after treatment, when other evidence of inflammation has remitted.
Pain in inflammatory arthritis can often be rapidly relieved by
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs, cyclo-oxygenase
[COX] inhibitors, either selective for COX2, or nonselective
COX inhibitors such as ibuprofen or naproxen). People may benefit from these acute anti-inflammatory effects by “as required”
dosing, when experiencing an inflammatory flare, or use NSAIDs
to provide pre-emptive analgesia to enable some valued activity,
for example going out for the day. More regular NSAID use over
a period of 2 weeks can provide additional analgesic benefit by
reducing COX-mediated peripheral sensitization. Glucocorticoids
also can reduce inflammatory pain if delivered either locally to
the site of inflammation (e.g., by intra-articular injection) or by
systemic (oral, intramuscular, or intravenous) routes. Glucocorticoids act through intracellular receptors, which in part regulate
inflammatory signalling, and largely by suppressing pro-inflammatory gene expression. Their analgesic benefit for inflammatory
pain might be rapid (within the day) and typically peaks 2 or 3
days after glucocorticoid initiation.
Analgesic response to NSAIDs or glucocorticoid might suggest, but does not prove, peripheral inflammatory pain mechanisms. Either might also modulate neuroimmune cells within the
CNS and thereby reduce central sensitization.
Furthermore, an
individual’s analgesic benefit from any intervention will incorporate placebo effects and depends on expectancy and other contextual factors. Also, the natural history of painful flares is often
to improve, even if only temporarily, and improvement following initiation of treatment does not necessarily indicate a specific
pharmacologic effect. Biomarkers of inflammation might therefore only weakly predict analgesic responses to anti-inflammatory
interventions, and acute symptomatic responses to anti-inflammatory interventions might only weakly predict underlying inflammation or response to subsequent suppression of inflammation by
disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs.
Pain in inflammatory arthritis may be driven by innate or
adaptive immune responses. In RA, antibodies may be directed
to natural or modified host antigens, such as immunoglobulins
(rheumatoid factor) or citrullinated peptides (anti-citrullinated
protein antibodies [ACPAs]), nuclear antigens (anti-nuclear antibodies [ANAs]), or matrix molecules (e.g., collagen). Antibodies
and antigens may form immune complexes. Some antibodies may
themselves sensitize peripheral nociceptors, as evidenced by passive transfer to nonarthritic rodents of purified IgG from patients’
blood or B cell clones, or immune complexes.
T cells also
respond to host antigens, releasing cytokines and chemokines
that further attract and activate lymphocytes, macrophages, and
resident cells such as fibroblasts. Elucidation of these specific
molecular pathways in inflammatory arthritis and the translation
into clinical practice of blocking antibodies that specifically target
individual molecules have revealed key mechanisms that, directly
or indirectly, drive arthritis pain.
The introduction of biologic and targeted immunomodulatory agents has transformed the lives of people with inflammatory
arthritis, enabling a return to normal activities and employment
for many. Antibodies or receptor fusion proteins that bind and
prevent TNF engaging its receptors are effective in reducing
inflammatory pain in RA, PsA, and AS. Biologic agents have been
developed against other specific molecular targets, including IL-1,
IL-6, IL-17, T cell co-stimulator molecules, and B cells. Each of
these effectively can reduce chronic pain in inflammatory arthritis, with effects that are both clinically and statistically significant
compared with placebos.
Differences between inflammatory arthritis diagnoses in analgesic efficacy of targeted treatments
reflect disease-specific balance between different immune pathways, each of which might cause peripheral sensitization.
Immunosuppression can improve pain by reducing joint
inflammation. Biologic and targeted agents often rapidly reduce
pain (e.g., within 2 weeks) and precede objective evidence of suppression of inflammation, suggesting possible direct effects on
nociception or sensitization.
The DRG is outside the blood–
brain barrier and therefore accessible to large molecules such
as antibodies, and immunomodulatory treatments might have
direct effects on primary nociceptor function and disrupt neuroimmune interactions. Much attention has focused on inhibitors
of the JAK/STAT pathways.
However, pain-generating factors
might be produced during subclinical inflammation that cannot
be detected by current clinical assessment and biomarkers. Antiinflammatory and direct analgesic responses at present cannot
definitively be distinguished.
TNF-blocking agents can retard or even abolish progression
of radiographic joint damage in RA, raising the expectation
that their ongoing use will reduce or prevent damage-associated
chronic pain over the succeeding years. This remains an unproven
hypothesis due to the complex, multifactorial nature of chronic
pain. Arthritis pain is often at most weakly associated with radiographic evidence of structural damage.
It might be anticipated
that effective suppression of inflammation would reduce the need
for joint replacement surgery in people with inflammatory arthritis. While hip and knee arthroplasties in Canada have decreased
by up to 25% for people diagnosed with RA since the introduction of biologic agents, they have increased during this period
for those diagnosed earlier in the prebiologic era.
Decisions to
pursue surgery are dependent on complex biopsychosocial factors,
not only joint damage but also pain severity, patient and clinician expectations about prognosis and potential for treatment
responses, and accessibility of surgery.
Despite major advances in the treatment of inflammation in
arthritis, many people continue to suffer distressing chronic pain.
For some this might reflect other painful comorbidities (such as
fibromyalgia or OA). For others it might result from persistent
peripheral or central sensitization, which can outlive the initiating inflammation. For some, the severity and high impact of
postinflammatory pain might reflect psychosocial factors, such
as persistent psychological distress or catastrophizing, or comorbidities. Noninflammatory mechanisms are increasingly recognized in people with inflammatory arthritis. Fibromyalgia occurs
with increased incidence and prevalence in populations with RA,
PsA, or ankylosing spondylitis.
Recognition and management
of fibromyalgia are key to relieving the suffering of people with

inflammatory arthritis, when suppressing inflammatory pain is
not sufficient.

### Osteoarthritis

OA displays elements of articular cartilage degeneration, although
more recent perspectives have emphasized metabolic changes
and the involvement of all joint structures, including cartilage,
bone, synovium, and ligaments. OA is now seen a clinical syndrome in which joint pain is the cardinal feature. The relationship
of pain with structural, cellular, and biochemical changes in OA
is complex and incompletely understood. Imaging studies have
emphasized the importance of synovitis and subchondral BMLs
as correlates of OA pain.
Both are also associated with cartilage pathology, and noninterventional studies cannot robustly distinguish the primary cause of pain between these intercorrelated
aspects of OA.
The case for inflammation as a contributor to OA pain is persuasive, because treatments that reduce inflammation (such as
intra-articular glucocorticoid injections
) or reduce the effects
of inflammation on nociceptors (such as NSAIDs
) are effective
analgesics in OA. The precise mechanisms by which OA inflammation drives pain are incompletely understood and might differ
from those in RA. Biologic treatments that reduce RA pain have
not demonstrated robust evidence for pain relief in OA in clinical
trials.
The case for BMLs as a cause of OA pain is also persuasive,
based on cross-sectional and longitudinal MRI studies.
BMLs
represent heterogeneous pathologic processes, and a lack of targeted interventions that reverse OA BMLs precludes definitive
conclusions on how BMLs might cause pain. OA BMLs might be
associated with inflammatory cell infiltration, cytokine and NGF
expression, and sensory nerve terminal growth within subchondral bone spaces. These changes resemble those observed in OA
synovium, and some inflammatory mechanisms, as well as neuroplasticity, might be shared between synovial and subchondral
sources of pain.
OA pain is induced or exacerbated by mechanical factors such
as weight bearing, and changes in the biomechanical or structural
properties of the joint make important contributions to OA pain.
Correspondingly, interventions that change joint mechanics can
reduce pain, including strengthening exercises, braces or splints,
walking aids, osteotomy, or arthroplasty. This reinforces perceptions of OA as a biomechanical disease, both by clinicians and the
general public. Several approaches to NGF inhibition, including
NGF-blocking antibodies and small molecular inhibitors of TrkA,
have successfully reduced OA pain in humans, veterinary practice,
and animal models.
Analgesic benefits from NGF inhibition
underline the key importance of peripheral nociceptor sensitization in OA pain.
OA is also associated with extensive evidence of central sensitization, gathered using techniques such as QST
and functional MRI.
Central pain mechanisms in part might explain
the discordance often reported between structural change and OA
pain.
OA pain qualities sometimes resemble those associated
with neuropathic pain, probably reflecting shared central pain
mechanisms.
Evidence of central sensitization might subside
following effective treatments to reduce peripheral drivers of OA
pain. For example, in people undergoing arthroplasty who have
good pain relief, evidence of central sensitization may resolve,
suggesting that it is the persistent nociceptive drive from the OA
joint that is driving central pain facilitation.
However, some
people may be at risk of or be diagnosed with chronic pain conditions that are associated with central sensitization syndromes, for
example fibromyalgia, long before they fulfill classification criteria
for OA.
A better understanding of the links between OA structural
changes and pain is essential for future treatment. Arthroplasty
is an effective intervention to reduce pain in some people with
OA, but up to 20% of people continue to suffer clinically important pain after knee replacement surgery.
Arthroplasty removes
diseased articular cartilage and reduces biomechanical forces
through subchondral bone when cartilage can no longer achieve
that. Arthroplasty does not always decrease synovitis and might
not eliminate BMLs.
Arthroplasty is accordingly less effective
at relieving pain in people with well-preserved articular cartilage.
Interventions might have adverse effects on joint structure
despite relieving pain. Treatment with NGF-blocking antibodies
can cause rapidly progressive osteoarthritis (RPOA),
and intraarticular glucocorticoid injection
or NSAIDs
have each been
associated with joint damage.
Structural adverse effects are not proportionate to the degree
of analgesia and therefore difficult to explain as simply resulting
from elimination of pain’s protective effects. NGF-neutralizing
antibodies, glucocorticoids, and NSAIDs might each have direct
effects on chondrocytes or subchondral bone cells, and molecular
pathways shared between neurons and other cells might explain
structural adverse events with selected analgesic agents. Whatever the mechanism, drug developers and analgesic trialists in OA
might be well advised to also evaluate OA structural progression,
both to capture beneficial OA disease modification that might
contribute to long term benefits and to identify unexpected structural adverse events.

### Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is a clinical syndrome with interacting symptoms:
widespread pain, sleep disturbance, diurnal fatigue, mood disturbance, and cognitive impact. People with fibromyalgia often
describe painful comorbidities such as irritable bowel syndrome,
headache, or gynecological pain and may describe sensitivity to
non-nociceptive stimuli (for example hyperacusis) or symptoms
suggesting abnormal activation of non-nociceptive neurons (e.g.,
paraesthesiae, stiffness, or feelings of tissue swelling). Fibromyalgia prevalence in various populations has been estimated to be
between 2% and 20%,
although pain in an even larger number
of people might share mechanisms with fibromyalgia. Fibromyalgia symptoms occur and may progress or regress along a continuum rather than defining a discrete population with disease.
It might be particularly prevalent in groups of people with inflammatory rheumatic diseases or OA, and health care utilization for
comorbid painful musculoskeletal conditions brings the management of fibromyalgia within the remit of a wide range of clinicians.
Fibromyalgia is an important differential diagnosis for
pain in people with rheumatic diseases.
The constellation of symptoms characteristic of fibromyalgia
suggest important mechanisms within the CNS, including in the
brain, but peripheral mechanisms undoubtedly contribute to the
pain of fibromyalgia. People with fibromyalgia report pain on
activity, localized to moving joints or muscles, and people with
premorbid fibromyalgia report increased pain from comorbid
rheumatic diseases such as RA or OA. The presence of fibromyalgia might predict worse pain outcomes from biologic therapy
or arthroplasty,
but these treatments may still provide clinically

important benefit to people with fibromyalgia with comorbid
joint disease. Recent evidence has led to the suggestion that the
widespread and persistent nature of fibromyalgia pain might be
explained by systemic and peripheral inflammatory mechanisms.
Binding of antibodies to Fc receptors on peripheral sensory neurons, autoantibodies directed to satellite cells within DRG that
can increase peripheral nociceptor sensitivity, and widespread
small fiber neuropathy each are reportedly increased in subgroups
of people who satisfy fibromyalgia classification criteria.
To date these laboratory evidences of peripheral inflammation
in people with fibromyalgia have not led to new and effective
interventions for fibromyalgia pain. However, clinicians need to
be open about the limits of the existing knowledge base. Explaining how fibromyalgia may have both central and peripheral
mechanisms might better represent a patient’s experience than do
explanations of fibromyalgia as an exclusively CNS or peripheral
condition. For many, OA or RA can explain where a patient experiences pain, whereas fibromyalgia might explain how severe that
pain is. People with fibromyalgia should not be denied peripherally targeted treatments for their painful comorbidities, unless
there is robust evidence that those treatments are not clinically
effective in this large subpopulation.

### Ehlers–Danlos Syndromes and Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder

Pain is highly prevalent in individuals with hypermobility, either
as part of Ehlers–Danlos syndrome or as hypermobility spectrum
disorder.
The Ehlers–Danlos syndromes (EDSs) are a group
of rare heritable connective tissue disorders that share clinical
hallmarks including generalized joint hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility and fragility, easy bruising, and abnormal wound
healing.
The multisystemic pathology in these syndromes is
caused by mutations in genes that are involved in collagen biosynthesis, fibrillogenesis, or supramolecular organization of collagen
fibrils. These collagen anomalies thus affect the properties of the
extracellular matrix in all tissues. Fourteen distinct and recognized
EDS types have been molecularly elucidated, and the term
hypermobile type
(hEDS) should be limited to other individuals with
features indicative of a systemic and/or mendelian connective tissue disorder.
Individuals with symptomatic joint hypermobility who do
not fulfil diagnostic criteria for hEDS are now considered to
have
hypermobility spectrum disorders
(HSDs).
Joint subluxations and dislocations, muscle and tendon ruptures, skin tears
and bruising, and nerve entrapments are common in EDS and
HDS and can all be sources of acute and localized pain. In addition, chronic musculoskeletal pain is a major complaint across
all hypermobility diagnoses. Chronic pain—described as either
“joint pain,” “musculoskeletal pain,” or “chronic widespread
pain”—is a diagnostic criterion for hEDS.
In clinical practice,
chronic pain presents a major challenge, reflected by high use of
analgesics, surgery, and physical therapy. Inadequately managed
chronic pain often severely compromises patients’ well-being.
Common additional complaints include burning sensations,
peripheral paresthesias, generalized hyperalgesia, allodynia, and
hypersensitivity to various other stimuli, such as light, sound,
and odors.
Pain distribution and description often overlap
with fibromyalgia.
Research addressing the prevalence, characteristics, etiology,
and mechanisms of pain in hypermobility has largely focused
on women. Although pain in hypermobility syndromes is often
attributed to biomechanical factors, aberrant interactions between
nociceptor terminals and an abnormal tissue matrix,
widespread
small fiber peripheral neuropathy,
and central pain mechanisms
might also contribute. A study in 20 females with
hEDS revealed reduced pain pressure thresholds and significantly
more temporal summation after repeated painful stimuli compared with 20 age-matched controls, suggesting increased central pain facilitation.
In comparison with the healthy control
group, the hEDS group demonstrated significantly less exerciseinduced hypoalgesia in the quadriceps. Of interest, one study in
Col5a1
mice, a model for classical EDS, reported that these
mice are hypersensitive to mechanical but not to thermal stimuli,
and this was accompanied by decreased intraepidermal nerve fiber
density.
Likewise,
tenascin-X
knockout (
Tnxb
) mice, another
model for classical EDS, also display mechanical but not thermal
allodynia or hypersensitivity.
Spinal dorsal horn abnormalities
in tenascin-deficient mice might either reflect augmented nociceptive inputs or intrinsic abnormalities in the CNS. Matrix–neuronal interactions are important for development and homeostasis
within the CNS, as well as in peripheral tissues.

## Conclusions

Pain is a personal experience, caused and influenced by many factors. The associations found between pain severity and any single
causal factor are often weak. Joint radiographs cannot indicate
synovitis. MRI cannot measure peripheral sensitization. CNS
processing modulates nociceptive signaling, often irrespective of
pathology in the musculoskeletal system. This complexity provides opportunities for managing pain through multiple treatment modalities but poses challenges when assessing for the best
interventions to be offered to the individual at the right time.
Peripheral nociceptive mechanisms are key to understanding pain
in rheumatic diseases. Blocking peripheral neurotransmission, for
example using local anesthetics or neuroablation techniques or by
suppressing inflammation or restructuring a deranged joint, each
might relieve pain. Pain, however, has a homeostatic and protective function, and complete analgesia can itself limit normal protective reflexes and initiate joint damage. Furthermore, neurons
have important efferent functions, and molecules involved in
nociception might also have other important biologic functions
on non-neuronal cells. Understanding the mechanisms of pain in
peripheral tissues should pave the way to novel treatments that
can more effectively and safely relieve musculoskeletal pain. At the
very least, contemporary understanding can help explain the mysteries of pain and help those who suffer from rheumatic diseases
to live fulfilling lives.
