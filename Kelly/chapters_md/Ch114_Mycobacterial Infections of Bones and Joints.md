## Introduction

The recognition of tuberculosis (TB) and other mycobacterial
infections of the musculoskeletal system has become a major
challenge for rheumatologists in the United States and other
high income countries. Before 1999, most rheumatologists
could easily go an entire year without seeing a single case of
mycobacterial infection. Such cases were rare even at academic
centers, where they would likely be presented as unusual teaching cases at clinical conferences.
The routine clinical use of TNF inhibitor therapy was introduced in 1999 in the United States, and with it came an unexpected increase in TB cases seen by rheumatologists. Fortunately,
the initiation of routine screening with tuberculin skin test (TSTs)
and interferon (IFN)-
γ
release assays (IGRAs) has led to a sharp
decline in the number of new cases, particularly those associated
with reactivation of latent infection. Nevertheless, the risk of primary infection with new exposure mandates continued vigilance
in these patients and potentially those on other biologic therapies. Moreover, for non-TB mycobacterial infections, there are no
screening tests available, making vigilance on immunosuppressive
therapy even more important.
Another major force driving the increase in mycobacterial infections is the HIV epidemic. In the pre-anti-retroviral therapy era,
21% of extrapulmonary TB cases in the United States were associated with underlying HIV infection. In low-middle income countries, the HIV epidemic has led to marked increases in osteoarticular
TB co-infection.
These cases are distinguished by disseminated
disease suggestive of hematogenous spread, rapid progression, and
more common co-existence with pulmonary infection.
HIV testing should be a routine part of the workup of any patient presenting with a musculoskeletal infection secondary to
Mycobacterium
tuberculosis
. The good news is that since the introduction of effective anti-retroviral therapy, the incidence of TB and nontuberculous
mycobacterial (NTM) infection in patients co-infected with HIV
has declined sharply in the United States.
TB is an uncommon disease in high income countries. In
the United States the incidence of TB has been steadily declining since 1990, and the majority of TB cases now may be considered imported (i.e., occurring in foreign-born individuals).
Worldwide, TB remains one of the world’s deadliest infectious
diseases.
In 2021, an estimated 10 million people developed
TB, and 1.6 million people died from the disease. Worldwide,
TB is the 13th leading cause of death and the leading cause of
death from a single infectious agent, exceeding even HIV/AIDS.
Tuberculosis cases and deaths increased during the COVID-19
pandemic, in part due to the disruption of TB services. More
alarming is the increasing rate of multidrug-resistant TB (MDRTB), which also increased during the COVID-19 pandemic, and
the continued emergence of extensively drug-resistant TB (XDRTB).
MDR-TB is TB that is resistant to rifampin and any fluoroquinolone (a class of second-line anti-TB drug). XDR-TB is TB
that is resistant to rifampin, plus any fluoroquinolone, plus at least
one of the drugs bedaquiline and linezolid.
Approximately one-fourth of the world’s population, 1.7 billion people, are latently infected with TB, providing a global reservoir that will continue to complicate its control.
Globalization
of the world economy promotes contact between populations of

the endemic and nonendemic regions. Immigrants to the United
States from areas endemic for TB constitute an ongoing reservoir
of patients with latent TB.
The challenge of diagnosing musculoskeletal mycobacterial
infection reaches beyond the rarity of the disease. Such infections
are often indolent and may lack the pain, fever, chills, and other
prominent symptoms that typically accompany bacterial infections of the musculoskeletal system. In addition, routine culture
techniques will not isolate the organism, so unless the diagnosis of mycobacterial infection is a consideration from the outset,
the diagnosis may be missed. Delayed diagnosis of mycobacterial infections of the musculoskeletal system is common and will
remain without a heightened index of suspicion.
For all these reasons, 21st-century rheumatologists need to be
well informed about a group of diseases with which they may have
had limited experience.

## Clinical Scenarios

To appreciate the entire spectrum of clinical problems a rheumatologist may encounter in dealing with mycobacterial infections,
it is useful to think in terms of several distinct categories. Investigators
provide a clinically useful division of mycobacterial infections into four major categories.

### Direct Involvement of the Musculoskeletal System

Musculoskeletal infections caused by mycobacteria typically
present as chronic, indolent, localized involvement of the bones,
spine, peripheral joints, or soft tissues that produces a focus of
nonspecific pain and, less often, swelling. Musculoskeletal TB
infections almost always represent reactivation of infection
acquired months, years, or decades earlier, which had hematogenously seeded bones and joints. Musculoskeletal NTM infections may occur as a result of hematogenous spread from the
lungs or direct inoculation of environmental contaminants. In
infections initiated by direct tissue inoculation, the traumatic
event is often trivial or remote in time from the onset of clinical disease. Diagnosis may be delayed for months to years, in
part because of minimal early symptoms and attribution of those
symptoms to a noninfectious disorder until disease progression
and disability prompt a more aggressive diagnostic investigation.
Constitutional symptoms are typically subtle or absent, and
laboratory indicators of inflammation are often normal. Synovial
effusion is frequently minimal, and the fluid, if it is obtainable,
shows nonspecific inflammation. Radiographic abnormalities may be delayed, although newer imaging techniques have
allowed the earlier detection of abnormalities and the distinction
of TB from other infections and neoplasm.
Characteristic
pulmonary or extrapulmonary findings are not always present;
for example, less than 50% of osteoarticular TB presents with
evidence of active or past pulmonary disease. TSTs and IGRA
testing may provide useful clues to the cause, but results are
not invariably positive, especially in debilitated or immunosuppressed patients. HIV co-infection is common. Correct diagnosis is highly dependent, in most cases, on demonstration of
the infectious agent by microscopic examination and culture of
affected tissue.
In HIV-infected patients, mycobacterial infections are often
diagnosed before the patients’ HIV status is known, sometimes
leading to its recognition.
Atypical presentations of pulmonary
TB and extrapulmonary (often multifocal) infection are common;
extrapulmonary infection occurs in 60% to 70% of such cases,
compared with 16% of all patients with TB.
The clinical patterns of musculoskeletal TB include spondylitis,
osteomyelitis, peripheral joint infection, and soft-tissue abscess. In
a series of 230 consecutive cases of TB from the pre-antibiotic era,
5.2% had skeletal involvement; the spine was affected in 60% of
these cases.
TB of the bones and joints is seeded hematogenously
from pulmonary infection. The sites most commonly affected are
the spine and hips, followed by the knees and wrists; other joint
involvement is uncommon. The typical symptoms of TB—fever,
night sweats, and weight loss—are uncommon in musculoskeletal TB and, when present, suggest TB in other organs. Vertebral
collapse because of spinal TB may initially be attributed to the
more common osteoporosis-caused spinal compression fracture.
TB only rarely involves skeletal muscle but must be considered in
the differential diagnosis of an enlarging muscle lesion.
Isolated cases involving tendons, trochanteric bursa, and fascia lata
illustrate the variety of possibilities.
Imaging studies do not
reliably distinguish TB from neoplasm. Biopsy and culture are
required for diagnosis.
In nonendemic areas, skeletal TB usually occurs in elderly, debilitated patients, most often in the form of solitary osteolytic lesions
in the axial skeleton. The development of skeletal disease is often
remote from the initial infection, which strongly implies reactivation of previous subclinical disease. Patients may have false-negative
TSTs and IGRAs for several reasons, including long-term corticosteroid use or co-existing debilitating diseases, such as rheumatoid
arthritis (RA) or chronic renal failure, that compromise resistance
and produce anergy (

The occurrence of spinal disease in children in nonendemic regions has mostly been eliminated
by effective medical therapy of pulmonary infection and identification and treatment of those with latent TB.
In contrast, in endemic areas with high infectivity rates, those
infected are more commonly children and young to middle-age
adults, and these individuals have a higher incidence of multifocal
skeletal involvement. Bone seeding occurs through hematogenous
spread, sometimes secondarily from another extrapulmonary site.
When pulmonary findings are present, a miliary pattern is typical. Spread to bone may also occur from infected nodes, either by
direct extension or through draining lymph channels.

### Emergence of Tuberculosis During Treatment of Rheumatic Diseases

Many patients with systemic rheumatic disease have dysregulated
immune systems that are treated with immunosuppressive drugs.
Such an impaired immune response may permit the reactivation

of latent TB. TNF plays a key role in granuloma formation and
stabilization, which promotes the containment of
M. tuberculosis
.
The rate of TB among patients with RA before the widespread
use of TNF inhibitors was approximately six cases per 100,000,
although some studies have suggested that the rate in RA is higher
than in the general population.
In one large patient registry,
the estimated incidence of TB associated with infliximab in RA
patients was in excess of 1000 per 100,000 person-years of exposure during the years 2000 to 2001.
In the United States, the rate
was 52 per 100,000 person-years during the same time period.
Etanercept may be associated with a lower risk for TB reactivation
than infliximab: 10 cases per 100,000 person-years of exposure
with etanercept, versus 41 per 100,000 person-years with infliximab, according to an analysis of U.S. Food and Drug Administration data.
Similarly, British and French registries have identified
a greater risk for TB associated with the use of adalimumab and
infliximab than with etanercept.
There are fewer data available
for the newer TNF inhibitors, and comparisons are complicated
by the widespread use of pre-treatment screening instituted after
the first agents in this class were approved for use. Monoclonal
antibodies to TNF may play a greater role in destabilizing granulomas than soluble TNF receptors. Nonetheless, similar precautions should be taken with any TNF inhibitor and all should be
presumed to increase the risk of both new infection and reactivation of latent infection. The European Alliance of Associations for
Rheumatology published updated consensus recommendations
that covered screening for tuberculosis and other chronic infections prior to the use of immunomodulatory therapy in 2022.
Non-TNF biologic agents have been associated with TB infection, although most of these cases occur in regions with endemic
disease. Despite labeling recommendations for screening prior to
therapy, cumulative clinical trial and post-marketing data suggest
that the risk of reactivation is quite low.
Biologics with mechanisms other than TNF inhibition may pose a lower risk for reactivation of TB because the mechanisms of action are not intimately
involved with host defense related to intra-cellular organisms.
Animal studies have suggested that abatacept does not impair the
ability of mice to respond to
M. tuberculosis
, but this has not been
confirmed in humans.
Little or no evidence indicates that B cells
play a major role in containing TB, and there does not appear to
be an increased risk of TB after treatment with rituximab.
Cases
of tuberculosis have occurred with tofacitinib, although most
occurred in regions with high background rates.
The median
time between drug start and diagnosis was 64 weeks, suggesting
new infection rather than reactivation of latent disease.
Glucocorticoid use also poses a significant hazard for patients
with latent TB. Many mechanisms account for this effect, including impairment of cellular immune responses and monocyte
chemotaxis and function, including the monocyte’s production
of TNF. The use of glucocorticoids has been associated with a
five-fold increased risk for TB in a case-control study involving
a large general practice database in the United Kingdom.
The
risk appeared to be dose related but was seen even at the physiologic dose of prednisone, 7.5 mg/day. Just as with TNF inhibitors, the risk for TB was greatest early in the course of treatment.
In a French study, prednisone at 10 mg/day or more but not lower
doses was associated with an increased risk for TB.
Reactivation of TB in the face of TNF inhibitor therapy typically occurs within 6 months of treatment and often manifests as
extrapulmonary disease. Institution of a protocol to screen and
treat for latent TB infection (LTBI) in patients with RA before
TNF inhibitor administration resulted in a 78% decrease in TB
disease.
Treatment of LTBI can effectively and safely lessen the
likelihood of TB disease in patients treated with TNF inhibitors. Indeed, it has been suggested that much of the blame for
the development of TB disease in this situation can be attributed
to incomplete compliance with screening protocols.
Conversely,
in a report of 84 patients at a single center who were treated with
etanercept after demonstrating a positive TST, no cases of TB disease were seen during a mean of 2 years of follow-up despite the
fact that 78 of 84 patients were prescribed treatment and only
52 completed the prescribed treatment regimen.
Although
screening for LTBI has reduced the incidence of TB disease, falsenegative TSTs and false-negative and indeterminate IGRAs can
undermine such good intentions (see
Table 114.1
).
The approach to a patient with latent TB who needs a TNF
inhibitor has not been standardized. Many authorities recommend that patients with positive TSTs or IGRAs and normal chest
radiographs who have never been treated for TB receive at least
1 to 2 months of therapy before beginning a TNF inhibitor, if
for no other reason than to be certain that they will be able to
tolerate the full course of latent TB therapy. Appropriate regimens
include 9 months of isoniazid, 4 months of rifampin, or 3 months
of directly observed weekly isoniazid plus rifapentine. No reactivation was seen in patients with latent infection receiving concurrent therapy with tofacitinib and isoniazid. Patients discovered to
have active TB should receive a complete course of standard antituberculous therapy before initiating a TNF inhibitor.
Although TNF inhibitors and steroids are predominant risk
factors for TB, all patients with rheumatic disease treated with
immunosuppressive therapy that impairs cellular immunity
should be considered at risk. This is especially true in older adults,
those who are malnourished, and immigrants from countries with
high endemic rates of TB.

### Rheumatic Disorders Precipitated by the Treatment of Tuberculosis

A variety of rheumatic conditions may be precipitated by the drugs
used in the treatment of TB. These include drug-induced lupus
caused by isoniazid (INH) and rifampin. As with other cases of
drug-induced lupus, they are associated with positive anti-nuclear
antibodies and the presence of anti-histone antibodies. Typically,
these patients follow a benign course, with resolution of disease
after the drug is discontinued.
Arthropathy and tendinopathy have been described with the
use of fluoroquinolones, especially ciprofloxacin and levofloxacin.
The risk of tendon rupture (usually the Achilles tendon) is greatest
in patients older than 50 years and increases with the concomitant
use of corticosteroids.
Pyrazinamide interferes with the renal tubular excretion of uric
acid and has been associated with the development of hyperuricemia and gout in adults.
Paradoxical reactions have been described in patients withdrawn from infliximab after the diagnosis and initiation of treatment for TB disease.
These reactions present as a worsening of
pre-existing TB lesions or the development of new lesions not
attributable to the normal course of disease in a patient initially
improving on treatment. The mechanism for such reactions is not
completely understood but has been categorized within the spectrum of immune reconstitution inflammatory syndromes. Such
reactions are more common in patients with HIV following the
initiation of anti-retroviral therapy. After infliximab therapy is discontinued, its anti-inflammatory effects persist for 3 or 4 weeks,

which may explain the timing of this delayed reaction. No changes
in anti-tuberculous therapy are necessary when a paradoxical reaction occurs; corticosteroids may ameliorate this response.

### Reactive Immunologic Phenomena in the Setting of Tuberculosis

A variety of reactive immunologic phenomena have been associated with
M. tuberculosis
infection. These are uncommonly seen
in clinical practice.
Poncet’s disease is an aseptic inflammatory polyarthritis that
occurs in the presence of TB disease. Although any joints can be
involved, the most commonly affected are the knees, ankles, and
elbows.
The mechanism is thought to be similar to other forms
of reactive arthritis secondary to remote infection. Most cases
resolve after satisfactory treatment of the TB. Reactive arthritis has
been described following the intravesicular instillation of bacille
Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine for bladder cancer.

seldom-encountered

patterns
described with
M. tuberculosis
include erythema nodosum, erythema induratum, and amyloidosis (AA type).

## Diagnosis

### Tuberculin Skin Test

The TST—also called the
purified protein derivative
(PPD)—has
been in use for nearly a century and remains the most widely used
screening test for TB worldwide. The test represents a crude mix of
antigens from
M. tuberculosis
and is plagued by both false-positive
and false-negative results. It is unable to distinguish latent infection from active disease and may be negative in the face of severe
active TB. Corticosteroids (
≥
15 mg/day of prednisone) may render the TST negative in people with latent TB. Elderly and malnourished patients may not exhibit a positive TST.
Table 114.1
provides a partial list of causes for a false-negative TST that are of
special interest to rheumatologists.
False-positive results may likewise occur in the case of infection
with nontuberculous mycobacteria or previous BCG vaccination.
TST positivity degrades with time in BCG-vaccinated patients at
a variable rate. The degree of positivity is influenced by a number of factors, including the number of BCG vaccinations and
the number of subsequent TSTs performed. Some patients may
retain a response as long as 15 years after BCG vaccination. Nevertheless, a TST result of 20 mm or greater is rarely the result of
BCG. In addition, if faced with a high-risk situation, such as the
initiation of TNF inhibitor therapy, one should probably make a
presumption of LTBI even if the diameter of induration measures
as little as 5 mm.
The important message for rheumatologists is that the TST is
an important but imperfect screening tool for latent TB infection,
with a sensitivity and specificity in the range of 70%. A negative
TST should not eliminate the clinician’s vigilance in monitoring
patients treated with TNF inhibitors for reactivated or new-onset
TB, especially in high-risk populations.
Interferonγ
Release Assays
The limitations of the traditional PPD skin test in terms of specificity and sensitivity in identifying latent TB have led to the development of new T cell–based testing. These assays measure the
production of IFNγ
by whole-blood mononuclear cells stimulated by specific
M. tuberculosis
antigens. The IGRAs, including
the QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus (QFT-Plus) (Qiagen; Venlo,
Netherlands) and T-SPOT.TB (Oxford Immunotec, Inc.; Oxford,
England) assays, appear to have equivalent or better sensitivity and
improved specificity compared with TST.
These assays contain specific
M. tuberculosis
antigens and are less likely to result
in a positive test in people exposed to nontuberculous mycobacteria, including
Mycobacterium avium
complex or the attenuated
Mycobacterium bovis
strain in the BCG vaccine. They also offer the
opportunity to circumvent operator error with TST administration and interpretation and do not require patients to return in
48 to 72 hours to have the test interpreted. IGRAs have replaced
the TST in many centers for routine screening for LTBI. Studies
of IGRAs in patients with RA and other inflammatory disorders
have found them to have comparable or better accuracy than the
TST, although a recent meta-analysis found that most of these
studies have a high risk of bias.
IGRA may be particularly
useful in patients treated with steroids, which can reduce the sensitivity of the TST.
IGRAs are not without concerns. Indeterminate results from
IGRAs in this setting have ranged from 1.2% to 28.6%, raising concerns about the performance of the assay from center
to center.
Within-subject result variability is an issue, especially when the TB antigen response is near the cutoff value.
Factors that might contribute to IGRA result variability include
reagent variability, blood volume, whether the blood tube is
shaken gently or vigorously, delays in processing, and PPDinduced boosting of IFNγ
response.
Positive IGRAs are more
closely related to TB risk factors than are positive TSTs.
Discordant IGRA
/TST
results are associated with prior BCG
vaccination, and discordant IGRA
/TST
results are related to
corticosteroid therapy, suggesting that the former represents a
false-positive TST and the latter a false-negative TST. Nevertheless, the meaning of a discordant result is uncertain enough
that patients with a positive test result with either IGRA or TST
should be considered for therapy of possible LTBI.
Additional
experience will be required to provide confidence that a negative
IGRA in the setting of a positive TST reliably excludes LTBI. If
only one testing modality is used, one analysis found that use
of an IGRA was more cost effective than the TST for patients
screened before TNF inhibitor therapy.
IGRAs have also been studied in patients receiving a TNF
inhibitor to identify newly acquired TB infection. Many authorities recommend ongoing annual monitoring for new TB infection in patients treated with TNF inhibitors, especially in highly
endemic areas. Rates of TST conversion in patients on TNF
inhibitor therapy are 33% and 37% from centers in Korea and
Taiwan; corresponding rates of IGRA conversion were 14% and
11%, respectively, and appeared to better correlate with the risk
of active TB.
Pending formal guidelines, in regions where
ongoing exposure to infectious TB may occur, routine monitoring
for TB on therapy—perhaps annually—is wise and the use of an
IGRA rather than a TST may limit the number of patients requiring preventive treatment.

### Imaging

Although imaging patterns suggestive of TB have been discussed,
there are no pathognomonic skeletal radiographic features that
can establish the diagnosis. Early features on radiographs may be
equivocal or nonexistent. Chest radiographs are often normal or
fail to show the posterior upper lobe cavitary infiltrates characteristic of reactivated pulmonary TB.

Conventional radiography is generally a useful approach for
defining bone destruction, the extent of disease, and adjacent softtissue lesions.
MRI is more effective in identifying early disease;
it may help distinguish TB from other infections and neoplasm
and can aid in evaluating the extent of disease.
Scintiscans
with technetium and gallium may also be helpful in localizing
bone and soft-tissue lesions, but early false-negative findings are
not uncommon.
CT can be helpful in guiding diagnostic needle
biopsy. Fine-needle biopsy is an acceptable alternative to core-needle biopsy and open biopsy for the diagnosis of osteoarticular TB
in both the axial and peripheral skeleton, and it has the advantage
of obviating general anesthesia.
Both CT and MRI may also
be helpful in monitoring therapy.

### Culture

Nearly all species of
Mycobacteria
are slow growing, with
M. tuberculosis
the slowest. Other bacteria may rapidly outgrow mycobacteria if the specimen is not inoculated on special isolation media.
The small number of mycobacteria found in clinically infected
areas further challenges the ability to confirm mycobacterial infection and accounts for the low yield of positive Ziehl-Neelsen staining (10% to 20%) in synovial fluid and other biologic fluids.
Synovial fluid and other body fluids are less likely than tissue
to yield a positive culture. If a joint is suspected of harboring TB,
a synovial biopsy should be obtained. Arthroscopically derived tissue yields higher positive cultures than needle biopsies. CT-guided
needle aspiration and biopsy can provide invaluable information
in the case of spinal involvement. Characteristic features on tissue pathology—caseating or noncaseating granulomas ± acid-fast
bacilli on special stains—may allow an early presumptive diagnosis of TB pending culture results, which may take 4 to 6 weeks to
be finalized.

Polymerase Chain Reaction
Molecular diagnostics by using polymerase chain reaction (PCR)
have proven very helpful in the rapid diagnosis of pulmonary TB,
demonstrating a high yield even in patients with sputum smearnegative pulmonary TB, and are now endorsed for routine use by
the World Health Organization (WHO).
The role of molecular diagnostic assays in extrapulmonary TB is still being defined,
but preliminary experience has demonstrated promise. In a small
cohort of patients with vertebral osteomyelitis, multiplex PCR
testing of bone biopsy specimens demonstrated 90% sensitivity
and 100% specificity for TB infection, illustrating the potential
for this technology to provide rapid and accurate diagnosis. Similarly, multiplex real-time PCR successfully distinguished TB from
brucellosis as the cause of vertebral osteomyelitis, demonstrating
a sensitivity of 87% and specificity of 100% in the diagnosis of
TB.
Nucleic acid amplification using the GeneXpert MTB/
RIF system has been proposed as a sensitive and specific method
for identifying TB infection, although the cost and availability of
the assay may limit its widespread use, particularly in resourcelimited nations.
The WHO has been working to increase the
availability of this test by underwriting its costs in highly endemic
areas. The GeneXpert was evaluated in a series of 50 patients with
bone and joint TB (75% with spinal TB) and demonstrated a
sensitivity of 82% and specificity of 100%.
The assay correctly
identified 100% of MDR-TB isolates. Among 106 patients with
suspected spinal tuberculosis, the Xpert MTB/RIF was 87% sensitive relative to histopathology.
In a series of 67 patients with
suspected bone and joint tuberculosis, the Xpert MTB/RIF assay
demonstrated a sensitivity of 93% and specificity of 62% relative
to culture and sensitivity of 79% and specificity of 74% relative to
histopathology.
The positive rate of the Xpert MTB/RIF assay
was significantly higher for granulation tissue specimens than for
caseous necrotic tissue, sequestrum, and other necrotic connective
tissues.

## Treatment

Patients with positive TSTs or IGRAs and no evidence of active
tuberculous disease should be treated for LTBI. Regimens currently recommended for treatment of LTBI include 4 months of
daily rifampin, 3 months of weekly isoniazid plus rifapentine, 3
months of daily isoniazid plus rifampin or, alternatively, 6 to 9
months of daily isoniazid.
Appropriate management of tuberculous infections of bones and
joints is complex. Selection of appropriate anti-tuberculous agents
and ongoing disease monitoring should involve co-management
with an infectious disease consultant. Nonetheless, rheumatologists
should be familiar with the basic treatment principles.
Treatment of
M. tuberculosis
infections that involve the musculoskeletal system consist of the same combination chemotherapy
regimens that are effective in pulmonary TB. Treatment regimens
for infections of the bone and spine are typically administered for
longer durations than those recommended for lung disease and
other extrapulmonary sites. These longer treatment courses for
musculoskeletal disease have been recommended based on poor
tissue penetration of anti-tuberculous agents into osseous tissues
and the higher rates of relapse associated with these infections.
Current guidelines on the treatment of TB published jointly by
the U.S. Centers for Disease Control, American Thoracic Society,
and Infectious Diseases Society of America recommend a 6-month
course of therapy for all sites except the bone (6 to 9 months) and
the central nervous system (9 to 12 months).
Rifampin is the
critical component that allows shorter-course therapy.
The standard approach to TB therapy (both pulmonary and
extrapulmonary) in the United States currently includes four drugs
to start: isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol, and pyrazinamide—also
known as
IREZ
therapy. Once the isolate is confirmed to be sensitive to INH, the ethambutol can be discontinued. Pyrazinamide is
administered for 2 months, and rifampin and INH are continued
for the duration of therapy.
Longer courses of therapy are required for patients who are
slow to respond. Radiographic features of mycobacterial disease
may not change much after 6 months of treatment, and response
to treatment is based mainly on clinical features, including reduction in pain, resolution of constitutional symptoms, and emergence of increased mobility. Longer courses are also advised for
cases of relapse and resistant organisms.
Surgical intervention is seldom indicated for the initial management of osteoarticular TB. Possible exceptions at presentation
include patients with advanced or progressive disease or existing
or impending spinal deformity. MDR-TB, which refers to isolates that are resistant to isoniazid and rifampin, may also provide a relative indication for surgical debridement. Patients who
have extensive joint destruction and immobility after an adequate
course of chemotherapy may also be candidates for surgery.
After successful antibiotic therapy, arthroplasty of the hip and
knee may be undertaken and is usually successful. Recurrence of
disease in the prosthetic joint is less likely if the surgery is performed years after the infection, and the tissue obtained at surgery is culture negative. This may be impractical, however, when a

patient is unable to ambulate after an adequate course of therapy.
In such cases, the TB therapy is continued through surgery and for
at least 3 months post-operatively. If TB recurs in the prosthetic
joint, it can sometimes be managed with antibiotic therapy alone.
In some cases, however, removal of the prosthesis is necessary to
cure the infection.
The increasing incidence of drug-resistant TB complicates the
selection of appropriate drugs.
Primary monoresistance to isoniazid occurs in approximately 8% of TB isolates in the United
States. When identified, this does not substantially impact the
treatment outcome. The U.S. MDR-TB rate has been relatively
stable for the past decade, 1.6%.
Worldwide, 3.6% of new TB
cases were MDR-TB in 2021 and 18% in previously treated individuals. The treatment of MDR-TB is complex and often requires
the use of multiple (often toxic) second-line agents for 18 to 24
months or longer. XDR-TB—that is, strains resistant to all firstline TB drugs and at least three second-line agents—was reported
by 77 countries in 2016, with particularly high rates in India, the
Russian Federation, South Africa, Belarus, and Ukraine.
On
average, an estimated 8.5% of individuals with MDR-TB have
XDR-TB. Options for treatment of XDR-TB are very limited,
and the risk of treatment failure and death is high.
Incomplete adherence with treatment is a particularly important risk factor for secondary drug resistance, and the likelihood
of drug resistance increases following relapse after treatment. The
WHO guidelines for treatment of drug-resistant tuberculosis were
updated in 2021.

## Osteoarticular Infections Caused by Nontuberculous Mycobacteria

NTM are ubiquitous in the environment, including soil, water,
and animal reservoirs. They are not typically spread from human
to human, and infections with these organisms have been reported
with increasing frequency.
Although the majority of infections caused by these organisms are pulmonary, skin and softtissue infections may be seen in healthy individuals; a localized
lymphadenitis may develop in children.
Osteoarticular infections are usually caused by either direct inoculation of the organism or spread of contiguous infection.
Blunt trauma has been
implicated as a risk factor in cases of vertebral osteomyelitis due
to NTM.
NTM infections of the musculoskeletal system may
develop in immunosuppressed individuals, but these infections
are much less common than TB. In contrast to TB, nontuberculous infection is more likely to cause tenosynovitis, synovitis,
or osteomyelitis and is less likely to cause spinal infection. A
review identified only 31 cases of vertebral osteomyelitis due to
NTM reported between 1965 and 2003.
Although there are
more than 120 species of NTM, the majority of musculoskeletal
infections are caused by
Mycobacterium marinum
,
Mycobacterium
kansasii
, and
Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare
(also called
M.
avium
complex, or MAC), but cases of musculoskeletal infection with
Mycobacterium haemophilum
,
Mycobacterium chelonae
Mycobacterium xenopi,
and
Mycobacterium conceptionense
have also
occurred.

reported: tenosynovitis, synovitis, and osteomyelitis.

• Other causes of disease, such as TB and fungi, should be

## Emergence of Nontuberculous Mycobacterial Infections During the Treatment of Rheumatic Diseases

No published data exist on the risk of NTM infections in the
setting of therapy with nonbiologic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs, including methotrexate. The decision to interrupt
or continue therapy in such instances should be made on a caseby-case basis and should involve an infectious disease specialist,
recognizing that alternative treatments, such as steroids and biologics, may carry an even greater risk.
NTM are increasingly recognized as important pathogens in
patients receiving a TNF inhibitor. Indeed, these infections now
occur twice as frequently as TB in the United States, presumably because of the use of screening tests for the latter.
The
complexity of these infections was illustrated by a review of 239
cases of NTM infection reported to the MedWatch database, in
which only 105 were established to be probable or confirmed
according to established disease criteria.
The most commonly
reported presentation was in elderly women with RA, possibly
because both NTM infections and RA are more common in this
group; most patients were also taking concomitant steroids and/
or methotrexate. Infections with several different species within
this group have occurred, with MAC the most common. As
with TB, the risk appears to be associated with all drugs in the
class, rather than a specific agent, although more infections have
occurred with infliximab.
Extrapulmonary disease is a common finding, which is unusual for these organisms. A paradoxical response to therapy, similar to that seen with TB, has been
reported with treatment of MAC following infliximab.
Not all
patients with NTM lung disease will deteriorate on TNF inhibitor therapy, but some patients worsen despite administration of
anti-NTM therapy.
Nevertheless, cessation of TNF inhibitor
therapy should be strongly considered following the development
of an atypical mycobacterial infection. Continuation or reintroduction of TNF inhibitors during treatment of NTM infections
has occurred.

Managing the risk of NTM infections in the setting of TNF
inhibitor treatment can be challenging. Although screening has
been helpful in reducing the risk of TB infection in this setting,
there is no currently available method of screening for atypical
mycobacteria. Because these infections are more common in individuals with pre-existing pulmonary disease, clinicians may wish
to consider more extensive evaluation of RA patients with bronchiectasis about to initiate TNF inhibitor therapy. Although MAC
prophylaxis is recommended for HIV-infected patients with CD4
counts of 50 or less, there is no experience with, and no guidelines
for, such prophylaxis with TNF inhibitor therapy. The complex
nature of these infections has been further demonstrated by a case
of
M. marinum
mimicking RA, with synovitis and subcutaneous
nodules developing during infliximab therapy.
