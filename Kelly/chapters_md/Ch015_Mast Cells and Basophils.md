## Introduction

Mast cells and basophils are hematopoietic lineages best known for
their roles in IgE-associated allergic reactions but are also thought
to have a variety of other functions. Basophils, the least frequent
of the three granulocytes (<1% of blood leukocytes in humans and
mice), ordinarily do not reside in tissues but circulate as mature
cells that can migrate into tissues at sites of immunologic or
inflammatory responses.
In contrast, mast cell progenitors arise
in hematopoietic tissues and then migrate into and complete their
maturation in most vascularized tissues.
Mast cells normally
reside in connective tissue, being particularly abundant beneath
epithelial surfaces, around blood vessels and nerves, and, in some
species, in serous cavities.
Blood basophils have a short life span of
a few days, but mast cells can remain for long periods in the same
tissue locations. Mast cell and basophil numbers are increased at
sites of parasite infection.
Increased numbers of mast cells and/
or basophils have also been reported in certain chronic allergic
diseases or other forms of pathology, although their functions in
such settings largely remain uncertain.
Human mast cells and basophils were described in the late 19th
century by Paul Ehrlich, who appreciated their morphologic distinction.
The relationship between mast cells and basophils has
been studied extensively; however, there is no convincing evidence
that mature basophils can give rise to mast cells (or vice versa),
and they are best regarded as distinct hematopoietic lineages with
overlapping functions.
The development in mice of genetic
approaches that enable deletion of mast cells and/or basophils, or
manipulation of expression of their products, have greatly facilitated our ability to study these cells in mice.
However, many of
the findings in mice have not been explored in humans.
Mast cells and basophils can contribute importantly to the
pathology associated with IgE-mediated allergic disorders. However, evidence suggests that they can also participate in protective
host responses associated with IgE production, especially those
directed against parasites, as well as in hosting defenses in innate
In mice, mast
immune responses to certain pathogens.
cells can also enhance innate, as well as acquired IgE-dependent,
defenses against animal venoms.
Mast cells and basophils can
participate in many other biologic processes, with or without
involvement of IgE, and can exhibit positive and/or negative effector or immunoregulatory functions via production of cytokines/
mediators and other mechanisms. Mast cells are normally present
in synovia, and their numbers (and evidence of released mast cell
mediators) can increase during synovial inflammation.
Mast
cells also have been proposed to participate in a variety of autoimmune conditions, including bullous pemphigoid, multiple sclerosis, and systemic sclerosis.

## Mast Cells and Basophils in Ancient Innate Immunity

Mast cells and basophils have been conserved in many animal species during evolution,
and primitive mast cells and basophils
are present in organisms that appeared over 500 million years
ago, before development of immunoglobulins.
These ancient
mast cell-/basophil-like cells primarily have been studied in urochordates. The “test cells” that surround the developing oocytes in
the ascidian
Styela plicata
and in the sea squirt
Ciona intestinalis
probably represent ancient counterparts of mammalian mast
cells. These cells degranulate and release heparin, histamine, and
tryptase in response to compound 48/80,
which also elicits
degranulation of rodent and human mast cells.
Basophil-like
granulocytes containing heparin and histamine have also been
found in the hemolymph of
S. plicata
.
In mammals, mast cells
and basophils have acquired the ability to bind IgE to their surface
with high affinity.
The first description of the mast cell was reported in 1863
by Friedrich von Recklinghausen, who described them as highly
granulated cells in the frog mesentery. Many subsequent studies
have investigated the participation of mast cells and, to a lesser

extent, basophils, in inflammation and other pathology in nonmammalian vertebrates.
Mast cells or mast cell–like cells have
been identified in many nonmammalian species, including fish,
amphibians, reptiles, and birds. Like their mammalian counterparts, such nonmammalian mast cells contain heparin, histamine, proteases, and neuropeptides.
Mast cells in bony fishes
can also produce anti-microbial peptides, suggesting a role in host
defense.
While there are fewer studies of basophils in lower vertebrates, basophils also exist in many animal species, including
teleost fish and reptiles.
Snapping turtles have large numbers of
basophils (>50% of blood leukocytes), which can degranulate and
release histamine after treatments with rabbit anti-turtle serum.

## Distinguishing Features of Mast Cells and Basophils

### Mast Cells

Morphology
Ehrlich described distinctive histochemical staining characteristics
of human mast cells and basophils in the late 19th century.
Mast
cells are poorly stained by routine histologic methods but can be
identified by basic dyes (e.g., toluidine blue) binding to granuleassociated sulfated proteoglycans (chondroitin sulfates and heparin), revealing
metachromasia
(a shift in the color of the dye from
blue to purple). Excellent visualization of mast cells is achieved
in 1-
μ
m sections or by electron microscopy (
Fig. 15.1
). In tissue
sections, mast cells typically are either round or spindle shaped,
ranging from 7 to 20
μ
m in diameter, usually with a round, nonsegmented nucleus with moderate condensation of nuclear chromatin, and contain prominent cytoplasmic granules. Mast cell
granules are smaller, more numerous, and generally more variable
in appearance than in basophils and contain scroll-like structures,
particles, and crystals, alone or in combination. In contrast to the
irregularly spaced blunt surface projections of basophils, mast cells
are typically covered by more uniformly distributed thin surface
processes. Mast cells also differ from basophils in having many
more cytoplasmic filaments and lacking cytoplasmic glycogen
deposits. Human mast cells can contain numerous cytoplasmic
lipid bodies (
Figs. 15.1 and 15.2
). Tissue mast cells can be localized by immunohistochemical staining of cell-associated tryptase
or chymase.
Natural History and Regulation of Development
Mature mast cells reside near or within smooth muscle and
mucus-producing glands and are particularly abundant near
blood vessels and nerves and in connective tissues beneath surfaces exposed to the external environment (e.g., the skin, gastrointestinal and urogenital tracts, and respiratory system), but mast
cells are also found in synovial tissue.
Some species, including
murine rodents, have mast cells within mesothelial-lined cavities.
They can be long-lived in tissues, and at least some mast cells can
migrate and proliferate in the tissues during a variety of inflammatory or reparative processes.
Tissue mast cells increase by
recruitment and differentiation/maturation of immature precursors and/or by local expansion of resident mast cells.
Mast cells in lower vertebrates are derived from lymphatic
organs, such as the thymus, and other hematopoietic tissues.
In mammals, except for a minor mast cell population in the
bone marrow, mast cell precursors first circulate before acquiring
more mature characteristics in peripheral tissues.
In mice,
some adult mast cells are derived from progenitors that arise in
the yolk-sac and other hematopoietic tissues early in development.
In agreement with the report in 1983 that mouse yolksac also contains mast cell progenitors,
data from recent studies
confirmed that most of the mast cells in mouse connective tissue
originate from erythromyeloid progenitors in the yolk-sac and
that some connective tissue mast cells can self-renew without the
involvement of the bone marrow.
On the other hand, mouse
mucosal mast cells originate from fetal hematopoietic stem cells
and their replacement later depends on adult hematopoietic stem
cells.
These studies suggest that mast cell numbers in connective
tissue are maintained by expansion of local progenitors independent of new input from bone marrow progenitors, and bone
marrow progenitors give rise to mucosal mast cells and, under
various forms of tissue perturbance, can also contribute to mast
cell expansion in connective tissues.
The extent to which
this applies to other mammals, including humans, is not yet fully
elucidated. Human mast cells can be generated from CD34
progenitors in the bone marrow, peripheral blood, and fetal liver and
cord blood, but the differentiation pathways of human mast cells
in vivo are not fully defined. Nevertheless, recent detailed analyses
of mouse
and human
mast cell transcriptomes and human
mast cell proteomes
has shown striking phenotypic diversity of
mast cells derived from different tissues.
Effects of Stem Cell Factor, Interleukin-3, and Other
Cytokines
Studies in mice, nonhuman primates, and humans indicate that
many aspects of mast cell development are critically regulated
by stem cell factor (SCF), the ligand for the KIT tyrosine kinase
receptor.
SCF is produced in biologically active membraneassociated and soluble forms.
Besides promoting the migration,
survival, proliferation, and maturation of cells in the mast cell lineage, SCF can directly promote and, at even lower concentrations,
augment IgE-dependent, mast cell mediator release.
KIT abnormalities (particularly gain-of-function mutations) are involved in
the pathogenesis of many types of mastocytosis,
whereas changes
in the production of SCF by fibroblasts and other cells may contribute to the alterations in mast cell numbers occurring during
many pathologic responses.
Other cytokines that can influence mast cell proliferation, differentiation, maturation, and survival in humans and
mice include interleukin (IL)-3 through 6, IL-9, and IL-10.
Transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
inhibits mast cell proliferation
and promotes mast cell apoptosis
in mice. Nerve
growth factor induces increased numbers of tissue mast cells in
neonatal rats
and in frog tadpoles.
In mice, mast cells can
be generated in culture medium containing IL-3 or SCF or
SCF plus IL-3. The phenotypic characteristics and function of
mast cells are thought to be profoundly influenced by the cytokines/growth factors present in their microenvironment. SCF
is regarded as the most critical growth factor for mast cell lineages in mice and humans, but, under certain settings, human
mast cells can develop in IL-3 and IL-6 containing medium
without SCF.
STAT5, GATA1, GATA2, FOG-1 (friend of
GATA1) and MITF (microphthalmia-associated transcription
factor) are the main transcription factors known to regulate
mast cell differentiation.

by proresolving lipid mediators, including lipoxins, resolvins, protectins and maresins.
For example, the lipid derivatives, resolvin

Sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) is a sphingolipid metabolite
that can be produced by mast cells and many other cell types
and that can regulate mast cell function. Mast cells express at
least two types of receptors for S1P, SIPR1 and S1PR2.
Fc

S1P plays an important role mediating mast

Chronic exposure to elevated S1P from a nonmast

mast cells.
Other Regulators of Mast Cell Function
Many endogenous peptides (VIP,
substance P,
endothelin-1
) and cytokines (IL-1, IL-3, IL-33, SCF, IFN
α
2, and IFN
γ
)
can trigger mast cell degranulation and/or cytokine release. Exposure of mast cells to these cytokines synergizes with stimulation of
other receptors, including Fc
ε
RI.
Mast cells can also be directly
activated by components of reptile or insect venoms,
and
mast cell functions can be modulated by diverse receptors, including the aryl hydrocarbon receptor
and the vitamin D receptor.
Mouse studies suggest that mast cell functions (including those
stimulated by IgE) can be influenced by circadian rhythm.
Because extensive mast cell degranulation can have catastrophic
consequences, such as fatal anaphylaxis, it is critical that mast cell
function be tightly regulated. There are a few inhibitory mechanisms identified that can downregulate mast cell activation.
The
CD300 and tetraspanin (CD9, CD63, CD81, CD151) receptor
families can positively or negatively regulate mast cell function,
depending on the setting.
Stimulation of corticotropin-releasing
factor receptors of subtype 1
and subtype 2
promotes and
inhibits IgE-mediated mast cell activation, respectively.
Siglec-7 and Siglec-8 inhibitory receptors are expressed by
mast cells and basophils. Siglec-7 inhibits IgE-mediated mast cell
activation but only moderately affects IgE-mediated activation in
primary human basophils.
Siglec-8 is expressed by human eosinophils and mast cells but weakly by basophils. Engaging Siglec-8
results in apoptosis of human eosinophils and inhibition of release
of pre-formed and newly generated mediators from human mast
cells, but has no effect on their survival.
Recruitment and ligation of Siglec-3 (CD33) suppresses IgE/Fc
ε
RI signaling in human
mast cells and dampens IgE-mediated anaphylaxis in transgenic
mice expressing human CD33.
Human mast cells and basophils
also express Siglec-6. Cytokine exposure and IgE receptor crosslinking can alter Siglec-6 expression in these cells.
Human mast cell activation is suppressed by the inhibitory
cytokine IL-10.
TGFβ
decreases Fc
ε
RI expression in mouse
and human mast cells
and reduces KIT in human skin-derived
mast cells.
TGFβ
inhibits Fc
ε
RI-dependent degranulation and
cytokine production
and IL-33-mediated cytokine release in
human mast cells.
In mice, CD4
CD25
T regulatory cells suppress mast cell degranulation in a cell-contact-dependent manner
via OX40-OX40L interactions between Treg cells and mast cells.
There are other intrinsic mechanisms that can suppress mast cell
activation (e.g., those mediated by RabGEF1
and SHIP
).
Mast cell differentiation and IgE-mediated activation can also be
inhibited by proton pump inhibitors.
Endocannabinoids are endogenous bioactive lipids that have
anti-inflammatory activities. Cellular receptors for endocannabinoids include type-1 (CB1) and type-2 (CB2) G protein–coupled
cannabinoid receptors, G protein–coupled receptor GPR55,
transient receptor potential channel of the vanilloid subfamily
1 (TRPV1), and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor
γ
(PPAR
γ
).
Mast cells express cannabinoid receptors and respond
to endocannabinoids, which inhibit mast cell maturation
and
mediator secretion.
Endocannabinoid anandamide–dependent inhibition of Fc
ε
RI-induced mouse mast cell degranulation
is mediated by a mechanism involving CB2 and GPR55 receptor
heteromers.
Products and Mediators
The cytoplasmic granules of mast cells contain proteoglycans,
consisting of sulfated glycosaminoglycans covalently linked to a
protein core,
which stain metachromatically with basic dyes.
In humans and murine species, individual mast cell populations
can contain variable mixtures of heparin and chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans.
Although the biologic functions of mast
cell proteoglycans are not fully understood, in mice, heparin is
required for normal packaging of certain neutral proteases in mast
cell cytoplasmic granules.
Activated mast cells expose negatively
charged proteoglycans on their cell surface, allowing binding of
the positively charged fluorescent tag avidin, which is being used
to track mast cell activation in vitro and in vivo.
Human and mouse mast cells synthesize and store histamine.
Although macrophages, neutrophils, and platelets, as well as basophils,
can produce histamine, mast cells represent the source of
virtually all the histamine stored in normal tissues in mice, with
the notable exceptions of the glandular stomach and parts of the
central nervous system. Murine and at least some human mast
cells can also store serotonin in their granules.
Upon activation, mast cells release rapidly and/or secrete de
novo a wide spectrum of biologically active mediators, many of
which can also be produced by basophils and other cells (
Table
15.1
). During IgE-dependent mast cell degranulation, mast cells
release into the extra-cellular space mediators pre-stored in their
cytoplasmic granules; for example, vasoactive amines (histamine
and serotonin), neutral proteases (tryptases, chymases, and carboxypeptidase A3 [CPA3]), proteoglycans (e.g., heparin), and
some cytokines and growth factors (e.g., TNF). A second wave
of secreted products is generated by de novo synthesis of proinflammatory lipid mediators, such as prostaglandins and leukotrienes. Finally, activated mast cells also synthesize and secrete a
long list of growth factors, cytokines, and chemokines (e.g., IL-1
through 6, IL-9 through 11, IL-13, IL-16, IL-17A, IL-18, IL-22,
IL-25, and TNF, vascular endothelial growth factor [VEGF],
TGFβ

### Basophils

Morphology
As with mast cells, routine histologic methods are poorly suited for
demonstrating basophils. Optimal visualization is best achieved
by electron microscopy (
Fig. 15.1A, 15.1C
). By ultrastructure,
human basophils are 5 to 7
μ
m in diameter, have a segmented
or unsegmented nucleus with marked condensation of nuclear
chromatin, and contain round or oval cytoplasmic granules surrounded by a membrane and containing a substructure of dense
particles, less dense matrix, and, in some granules, membrane
whorls and Charcot-Leyden crystals (
Fig. 15.1A, 15.1C
). A minor
population of small, uniform granules is characteristically located
near the nucleus. The cytoplasm of mature human basophils also
contains glycogen particles, mitochondria, free ribosomes, and
small membrane-bound vesicles, but lipid bodies are rare. The
basophil’s prominent metachromatic cytoplasmic granules allow
unmistakable identification in Wright-Giemsa-stained films of
blood (
Fig. 15.4
) or bone marrow. Mouse basophils in tissue can
be localized by the immunohistochemical staining of mMCP8, a
protease largely restricted to basophils.
Natural History and Regulation of Development
Basophils, the least common granulocyte in human blood (
∼
0.5
to 0.6% of total leukocytes and
∼
0.3% of nucleated marrow
cells), have a short life span of a few days and retain granulocytic features even after emigrating into tissues. Like other
granulocytes, basophils appear to lack the ability to proliferate and are eliminated by apoptosis after participating in tissue
responses. The differentiation trajectory of basophils, and its
relationship with that of other hematopoietic cell lineages, have
not been fully delineated. However, much evidence indicates
that basophils share a common precursor with other granulocytes and monocytes.
Several transcription factors—STAT5,
P1-RUNX1, GATA1, GATA2, and C/EBP
α
—have been implicated in basophil differentiation,
whereas C/EBP
α
and MITF
act antagonistically to determine basophil versus mast cell lineage development.
IL-3 is a major growth factor for basophils, promoting production and survival of human and mouse basophils in vitro and
inducing basophilia in mice.
IL-3 is also important for the
bone marrow and blood basophilia associated with the Th2-associated immunologic responses induced by infection with certain
parasites (e.g.,
Nippostrongylus brasiliensis
or
Strongyloides venezuelensis
).
Thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP) is another
cytokine that can elicit basophilia associated with
Trichinella spiralis
infection
and can induce dermal basophil infiltration in atopic
dermatitis-like skin lesions in mice.
However, findings in
IL-3−/−
and TSLPR−/−
mice indicate that neither IL-3 nor
TSLP is essential for the baseline development of normal numbers
of bone marrow or blood basophils, suggesting the involvement of
additional growth factor(s) in basophil development.
Increased basophils are noted in settings with high IgE levels
in humans and mice, including in germ-free mice
and in mice
treated with antibiotics.
In humans, increased frequencies of
circulating basophils are detected in immunodeficient allergic
subjects who exhibit high IgE levels.
IgE also can regulate basophil Fc
ε
RI levels in wild-type mice.
Positive and Negative Regulation of Function
Basophils are activated by a variety of stimuli, including immunoglobulins, complement components, cytokines/growth factors,
products of pathogens, pathogen- and allergen-derived proteases,
and so on. Similar to mast cells, the best studied pathway for
basophil activation is via crosslinking of Fc
ε
RI by the binding
of antigens to antigen-specific IgE, which triggers degranulation
(
Fig. 15.5

LTC

intra-cellular membrane events and fusion of granules with the
cell membrane, leading to elevated expression of activation markers (e.g., CD11b, CD13, CD63, CD107a, CD203c, CD200R,
and CD300a) on their surface. Because basophils are readily
accessible in the peripheral blood, and given the rapid upregulation of activation markers following Fc
ε
RI crosslinking, basophil
activation tests (BATs) were developed in the early 1990s as noninvasive tools for assessing allergy severity and for monitoring the
efficacy of allergen-specific immunotherapy.
Recent efforts
are attempting to improve the technology for BATs so that they
can be performed much more rapidly and with smaller quantities
of blood.

Products and Mediators
Basophils are the source of most (if not all) histamine present in
normal human blood.
The sulfated glycosaminoglycans of normal human blood basophils have not yet been characterized. However, two studies (of five myeloid leukemia patients with 10–75%
basophils) showed that basophils may produce solely chondroitin sulfates
or a mixture of chondroitin sulfates (50–84%) and
heparin (8–43%).
Basophils generate many products that can influence the course
of inflammation. These substances are either pre-formed, granuleassociated (e.g., histamine, neutral proteases, proteoglycans) or
produced during cell activation (e.g., LTC

appears to be more limited than mast cells but includes IL-3, IL-4,
IL-13, IL-25, IL-31, VEGF; certain chemokines; and, at least in
mice, IL-6, TNF, and TSLP.
Human basophils make and are

proteases), which can be associated with helminth infection or
allergen (HDM) exposure.

## IgE-Dependent Upregulation of Fc ε RI Expression and Fc ε RI-Dependent Function

As plasma levels of IgE increase (e.g., in subjects with allergic
diseases or parasite infections), levels of Fc
ε
RI expression on the
surface of basophils and mast cells also increase.
Compared
with cells with lower “baseline” levels of Fc
ε
RI expression, such
mast cells can bind more IgE, release mediators in response to
lower concentrations of allergens, and produce significantly larger
amounts of pre-formed and lipid mediators and cytokines.

## Mast Cell and Basophil Heterogeneity

Variation in the morphology, biochemistry, and/or function of
mast cells from different anatomic locations or from the same
organ or site has been reported in several mammalian species,
including humans.
Two subtypes of mast cells named
connective tissue mast cells
or
mucosal mast cells
were described in
rodents based on their anatomic location, metachromatic staining characteristics, and cytoplasmic granule proteoglycan content.
In humans, mast cell subtypes have been demonstrated
based on their content of tryptase (MC
) or tryptase and chymase
(MC
).
However, a recent study shows that, based on the
products expressed at the mRNA level in single mast cells, at least
six “subtypes” of human mast cells can be identified.
The phenomenon of identifying distinct “varieties” of mast
cells, often referred to as
mast cell heterogeneity
, raises the possibility that mast cells of different phenotype express different
functions in health or disease and may exhibit different sensitivities to pharmacologic manipulation. At least four mechanisms
may account for phenotypic variation in mast cell populations:
(1) factors promoting branching within the mast cell lineage,
(2) factors influencing differentiation/maturation (within single
pathways or if they occur within multiple pathways), (3) factors
modulating mast cell function, and (4) factors influencing local
concentrations of exogenous substances not derived from mast
cells but taken up and stored in mast cell granules. Of these four
mechanisms, experimental evidence has been obtained for all but
the first.
Human basophils can exhibit some variation in phenotypic
characteristics also, such as immunoreactivity for tryptase, chymase, and carboxypeptidase A
or levels of expression of surface
structures, including human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-DR, CD32
(Fc
γ
RII), and receptors for cytokines. Such variation may reflect
individual differences among different subjects and/or the effects
of disease processes
or the consequences of immunotherapy.
Basophil heterogeneity has also been noted in mice.

## Genetic Approaches for Analyzing Mast Cell and Basophil Function

Before the availability of genetically mast cell- and/or basophildeficient mice, speculation about the roles of mast cells and basophils was based on more indirect lines of evidence, such as assessing
changes in the numbers of mast cells or basophils at sites of pathology (
Tables 15.2 and 15.3
) or by pharmacologic approaches nonspecifically targeting mast cell or basophil mediators. Studies of
basophil function in guinea pigs
and mice
have employed
antibodies to deplete basophils, and such approaches have also
been used to deplete mast cells in mice
; however, the antibodies
used may also influence other cell types. Other approaches have
employed pharmacologic inhibitors to probe mast cell functions,
but none of these drugs appear to target mast cells specifically. For
example, cromolyn has been widely used as “mast cell stabilizer”
to investigate biologic functions of mast cells in vivo, but there is
evidence that cromolyn does not selectively and effectively block
mast cell activation, at least in mice.
Various mutant or transgenic mice that exhibit constitutive or
inducible depletion of some or all mast cell populations, as well
as mast cell–deficient mice that have been selectively engrafted
with wild type or genetically altered mast cells (so-called “mast cell
knock-in mice”), have been used in efforts to define and quantify the contributions of mast cells to many different biologic
responses.
Additional transgenic mice that lack certain mast cell–
associated products (either globally or in selected cell populations)
have been generated to interrogate the contribution of mast cell–
specific products in biologic or inflammatory responses.
The contributions of mast cells and mast cell–associated products can be further investigated by a combination of genetic and
in vivo imaging approaches in different disease models.
For
example, a fluorescent imaging approach that enables the selective
labeling and tracking of mast cell secretory granules by real-time
intravital two-photon microscopy in living mice, and thereby permits the identification of such mast cells as a potential source of
cytokines, has been used to sort out the controversial roles of mast
cells in contact hypersensitivity reactions in mice.
Genetically engineered mice that exhibit constitutive or inducible depletion of basophils or basophil-associated products are available for
the investigation of basophil functions in vivo.
As has been extensively reviewed elsewhere,
the availability of such models has the potential to advance substantially our
understanding of the roles of mast cells and basophils in health

mast cell-derived exosomes are highly potent in inducing maturation (e.g., CD40 and CD80 expression) and functional activation
(secretion of IL12p70) of DCs and can potentiate the efficiency of
antigen presentation by DCs.
In addition, mouse mast cells can
form “immunologic synapses” with immature DCs to transfer internalized mast cell-associated antigens to DCs; the transferred antigen is then processed and presented by DCs to activate T cells.
Conversely, mouse mast cells can acquire major histocompatibility

complex class II (MHCII) proteins from DCs via direct synapse-like
cell–cell contacts.
Furthermore, mouse mast cells can enhance
antigen presentation indirectly by internalizing antigen bound to
Fc
ε
RI-associated IgE; this mechanism is independent of mast-cell
MHCII expression but requires that such mast cells undergo apoptosis and then are phagocytosed by other antigen-presenting cells.
Mast cells are normally anchored in tissues, but mouse mast
cells can migrate to lymph nodes upon activation.
Close
contacts of mast cells and lymphocytes can also be detected in
peripheral tissues at sites of inflammation in mice and humans.
Mouse mast cells may also influence T cell activation by their
release of exosomes, which can induce lymphocyte proliferation
and IL-2 and IFN
γ
production in vitro.
Also, mast cells can
remotely influence pathology at distant sites, in part via release of
their granule contents.
As a key driver of Th2 immunity in allergy and parasite infection, IL-4 is arguably one of the most important immunomodulatory molecules produced by basophils.
They produce IL-4
by IgE-dependent and independent mechanisms. TLR ligands,
cytokines (e.g., IL-3, IL-18, IL-33, TSLP), pathogen-derived
molecules, and proteases associated with allergens (papain, house
dust mite) and parasites, activate basophils to produce IL-4 with
or without concomitant Fc
ε
RI crosslinking.
Immunoregulatory effects of basophil-derived IL-4 potentially influence DCs,
T cells, B cells, monocytes/macrophage, innate lymphoid cells
(ILCs), and structural cells.
For example, basophil-derived IL-4
can be detrimental in the pathogenesis of humoral autoimmunity
by contributing to autoantibody production in mice susceptible
to systemic lupus erythematosus–like disease.
Basophils also
can contribute to humoral responses by their production of B cell
activating factor (BAFF) after anti-IgD crosslinking of IgD
or
TLR activation.
Activation of TLRs can enhance production
of BAFF and IL-13 in basophils, inducing production of large
amounts of IgG4 by B cells.
In humans, basophil IL-4 and IL-13 can potentiate monocyte responses to IL-3 and the production of CCL17, promoting
alternative differentiation of monocytes.
Human basophil IL-4
also can upregulate Fc
γ
RIIB and IL-10 production by monocytes,
whereas basophil-specific histamine can inhibit LPS-induced production of the pro-inflammatory cytokine TNF and upregulation
of the costimulatory molecule CD80 in monocytes.
Several reports suggest that mouse basophils can perform antigen
presentation and promote Th2 cell differentiation via their expression of MHCII and costimulatory molecules, CD40, CD80, and
CD86.
Whether human basophils can perform antigen presentation, either by their own HLA-DR expression or by a cell-contact-dependent process called
trogocytosis
(during which they acquire
peptide-MHCII complexes from DCs),
remains to be determined.
In summary, the immunoregulatory functions of mast cells and
basophils have the potential to influence inflammation, hemostasis, tissue remodeling, cancer, metabolism, reproduction, behavior, sleep, and many other biologic responses.

### Roles in Host Defense in Innate and Adaptive Immune Responses

Studies in humans
and in experimental animals (including
those employing mice genetically deficient in mast cells, basophils or IgE) (reviewed in refs. 1,10,11) strongly suggest that one

beneficial role of IgE, mast cells and basophils is to help defend
the host against ectoparasites such as ticks and to diminish the
numbers of parasites and burden of disease in mammals infected
with certain helminths. However, besides parasites, vertebrates
also have been subjected to evolutionary pressure through millions of years of coevolution with venomous arthropods, reptiles,
and other species. Moreover, there is now evidence that mast
cells
and IgE-associated Th2 immunity
can
enhance host defenses against animal venoms (
Table 15.4
).
In contrast to circulating basophils, mast cells are long-lived
tissue-dwelling cells, which permits them to participate in different phases of the immune responses, including resolution of
inflammation and tissue remodeling. During infections, appropriate activation of mast cells and basophils can be beneficial and
can enhance immunity and host defense. However, there is also
evidence that excessive mast cell and basophil activation after
infection can result in detrimental tissue damage. Potential protective versus pathologic roles of mast cells in various bacterial,
viral,
and fungal
infections have been reviewed elsewhere.
Thus mast cells and basophils may have complex roles in innate
immune responses, with some actions promoting host defense and
survival and others enhancing the pathology associated with their
activation.
In contrast to parasites, bacteria, and viruses, most allergens
do not pose a direct threat to the nonsensitized host. This is why
type 2 immune responses associated with allergen exposures are
widely considered to be “misdirected” or “maladaptive” immune
responses. However, in 1974, IgE-dependent “allergic” responses
to insect bites and stings were noted to confer immediate advantages to hosts, causing them to flee the attacks of such creatures.
In 1991 a broader concept was proposed, namely that acute allergic reactions (e.g., sneezing, coughing, vomiting, and diarrhea)
evolved as protective mechanisms allowing the sensitized host to
respond rapidly to and to expel, neutralize, and/or avoid diverse
noxious substances that might be potentially life-threatening.
This “toxin hypothesis” has recently been tested, and supported,
by experiments in mice sensitized and challenged with animal
venoms.
Specifically, work in mice deficient in mast cells, IgE,
or components of Fc
ε
RI indicates that mast cells can contribute
to the IgE antibody- and Fc
ε
RI-dependent enhanced resistance
to challenge with potentially lethal amounts of honeybee
or
Daboia russelii
(Russell’s viper)
venom that is observed after an
initial exposure to a sublethal amount of that venom.
Work in mast cell–deficient, mast cell knock-in, and mast cell
protease–deficient mice indicates that mast cells also can enhance
the innate resistance of mice to diverse animal venoms and/
or their toxic components. These include the whole venoms of
four species of poisonous snakes (the Israeli mole viper
[Atractaspis engaddensis]
, two North American pit vipers, the western
diamondback rattlesnake
[Crotalus atrox]
and the southern copperhead
[Agkistrodon contortrix contortrix]
,
and Russell’s viper
[Daboia russelii]

the Gila monster,
and
two species of scorpions,
as well as two individual venom toxins, sarafotoxin 6b (the major toxin in Israeli mole viper snake
venom

Notably, CPA3 or mMCP4 (the mouse functional counterpart
to human chymase) appeared to account for much or all of the
protective effects of mast cells against various venoms.
CPA3 and mMCP4 can degrade both endogenous biologically
active peptides (endothelin-1 [ET-1]
and vasoactive intestinal peptide [VIP],
respectively) and similar peptides present
in animal venoms (sarafotoxin 6b and helodermin, respectively),
which are thought to act in mammals via the same receptors that
bind the similar endogenous peptides.
Indeed, one of the reasons that mast cells of various species
contain several different proteases in their cytoplasmic granules
may be that these cells, positioned in large numbers in the most
common site of envenomation, the skin, are equipped to release
a panel of proteases with the potential to degrade a variety of
structurally distinct toxic compounds contained in animal venoms. However, there may be species variation in which proteases
are important. For example, a study employing embryonic zebra
fish to screen for venom toxicity showed that human mast cell
tryptase, rather than human mast cell chymase or CPA3, is more
capable of degrading several different snake venoms.

Increases in the numbers and/or the activation of mast cells and
basophils at affected tissues have been reported in a variety of

these autoimmune disorders.

## Conclusion

Mammalian mast cells and basophils share several notable biochemical and functional characteristics, but they are not identical.
In humans, basophils are the least frequent of the three granulocytes, typically circulate as mature cells, and can be recruited
into tissues at sites of immunologic or inflammatory responses. In
contrast, mast cells are derived from yolk-sac or blood precursors
and complete their maturation and, in some cases, survive for long
periods of time in virtually all tissues. Mast cell expansion can
occur by recruitment and local maturation of blood precursors
and by proliferation of resident mast cells.
Both cell types can be triggered to release potent mediators
in response to activation via Fc
ε
RI (e.g., when their cell-bound
IgE recognizes bivalent or multivalent allergens), and both are
regarded as important effector cells in allergic disorders. However,
several lines of evidence indicate that mast cells and basophils also
can contribute to protective host responses associated with IgE
production, especially those directed against certain parasites.
Many systemic disorders have been associated with changes in
numbers of blood basophils, and many pathologic processes, including various types of arthritis, can be associated with changes in the
numbers of tissue mast cells. However, the exact nonredundant functions of mast cells and basophils in these conditions largely remain to
be determined. Finally, patients with primary deficiencies in basophils
are exceedingly rare (if they exist at all), and patients with a primary
deficiency of tissue mast cells have not yet been reported.
