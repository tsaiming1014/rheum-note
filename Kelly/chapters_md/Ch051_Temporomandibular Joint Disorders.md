> Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) clicking generally does not need

> treatment.

> In the general population, 3.6% to 7% of people have TMDs severe

> enough that they seek treatment.

> Management of TMDs should include the identification of contributing

## Introduction

This chapter describes the entities included in the current classification system for temporomandibular disorders (TMDs).
Although pain is the most common reason patients seek professional care for TMDs, some entities such as idiopathic condylar
resorption may be pain free but have significant functional effects
that provoke a visit to the dentist.
Chronically painful TMDs are prevalent and debilitating,
afflicting greater than 5% of Americans
with prevalence higher
for females than males
and estimated annual costs of $US 4 billion.
This condition is costly because, to date, therapies are not
based on sound scientific evidence and do not predictably prevent
or cure conditions. A “complex web of causation” is implicated.
As noted in a 2020 consensus report,
clinical studies that include
assessment of psychosocial, physiologic, behavioral, and biomechanical mechanisms have so far not been measured collectively in
individuals with and without chronic TMD pain.
With respect to the temporomandibular joint (TMJ), osseous changes to the mandibular condyle are late-stage characteristics of osteoarthritis
that are found in the TMJs of 20- to
30-year-olds. This is over a decade earlier than in other human
post-cranial joints like the hip and knee.
Although TMJ disk
displacement leads to osteoarthritis in animal models
and in
some humans,
most cases of TMJ disk displacement, based on
imaging characterization, remain stable or improve over time.
Why TMJ tissues prematurely fail compared with post-cranial
joints and why patient-reported pain is not correlated with imaging data are not understood, but most likely the pain phenomenology goes beyond structural changes.
As noted in the National Academy consensus report, osteoarthritis of the TMJ is believed to be a consequence of chronic
abnormal mechanical joint loading. However, the magnitude
and frequency of mechanical work required to cause degenerative
changes in the TMJ are unknown. By definition, for the same joint
load magnitudes, loading areas of smaller compared with larger
sizes result in higher stresses. Smaller mandibular condylar volumes in females compared with males have been documented,
but the effects of these size differences are so far unknown. Specific TMJ mechanics and jaw-use behaviors that result in high
magnitudes and frequencies of energy input to a given volume
of cartilage are likely to promote fatigue of the TMJ articulating
surfaces.
Therefore research in TMJ osteoarthritis is needed to
identify important “mechanobehavioral” variables. That is, variables that have the predictive value to identify individuals at risk
for precocious development of degenerative changes in the soft
and hard tissues of the TMJ versus those who are likely to improve
or remain stable with time, thus establishing a mechanobehavior
axis.
Diagnoses of TMDs require consideration of the potential
sources of signs and symptoms that are similar to TMDs (see
Tables 51.1 and 51.2
) with respect to pain and limitation of mandibular movement. Because of the proximity of other structures,
pathologies involving these structures can be confused with conditions arising from the TMJ and/or muscles of mastication. As a
simplified means of organization,
Table 51.3
lists the conditions
associated with TMDs that specifically involve the TMJ. More
detailed descriptions of these entities are provided below.

### Acquired Anomalies

Mandibular Condylar Hypoplasia or Hyperplasia
In children mandibular condylar hypoplasia or hyperplasia can
manifest as progressive undergrowth or overgrowth.
Differential diagnosis for ipsilateral hyperplasia versus contralateral TMJ
hypoplasia has been based on radioisotope imaging,
but this
has lacked in sensitivity and specificity. An optional consideration
is the application of a simple screening protocol using condyle–
ramus length in comparison to longitudinal mandibular growth
templates.
The mechanism of condylar hyperplasia remains unknown.
There is a theme of an asymmetric somatic mutation that controls cellular growth but does not involve overgrowth genes.
More likely it is a unique phenomenon of secondary cartilages,
given the need for mechanical perturbation for continued growth.
The hypothesis is based on increased sensitivity to mechanics. This
enhanced sensitivity to mechanics and a proliferative response of
the secondary cartilage of the mandibular condyle may involve
signaling factors such as insulin growth factor 1 (IGF-1), its receptor, and transient receptor potential cation channel subfamily V
member 4 (TRPV4).
Fractures
Direct trauma can injure all components of the masticatory system. Fracture of the mandibular condyle may create intra-articular
hemarthrosis. Sequelae include adhesions, ankylosis, and joint
degeneration. Cone beam computed tomography (CBCT) imaging is the gold standard for diagnosis.

Disk–Condyle Complex Disorders
Four categories of internal derangement disorders are identified in
the sagittal plane (see
Fig. 51.1
). The gold standard for diagnosis
is magnetic resonance (MR) imaging in the closed and open positions. Based on the Diagnostic Criteria for Temporomandibular
Disorders (DC-TMD),
most of the clinical phenotypes have a
screening value based on low sensitivity and specificity. Category
1 is disk displacement with reduction. There is a self-reported
history of joint sounds during the last 30 days. During clinical
assessment, the examiner identifies a combination of TMJ clicks
during opening and closing (vertical range of motion) or during
vertical and latero-protrusive mandibular movements. Category
2 is disk displacement with reduction with intermittent locking.
Intermittent locking is an additional history and/or observable
parameter that is characterized by a momentary inability to open
the mouth. Categories 3 and 4 are disk displacement without
reduction with and without limited opening, respectively, based
on a 40-mm cutoff of maximum assisted opening. For both Categories 3 and 4, the history elements are established by self-report
of limited opening that interferes with the ability to eat. Among
the disk displacement categories, only the disk displacement
without reduction with limited opening (Category 3) has acceptable clinical validity.
Hypomobility Disorders
Adhesions and ankylosis are determinants of TMJ hypomobility. With respect to adhesions, 28% of TMJs with disk displacement have adhesions confirmed by arthroscopic surgery. Greater
than 80% of adhesions are found in the superior joint cavity.
Pathogenesis and etiology of adhesions have not been elucidated.
Given the predominance of adhesions in the anterior recess of
the superior joint space in subjects with disk displacement, it is
thought that the combination of synovial lining with retrodiscal
tissues in a small area initiates fibrotic tethers and inflammatory
responses.
Ankylosis of the TMJ may be either fibrous or bony in nature.
With fibrous ankylosis there are no gross bony changes and no
radiographic findings other than the absence of condyle translation on opening. Bony ankylosis is characterized by fusion of the
bones of the TMJ and by radiographic evidence of bone proliferation with marked deflection of the mandible to the affected side.

Hypermobility Disorders: Subluxation and Luxation
Hypermobility disorders are characterized by TMJ dislocation
in which the disk–condyle complex is positioned anterior to the
articular eminence upon full jaw opening. If the patient is able to
resolve the position of the complex and close the jaws, the term
subluxation is used. If intervention by a clinician is required to
allow the jaw to close again, the term luxation or dislocation is
used. Both subluxation and luxation phenomena are understood
as open lock jaw positions.

## Temporomandibular Joint Diseases

### Localized

Osteoarthritis
Osteoarthritis is defined as a degenerative condition characterized by structural changes of the articular tissues with concomitant remodeling of subchondral bone (
Figs. 51.2A and 51.3
). The
common etiologic factor is thought to be “overloading.” There
is progressive loss of articular cartilage, thought to be due to an
imbalance between anabolic and catabolic processes controlled by
chondrocytes. The process accelerates with loss of proteoglycans
and compromised mechanical integrity of the collagen matrix.
Inflammatory markers include interleukin-6 (IL-6), tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase-1 (TIMP-1), matrix metalloproteinases
(MMPs), and transforming growth factor beta (TGF
β
), among
others. Objective measures of these markers to date do not have
prognostic value.
Osteoarthritis is difficult to diagnose in its initiating phase.
Based on DC-TMD, osteoarthritis is defined as deterioration of
the articulating surfaces with changes in condyle and/or TMJ eminence architecture (
Fig. 51.2
). Criteria for diagnosis are history of
TMJ noise during function in the last 30 days, and identification
of crepitus during mandibular movements. The clinical diagnosis
has low sensitivity and specificity. Computed tomography (CT)
imaging, as the gold standard, is characterized by the findings of
generalized sclerosis, subchondral cyst(s), erosions, and/or osteophytes. Current diagnostics are limited to late-stage events, given
that loss of articular zone integrity is the initiating factor in disease progression. Once thought to be a linear process starting with
disk displacement, it is now understood that the process is nonlinear,
with evidence based on longitudinal data of mechanics and
behavior,
which differentiates between disease progression
and remission groups.
Idiopathic Condylar Resorption (Condylisis)
Idiopathic condylar resorption is an idiopathic degenerative
condition leading to loss of condylar volume and posterior
vertical dimension of the mandibular ramus (
Figs. 51.2B and
51.4
). It occurs spontaneously, is usually bilateral in presentation, and is predominantly found in adolescent and young
adult females. The presence of pain or joint sounds is variable.
The phenomenon manifests as a progressively developing anterior open bite due to loss of posterior vertical dimension of
the mandible and steepening of the mandibular plane relative
to a cephalometric reference plane such as Frankfort horizontal. CBCT imaging shows partial or total condylar resorption.
Serial superimposition of standardized lateral cephalograms
shows clockwise rotation of the mandible in right lateral view
and steepening of the mandibular plane. Serologic tests for
rheumatologic diseases, including systemic arthritides, must be
negative.

Osteochondritis Dissecans
Osteochondritis dissecans is a condition in which a piece of cartilage or small bone fragment breaks loose and is free within the
joint space. The etiopathology is unclear. With respect to the TMJ,
the phenomenon is rare; it is more common in the knee and elbow.
CT imaging is the gold standard for confirmation of the diagnosis.
Osteonecrosis
Osteonecrosis is a painful condition that is more commonly found
in the shoulder and knee. Diagnosis is based on MR imaging.
In the mandibular condyle, there is decreased signaling in T1
weighted images, and T2 weighted images show increased signaling, indicating edema. The etiology is unknown, so the diagnosis
is based on fulfillment of the criteria of arthralgia, MR T1–T2
imaging, and negative serologic evidence of rheumatic disease.
Neoplasms
Neoplasms are characterized by new uncontrolled growth of
abnormal tissue arising in the TMJ. Approximately 3% of malignant neoplasias metastasize to the jaws and hence may appear in
the TMJ structures. Most common signs and symptoms are limited range of motion, crepitation, pain, and swelling.

Synovial Chondromatosis
Synovial chondromatosis is a cartilaginous metaplasia of the mesenchymal remnants of the synovial tissues. Cartilaginous nodules
may detach from the synovial membrane, becoming loose bodies
in the joint space. CBCT imaging is required for definitive diagnosis. Etiology is unknown.

## Arthralgia

The etiologies of TMJ pain can include infection, trauma, or
inflammation related to cartilage degeneration. Arthralgia was
previously described as synovitis/capsulitis due to the common
thought that it was due to inflammation of the synovial lining.
It has also been called retrodiscitis because of evidence that the
condyle was loading upon the innervated retrodiscal tissue of
anteriorly displaced TMJ disks. Analysis of joint aspirates for
various inflammatory cytokines has proven to be difficult to interpret. Relative concentrations of inflammatory cytokines have not
yielded diagnostic categories that can be used to assess joint health
or progression of disease, thus limiting the clinical value of aspirate analysis.
Based on the DC-TMD, arthralgia is defined as pain of TMJ
origin that is modified by jaw function or parafunction. In addition to the history of TMJ pain and a positive report of modifier
factors, the diagnosis requires confirmation of location and replication of pain during clinical examination. Replication can be
provoked by mandibular movements and/or TMJ palpation.

## Masticatory Muscle Pain

TMD-related muscle pain is the most common motivating factor
for patients seeking treatment. Orofacial pain affects approximately
39 million adults in the United States,
and muscle-related pain
is considerably more frequent than arthralgia,
Various theories
have been proposed for the mechanisms that account for the relationship between masticatory muscle pain and motor function,
in which nociception, neuroplasticity, and nociplasticity
are
thought to account for the transition from acute to chronic pain.
No single etiologic factor has been identified, but an interaction
of biologic and environmental elements plays a role in this multifactorial condition.

### DC-TMD Painful Muscle Conditions

Only myalgia and myofascial pain with referral are clinically validated diagnostic groups. Both conditions are pain of muscle origin
that is modified by jaw function and parafunction. Both diagnoses
have excellent sensitivity and specificity, supporting their clinical
validity.
Myalgia
Two history elements include the presence of pain during the
last 30 days and endorsement of modifying factors. Upon clinical assessment, the examiner must confirm the pain in the masticatory muscles and replicate the pain during provocation tests
that include the maximum unassisted and assisted opening and/or
muscle palpation. The diagnostic criteria have limited specificity
to the masseter and temporalis muscles due to the high prevalence
of pain associated with these muscles and difficulty in palpation of
other muscles of mastication.

Myofascial Pain With Referral
In addition, to the criteria presented above, this diagnosis involves
the localization of the pain beyond the boundaries of the muscle
(i.e., ear, tooth, eye) upon palpation.

### Nonclassic Sources of Muscle-Related Pain

Concerning nonclassical sources of muscle pain, none of the following historical diagnoses have been clinically validated.
Tendonitis
Tendonitis is pain of the muscle tendon, where the pain experience is affected by jaw movement. There is replication of the
familiar pain by a provocation test. Limitation of movement may
be evident.
Myositis
Myositis is pain of muscle origin, with clinical characteristics of
inflammation or infection (edema, erythema, elevated temperature). Generally, myositis arises due to trauma or infection or with
autoimmune disease.
The limitation of movement is secondary
to the pain. Calcification (myositis ossificans) has been reported.
Serologic tests may reveal elevated creatine kinase levels.
Spasm
Spasm is characterized by an abrupt involuntary reversible contraction of a muscle. It may produce an acute malocclusion due to
unilateral contraction of a masticatory muscle. Diagnosis is based
on immediate onset of muscle pain modified by function and limited range of motion. The gold standard for this diagnosis is the
implementation of intramuscular electromyography (EMG).
Contracture
Contracture is the shortening of a masticatory muscle. This may
be due to fibrosis of tendon, ligament, or muscle fibers. Pain is

Hypertrophy of the Muscles of Mastication
Hypertrophy of the muscles of mastication is usually not associated with pain. There is evidence of familial influence. Diagnosis

Tissue proliferation related to neoplasm may be benign, malignant, or metastatic. Neoplasms are uncommon. Tissue proliferation presents as swelling with pain during function. There may
be sensory–motor effects. Diagnostic imaging and biopsy are
required for definitive diagnosis.
Dystonia
Dystonia is excessive involuntary sustained muscle contraction
of a masticatory muscle. The phenomenon may be a component
of a central nervous system (CNS) disorder, such as Parkinson’s
disease, or the adverse effect of medications such as neuroleptics.
Trauma may trigger onset of transient or permanent dystonia. The
affected muscle is painful, which impairs function. The gold standard for diagnosis is intramuscular EMG.

### Theoretical Models of TMD Myogenous Pain

Cycle of Pain Model
First proposed by one group,
the cycle of pain model is based
on cycles of muscle hyperactivity and pain as self-perpetuating.
Abnormal postures and excessive or aberrant movements initiate
pain, which in turn leads to muscle hyperactivity and fatigue.
There is limitation of motion in reaction to the pain. Muscle
spasm causes pain, and the pain reflexively produces muscle
spasm as a self-perpetuating cycle. Repetitive cycles of hyperactivity–pain results in loss of proprioceptive acuity, muscle
incoordination, and accumulation of inflammatory substances
within the muscle proper. To date, there is no evidence of accumulation of metabolic byproducts nor evidence of differences
in muscle coordination or proprioceptive functional differences
among symptomatic groups.

Pain Adaptation Model
First proposed by one group,
the pain adaptation model proposes that dysfunction is characteristic of conditions involving chronic pain and is a normal protective adaptation. To date
there is evidence that subjects with pain do not change behaviors
because of pain. Polysomnography and ambulatory EMG recording of subjects with pain in comparison with control groups fails
to show any differences in magnitudes of muscle activities or neuromuscular organization during function.
Integrated Pain Adaptation Model
Proposed by one group,
the integrated pain adaptation model
proposes that pain results in a new recruitment strategy to minimize pain. Polysomnography and ambulatory EMG recording of
subjects with and without pain fail to show any differences in magnitudes of muscle activities or neuromuscular organization during
function. Numerical model and human experiments did not find
differences in objective function between diagnostic groups.
Motor Adaptation Model
Proposed by one group,
the motor adaptation model assumes
that there are modifications of mechanical behavior with the objective to protect tissue from further pain or injury or threatened
pain. As described above, numerical modeling of neuromuscular
objective functions, in comparison with human experiments, did
not find differences in objective function between subjects with
and without pain.
Biopsychosocial Model
The biopsychosocial model takes into consideration the association between muscle activity and psychosocial state. Recent publications point to the role of sympathetic nervous system activity
and its influence on masticatory muscle motor neuron and muscle
spindle gamma motor neuron excitability. Additionally, the model
takes into consideration the changes in CNS processing of nonnociceptive low-threshold mechanoreceptors (LTMRs) afferent
signaling. The abrogation of disinhibition of pain transmission
under the egis of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) facilitates
LTMR sensory input to gain access to pain interneurons, which in
turn route pain information to higher order centers.

## Neuroplasticity, Nociplasticity, and Chronic Pain

Among musculoskeletal conditions, the incidence of chronic pain
associated with TMDs is second only to chronic low back pain.
TMD chronic pain is often refractory to treatment. It frequently
presents with comorbid conditions and is rarely accompanied
by clinical signs of injury or inflammation. TMD chronic pain
lacks histopathologic or other objective evidence that can directly
and proportionally explain the pain severity.
The term
nociplastic pain
has been applied to describe chronic pain with which
there is an absence of peripheral pathology (
Table 51.4
) and differs with respect to features and responsiveness to interventions
(
Table 51.5
). Nociceptive and neuroplastic pain are associated
with activation of peripheral nociceptors and pathophysiology of
the somatosensory nervous system. In contrast, the mechanism
of nociplastic pain is dysregulated sensory processing within the
CNS. Under normal physiologic conditions there is inhibition
of non-nociceptive sensory input to secondary pain interneurons. This inhibition of transmission is controlled by the ANS.

## TMD Management Strategies

Reference publications
outline updated intervention strategies for management of TMDs. Advances in the management of
TMDs are based on improved knowledge in the areas of TMJ and
muscle physiology and the newest findings as relates to dysregulated CNS sensory processing. Progress in management has been
aided by the DC-TMD,
which have refined diagnostic criteria
for application in clinical settings. The most common pain-related
TMDs are myalgia, myofascial pain with referral, and arthralgia.
Treatment for these painful diagnoses is directed toward identifying and addressing the multiple factors that play a role in the
patient’s symptoms. Treatment often requires a multidisciplinary
approach utilizing a team of professionals including orofacial pain
specialist, primary care physician, neurologist, rheumatologist,
sleep medicine therapist, mental health provider, and physical
therapist working in conjunction with the patient. Based on randomized clinical trials, conservative treatment options to reduce
the signs and symptoms of TMDs include patient education,
therapeutic exercises, physical therapy, pharmacotherapy, and oral
appliances.
Concerning intra-articular conditions, past management strategies for articular disk displacements were aimed at surgically
repositioning the articular disk or utilizing an oral appliance to
advance the mandible to a point where the condyle was repositioned on the articular disk. These early management approaches
failed to alleviate pain and dysfunction. Today, management strategies have evolved into less invasive approaches. Surgical management is reserved for cases where restoration of structural loss of
the mandibular condyle is needed. The surgical interventions for

## Conclusion

Studies in TMDs are challenging because they require integration of knowledge from psychology, biology, and bioengineering.
Many TMDs do not exist in isolation but rather are frequently
associated with other systemic painful conditions such as headache, neck and back pain, irritable bowel syndrome, fibromyalgia, sleep disorders, and chronic fatigue syndrome. It is currently
recognized that TMDs result from a complex interplay between
biologic, biomechanical, psychological, and social factors that
transcend elementary explanations. Opportunities exist to
develop new research tools and technologies that can expand the
current understanding of the etiology and progression of TMDs.
There continues to be a need for randomized clinical trials regarding nonsurgical and surgical TMD treatments. Current evidence
indicates that TMDs are best managed with conservative reversible interventions. The future development of management strategies requires rigorous scientific investigation, which focuses on
the variables that influence the ontogeny of the disparate entities
currently included under the rubric of DC-TMD. As noted in the
National Academy of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine consensus report on TMD,
there is a need for “new tools, metrics,
and biomarkers to diagnose TMD and forecast their trajectory.”
