## Introduction

Functional limitation, pain, fatigue, and poor quality of life are
prevalent problems reported by individuals with rheumatic and
musculoskeletal conditions despite great advances in pharmacologic management of rheumatic disease in the early 21st century.
Members of the rehabilitation disciplines, particularly occupational and physical therapy, are specifically trained to address these
impairments. Utilization of occupational and physical therapy
varies widely among rheumatic and musculoskeletal conditions
and has evolved over time. Yet the goal of these rehabilitation disciplines remains the same: optimize function and quality of life in
the people they serve. In this chapter, we review the role of rehabilitation in rheumatology, specifically occupational therapy and
physical therapy examination and treatment approaches, detailing
the typical interventions provided.

## Role of Rehabilitation in Rheumatology

The role and understanding of rehabilitation and exercise in rheumatology has evolved over the last 50 years. Current evidence supports the use of rehabilitation and exercise as safe, beneficial, and
often essential for individuals with rheumatic conditions. Before
the advent of effective disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs and
biologic therapies around the turn of the 21st century, the role and
recommendations for rehabilitation and exercise for adults with
rheumatic conditions were considerably different. Physical activity
and exercise were generally viewed as detrimental to joint health
and a potential accelerant for joint destruction. While gentle exercise and resistance training were encouraged when disease activity
was low, patients were encouraged to rest when in pain and avoid
strenuous or high-impact activities to protect the body.
Physical and occupational therapists at that time focused on providing
palliative care to help patients manage pain, with approaches like
thermal modalities (e.g., ultrasound, paraffin baths) and electrical stimulation.
They also focused on compensatory strategies to
help patients with impairments to function in their daily lives by
introducing adaptive tools to execute tasks (e.g., opening a jar,
cutting vegetables) and providing custom orthoses to improve
joint positioning and protection for those experiencing severe
joint impairment in their hands and wrists.
As rheumatologic
research and drug intervention progressed, the opportunities for
occupational and physical therapy to improve the health and lives
of people with rheumatic conditions have shifted. Currently pharmacologic strategies, like treat-to-target approaches and biologic
therapies, reduce overall disease activity and increase the proportion of people managing rheumatic conditions with remission or
low disease activity.
For those with early and successful pharmacologic management, permanent damage, or joint destruction,
is no longer a prevalent hallmark of rheumatic disease, yet functional limitation and disability remain common.
At the same time that pharmacologic care was advancing, new
understanding of the role of physical activity and exercise was
emerging. Evidence demonstrated that physical activity and exercise are not harmful
and they actually help patients by reducing
pain and increasing function.
For rehabilitation professionals,
this means that occupational and physical therapy is no longer
limited to palliative and compensatory approaches to care. While
these may still be necessary in advanced disease, early control of
disease activity expands the occupational and physical therapy
approaches to include restorative and preventive strategies in
managing rheumatic disease. Restorative strategies serve to assist
patients in regaining their prior level of function, whereas preventive strategies delay or prevent loss of function and onset and
progression of disability. Palliative and compensatory approaches
remain important for those who need it (e.g., advanced or aggressive disease). Taken together, occupational therapy and physical
therapy help rheumatologists capitalize on the improved control

## Components of Rehabilitation

Occupational and physical therapy episodes of care share a similar
process and structure. An episode of care begins with an examination, which informs intervention approaches, and ends after
reexamination indicates that goals are met or conditions have stabilized. The following sections describe the process of examination
and intervention.

### Physical Examination

Assessment in occupational and physical therapy uses a holistic
approach to understand the impact of disease-related impairments on a patient’s functional performance, that is, the capacity
to participate in meaningful, important tasks essential to everyday life. The structure of the initial examination is similar to a
rheumatologist examination, as it consists of a combination of
structured interviews (e.g., patient history), clinical observations
(e.g., physical examination and clinical tests), and standardized
assessments (e.g., patient-reported or performance-based outcome
measures). However, the lens through which rheumatologists
and rehabilitation providers approach this examination differs
(
Table 38.3
). While rheumatologists use the examination process
to assess the activity of the disease itself, rehabilitation providers
focus on assessing the functional ramifications of the disease on
the patient’s activities and participation in life.
During the initial interview, therapists gather information on
patients’ history, medications, physical function, emotional status, and sociocultural backgrounds. Occupational therapists collect information on the physical, cognitive, and environmental
demands of activities and occupations relevant to the patient.
Data obtained during assessment allow the understanding of
how occupations are impacted by rheumatic conditions and
the elaboration of a therapeutic plan to enable occupational
performance. Examination in occupational therapy considers the ergonomic aspects of an activity, such as body posture,
joint movements, and the task’s pacing, cognitive requirements,
and concomitant demands (i.e., cooking while taking care of
children, typing minutes during a business meeting).
Physical
therapists gather detailed information about the patient’s mobility. This includes careful observation of movement patterns during common movements such as locomotion and rising from a
sitting position. This movement analysis is combined with data
from an extensive physical examination of the patient’s musculoskeletal system, including strength, range of motion, and joint
integrity assessed by standardized special tests, to provide an
understanding of the patient’s mobility barriers and to generate
a plan to optimize mobility.
Occupational and physical therapists use standardized outcome measures to complement their interview, observations,
and physical examination. Standardized outcome measures
include patient-reported outcome measures (PROMS), such
as the Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand (DASH)
questionnaire
and the Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Arthritis Index (WOMAC),
and performance measures such as the timed stair climb and the 6-minute walk
test.
These measures provide information on patients’ functional level, satisfaction, and physical and emotional status
and are repeated over the episode of care to evaluate response

Self-Management
A central tenet of rehabilitation intervention is a progression to
independent patient self-management. In other words, occupational and physical therapists strive to help patients learn to use
therapist-provided interventions to manage their own disease and
symptoms. Toward this goal, occupational and physical therapy
interventions require active participation by the patient and are
progressed with the overarching goal of self-management. Optimizing a patient’s self-management skills is integral to all intervention strategies.
Self-management is the ability to regulate one’s own behaviors,
thoughts, and emotions to optimize a relevant outcome. Skills in
self-management lead to a sense of
self-efficacy
, which is an individual’s belief that they have the capacity to behave in ways necessary
to produce specific performance attainments. Development of
self-management skills and an improved sense of self-efficacy is an
integral part of treatments provided by occupational and physical

life.

Physical Activity
Physical activity (PA)
is defined as any volitional bodily movement that uses voluntary muscles and requires energy expenditure. PA includes all activities, at any intensity, performed at
any time. It includes any daily activity and exercise, which is
a special form of PA discussed in the next section. Examples
of PA include walking, dancing, raking leaves, washing dishes,
and gardening.
PA improves function and reduces pain and fatigue in people
with most forms of arthritis. Abundant research exists demonstrating the benefits of PA in patients with OA.
Research also
supports its use in RA,
juvenile arthritis (JA),
spondyloarthropathies,
fibromyalgia,
systemic sclerosis (SSc),
and
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).
Treatment guidelines from
the ACR
and from the European Alliance of Associations for
Rheumatology (EULAR)
strongly recommend the use of PA by
individuals with OA. Importantly, little evidence of adverse events
from PA exists in the literature for any rheumatic disorder.
Patients often wonder how much PA is safe for them. Evidence
suggests that people with arthritis can safely follow the PA guidelines established by the American College of Sports Medicine
(ACSM) for healthy individuals (a minimum of 30 minutes of
moderate aerobic activity 5 days per week and muscle-strengthening activities at least twice weekly). EULAR recommends that
patients with inflammatory arthritis, including spondyloarthropathies and RA, or OA aim to exercise at the recommended ACSM
levels for healthy adults.
However, people with arthritis may
need to begin activity more gently and progress the dosage and
intensity more gradually. Instruction and supervision by a physical
therapist may help an individual with arthritis in determining an
appropriate PA regimen.
There are many evidence-based community resources available
to assist patients as they begin to incorporate PA in their lives,
many of which can be accessed through the Arthritis Foundation,
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and American Physical Therapy Association. Some programs are provided in person in

Exercise
Exercise is a special form of PA. Specifically, it is planned, structured, and repeated. Examples include strengthening exercises
such as straight leg raises and minisquats to improve quadricep
muscle function, range-of-motion exercises to improve flexibility,
and walking to increase endurance. The main categories of exercise
are strengthening (resistance), flexibility, and endurance. Like PA,
these exercises have been shown to reduce pain and fatigue and
increase function in individuals with arthritis, including OA, RA,
JA, ankylosing spondylitis, SSc, and SLE.
Treatment
guidelines from the ACR strongly recommend the use of exercise
by individuals with OA and RA.
As in PA, there is little or no
evidence of harm from any of these exercise types.
Flexibility exercises are designed to increase or maintain joint
mobility. These exercises are particularly important to patients
with arthritis. When a joint is inflamed, it tends to stiffen. Similarly, when a joint is not moved regularly, surrounding muscles
and ligaments tend to adapt and shorten, reducing joint range of
motion. Flexibility exercises are needed to avoid a loss of flexibility
or range of motion in both cases. Flexibility exercises should be
performed slowly and gently and can be performed safely on a
daily basis.
Strengthening, or resistance, exercises increase muscle strength
(the ability of a muscle to lift a load). These exercises are particularly

important for the large muscle groups of the shoulders, elbows,
hips, knees, ankles, and trunk. They are typically performed two
or three times weekly and must be sufficiently challenging to
fatigue the muscles so that muscle adaptation can occur.
Aerobic, or endurance, exercises are long-duration exercises
with little or no resistance. They increase heart and respiratory
rates during exercise and are recommended to be performed for
up to about 30 minutes several times weekly.
Two additional types of exercise are commonly offered to individuals with arthritis: balance exercises and neuromuscular exercises. Balance exercises are beneficial to many individuals with or
without arthritis, including older adults.
Examples of balance
exercises include standing on one leg, standing on a soft or moving
surface, or standing on two feet with the heel of one foot directly
in front of the toes of the other foot. The challenge of the activity
increases by performing the activity with the eyes closed. Improving balance decreases the risk of falls, a danger to older adults and
to patients with arthritis.
Neuromuscular exercises are similar to some weight-bearing
strengthening exercises. However, the focus of neuromuscular exercises is on the quality of the movement and less on the
intensity of the dose. The goals of neuromuscular exercises are to
improve sensorimotor control and dynamic joint stability. Examples include stepping up one step or coming to standing from a
sitting position. Individuals beginning a neuromuscular exercise
program require instruction from a physical therapist initially to
ensure quality of exercise performance. Neuromuscular exercises
improve function and decrease pain in individuals with knee
OA.
There is insufficient evidence available to recommend one form
of exercise over another in individuals with rheumatic conditions.
However, the patient’s disease activity and joint integrity may dictate which exercises are most tolerable to the patient. It is likely
that most patients will benefit from an exercise program that incorporates flexibility, strengthening, and endurance exercises. Balance
and neuromuscular exercises may also improve a patient’s function
and safety. Like PA, evidence suggests that patients with arthritis
can safely participate in exercise at the dosage and intensity recommended by the ACSM for healthy individuals (
Table 38.5
). Data
demonstrate that patients with OA and RA can safely exercise at
moderate to vigorous levels of intensity. However, people with
arthritis may need to start at a low dose and progress slowly to
avoid any increase in joint symptoms. A knowledgeable physical
therapist can help the patient to identify and modify exercises to
avoid undue loads on involved joints and to optimize the exercise
regimen to meet the patient’s goal.
Regular exercise helps to reduce pain and fatigue and to increase
function. Exercise for flexibility, strength, and endurance should
be a part of every patient’s plan of care.
Joint Protection and Energy Conservation
Joint protection and energy conservation programs are interventions that use ergonomic principles to reduce pain, manage
fatigue, and help preserve joint integrity during daily activities.
Such interventions combine biomechanics, behavioral, and cognitive concepts to analyze and modify movement, posture, and pace
during self-care, work, and leisure activities.
The goals of joint
protection and energy conservation interventions are to eliminate
ineffective, automatic movement patterns and replace them with
motor strategies that preserve joint alignment and reduce the risk
of joint deformities, incorporating resting breaks, pacing, and
time-management strategies to minimize fatigue, reduce pain,

delivered by an occupational therapist.

simulations, and reinforcement strategies.
Although flexibility

group settings should be considered given their positive
effect on self-efficacy by fostering collective problem-solving, peer
support, and exchange of ideas.

ACR treatment guidelines recommend the use of orthoses for carpometacarpal joint of the thumb
in individuals with thumb OA and for the knee, including the
tibiofemoral and patellofemoral joints, for individuals with knee
OA.
For conditions other than OA, use of orthoses is conditional
to disease and patient-specific factors, with different levels of
evidence supporting (or not) its use as a therapeutic modality.
Patients with inflammatory arthritis report positive effects on pain
and function during the use of orthotic devices, particularly rigid
orthoses involving the wrist and fingers.
Treatment guidelines from the EULAR and the ACR provide conditional recommendations for the use of rigid and compressive orthoses for the wrist, hand, knee, ankle, and foot in
individuals with RA.
Overall, the low quality of studies and
substantial variations in orthotic design due to patient-specific
needs make it difficult to generalize such findings to other rheumatic disorders.
For instance, at this moment, there is limited evidence to support the use of orthotic devices for patients with SLE, SSc, and
other rheumatic conditions. Most orthoses for such conditions
are indicated based on an expert’s clinical judgment, considering
the biomechanical and functional issues experienced by patients.
Due to the lack of conclusive evidence available, orthoses are currently contraindicated for clients with SSc, particularly devices
designed to reduce finger contractures, given their increased risk
of adversely affecting peripheral vascular perfusion.
It is worth mentioning that patients may present symptoms
caused by musculoskeletal conditions that can benefit from
orthotic interventions, such as carpal tunnel syndrome, lateral
epicondylitis, and plantar fasciitis, among others. Despite having a similar lack of evidence on their effectiveness, there is a
larger number of studies supporting the use of orthoses for treating musculoskeletal conditions, with recommendations on dosage and specific devices.
However, caution is suggested while
prescribing orthotics for patients in such situations, as regional
symptoms may be exacerbated by the systemic nature of rheumatic conditions. For instance, off-the-shelf orthoses for carpal
tunnel syndrome usually stabilize the wrist at 30 degrees of extension, a position that could further increase the pressure over the
median nerve if a patient with RA experiences inflammation over
the wrist due to increased disease activity. Occupational therapists
or certified hand therapists (who may be either occupational or
physical therapists) are essential in prescribing and fitting orthotic
devices for the hand and upper extremities, and physical therapists are experts in prescribing and fitting orthoses for the lower
extremities. Podiatrists may also help in recommending and fitting orthoses for the foot.
Assistive Technology (Assistive and Adaptive Equipment)
Assistive technologies are pieces of equipment whose purpose is
to improve or maintain patients’ function in a variety of tasks and
contexts.
This definition includes a broad range of devices, from
sophisticated, computer-based communication systems to lowtech devices such as canes and adapted cutlery.
Patients with rheumatic conditions experiencing limited
mobility are typically seen by physical therapists who can
determine the need for equipment such as canes, crutches,
and walkers to facilitate functional mobility. These devices
increase patient independence, safety, and overall function in
domestic and community environments (
Fig. 38.2
). Although
canes, crutches, and walkers are readily available to patients at
their neighborhood pharmacy, it is important that the assistive device is appropriately fitted to the patient and that the
patient is correctly instructed in its use. Anyone who has ever
sprained an ankle knows that navigating stairs with a cane or
crutches is daunting. Careful education in the proper use of
the device ensures patient safety and enhances the likelihood
of treatment adherence. Similarly, proper fitting of an assistive
device is essential to proper use and to prevent secondary injuries to other joints. A referral to a physical therapist for device

is conditionally recommended by the ACR for patients with RA
and for patients with OA of the hands, hips or knees.
The goal of electrotherapy is either to decrease pain or to
improve muscle function. There are two distinct forms of electrotherapy: transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)
and neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES). TENS is used
to decrease pain and NMES is used to improve function. Both
forms of electrotherapy should be initiated by either an occupational or physical therapist. Evidence on the benefits of electrotherapy is conflicting, and the financial burden to the patient is
significant. The ACR treatment guidelines recommend against the
use of TENS in both OA and RA.
The benefits of adjunctive
physical agents are unclear. At best they may reduce an individual’s
symptoms enough for the patient to engage in other activities with
more likely benefits.
Patients with arthritis often use massage and acupuncture to
relieve pain. Occupational therapists and physical therapists do
not typically provide these interventions. Rather, patients must
identify other professionals who offer them. The benefits of these
interventions to patients remain controversial. Benefits of massage for OA-specific complaints are limited, and the ACR conditionally recommends against its use in OA
but conditionally
recommends for its use in patients with RA.
In both cases the
evidence is limited, the burden to patients is primarily economic,
and patients report benefits.
Like massage, acupuncture is provided by trained acupuncturists who are rarely occupational or physical therapists. While evidence is limited, the ACR conditionally recommends the use of
acupuncture in patients with OA of the hand, hip, or knee and in
patients with RA.
Additional integrative interventions that patients may consider
outside of occupational and physical therapy include cognitivebehavioral therapy (CBT), mind–body exercises such as yoga or
tai chi, vocational rehabilitation and work-site evaluations, and
chiropractic therapies. Evidence does support the use of CBT and
mind–body exercises in patients with OA and RA. These interventions are conditionally recommended within the treatment guidelines from the ACR.
The ACR conditionally recommends
against chiropractic interventions in patients with RA based primarily on the risk of harm.
In summary, occupational and physical therapists offer many
interventions directed toward decreasing pain and fatigue and
increasing function. The use of any of these interventions depends
on the patient’s specific needs based on symptoms, functional status, and goals. Interventions are likely to vary across the course
of the disease. Additional interventions outside of physical and
occupational therapy may be beneficial for some patients with
arthritis, but they may carry some risk of harm as well. Consequently, it is essential that the patient be followed by a rehabilitation specialist with specific familiarity with rheumatic diseases.
The next section provides insights on how the rheumatologist and
occupational or physical therapist can collaborate to optimize the
patient’s outcomes.

## Incorporating PT/OT Into Rheumatology Practice

### Models of Physical Therapy/Occupational Therapy Integration Into Rheumatology Care

Many approaches exist for integrating physical therapy or occupational therapy into routine rheumatology care.
Rehabilitation Integration
In an integrated model of care, physical and occupational therapists practice onsite full time alongside rheumatology and other
providers in an interdisciplinary clinic. Evaluation and treatment
space, including a therapy gym and/or activity area, would be
dedicated for rehabilitation services. An interdisciplinary clinic
approach may facilitate provider communication and care coordination. This model of care may be more commonly used in pediatric rheumatology clinics.
Rehabilitation Consultants
Rheumatology clinics and providers may incorporate rehabilitation through the use of consultants. In this model, rheumatology
clinics and/or providers regularly consult or refer to specific clinics or clinicians with whom they have a close and collaborative
professional relationship. This may or may not include an occasional onsite presence (e.g., physical therapist works in the clinic
1 day per week). This approach maintains open and consistent
lines of communication between providers for care coordination.
Patients may value access to rehabilitation providers who work
regularly with their rheumatology providers, as this may enhance
care coordination.
Rehabilitation Referrals
Rheumatology clinics and providers may incorporate rehabilitation through a referral to a community practice. In this model
of care, there is no established professional relationship between
the rheumatology and the rehabilitation provider. Rather, the
rheumatology provider facilitates care by providing a referral for
physical therapy or occupational therapy services at a clinic of the
patient’s choice. While this model of care may make care coordination between providers more challenging, it may be the most
feasible choice in many cases. Many patients must travel a distance
to access a rheumatology clinic; however, they may have access to
occupational and physical therapy services closer to home. Telehealth may help bridge these access issues by allowing patients to
reach providers remotely. Referrals to rehabilitation professionals
in community practice is a common arrangement and still allows
direct communication between rheumatologist and therapist to
optimize care.

### Making the Referral

When Should I Refer a Patient to a Rehabilitation
Provider?
Rheumatology providers should consider providing a referral to
rehabilitation services for patients with a rheumatic disease when
the patient reports new or worsening functional limitations.
Often, this may coincide with the time frame of the initial diagnosis or after an exacerbation or disease flare. Referrals near the
time of an initial diagnosis can help patients optimize their function while trying various pharmacologic management strategies
for control of disease activity. Exposure to rehabilitation early after

diagnosis may also help patients recognize when they may benefit
from rehabilitation later, as their disease progresses.
Rheumatologists also often see patients with regional musculoskeletal concerns. Sometimes these concerns may be concurrent with, but unrelated to, an existing rheumatologic condition.
Other times, a patient may be evaluated for a suspected rheumatologic disorder that is subsequently ruled out, but the regional
musculoskeletal impairments persist. In these cases, a referral to
physical or occupational therapy is likely appropriate. Physical
and occupational therapists frequently evaluate and treat patients
for regional musculoskeletal concerns, including rotator cuff disorders, lateral epicondylitis, patellofemoral joint pain, or iliotibial
band syndrome, among many others. Physical and occupational
therapists are also trained to discern if the patient presentation is
appropriate for rehabilitation versus referral to another provider.
Providing a referral to occupational or physical therapy when you
see the patient, instead of referring back to their primary care provider or other specialist, can accelerate the patient’s recovery and
avoid unnecessary cost and time delays.

What Information Should I Include in the Referral?
Referrals offer rheumatologists the opportunity to communicate
goals and concerns with the rehabilitation provider. In particular, the rheumatologist should include any unique precautions or
contraindications that they are concerned about for the patient.
Sharing the functional goals that prompted the referral gives the
rehabilitation provider insight into the primary concerns at the
time of the rheumatology visit. A specific prescription of interventions, such as detailed exercises or adjunctive treatments, is unnecessary. As mentioned earlier, occupational and physical therapists
conduct comprehensive examinations at the beginning of an episode of care. The results of the examination, combined with the
goals of the patient, guide the choice of interventions.
To Whom Should I Refer?
All occupational and physical therapists are trained to evaluate and
treat functional limitations (see
Tables 38.2 and 38.3
). However,
some patients may have specific functional needs that may benefit
from a referral to a specialized rehabilitation provider. For example, certified hand therapists, who may be occupational or physical therapists, have specific training to meet the needs of patients
with primary limitations in hand and finger function. Some physical therapists have advanced training in fabricating custom foot
orthoses, and others are board certified in orthopedic physical
therapy. Pediatrics occupational and physical therapists are skilled
at adapting interventions for children. There is no universal certification to indicate specialization in rheumatology for rehabilitation providers. Developing relationships with local rehabilitation
providers may assist you in providing referrals to therapists who
understand the needs of patients with rheumatic conditions.

## Conclusion

Advances in rheumatology practice have changed the care provided by occupational and physical therapists, moving from a palliative model to patient-centric treatment emphasizing functional
outcomes. Current approaches position patients as active participants in their treatment, setting meaningful goals, incorporating
lifestyle changes, and enabling their participation in meaningful
activities. Occupational and physical therapists are an integral
part of the interprofessional team, bringing specialized skills to
improve the lives of patients living with rheumatic conditions.
Further high-quality research, including randomized controlled
trials and implementation studies, are needed to enhance the integration of rehabilitation into routine rheumatology care to optimize patient quality of life.
