# Vaccination in Rheumatic Disease

KIANA VAKIL-GILANI, INGRID LINDQUIST,
MARCIA FRIEDMAN, AND KEVIN L. WINTHROP

KEY POINTS

> Patients with rheumatic disease are at higher risk for serious and

> opportunistic infections, including those potentially preventable by

> vaccination.

> DMARDs can negatively affect vaccine immunogenicity, most notably

> methotrexate and B-cell depletion therapy.

> Some biologics (interleukin-6R [IL-6R], IL-12/IL-23, and IL-17 inhibitors)

> have little observed impact on vaccine immunogenicity.

> Strategies for improving vaccine response include vaccinating prior to

> DMARD start and, in some cases, temporary hold of the DMARD

> after vaccination.

## Introduction

Patients with rheumatic diseases have an increased risk of
contracting vaccine-preventable infections and suffer morbidity and mortality due to these infections.
Vaccination
of these patients can reduce the risk of infectious complications
; however, immune suppressing drugs used to treat
rheumatic diseases can sometimes blunt vaccine-induced
immune responses, making timing of vaccination in relation
to immunosuppressive therapy sometimes critical. Further,
despite their potential for providing protection, vaccines continue to be underused in practice.
In this chapter we discuss
the latest data and guidelines from the American College of
Rheumatology (ACR), the European Alliance of Associations
for Rheumatology (EULAR), the U.S. Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention (CDC), and the Advisory Committee
on Immunization Practices (ACIP) on the effect on vaccine
response in adult patients on disease-modifying anti-rheumatic
drugs (DMARDs). Therapies considered include conventional
synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs), which include methotrexate, leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine, and sulfasalazine; targeted synthetic DMARDs (tsDMARDs), which include Janus
kinase (JAK) inhibitors; and biologic DMARDs (bDMARDs,
or
biologics
), which are targeted, usually antibody-based drugs
that inhibit cytokine pathways (TNF, IL-17A and IL-17F,
IL-23, IL-6, and IL-1), T cell costimulatory-signal of CTLA4
(abatacept), or targeted B-cell depletion via CD19 inhibition
(rituximab). For most studies evaluating DMARD effects upon
vaccine responses, vaccine immunogenicity has been used as a
primary outcome measure, as few studies are powered to detect
clinical efficacy. Also, it should be noted that recommendations regarding severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) vaccination are changing rapidly at the
time of this chapter’s preparation. Lastly, outcome measures
(definitions of response, how response is measured, and timing
of response)
and study designs are often inconsistent across
studies, making it more difficult to compare the effects of different DMARDs upon different vaccines. This chapter focuses
on the vaccines of most clinical relevance to the practicing
adult rheumatologist, including the influenza, pneumococcal, herpes zoster, hepatitis B virus (HBV), tetanus diphtheria
pertussis (Tdap), human papilloma virus (HPV), yellow fever
(YF), and SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19) vaccines.

## Influenza Vaccine

All influenza vaccines available in the United States this season are quadrivalent; they contain two influenza A and two
influenza B virus antigens.
The intranasal live attenuated
influenza vaccine is contraindicated for those on immunosuppressive therapies. Influenza vaccine efficacy is estimated using
a surrogate of hemagglutinin inhibition (HI) titers. An HI titer
of 1:40 is considered “seroprotective” (defined as 50% vaccine
efficacy).

### DMARD Effect on influenza Vaccine

Rituximab
severely
reduces
influenza
vaccine
immune
response.
Abatacept may impair influenza vaccine response,
but data to support this are limited.
Anti-TNF therapies
and JAK inhibitors
lead to lower fold increases in
antibody titers; however, the proportion of patients achieving
seroprotection appears to be similar to those not using these
therapies. Data suggest that IL-6, IL-12/23, and IL-17 inhibitors
do not affect influenza vaccine immunogenicity.
Methotrexate (MTX) decreases humoral responses to influenza vaccine, and
temporarily holding MTX at the time of vaccination is a recently
recommended strategy for improving the proportion of patients
that reach seroprotection.

### Influenza Vaccine Recommendations in Rheumatology Patients

Annual influenza vaccines are recommended for all who are ages 6
months and older.
The ACR and EULAR recommend annual
intramuscular vaccinations for all rheumatoid arthritis (RA)
patients.
The high-dose influenza vaccine is more effective in patients
with rheumatic diseases. While high-dose influenza vaccine is
recommended in the general population 65 years of age and
older, the 2022 ACR guidelines have conditionally recommended this for those over age 18 who are on immunosuppressive medication.
If high-dose or adjuvant vaccine is
unavailable, then any influenza vaccine is preferred over no
vaccine.

### Timing of Influenza Vaccine Relative to Immunosuppressive Therapy

Generally, influenza vaccination does not need to be delayed based
on DMARD therapy schedule. However, there are data on optimized timing of methotrexate and rituximab dosing. Patients on
methotrexate can improve their influenza vaccine immunogenicity by holding methotrexate for 1 to 2 weeks after vaccination
especially in those on
≥
15 mg/week.
One study of RA patients
showed that a temporary discontinuation of methotrexate for 2
weeks after vaccination improves immunogenicity of seasonal
influenza vaccine without increasing RA activity.
The latest
EULAR guidelines acknowledge this fact
but do not include this
vaccine timing as a recommendation.
Those with planned rituximab initiation should receive the
influenza vaccine prior to starting therapy.
Previous recommendations stated that for those already receiving rituximab, influenza
vaccination should be administered 2 to 4 weeks prior to the next
scheduled dose to maximize time since last dose. The 2022 ACR
guidelines, however, recommend continuing rituximab as previously scheduled, although trying to maximize the time between
last administration and vaccination while taking into account the
need to vaccinate just prior to influenza season.
Abatacept likely
impairs influenza vaccine response, but data are limited. There are
currently no guidelines for timing of the influenza vaccine when
using this medication.

## Pneumococcal Vaccination

Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine 13 (PCV13) and pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine 23-valent (PPSV23) have been used
together for the last decade to prevent pneumococcal disease.
Recently the conjugate vaccine has been updated to include a
greater number of serotypes, with both PCV15 and PCV20 being
recently approved for use in adults.

### DMARD Effect on Pneumococcal Vaccine Response

Although studies conducted to evaluate DMARD influence upon
pneumococcal vaccine immunogenicity have not been large, the
results are similar to the influenza vaccine: rituximab
and methotrexate
reduce immune response to pneumococcal vaccines. Abatacept
and Jak inhibitors
modestly
reduce immunogenicity, but other DMARDs do not.
Please note, however, that almost all these data are for the
PPSV23 vaccine specifically. There are several studies assessing
effects of various JAK inhibitors on PCV13 immunogenicity, but
these studies were uncontrolled.
Additionally, we do not have
any data available regarding PCV15 or PCV20 vaccine.

### Pneumococcal Vaccine Recommendations

Guidelines from the ACR, EULAR, CDC, and ACIP recommend pneumococcal vaccinations for patients with rheumatic
disease taking DMARDs.
For pneumococcal vaccine–naïve patients, a single dose of the PCV20 provides maximal benefit
(CDC–ACIP recommendations,
https://www.
cdc.gov/vaccines/acip/recommendations.html
). The CDC recommends that patients who have never received a pneumococcal vaccine should receive a single dose of PCV20 or start with
one dose of PCV15 followed by a dose of PPSV23 at least 1 year
later
(CDC–ACIP recommendations,
https://www.cdc.gov/
vaccines/acip/recommendations.html
). A minimum of 8 weeks
between PCV15 and PPSV23 can be considered for patients on
DMARDs
(CDC–ACIP recommendations,
https://www.cdc.
gov/vaccines/acip/recommendations.html
). If a patient has only
received PPSV23, the CDC recommends administering one dose
of PCV15 or PCV20 at least 1 year after PPSV23 was given
(CDC–ACIP recommendations,
https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/
acip/recommendations.html
). In immunosuppressed patients
who have received PCV13, the CDC recommends an additional
two doses of PPSV23 before age 65 years and one dose of PPSV23
at age 65 years or older. This is with the caveat that the amount
of benefit with the addition of PCV15 or PCV20 in those who
have received PCV13 with prior PPSV23 had not been evaluated
at the time of their recommendations
(CDC–ACIP recommendations,
https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/acip/recommendations.
html
). Recent data, however, show improved immunogenicity
with receipt of PCV20 after initial PCV13/PPSV23.

### Timing of Pneumococcal Vaccine Relative to Immunosuppressive Therapy

Pneumococcal vaccination can be given irrespective of DMARD
therapy timing with the exception of rituximab. Similar to influenza vaccination timing, those with planned rituximab initiation
should receive the pneumococcal vaccine prior to the first infusion.
If rituximab therapy is ongoing, the pneumococcal vaccine should be administered when the next rituximab dose is due,
delaying rituximab infusion by 2 weeks if disease activity allows.
Until further data are available with regard to methotrexate or
other DMARDs and pneumococcal vaccine response, the ACR
recommends other DMARDs including methotrexate can be continued without alteration to their dosing schedule.

## Herpes Zoster Vaccination

### Background

Rheumatic patients on immunosuppressive therapy are at higher
risk of developing herpes zoster infection compared with the
general public especially those with inflammatory myositis and
systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), as well as those using JAK
inhibitors.
There are two herpes zoster vaccines approved: the
recombinant zoster vaccine (RZV) (Shingrix) and the live zoster

vaccine (ZVL) (Zostavax).
The Shingrix vaccine appears more
effective, and Zostavax is no longer marketed in the United States
but is still available in many parts of the world.
Zoster vaccine
response is measured by humoral varicella zoster vaccine IgG or
cell-mediated varicella zoster virus-specific T cell enumeration,
which correlates more strongly with the risk of future shingles.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on Vaccine Efficacy

There are limited data regarding whether DMARDs alter the
immunogenicity of the herpes zoster vaccine. In one randomized
trial of RA patients vaccinated with Zostavax and randomized to
tofacitinib versus placebo, patients had similar responses in both
groups.
Patients on TNF inhibitors vaccinated with Zostavax
had a 30% increase in humoral and cell-mediated responses
compared with placebo.
Rheumatology patients on rituximab
have not been studied relative to their response to zoster vaccine;
however, those with hematologic malignancies on anti-CD20
therapies (alone or with other chemotherapies) who received Zostavax mounted significant T cell responses (Agency EM. Shingrix
2020; available from:
https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/medicines/
human/EPAR/shingrix
, accessed 6/29/2021). Immunogenicity of
zoster vaccine for those on other biologics, abatacept, and JAK
inhibitors have not been reported. Currently there are no published data evaluating the influence of any DMARDs upon the
immunogenicity of RZV.

### Safety of Zoster Vaccines in Immunocompromised Patients

Recent studies indicate that the zoster vaccine live (ZVL)/Zostavax may be safer in certain immunocompromised groups than
previously thought. A cohort study of Medicare beneficiaries age
60 and older did not show increased incidence of herpes zoster
infection 42 days after vaccination.
ZVL was also safely administered to a small cohort of SLE patients without increased risk of
infection 12 weeks post-vaccine.
A recent randomized control study of over 600 patients on
TNF inhibitors receiving ZVL versus placebo did not have any
confirmed varicella infections up to 1 year after vaccination
with live vaccine.
Another randomized control trial of 112 RA
patients, 55 of whom received ZVL 2 to 3 weeks prior to initiation of tofacitinib versus placebo, only 1 patient of 55 (2%)
developed cutaneous vaccine strain varicella infection, and later
testing found that she lacked pre-vaccination immunity to varicella.
Together, these data suggest that ZVL can be safely used
in those actively using TNF inhibitors with or without concomitant methotrexate or low-dose steroids, but for those using JAK
inhibitors, the data are less clear. One study evaluated the effect
of ZVL in subset of RA patients who received the vaccine prior to
initiation of tofacitinib (5 mg twice a day) or tofacitinib combined
with MTX or adalimumab (40 mg every 14 days) plus MTX.
The risk of herpes zoster infection was similar between the three
treatment groups and similar to the nonvaccinated group as well.
Notably, all patients on tofacitinib who developed zoster infection
also received corticosteroids at baseline.
Currently just a few observational studies exist evaluating the
reactogenicity and safety of the recombinant zoster vaccine in
rheumatic disease populations. One study that assessed the risk of
disease flare after receiving zoster recombinant adjuvanted vaccine
found an incidence of 7% or less, and none required changes in
anti-rheumatic regimen.
Another retrospective study of patients
with immune-mediated inflammatory diseases who had received
the recombinant zoster vaccine revealed that the vaccine was generally well tolerated, although a mild disease flare was not uncommon the first 12 weeks post-vaccination.
A higher incidence of
flares was seen in patients on glucocorticoids and thus higher disease activity.

### Recommendations

The 2022 ACR guidelines do not recommend live vaccines in
patients actively receiving immunosuppressive therapy. This
includes ZVL,
and such therapy should be stopped for some
time period before receipt of a live vaccine. In areas of the
world where RZV is available, the ACR recommends giving
this vaccine to those over 18 years old using immunosuppressive therapy.
The EULAR suggests that ZVL may be considered in highrisk immunosuppressed patients given the data stated above under
“Safety of Zoster Vaccines in Immunocompromised Patients.”
Under limited circumstances, if ZVL is used over RZV, then similar to other live vaccines, this live vaccine should be administered
4 weeks prior to initiation of bDMARDs or tsDMARDs but not
during treatment.
Per EULAR recommendations, in those with
uncertain varicella exposure, evaluation of serostatus can be considered before administration of live vaccine to prevent primary
infection with the live vaccine.

### Timing of ZVL and RZV in Relation to DMARD Therapy

Direct evidence for optimal therapy hold time prior to live vaccines is missing, but the 2022 ACR guidelines recommends a
pre-vaccination hold time of 4 weeks for csDMARDs, one dosing interval for bDMARDs, and post-vaccination hold time of 4
weeks for all DMARDs.
No therapy schedule change is needed
for immunosuppressive therapy except rituximab, which should
be delayed by 2 weeks.
For RZV, there is no published data evaluating whether DMARDs adversely affect vaccine response and
whether holding might improve such response.

## SARS-CoV-2 Vaccination

### Background

A growing number of SARS-CoV-2 vaccines are in use worldwide,
including mRNA, adenoviral vector, protein subunit, and inactivated virus vaccines.
On August 31, 2022, the U.S. Food and
Drug Administration (FDA) also approved the bivalent mRNA
COVID-19 vaccine booster, which contains the original SARSCoV-2 strain used in the monovalent vaccines and the newer variants of SARS-CoV-2: Omicron BA.4 and BA.5.
The BNT162b2 (Pfizer) mRNA vaccine was 95% effective (95% CI, 90.3 to 97.6), and the mRNA01273 (Moderna) vaccine was 94.1% effective (95% CI, 89.3 to 96.8)
in preventing symptomatic COVID-19 infection following
the second dose in phase III trials.
The Ad26.COV2.S
(Janssen/Johnson & Johnson) vaccine single-dose vaccine was
66.9% effective (95% CI, 59.0 to 73.4), and the ChAdOx1
nCoV-19/AZD1222 (University of Oxford, AstraZeneca,
and the Serum Institute of India) vaccine was 70.4% effective

(95% CI, 54.8 to 80.6) following the second dose in phase
III trials.
SARS-CoV-2 vaccine immunogenicity can be
measured by humoral IgG to spike protein or cellular T cell
reactivity via interferon (IFN)-
γ
response to SARS-CoV-2
peptide.
We still do not yet know how immunogenicity
cutoffs correlate with efficacy, as no threshold of antibody
or cell-mediated response has clearly been established as
protective.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on SARS CoV-2 Vaccine Efficacy

Early data in this setting are largely consistent with that from
other vaccine studies. Data suggest that rituximab, glucocorticoids, MTX, abatacept, mycophenolate mofetil, and JAK inhibitors impair SARS-CoV-2 vaccine responses to varying extent in
many patients.
The largest observational study to date evaluated the
BNT162b2 (Pfizer) mRNA vaccine in 686 patients with
rheumatic diseases. Compared with controls, in whom 100%
seroconverted to vaccination (i.e., newly positive spike IgG),
seroconversion was reduced among patients on rituximab (39%,
P
< .0001), mycophenolate mofetil (64%,
P
< .0001), abatacept
(71%,
P
< .0001), JAK inhibitors (90%,
P
= .02), and MTX
(92%,
P
= .02), while other DMARDs (leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine, TNF, IL-6, and IL-17 inhibitors) did not significantly affect seroconversion.
Another prospective study of 133
patients with immune-mediated inflammatory diseases on various DMARD therapies and 53 controls vaccinated with mRNA
vaccines found that rituximab significantly reduced mRNA
vaccine immunogenicity, JAK inhibitors and MTX moderately
reduced antibody titers, and other therapies (TNF, IL-12/23,
and integrin inhibitors) had a modest impact on antibody
formation.
Risk factors for a poor humoral response on rituximab include
a shorter duration between rituximab dose and vaccine and lack
of B-cell reconstitution.
Rituximab-treated patients vaccinated 6 months after their last rituximab dose had seropositivity
rates of only around 20%, and those vaccinated 1 year after the
last rituximab dose had rates around 50%.
Despite a reduced
humoral response, early data suggest that at least a subset of rituximab-treated patients mount a cellular vaccine response similar to
healthy controls, such that the net impact on clinical protection
is not clear.
MTX appears to reduce some aspects of the SARS-CoV-2
vaccine response.
One study found adequate humoral
antibody titers (defined as IgG to spike protein >5000 units)
compared with healthy controls.
Patients on MTX, however,
had reduced activated CD8
T cells response but a preserved
CD4
T cell response.
In another study, 84% of all MTXtreated patients and 92% of patients on MTX monotherapy
seroconverted, compared with 100% of controls (
P
< .05).
This is of increased significance in older individuals in whom
MTX hold provides benefit in terms of improving immunogenicity to the COVID-19 vaccine.
TNF inhibitors appear to mildly reduce SARS-CoV-2 postvaccination titers but do not seem to significantly affect rates
of seroconversion. Among 865 infliximab-treated inflammatory
bowel disease patients, a single dose of the BNT162b2 mRNA
vaccine or the ChAdOx1 nCoV-19 adenoviral vaccine was associated with lower antibody concentrations and seroconversion
rates compared with those on vedolizumab.
There was
no difference in the rate of seroconversion, however, in those
studies after a second dose of the mRNA vaccine.
Another
study of patients on TNF inhibitors fully vaccinated with
BNT162b2 mRNA vaccine showed no difference in seroconversion rates compared with healthy controls.
Whether these
reductions in quantitative humoral responses are of clinical significance is unknown.
JAK inhibitors likely reduce antibody titers and have a mild
effect on seroconversion, although the clinical importance of
these observations is unknown, and data are scant. Ten patients
on JAK inhibitors in one study had a >6-fold reduction in titers
compared with controls.
However, in another study of 21
patients on JAK inhibitor monotherapy and 24 on JAK inhibitor combination therapy, 19 (90%) and 22 (92%), respectively,
seroconverted, neither of which were significantly different from
controls.

### Safety in Patients With Rheumatic Diseases

Because of its substantial immunogenicity, there is concern
that the SARS-CoV-2 vaccine may induce flares in patients
with inflammatory diseases. In a large multicenter study, there
was no evidence of significant disease flare across different
autoimmune inflammatory rheumatic diseases after receiving
mRNA SARS-CoV-2 vaccine.
Data from the EULAR Coronavirus Vaccine (COVAX) physician-reported registry also
showed similar results with rare reports of disease flare after use
of mRNA vaccines and AstraZeneca.
Adverse effect concerns,
however, have been supported by reports of thrombocytopenic purpura
and myocarditis/pericarditis after vaccination.
There have additionally been observational reports
of new onset immune-mediated disease
and/or disease flares
after SARS-CoV-2 vaccination,
which must be weighed
against the risk of immune-mediated disease resulting from
SARS-CoV-2 infection itself.
One study documented two fatalities post-vaccination: one
ANCA-vasculitis patient developed cutaneous vasculitis with subsequent fatal sepsis 3 weeks after the second vaccine dose and the
second had a history of cardiovascular disease and died of a myocardial infarction 2 months after the second vaccine dose. Other
adverse events of note were two cases of uveitis, one case of pericarditis, six cases of herpes zoster, and one case of herpes labialis.
Small prospective studies thus far have not found an increase in
underlying inflammatory disease activity measures after SARSCoV-2 vaccination.
More prospective data are needed to
understand the safety of these vaccines and risk of disease flare in
patients with rheumatic diseases.

### Recommendations

The original ACR guidelines provided detailed recommendations
for management of DMARD therapy in the setting of the SARSCoV-2 vaccine. The 2022 updated ACR guidelines, however,
excluded updated recommendations for COVID-19 due to the
fast-growing pool of evidence and rapid changes in recommendations based on new data. With the latest bivalent COVID-19
booster, the CDC has updated the vaccine schedule for vaccine
boosters in immunocompetent and immunosuppressed patients.
These recommendations include that patients 12 years of age and
older, after receiving the primary vaccine series, should receive one

bivalent mRNA booster. The new one-time bivalent booster dose
replaces all prior monovalent mRNA booster vaccines for those
12 and older.

### Timing of COVID-19 Vaccine

The 2021 EULAR and ACR guidelines are in line regarding
optimal timing of COVID-19 vaccine series, but the ACR offers
much more detail. In general, for patients who have not initiated
immunomodulatory therapy, COVID-19 vaccination should be
given prior to initiation of therapy if possible due to impaired
immune response with certain medications.
The following recommendations are based on the latest (version 5) ACR COVID-19 task force recommendations to help
increase immune response to the COVID-19 vaccine primary
series and boosters. Patients on intravenous (IV) abatacept should
receive their vaccine 1 week prior to the next dose of IV treatment,
while those on subcutaneous abatacept should hold their treatment (if disease activity allows) for one to two doses after receipt
of a COVID-19 vaccine.
Patients being treated with belimumab
should hold therapy for 1 to 2 weeks after COVID-19 vaccination.
IV cyclophosphamide should be timed a week after receipt
of a COVID-19 vaccine when possible.
Those on JAK inhibitors and mycophenolate should withhold therapy for 1 to 2 weeks
after a COVID-19 vaccine if disease activity allows.
No therapy
interruption is needed in those on hydroxychloroquine and intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG).
The task force failed to reach
consensus on whether to temporarily disrupt the following therapies after receiving COVID-19 primary vaccination series and
boosters: TNF inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors, IL-1 inhibitors, IL-17
inhibitors, IL-12/23 inhibitors, and IL-23 inhibitors.
The ACR
task force also does not specify timing of COVID-19 vaccines for
those on B cell–depleting therapy and recommends discussing
optimal timing with their rheumatology provider.
An evidence-based vaccine guideline published in 2021
(nonsystematic review) recommended that any vaccine including
COVID-19 vaccine be given at least 4 weeks before or 6 months
after the administration of rituximab. If this is not achievable,
then they recommend vaccination at time when immunoglobulin levels are normal.
They also recommend COVID-19 vaccine during the inactive period of rheumatic diseases and while
patient is on lower doses of corticosteroids.
Based on data
extrapolated from other vaccines, they recommend skipping 1 to
2 weekly doses of MTX after a COVID-19 vaccine to balance
between effectiveness of the vaccine and avoiding disease exacerbation.
Despite limited data showing that immunogenicity to
the COVID-19 vaccine is not likely affected by TNF inhibitors,
secukinumab, tocilizimab, IL-1 inhibitors, and abatacept, one
group
report that vaccination prior to the next scheduled dose
with a plan to restart biologics after 14 days could allow time for
antibody development. They also recommend holding tofacitinib
prior to vaccination and resuming after 14 days.

## Hepatitis B Vaccination

### Background

There are three different single-antigen recombinant HBV vaccines
available worldwide and several combination vaccines. However,
the most common HBV vaccine is a yeast-derived single-antigen
vaccine. HBV vaccine immunogenicity is measured by anti-HBV
surface antibody.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on Vaccine Efficacy

TNF and IL-12/23 inhibitors reduce HBV vaccine immunogenicity, but most other immunosuppressive agents have not been
extensively evaluated.
TNF inhibitors decrease HBV vaccine immunogenicity,
but there may be differences between
TNF inhibitors: lower antibody response rates for infliximab
and higher response rates for etanercept.
In a small study,
ustekinumab was found to moderately reduce HBV response.
In one trial, using high-dose HBV vaccine in DMARD-treated
patients resulted in higher antibody response rates compared
with a standard-dose vaccine, but this result did not reach statistical significance.

### Recommendations

In the United States HBV vaccination is recommended for
adults at risk for HBV.
Ideally patients who require
HBV vaccination should be vaccinated prior to starting
DMARD therapy, particularly for high-risk patients starting
rituximab.

## Human Papilloma Virus Vaccination

### Background

Three HPV vaccines are approved; however, the 9-valent vaccine
is the only HPV vaccine currently available in the United States.
Women with rheumatic diseases on immunosuppressive therapies
are at increased risk of HPV and cervical cancer, especially patients
with SLE.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on Vaccine Efficacy

The effects of MTX and TNF inhibitors on HPV vaccine
immunogenicity have been studied in patients with juvenile
idiopathic arthritis, juvenile dermatomyositis, inflammatory
bowel disease, and systemic lupus erythematosus. In these
studies, MTX and TNF inhibitors did not affect vaccine seroconversion rates.
SLE patients on mycophenolate mofetil
combined with low-dose glucocorticoids moderately reduced
seroconversion rates for HPV6 and HPV16 but not other
subtypes of HPV vaccine.
Other DMARDs have not been
evaluated.

### Recommendations

The CDC recommends HPV vaccination for all patients (regardless of sex) at age 11 or 12 up through age 26.
The ACIP recommends HPV vaccine for all ages 11 to 26 and in ages 26 to
45 based on shared decision making. The EULAR HPV vaccine
recommendations align with those for the general population.
The 2022 ACR guidelines conditionally recommend considering
vaccination against HPV in those between ages 26 and 45 who
are on immunosuppressive medications and not previously vaccinated.
This recommendation was not specified to a certain
sex, however.

## Tetanus Vaccination

### Background

The tetanus toxoid, reduced diphtheria toxoid, and acellular pertussis (Tdap) vaccine is a single-dose vaccine. Tetanus toxoid is a T
cell–dependent antigen. Tetanus vaccine immunogenicity is typically measured by antitetanus toxoid IgG concentrations 4 weeks
post-vaccination, where an antibody concentration of
≥
0.10 IU/
mL is typically considered seroprotective; however, an endpoint
of
≥
4-fold increase in antibody concentration is also sometimes
used.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on Vaccine Efficacy

Rituximab reduces tetanus vaccine immunogenicity; however,
the degree of effect differs in various studies.
Studies of
TNF inhibitors, JAK inhibitors, and abatacept suggest a modest impairment in immune response.
IL-6, IL-17, and
IL-12/23 inhibitors have not been found to affect tetanus vaccine
immunogenicity.
Rituximab may have less of an effect on tetanus immunogenicity because of likely prior Tdap/DTaP vaccinations in most
patients, leading to tetanus-specific memory B cells. In one study,
RA patients on MTX monotherapy versus MTX + rituximab
mounted similar humoral response.
Another study, however,
showed lower rates of protective antibody titers in patients on
rituximab.

### Recommendations and Timing of Tdap Vaccine

Adults and adolescents should receive a Tdap followed by
boosters of tetanus and diphtheria toxoids (Td) every 10 years
or when indicated due to a wound, although a booster may
be either Td or Tdap.
Tetanus vaccination should ideally
be done prior to starting rituximab therapy. The 2022 ACR
guidelines recommend timing vaccination for when the next
rituximab dose is due and then holding rituximab for 2 weeks
after vaccination. No other changes in therapy schedule are
recommended.

## Yellow Fever Vaccination

### Background

The YF vaccine is a live vaccine and recommended to immunocompetent persons who live or travel to endemic areas.
YF
vaccine immunogenicity is measured by post-vaccination neutralizing antibody titers.

### Effect of DMARD Therapy on Vaccine Efficacy

Few studies have addressed yellow fever vaccine immunogenicity in immunocompromised patients given this is a live vaccine.
In a Brazilian study involving 31 patients on biologics who were
inadvertently revaccinated (patients had primary immunity from
a prior vaccination), the patients had lower but adequate antibody titers.
Another observational study of 17 patients on infliximab + MTX achieved satisfactory antibody levels in all but
1 patient.
In another small study, all 15 patients on MTX
achieved seroprotection.
Patients on corticosteroids (mean, 7
mg/day; range, 5 to 20 mg/day), 18 of 34 of whom were vaccinenaive, had satisfactory vaccine titers.

### Yellow Fever Vaccine Safety in Patients With Rheumatic Diseases

Small studies suggest that the yellow fever vaccine may be safer
than previously thought for patients on MTX, infliximab, and
corticosteroids at <20 mg/day.
A retrospective Swiss
study of 92 patients on immunosuppressive medications who
received the yellow fever vaccine (primary or booster) developed
similar rates of side effects as healthy controls (controls had a similar proportion of patients with a primary YF vaccine history) and
no serious adverse events.
A prospective study of 15 patients
on MTX (
≤
20 mg/week) receiving a primary YF vaccine found
slightly increased rates of yellow fever RNA viremia in MTXtreated patients relative to controls (
P
> .39); however, these
levels were never of clinical significance.
In the earlier mentioned study from Brazil, patients revaccinated on biologics had
no adverse events.
In an observational study involving 17 RA
patients who underwent yellow fever revaccination 1 month after
infliximab, none developed yellow fever symptoms.

### Recommendations

The 2022 ACR guidelines conditionally recommend against
administration of live-attenuated virus vaccines to patients on
csDMARDs, bDMARDs, and tsDMARDs. Exceptions are made
for those on low-dose immunosuppression defined as methotrexate less than or equal to 0.4 mg/kg/week, azathioprine doses less
than or equal to 3 mg/kg/day, prednisone less than 20 mg/day, or
alternate-day glucocorticoids.

### Timing of Yellow Fever Vaccine

For travel considerations or for patients living in endemic areas at
very high risk, patients and their providers may consider holding
immunosuppressive therapy for vaccination. Direct evidence for
optimal therapy hold time prior to live vaccines is missing, but the
ACR guidelines recommend pre-vaccination hold time of 4 weeks
for csDMARDs, one dosing interval for bDMARDs, and postvaccination hold time of 4 weeks for all DMARDs.

## Conclusions

Taken together, we note that for the majority of vaccine-preventable illnesses, DMARD dosing and vaccine schedules can proceed
independent of one another. The notable exceptions are rituximab and methotrexate. To maximize immune response, patients
should have as much time as possible since last rituximab dose
(recommended to be given at next rituximab due date with delay
of rituximab by 2 weeks) and methotrexate should be held 1 to
2 weeks after vaccination. Further research is necessary to clarify
the benefits/risks of potentially holding other DMARDs for certain vaccinations. Specific evidence of Shingrix immunogenicity
and efficacy in rheumatic diseases is currently missing, and understanding optimal ways of using this vaccine and others in the
setting of various DMARD exposures continues to represent an
unmet need in the field of rheumatology (
