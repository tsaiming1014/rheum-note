## Introduction

Whipple’s disease was first discovered to be an infectious disease by
Relman and colleagues.
Tropheryma whippleii
is a gut pathogen
that can also reside in the small intestine of healthy subjects, suggesting that Whipple’s disease is one of the first chronic rheumatic
diseases causally linked to a gut microorganism. Associations of
oral or gut microorganisms with rheumatic diseases, however,
are long-standing. Over a century ago, rheumatoid arthritis (RA)
had been associated with microorganisms and microbially driven
inflammatory disease at barrier sites, in particular the mouth. In
the late 19th and early 20th century, RA was described to co-occur
with periodontitis (PD), and periodontal treatment improved disease activity when evaluated months later.
The incidence of RA
was linked with the spread of sugar trade, which promoted the
development of PD.
More recent studies have associated specific
periodontal pathogens with RA, which are described in more
detail below.
Other inflammatory triggers on mucosal barriers such as the
gut and the lungs were also recognized more recently in patients
with RA.
The overall concept of mucosal sites participating in the
pathogenesis of RA was coined the mucosal origins hypothesis.
The hygiene hypothesis connects epidemiologically the decline in
pathogen infections due to increased hygiene with the rapidly rising
incidence of immune-mediated diseases over the last decades.
The
central theme of this hypothesis is that modern living conditions
provide too little microbial stimuli for the hosts’ immune systems to
develop properly and that may lead to allergic and autoimmune diseases in genetically prone individuals.
Besides RA, other rheumatic
diseases, particularly spondyloarthritides (SpA), a cluster of diseases
characterized by shared clinical features and genetic predisposition
such as human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-B27, have historically
been associated with mucosal inflammation and dysbiosis. Dysbiosis is an ill-defined term for a deviation of the normal composition
of the microbiota (the collection of all microbes colonizing a host).
The first evidence for subclinical gut involvement in the SpA
spectrum of diseases was provided in the mid-1980s by a histologic
study in patients with HLA-B27-positive reactive arthritis (ReA)
presenting with oligoarthritis with or without axial involvement.
In this small study of 12 patients, all had signs of microscopic
inflammation in the terminal ileum that was clinically asymptomatic in the majority of cases. It was suggested to be a subclinical form of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
In the following
years, similar findings were observed in larger groups of ReA and
SpA patients with or without peripheral arthritis irrespective of
the HLA-B27 status.
In 1987, one group found signs of ileitis,
colitis, or ileocolitis in 62% of ReA/SpA patients.
The authors
described two histologic patterns of gut inflammation: acute and
chronic. The acute type of inflammation resembled infectious
enterocolitis characterized by infiltration of the epithelium with
neutrophils and eosinophils, crypt abscess formation, and infiltration of the lamina propria with polymorphonuclear cells. The normal mucosal structure was, however, preserved. The chronic type
of inflammation was histologically indistinguishable from early
Crohn’s disease and characterized by a disturbed mucosal architecture, crypt distortion, villous blunting and fusion, increased mixed
lamina propria cellularity, and the presence of basal lymphoid

aggregates. Repeat ileocolonoscopy in a subgroup of SpA patients
showed that gut and joint inflammation generally evolve in parallel. Gut inflammation disappeared in patients reaching articular
remission, while it was persistent in patients with ongoing active
joint disease.
Gut inflammation was persistent especially in
patients with chronic gut inflammation; this phenotype was later
identified as carrying the highest risk for evolution toward Crohn’s
disease (6.5% over 5 years).
Crohn’s disease–associated SpA is
the most obvious historical connection with the gut microbiome.
The associations of gut microbial dysbiosis with other rheumatic
diseases developed later and are described further below.

### The Oral Microbiome

The oral cavity is a barrier site maintaining an important crosstalk between oral bacteria and the host immune system. Unlike
in the GI tract, where microorganisms are compartmentalized
within the mucosal layers, the oral cavity is distinct where bacteria
may form biofilms on the surfaces of the teeth. The mucosal barrier may be broken by bacteria during infection when pathogenic
strains penetrate the underlying soft tissue, for example, when
invading the periodontal epithelium around the teeth. Like the GI
mucosal immune system, antimicrobial peptides, immunoglobulin (Ig)A, and mucins provide a barrier-sealing function against
infection. However, mouth epithelia are multilayered and vary
in their permeability and function.
They include both keratinized and nonkeratinized epithelia, which differ substantially
from intestinal epithelia. These epithelia provide added protection
against invasive pathogens and also promote oral health by sustaining immune tolerance. Various interactions between the oral
microbiota and the host immune system have been suggested to
underlie numerous disease states such as RA or IBD, metabolic
(type 2 diabetes), cardiovascular, and even neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s disease.
Moreover, there seems to
be an intermucosal connection between oral and gut inflammation (gut-mouth axis), both through translocation of pathobionts
(directly) and generation of pathogenic Th17 cells (indirectly).
These observations suggest that the oral microbiota may influence
inflammatory responses both locally and systemically, but causal
links are largely missing.

### The Lung Microbiome

Compared with the gut and oral microbiomes, much less is
known about the role of the lung microbiome in health and disease, in part due to the difficulty studying the microbiota composition or function in the respiratory tract. Importantly, the
tracheobronchial tree maintains environmental gradients that
influence the colonizing microbiota based on oxygen tension,
blood perfusion, and pH.
In cystic fibrosis, the three-dimensional microbiome revealed various genera in distinct areas of
the human lung.
In rheumatic diseases, such sophisticated
studies have not yet been performed but could shed light on
the biogeography of the lung microbiota in RA-interstitial lung
disease (ILD), for instance.
Airway inflammation and autoimmunity have been linked in
cystic fibrosis and RA with citrullination as a post-translational
modification of autoantigens.
Smoking, a well-established risk
factor for developing RA, has been put forth as one mechanism of
citrullination of autoantigens.
Whether smoking affects the lung
microbiota in RA, and thereby its pathogenesis, is unknown, but
cigarette smoking has demonstrable effects on intestinal inflammation and the gut microbiota.
Mechanisms of how the lung
microbiota contributes to diseases are best understood in animal
models. Mouse models have revealed that the lung microbiota is
influenced by distal microbial signals from the gut microbiota.
In fact, a gut-lung axis is well established and influences airway
inflammation, autoimmunity,
and host resistance.
It may also
explain how smoking contributes to the pathogenesis in RA. The
murine lung microbiome has recently been demonstrated to also
modulate brain autoimmunity,
suggesting a broader role of
pulmonary microbiota in distant organ inflammation than previously appreciated. If and how the lung microbiome contributes
to RA and other rheumatic diseases with pulmonary involvement
such as polymyositis, scleroderma, or sarcoidosis remains to be
determined. Similarly, genetic polymorphisms such as those in
mucin genes
(MUC5B)
associated with pulmonary fibrosis and
RA-ILD
that affect the human lung microbiota composition
and thereby disease pathogenesis are obvious areas to investigate in
this understudied field of respiratory microbiota–host interactions
in autoimmune diseases.

### The Skin Microbiome

The skin microbiota colonizing a barrier site frequently inflamed
in rheumatic diseases is an active area of basic and translational
research that was recently summarized.
The skin microbial
biomass is much less than in the gut microbiome, which poses
technical challenges and leaves skin microbiome studies prone to
experimental and environmental biases. The human skin microbiome, however, is more stable than the gut microbiome,
which
facilitates longitudinal studies besides the ease of access of this
barrier surface compared with the small intestinal or colonic surfaces. On the other hand, obvious topographic differences across
the human skin support different bacterial and fungal microbiota
based on the specific conditions within each niche (such as moist
vs. dry sites).
Overall, the human skin microbiome is complex to
study and requires body site–matched sampling in both patients
and control subjects.

the cutaneous microbiome is regulated by various components of
the host immune system, in particular by antimicrobial peptides
and type-17 immunity (reviewed in ref. 49). Dysregulation in the

the 16S rDNA (reviewed in refs. 59-61). Technical variations
and confounder effects are to be considered (

besides

### Culture-Based Approaches

Culture-based approaches are not as unbiased as sequencingbased approaches to the human microbiome but have the obvious advantage of recovering viable bacteria. Isolating bacterial
strains for downstream mechanistic in vitro and in vivo studies
is a crucial step to understand host–microbiota interactions that
go beyond association studies. Aerobic cultures are more prone to
contamination than anaerobic cultures that require an anaerobic
chamber and significant microbiologic expertise. One downside
of attempting to culture gut microbiota is that they are adapted
to the GI environment and are often difficult to recover in conventional culture media. In addition, some species such as
Actinomyces
spp. are slow-growing and require longer than usual culture
periods. Using rich, gut microbiota–adapted culture media and
developing clonally arrayed culture collections allowed for culturing most human gut bacteria.
Similar culture-based approaches
linked to whole-genome sequencing and computational modeling defined new isolates of previously unculturable bacteria
and
revealed eco-evolutionary dynamics within individuals.
Studying viable murine or human gut bacteria isolated from
healthy subjects, patients or disease models can be advantageous
for many reasons. First, some pathogenic aspects such as expression of cross-reactive proteins are only evident at a certain growth
state.
Second, cultured isolates can be used for gavage into germfree mice. Utilizing susceptible animal models, gnotobiotic in vivo
studies can support the pathogenic role in rheumatic diseases such

as lupus, spondyloarthritis, or RA.
Lastly, culture-based
approaches allow for identification of low-abundance strains
in largely sterile tissues of internal organs that would otherwise
be missed or difficult to discern from contaminating microbial
DNA.
Culturing translocated microbiota has led to the identification of pathobionts in extraintestinal organ-specific and systemic autoimmune diseases.

### Gnotobiotics

As pointed out in the previous section, culturing bacteria from
human or murine microbiomes is useful for mechanistic in-vivo
studies. A highly reductionist approach to studying the role of
the microbiota in rheumatic diseases is using germ-free animals
that are devoid of any microorganisms. These can be achieved by
germ-free rederivation of genetic disease models or by acquiring
commercially available germ-free mice of common strains. Germfree animals can be gavaged with viable bacteria, either single bacteria—so-called monocolonizations—or multiple with a defined
consortium. These approaches are called gnotobiotic and reduce
the complexities of a full set of microbiota and have been useful
in studying host–microbiota interactions in autoimmune diseases
(reviewed in refs. 6, 18).
An alternative approach to reducing the complexity is the
depletion of the resident gut microbiota with oral antibiotics,
which has the advantage of a normal immune maturation after
weaning of animals but the obvious downside of not completely
removing all microbiota.
Cohousing of mice with different
microbiota is another functional manipulation that leads to transfer of microbiota via coprophagy from one host to another with
either dominant or subordinate effects, depending on the presence of pathobionts and their interactions with other microbiota.
So-called microorganism-phenotype triangulation may identify
causal bacteria from complex microbial communities by studying diseased, healthy, and intermediate phenotypes.
Finally, gut
microbiota can also evolve within a host, changing their pathogenic potential over time.
This concept of within-host evolution
may explain the time-dependent penetrance of systemic autoimmune diseases in susceptible hosts, which would be difficult to
study without gnotobiotic models.

### Emerging Technologies

Recent advances in studying host–microbiota interactions led to
new insights in health and disease but have not yet been applied
to rheumatic diseases. A few examples are given to illustrate which
technologies and emerging approaches may enhance the study
of the microbiota in rheumatology. For instance, organoids are
increasingly used in the study of immunologic diseases (reviewed
in ref. 84). Bacterial-organoid coculture systems allow for mechanistic dissection of host–microbiota interactions as done with
pathobionts identified in primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC).
Organs on-a-chip are increasingly tested for disease-related
research. Addition of gut bacteria is particularly challenging given
the anaerobic culture conditions often needed. The development
of an anaerobic intestine on a chip
may serve as a future discovery tool to test host–microbiota interactions and therapeutic
interventions.
A murine 3D-gut culture system enables the ex-vivo study of
microbiota-immune interactions in the context of the nervous system and other cell types normally lacking in in vitro systems.
This unique experimental setup enables the study of human
microbiota from multiple sclerosis patients and its metabolites
in the pathogenesis of this chronic, neuroinflammatory disease.
Infusing human microbiota from rheumatic disease patients in a
3D-intestine ex vivo should equally allow for new insights into
host–microbiota interactions in the field of rheumatology.

## The Microbiota in Rheumatic Diseases

### Spondyloarthritis

Gut and Joints Are Interconnected in Spondyloarthritides
The close relationship between gut and joint inflammation in SpA
has been recognized for decades. The association between SpA and
IBD is not only based on clinical observations but also arises from
a genetic, immunologic, and therapeutic point of view.
As for the genetic linkage, SpA and IBD share several susceptibility loci such as
IL23R
,
IL12B
,
STAT3
,
CARD9
, and
TNFSF15
.
Another example are polymorphisms in the
CARD15 (NOD2)
gene. Individuals that are homozygous or compound heterozygous
for mutations in
CARD15
carry a 20- to 40-fold increased risk of
developing IBD.
CARD15
codes for a protein that acts as an intracellular pattern-recognition receptor. Upon binding to bacterial
components, it activates nuclear factor kappa B, a key transcriptional regulator controlling the expression of pro-inflammatory
cytokines, adhesion molecules, and growth factors.
One group
found an association between
CARD15
and isolated sacroiliitis,
although the association with ankylosing spondylitis (AS) could not
be confirmed in other studies.
On the other hand, another study
found a similarly high frequency of
CARD15
mutation carriers in
SpA patients with chronic gut inflammation (38%) and in patients
with Crohn’s disease (49%).
These proportions were significantly
different from SpA patients without gut inflammation or a control
population without SpA, suggesting a genetic link between intestinal and joint inflammation.
Another study analyzing mucosal
gene expression found similar transcriptomic profiles in the intestine of SpA patients (especially those with chronic subclinical gut
inflammation) and patients with Crohn’s disease.
Several lines of immunologic evidence support the link between
gut and joint inflammation. First, upregulation of the adhesion
molecule E-cadherin has been demonstrated in IBD and in SpA
patients with subclinical gut inflammation.
Its ligand
α
β
integrin was also upregulated on mucosal T cells from AS patients in
the absence of histologic signs of inflammation.
Interestingly,
an enrichment of T cells carrying
α
β

identified in inflamed synovial tissue of SpA patients, suggesting
an intestinal origin.
More recently, loss of the mucosal barrier
integrity was described in patients with SpA, with marked changes
in zonulin expression in the intestinal epithelium.
These findings mirror the emerging picture that loss of barrier integrity represents a key step in the development of intestinal inflammation
in SpA. This concept is not restricted to SpA but is also emerging in other rheumatic diseases such as lupus (discussed below)
and RA.
Second, in ReA, identical clonally expanded T cells
have been identified in the intestine and the joint.
However,
only a few expanded T cell clones were shared by both compartments. Third, simultaneous upregulation of a macrophage subpopulation expressing scavenger receptor CD163 was observed
in the synovium and intestine (including noninflamed areas) of
SpA patients, illustrating that not only T cells but also antigenpresenting cells account for the relationship between joint and
gut inflammation.
In addition, not only macrophages but also
activated lymphocytes isolated from the human intestinal lamina

propria are able to bind vessels from inflamed synovial tissue
in vitro via various adhesion molecules.
Fourth, gut-associated
IL-17- and IL-22-positive innate lymphoid cells were found in the
peripheral blood and synovial fluid of patients with AS.
In addition, there is emerging evidence of translocation of
lipopolysaccharide throughout the bowel wall in spondyloarthritis.
This is in line with earlier studies on the presence of bacterial
DNA in synovial tissues from SpA patients.
Finally, chronic TNF overproduction, which occurs in the
TNF

ARE mouse model, is sufficient to lead to combined gut
and joint inflammation and tissue damage through TNFRI signaling in synovial fibroblasts and intestinal myofibroblasts (reviewed
in ref. 16). Experimental evidence using pre-clinical mouse models
and ex vivo analyses suggest immune cell trafficking from the gut to
the joint, although this has not been firmly established in humans.
Other pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-23 may also induce
combined gut and joint inflammation. However, although IL-23
induces peripheral joint inflammation in an animal model, only very
limited signs of sacroiliitis are seen compared to the TNF-overproducing model.
This finding mirrors the failure of IL-23 blockade
in treatment of patients with AS.

Intestinal Dysbiosis in Spondyloarthritis
Many factors may influence the gut microbial composition, such
as sex, comorbidities, diet, immunity, infections, antibiotic use,
route of delivery, hygiene, and stress, among others.
Gut
dysbiosis, in particular a restricted diversity of the gut microbiota, is emerging as a common alteration among systemic inflammatory diseases. Several research groups have reported decreased
bacterial richness and diversity in SpA patients.
Some taxa
such as
Ruminococcus gnavus
in fecal samples and
Dialister
spp.
in mucosal biopsies are more abundant in SpA and correlate
with disease activity in these patients.
In another study, AS
patients demonstrated increased
Prevotella copri
and
Bifidobacterium
, while
Bacteroides
spp. were decreased.
Also, in patients
with psoriatic arthritis and skin psoriasis, decreased microbial
diversity was observed compared with healthy controls. Patients
with psoriatic arthritis have a lower relative abundance of multiple intestinal bacteria, especially reportedly beneficial taxa.
Interestingly, the host genetic background seems to drive dysbiosis. In healthy subjects without AS, significant differences were
observed in microbiota composition between HLA-B27-positive
and -negative individuals.
Studies in HLA-B27-transgenic
rats similarly showed significant differences in the gut microbiota between transgenic animals and wild type animals, with an
increase in
Prevotella
spp. and a decrease in
Rikenellaceae
.
Furthermore, it was shown in animal models of SpA that the effect
of HLA-B27 on dysbiosis depends on the interplay between
the host genetic background and the environment, although
immune dysregulation involving IL-23/IL-17, TNF, and IL-1
pathways also plays into it. An ecological model of dysbiosis
was put forth with the effects of multiple microorganisms contributing to the aberrant immune response rather than a single
pathobiont or a small number of microorganisms driving the
pathogenesis.
Evidence for the pivotal role of the gut microbiota in arthritis initiation comes from the HLA-B27-transgenic rat model that
spontaneously develops similar manifestations as SpA patients,
namely colitis, peripheral arthritis, sacroiliitis, and spondylitis,
respectively. When rederived in germ-free conditions, these rats
were completely protected from colitis and arthritis while recolonization with commensal microbiota was sufficient to trigger gut
and joint inflammation.
In humans, the role of gut dysbiosis in modulating the local and systemic immune responses
leading to SpA-related manifestations is not yet fully understood.
One study revealed a unique role for the bacterial genus
Dialister
which appeared to be more abundant in microbiota from SpA
patients with microscopic gut inflammation compared with those
without.
In addition, bacteria such as
Akkermansia
and
Prevotella
can contribute to intestinal barrier disruption through their
mucolytic capacities. On the other hand, dysbiosis may equally
affect distant immune functions. A study in patients with AS demonstrated an association between gut dysbiosis, impaired mucosal barrier function, and epithelial release of zonulin. This tight
junction molecule, as well as bacterial products, induced a proinflammatory M2 phenotype in circulating monocytes.
Mucosal
antigens derived from commensal microorganisms consequently
trigger a systemic pathogenic immune response following bacterial translocation.
For example, lipopolysaccharide, a bacterial
product that downregulates the expression of CD14 on circulating monocytes, could rescue these CD14-depleted cells from their
anergic state when combined with soluble CD14.
This resulted
in upregulation of IL-23, a pivotal cytokine in SpA pathogenesis,
which promotes gut inflammation by inhibiting Th1 and Treg
cells and by promoting Th17 cells through upregulation of the
OverexpresTh17-related transcription factor ROR
γ
t.
sion of IL-23 is thus a pivotal feature of subclinical gut inflammation in AS. However, upregulation of IL-17 was only observed in
the gut of Crohn’s disease but not in AS patients.
Collectively,
these data illustrate that unbalanced intestinal microbial communities directly or indirectly influence gut mucosal homeostasis. In
turn, these events impact local (gut) and systemic immune functions, thereby contributing to the different manifestations of SpA.
More recently, research has focused not only on the potential
pathogenic role of certain microbes but also on the role of perturbed metabolic pathways (“metabolome”). Rather than a single
pathobiont, key pathogenic features might be due to metabolic
functions of a microbial community. These may in turn depend
on the gut microbial community structure and host genetic context. Thus some microorganisms are deemed commensals in a
healthy state but can act as pathobionts under certain environmental and host genetic influences. Nuclear magnetic resonance
spectroscopy revealed that fecal metabolites of patients with AS
contain reduced levels of the SCFAs butyrate and propionate
but increased tryptophan concentrations compared with healthy
controls.
This might be mechanistically important as administrating microbiota-derived metabolites such as propionate in the
HLA-B27-transgenic rat model significantly reduced the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-1
β
, IL-17A, IFNγ
, and
colonic inflammation.
In an inducible model of experimental autoimmune uveitis, exogeneous administration of propionate attenuated uveitis through regulatory T cell induction and
suppressed migration of effector T cells to gut or extraintestinal
tissues.
Based on the accumulating evidence for a pathogenic role of
dysbiosis in SpA, direct or indirect modulation of the gut microbiota might provide novel treatment options for these patients.
Probiotics containing a cocktail of beneficial live bacteria and
yeast, however, did not show significant benefit over placebo in
improving disease activity and physical functioning of active axial
SpA patients.
This may just reflect the need for a better understanding of which microorganisms are beneficial in which context,
likely requiring a personalized approach with knowledge of the
pre-existing microbiota, which was not studied in this trial. Restoration of a healthy gut microbiota using fecal microbial transplant
(FMT) is another, more disruptive option of microbiota manipulation. FMT is highly effective in treating refractory
Clostridioides
difficile
infection, but results in SpA are discouraging so far.
,
Given the complex and dynamic interactions between the gut
microbiota, host background, and immune dysregulation, more
data from human studies are needed before recommending therapeutic modulation of the microbiota in SpA.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

An association between poor dental status and arthritis has long
been known. This link has been corroborated more recently in
studies on periodontal disease in RA patients.
Periodontal
diseases affect the gingiva, the supporting connective tissue, and
the alveolar bone. Periodontitis (PD) is defined as the inflammation of the tooth-supportive tissues. It leads to loss of attachment
and bone destruction. Because the oral cavity is colonized by many
different microorganisms, with more than 700 species of oral bacteria being described in biofilms present in dental plaque,
it
has been postulated that this host–microbiota interplay may be
instrumental in onset of PD. Several studies showed an association

between PD and three periodontal pathogens,
Porphyromonas
gingivalis (P. gingivalis)
,
Tannerella forsythia (T
.
forsythia)
, and
Treponema
denticola (T. denticola)
, respectively. This has been
recently extended to
Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans (A.
actinomycetemcomitans)
,
Filifactor alocis (F. alocis)
, and other species belonging to the genera
Peptostreptococcaceae
,
Desulfobulbus
,
and
Synergistetes
.
Mechanistically, it has been suggested that
these bacteria modulate the host immune responses, thereby contributing to the onset of PD. Interestingly, the pathogen
P. gingivalis
itself does not cause PD in mice lacking commensal bacteria,
supporting this paradigm.
How oral microorganisms may promote RA has been explored
in multiple studies including large metagenomic studies of oral
and gut microbiomes.
RA is characterized by a destructive,
symmetric polyarthritis associated with autoantibodies directed
against citrullinated proteins. Citrullination or deamination
is a post-translational modification that converts a positively
charged arginine into a neutral citrulline residue. This reaction
is catalyzed by calcium ion-dependent enzymes called peptidylarginine deiminases (PADs). While citrullination of proteins is a
physiologic process, in genetically predisposed individuals it may
trigger the onset of autoimmunity against citrullinated antigens.
Thus the interaction between RA-associated major histocompatibility complex-II polymorphisms, smoking, and PD has been
linked to development of anti-citrullinated protein antibodies
(ACPAs). Several citrullinated autoantigens have been described,
among them against collagen type II, fibrinogen, vimentin, and
α
-enolase.
Smoking is considered a key environmental risk
factor for RA and PD. It is unclear whether smoking promotes
autoimmunity against citrullinated antigens directly or indirectly.
Smoking itself may foster peptide citrullination by PAD2 and
PAD4,
but smoking also promotes the growth of bacteria in
the oral cavity, which may worsen both PD and RA, respectively.
Hence, an association with increased
T. forsythia
,
Peptostreptococcus micros (P. micros)
,
Fusobacterium nucleatum (F. nucleatum)
, and
Campylobacter rectus (C. rectus)
in subgingival dental plaque has
been reported in smokers versus nonsmokers.
How such periodontal pathogens may modulate immune
responses has been the subject of several studies and entails multiple mechanisms.
For example, proteases secreted by
T.
forsythia
,
F. nucleatum
, or
P. gingivalis
cleave proteins in the extracellular matrix, which results in tissue degradation.
P. gingivalis
peptidylarginine deiminase (PPAD) is secreted by this pathobiont
and citrullinates C-terminal arginine residues of proteins and peptides, thereby generating new epitopes recognized by ACPAs. The
activity of PPAD along with deiminases from other PD-promoting bacteria such as
F. alocis
and
Cryptobacterium curtum
results in
an increased pH in the oral cavity. In contrast,
A. actinomycetemcomitans
secretes a toxin (leukotoxin A) that binds to lymphocytes,
macrophages, and neutrophils and induces pyroptosis. Importantly, this toxin also induces hypercitrullination in neutrophils,
thereby inducing the formation of neutrophil extracellular traps
(NETs) and promoting the production of ACPAs.
NETs are
in turn degraded by nucleases expressed by
Prevotella intermedia
,
which leads to endogenous PAD release. In addition,
F. nucleatum
outer membrane vesicles were shown to translocate to the joints
of RA patients and mediate local inflammatory responses via the
virulence factor FadA.
Recently a paired analysis of human and bacterial transcriptomics
in longitudinal blood samples from RA patients was reported.
Interestingly, patients with RA and periodontal disease experienced repeated oral bacteremias associated with transcriptional
signatures of ISG15
HLADR
and CD48
S100A2
monocytes. These monocyte subsets have previously been identified in
inflamed synovia and blood of RA patients suffering from flares.
The oral bacteria observed transiently in the blood were broadly
citrullinated, and the in-situ citrullinated epitopes were recognized by extensively somatically hypermutated ACPAs encoded
by blood plasmablasts from RA patients. These data are compatible with a scenario that PD results in repeated breaches of the
oral mucosa leading to release of citrullinated oral bacteria in the
circulation, which in turn activate inflammatory monocyte subsets and ACPA-producing B cells. This process promotes affinity
maturation and epitope spreading to citrullinated antigens.
Another recent study used dual IgA and IgG family plasmablast-derived monoclonal autoantibodies obtained from the
peripheral blood of individuals at risk for RA to identify crossreactivity between RA-relevant autoantigens and bacterial taxa in
the closely related families
Lachnospiraceae
and
Ruminococcaceae.
Bacterial isolates from these families bound by the monoclonal
autoantibodies revealed strains within the genus
Subdoligranulum
from the feces of an individual that activate CD4
T cells from
RA patients. Intriguingly, some
Subdoligranulum
isolates, but not
all, stimulated Th17 cell expansion, serum RA-relevant IgG autoantibodies, and joint swelling reminiscent of early RA. Germ-free
mice monocolonized with a
Subdoligranulum
strain developed
signs of early RA in vivo with antibody deposition and complement activation in the affected joints (
Fig. 23.8
).
Another gut pathobiont that was originally identified in newonset RA patients and linked to Th17 responses in vivo in gnotobiotic models is
P. copri.
It was later also associated with
pre-RA in at-risk individuals
that mount antibodies against
P.
copri
antigens.
Patients with RA carry antiP. copri
antibodies as
well that correlate with RA-relevant autoantibodies in the serum
of RA subsets.
RA-derived
P. copri
was further shown
to harbor more pathogenic potential than
P. copri
isolated from
healthy subjects, suggesting strain differences that are relevant to
disease.
Collectively, these findings suggest a mechanism in the pathogenesis of RA by which a specific gut bacterial strain may drive
systemic autoantibody generation and joint-centered antibody
deposition and immune activation.
Taken together, multiple mechanisms may contribute to the
pathogenesis of RA ranging from oral pathobionts citrullinating
autoantigens, gut bacterial outer membrane vesicles translocating
to the joints of RA patients, thereby promoting local inflammation, to gut pathobiont-induced systemic Th17 expansion and
ACPA induction in early RA via yet to be determined molecular
mechanisms.

### Other Rheumatic Diseases

The role of the microbiome in other rheumatic diseases than SpA,
RA, and SLE is less studied. Given the clinical and pathogenetic
relatedness of rheumatic diseases, similar microorganisms may
contribute to different rheumatic diseases. In particular, the connective tissue diseases may share common microbial triggers. The
above-mentioned SLE-associated Ro60 ortholog-expressing bacteria
are likely to also play a role in Sjögren’s syndrome (SjS)
besides lupus. One of these taxa,
Corynebacterium amycolatum
, is
physiologically found in lacrimal glands. The anatomic location
of this bacterium within a target organ of SjS suggests it could be
a theoretical candidate for promoting sicca symptoms via Ro60-
cross-reactive immune responses. Using the unbiased approach of
16S rDNA sequencing, the human gut microbiome of SjS patients
has been profiled together with studies in a corresponding animal
model, which revealed reduced diversity of the gut microbiota in
SjS compared with controls.
Studies with a larger cohort of SjS
confirmed gut dysbiosis, which was associated with disease activity.
The buccal microbiome in SjS is also dysbiotic but is influenced by the reduced salivary secretions.
Mechanistic research
is needed to define which taxa may be causally related to SjS as
opposed to secondary effects such as salivary gland dysfunction or
other host variables.
In APS, a hypothesis-driven study identified by systematic
homology a common human gut bacterium,
Roseburia intestinalis
, as a cross-reactive trigger for the major autoantigen
β
-
glycoprotein I.
R. intestinalis
gavage in a genetic model for
SLE-related APS led to increased thrombotic disease, supporting
a pathogenic role in APS for this otherwise beneficial commensal
bacterium. A broader role of the microbiome in APS was recently
reviewed
and is likely given the long-standing role of infectious
triggers in transiently inducing antiphospholipid antibodies.
The gut microbiota in systemic sclerosis (SSc) was explored in
various association studies (reviewed in ref. 184). One study compared the SSc microbiome between patients with and without GI
involvement, which revealed that GI disease is associated with more
overt dysbiosis and certain taxa such as
L. reuteri
.
Since
L. reuteri
translocation can aggravate the type I IFN signature (as discussed in
the section on SLE),
it is plausible that the dysbiotic SSc microbiome might also contribute to the type I IFN signature in SSc,
which would be testable. A more in-depth metagenomic study of
patients with SSc and IgG4-related disease demonstrated similar
taxa and disease-associated pathways in both disease states.
These
results suggest similar processes in fibrosing autoimmune diseases,
which warrant further studies. In addition, this study confirmed the
enrichment of lactobacilli in SSc as seen in other association studies
such as the one on GI tract involvement.
The involvement of the microbiota in vasculitides is little studied. A thorough analysis of temporal artery biopsies for microbial
signals did not reveal a significant association with specific microbiota that may have migrated to these sites.
An altered gut barrier and increased IgA production was, however, mechanistically
linked to extraintestinal vasculitis in a Kawasaki disease model.
If translocation of the gut microbiota as in lupus
is involved
in vascular inflammation of the vasculitides remains to be determined. Human microbiome studies in small-vessel vasculitides
have recently been summarized
and appear to be inconclusive
besides an association between nasal
Staphylococcus
species and
granulomatosis with polyangiitis.
Little is also known about a contribution of the human gut
microbiota to autoinflammatory diseases. A mouse model for
STING-associated vasculopathy with onset in infancy revealed
a protective gut commensal.
Another murine study suggests
a modulatory role in chronic recurrent multifocal osteomyelitis.
A genetic mouse model for this autoinflammatory disease was
markedly protected by a high-fat diet and reductions in
Prevotella
spp.
The protective effect was mediated by reductions
in IL-1
β
in neutrophils, which was microbiota dependent.
If
these findings relate to human autoinflammatory disease and if
other autoinflammatory monogenetic diseases with variable disease phenotypes may be influenced by the microbiota remain to
be determined.

the microbiome is still in the distant future but can be envisioned
given the rapid progress made in the field over the last two decades.
The emerging field of pharmacomicrobiomics

adverse events to certain medications. Targeting the microbiome

dietary interventions to live biotherapeutics to highly specific
depletion of pathobionts by bacteriophages. While the latest proofof-concept studies with pathobiont targeting by a bacteriophage
cocktail in IBD are promising,

microbiota targeting. One can envision a personalized approach,

## Conclusion

Several approaches are in development that may target the host
barriers or the microbiota. Combinations of these treatment
strategies with conventional immunosuppressives may someday
put patients with rheumatic diseases into long-lasting remission
or prevent the onset of autoimmune diseases in the first place.
Although far from current practice, the progress made by hostmicrobiota research over the last decades has greatly enhanced our
understanding of the multifactorial pathogenesis of rheumatic
diseases and may also facilitate the development of new treatment
strategies beyond conventionally targeting the immune system.
