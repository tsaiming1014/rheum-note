## Introduction

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is generally defined by the presence of inflammatory arthritis with onset before age 16 years without
another identifiable etiology. JIA is further subdivided into seven distinct subtypes, each of which likely reflects individual disease states,
highlighting the massive heterogeneity that encompasses all of the
phenotypes included by this nomenclature.
In this chapter, we will
consider the host and environmental factors that contribute to the
common phenotype of chronic arthritis in JIA while also considering
the pathogenic mediators of each of the specific subtypes separately.
The ability of some therapeutic approaches to address the common
features, such as treatment of arthritis with TNF blockade, does suggest that there are common nodes of pathophysiology within JIA.
However, increasing sophistication of both basic and translational
studies, as well as targeted therapeutic agents, has begun to highlight
the differences between subtypes, making consideration of these differences from a pathoetiologic perspective critical.
The International League of Associations for Rheumatology
(ILAR) has defined diagnostic criteria for JIA, which generally
requires 6 weeks of arthritis with onset before 16 years of age.
A
complex set of inclusion and exclusion criteria further subset JIA
into the following subtypes: oligoarticular (four or fewer involved
joints), rheumatoid factor (RF) negative polyarticular (five or more
involved joints with absent RF serology), RF
polyarticular (five or
more involved joints with positive RF serology), systemic (extraarticular features of systemic inflammation), psoriatic (extra-articular features of psoriasis), enthesitis-related (axial arthritis and/or
enthesial involvement), and undifferentiated (failing to meet criteria
for the previous six categories). While these criteria have provided
some utility in homogenizing populations for study, it is clear that
certain features cross multiple subtypes (asymptomatic uveitis, antinuclear antibody [ANA] positivity), while other features are so distinct, it is harder to justify a nomenclature that implies a common
link (for example, the autoinflammatory features of the systemic
subtype). Among these, oligoarticular JIA appears to be a disease
truly specific to the pediatric age group. Additionally, research on
the pathophysiology of systemic JIA is far more abundant than that
in its counterpart, adult-onset Still’s disease (AOSD). As such, we
have focused in this chapter on the pathoetiology of disease features
more specific or prevalent in pediatric-onset disease.
Reclassification to try to better homogenize and define these
different features is once again underway.
For polyarticular (both
RF
and RF
), psoriatic, enthesitis-related, and systemic JIA,
there is increasing momentum to merge classification with the
adult criteria to remove the age distinction. Other attempts are
ongoing to reclassify homogeneous populations of patients on
the basis of biomarkers, demographics, and clinical features using
machine learning techniques, although no formal results of these
approaches have been published. Such alternate means to classify
these diseases may ultimately define more biologically relevant
populations of patients. However, until there is widespread use
and agreement on these definitions, the ILAR criteria remain the
gold standard for classification and will be the construct on which
etiology and pathogenesis are discussed in this chapter.

## Epidemiology

JIA affects 1 in 1000 children, with 50% to 60% of cases being
of the oligoarticular form and the remaining subtypes distributed
approximately equally. While there is a female predominance
in the oligoarticular, polyarticular, and psoriatic forms, there is
more equal sex distribution in the systemic and enthesitis-related
subtypes. The oligoarticular form is more often associated with
young age of onset, with a peak between 1 and 2 years of age.
This is in contrast to the enthesitis-related type, which has a mean

age of onset of 11.7 years. Other subtypes have broader peaks of
age at onset or even bimodal distributions in the case of psoriatic
arthritis.
JIA appears to be a worldwide phenomenon, although the
distributions of the different subtypes do appear to be different depending on location. Like other autoimmune diseases,
this is likely due to differences in both genetic ancestry (e.g.,
human leukocyte antigen [HLA] allele distribution) and local
environmental/infectious exposures. Oligoarthritis is much
more prevalent in European populations. Enthesitis-related
arthritis demonstrated high pockets of prevalence in certain
populations, such as the Haida people of Western Canada,
likely reflective of the prevalence of the HLA-B27 allele in this
population.

## Histopathology

### Synovium

The histopathology of the inflamed synovium of JIA is akin to that
seen in adult-onset rheumatoid arthritis (RA) (
Fig. 108.1
). There
is hyperplasia of synoviocytes, with increased vascularity that can
be highlighted with endothelial stains. There may be more hypervascularity in the polyarticular subtype, more inflammatory cells
in the oligoarticular subtype, and an increase in synovial lining
hyperplasia in the enthesitis-related subtype, although in general there is a large overlap in histology among all of the different
subtypes.
Fibrin may be found layered on the synovial surface,
consistent with an inflammatory process. A lymphocytic infiltrate
is generally present consisting of mostly T cells, although B cells
are also found, sometimes forming germinal centers. Macrophages
and dendritic cells are also found throughout the inflammatory
infiltrate.

capillaries and venules.

review of cases of the related AOSD rash demonstrates a wide range
of neutrophilic, lymphocytic, or mixed inflammatory infiltrates.

### Bone Marrow

The bone marrow is also not often sampled in JIA, with the exception of systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis (SJIA) in order to
rule out malignancy. The bone marrow of SJIA can have activated
macrophages and hemophagocytes present, even in the absence
of overt macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), although these
features are often more pronounced in the setting of associated
florid MAS.
Hemophagocytes are activated macrophages that
have engulfed other hematopoietic elements (red blood cells,
platelets, leukocytes) and can often contain multiple other cell
types within them (
Fig. 108.3
). They are most easily identified in
preparations where single cells are visible, for example, bone marrow aspirate smears and lymph node “touch preparations.” When
examining tissues in situ, for example, bone marrow, spleen, or
lymph node biopsies, hemophagocytes may be less readily visible
because it is often difficult to tell whether the hematopoietic cells
are inside or simply adjacent to macrophages. Immunohistochemical staining with a macrophage cell surface marker, such as CD68
or CD163, can be helpful in distinguishing these two possibilities, with hemophagocytes demonstrating multiple cells contained
within a single macrophage cell membrane (
Fig. 108.4
).

### Secondary Immune Organs

As with the bone marrow, lymph nodes are typically only sampled in SJIA and not in patients with other JIA subtypes. The
lymphadenopathy of SJIA is nonspecific, appearing as follicular
hyperplasia, but it can sometimes resemble lymphoma.
Further
evaluation of cell surface markers should generally be able to distinguish this possibility. When overt MAS is present, hemophagocytes may also be found in the lymph nodes. Hepatitis can also
be a prominent feature of SJIA, especially in MAS, and a limited
examination of liver biopsies demonstrated CD68
histiocytosis,
as well as a preponderance of CD8 T cells.

## JIA Pathogenesis

### Environmental Triggers

While much of the focus on etiologic factors for JIA has been on
genetic determinants, undoubtedly environmental triggers also
play a role. It has been long observed that flares of arthritis are often
preceded by typical childhood infections such as upper respiratory
tract infections (URIs) or otitis media. More recently, attention has
been given to alterations in the microbiome, often as a result of the
use of antibiotics for infections, as an environmental risk factor for
multiple subtypes of JIA.
Other exposures have been ruled out as
associated with the development of JIA, such as breastfeeding, secondary smoke, pets, or residential area.
History of rubella vaccine
does not appear to be associated with JIA,
and measles/mumps/
rubella vaccination does not aggravate flare of disease.
As the
potential universe of exposures is virtually limitless, it is of course
impossible to catalog all of the possibilities even without considering gene/environment interactions. Nonetheless, continued efforts
to better characterize the environmental factors that lead to the
development and flare of JIA continue to be undertaken.

### Host Contributors

Genetic/Epigenetic
The relative paucity of described monogenic causes of JIA support the perception that, in general, the genetic contribution to

JIA is polygenic. Nevertheless, the few monogenic causes of juvenile arthritis may help illuminate specific mechanisms and such
patients may have been classified as JIA in the era prior to widespread genetic testing. Pathogenic variants in several genes cause
syndromes that can include chronic arthritis, including
SYK,
STING,
TBK1,
C2orf69,
MAN2B2,
LACC1
/FAMIN,
MYD88,
and
XIAP.
The importance of polygenic causes of JIA is made apparent
by the fact that there is 20% to 40% concordance in monozygotic twins
and an increased risk of disease of the same subtype
in the siblings of an affected proband.
It should be noted that
while this relative risk is increased, the absolute risk of JIA in sibling pairs remains extremely small, so that families with multiple
affected children are rare.
As with many immune diseases, the major histocompatibility
complex (MHC) locus has the largest number of genetic associations with JIA. The enthesitis-related arthritis subtype, which
mirrors the adult spondyloarthropathies in its clinical presentation, has been associated with HLA-B27.
Similarly, HLADRB1*0401 is associated with the RF
polyarticular JIA,
much
as it is in adult RF
RA. These findings suggest that the age cutoffs
distinguishing the adult forms of these diseases are entirely arbitrary and that the juvenile forms can simply be considered early
onset of the classical adult syndromes.
HLA-DRB1*0801 and *11 and DRB1*1301 have been
associated with oligoarticular JIA. The DRB1*1301-DQA1*01-
DQB1*06 haplotype in particular has been suggested to distinguish extended versus persistent endotypes of oligoarticular JIA.
Just as the shared epitope hypothesis has been used to provide
mechanistic meaning to the HLA-DR4 association with RF
disease, it is tempting to suspect that the HLA association with
oligoarticular JIA, which has a strong seropositive association
with ANA, also relates to the presentation of some autoantigen.
Numerous articles have been published cataloging HLA associations with multiple different subphenotypes of JIA, including age,
sex, and other characteristics, and a catalog of these associations,
both risk-conferring and protective, is beyond the scope of this
discussion.
The array of these class II MHC associations seems
to implicate a role for CD4
T-lymphocytes in JIA pathogenesis.
It is noteworthy, however, that SJIA, which is classically seronegative and more autoinflammatory, is associated with a distinct
group of MHCII haplotype associations: HLA-DRB1*11-HLADQA1*05-HLA-DQB1*03.
This could suggest an unappreciated role for antigen in SJIA or that the mechanistic role of HLA
association with JIA is more complex than simple antigen presentation, similar to what has been suggested for HLA-B27 and its
linkage to increased unfolded protein response rather than presentation of an arthritogenic peptide. Alternatively, perhaps there is
a role for antigen in SJIA despite its classification in the pantheon
of autoinflammatory disorders. More recently, the complication
of SJIA with interstitial lung disease (histologically pulmonary
alveolar proteinosis/endogenous lipoid pneumonia) was strongly
associated with the HLA-DRB1*15 haplotype previously associated with multiple sclerosis.
Non-HLA loci have also been associated with JIA. Broadly
speaking, cytokines and their receptors, signaling molecules, and
unfolded protein response genes have been the major themes
of discovery in this area. While dozens of genes have been
associated with JIA, few genes have stood up to replication in
multiple candidate gene studies. Although not a risk factor for
disease onset, variants in or near the gene encoding interleukin-1
receptor antagonist (ILRN, the endogenous version of the drug
anakinra) have been associated with response to IL-1 blockade
in SJIA.
As many single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs)
are not in coding regions, increasing interest in the effects of
enhancer/promoter polymorphisms and other epigenetic effects
has arisen as inheritable elements that might contribute to disease risk.
Given the large amount of heterogeneity in JIA, it is not surprising to find that many associated SNPs are not with JIA at
large but rather with subtypes. For instance, the genetic loci of
IL-23R and endoplasmic reticulum aminopeptidase-1 (ERAP)
have been associated with ERA,
consistent with these same
associations in adult spondyloarthropathy. IL-23 signaling
through its receptor may promote T helper (Th)17 cell survival,
a CD4
T cell fate associated with both JIA and adult spondyloarthropathy. ERAP is similarly associated with proper trimming and folding of proteins and the degree of unfolded protein
response, another well recognized pathway in the pathogenesis
of the spondyloarthropathies. In contrast, IL-6 polymorphisms
have been associated with SJIA.
Supporting the relevance of
this association, IL-6 has been a successful therapeutic target in
this subtype.
Studies evaluating the epigenetic changes associated with
JIA are limited. The peripheral CD4 T cell methylation pattern
observed in RA was not present in oligoarticular JIA.
However,
active JIA CD4 T cells showed differential chromatin accessibility
that was not accompanied by changes in three-dimensional chromatin architecture.
A role for epigenetic imprinting of monocytes is emerging as well.
Studies in different JIA subtypes and
using tissue-resident cells may further elucidate both JIA susceptibility and pathogenesis.

Adaptive Immunity

HLA associations with JIA, it is tempting to speculate that there
must exist arthrogenic peptides that are being presented to these T

of restricted T cell receptor repertoires.
However, with the

CD4

of which have different functions, particularly with respect to
the types of cytokines they secrete. Accordingly, aberrations in a
number of functions of these CD4
T cell lineages could result
in pathology. Both Th1 and Th17 CD4

been associated with JIA in the joint space and in the peripheral

Therapeutically, this has translated to successful use of
IL-17 blockade in adult psoriatic arthritis and ankylosing spondylitis, with ongoing trials in pediatric disease. IL-17 promotes
neutrophilic inflammation, which in turn can lead to bone and
cartilage destruction, providing another rationale for blocking
IL-17 as a therapeutic modality.
Regulatory T cells (Tregs) act to suppress immune responses
and limit inflammation. Dysregulation of this suppressive effect
may allow pathogenic inflammation. Indeed, it has been noted
that expression of the master transcription factor required for their
function and maintenance, Foxp3, is low to absent in the Tregs in
the synovial fluid of JIA patient.
Furthermore, perturbations in

Treg function that allow them to act in an inflammatory manner
has also been noted in JIA.
B Cells.
The effectiveness of B cell–depleting therapy in the
form of the anti-CD20 monoclonal rituximab in RA provides
striking empiric evidence for the role of B cells in disease pathogenesis. Additionally, many JIA subtypes are associated with autoantibodies: ANA, RF, and anti-citrullinated protein antibody
(ACPA) among others, although a direct pathogenic link between
these autoantibodies and disease has not been established. In the
K/BxN animal model of inflammatory arthritis, the anti–glucose6-phosphate isomerase (anti-G6PI) autoantibodies fulfill Koch’s
postulates and are clearly pathogenic.
However, anti-G6PI antibodies have not been found to be associated with human disease,
and it therefore remains unclear whether antibodies themselves
play any role in JIA or other autoimmune arthridities. Synovial
B cells in JIA appeared activated, with increased levels of CD80
and CD8.
Functionally, synovial B cells were better at activating T cells than their peripheral counterparts. Taken together, this
suggests the JIA B cells may act as antigen presenting cells, and
the effects of B cell depletion may correlate more with this function than with reduction of autoantibodies. In adult RA, this is
further suggested by the fact that disease activity correlated better
with CD4
T cell numbers than B cell numbers after rituximab
therapy.

As described for B cells, JIA DCs express higher levels of CD80 and CD86. Plasmacytoid DCs may play a regulatory

Macrophages/Monocytes.
Monocytes and monocyte-derived

are found in the synovial fluid and the synovial tissue itself, which

More recently, increased activation and cytokine production by monocytes has been noted, specifically in the ERA
subset of JIA, in particular by production of IL-23, which in turn
induces Th17-directed responses.
Neutrophils.
Neutrophils are the most abundant cells found
in synovial fluid from JIA-inflamed joints. However, their role in
disease pathogenesis remains poorly understood. It has been suggested that aberrant epigenetic modifications in neutrophils leads
to altered mRNA transcription and function in JIA.
Specific
neutrophil effector functions have not been established in disease
pathogenesis. Recent data have suggested an increase in low-density granulocytes in JIA, a neutrophil subset particularly associated
with inflammatory responses.
In SJIA, well known for peripheral blood neutrophilia, elevated myeloid related protein 8/14 (MRP 8/14, also known as

Furthermore, IL-1

may be one of the important targets of IL-1

Stromal Cells.
While the immune response is clearly important in the inflammation of JIA, stromal elements such as synovial fibroblasts are likely an important target of many of the
inflammatory mediators and therefore contribute to disease as
well. Many of the aforementioned cytokines upregulate adhesion
molecules on synovial fibroblasts, and synovial fibroblasts from
JIA are more sensitive to this process.
JIA synovial fibroblasts
also appear more sensitive to the effects of TGFb signaling, altering their function.
Thus, targeting not just the immune system,
but also the fibroblasts being altered by immune effector functions, may be a future therapeutic strategy.

## Subtype-Specific Pathogenesis

### Systemic

Systemic JIA is clearly set apart from the other JIA subtypes both
in its clinical presentation and in its pathogenesis. The lack
of identifiable autoreactive serologies and the large degree of
systemic inflammation suggest that this illness is better characterized as an autoinflammatory rather than autoimmune
process. Accordingly, the dramatic response of many patients
to IL-1 blockade conceptually fit with the inflammasome activation often seen in autoinflammatory disease. Unlike other
JIA subtypes, several biomarkers are useful for diagnosis and/
or monitoring. The S100 proteins S100A8/S100A9 (also called
MRP8/14 or calprotectin) and S100A12 (also called calgranulin) are highly abundant in activated monocytes and neutrophils and show diagnostic specificity for SJIA (as well as familial
Mediterranean fever and a few other monogenic autoinflammatory diseases) among other potential causes of fever of unknown
origin.
A soluble factor found in the serum of SJIA patients
is sufficient to induce IL-1
β
production by mononuclear blood
cells.
This factor may well be S100A8/S100A9, as removal of
this protein from the serum attenuates this effect. Treatment
response to IL-6 antagonists suggests involvement of this pathway as well. IL-6 is elevated in the serum of SJIA patients and
may be the direct explanation for growth failure and osteoporosis in the disease.
IL-6 may also impair natural killer cell function, which has been implicated in the inflammation associated
with SJIA.
However, the role of the natural killer cell has
been debated in SJIA, with conflicting reports about functional
deficits in this cellular population.
It is not clear whether the
reported defects in natural killer cells are a cause or a consequence of the SJIA inflammatory milieu. The arthritis of SJIA
may relate to excess IL-17, as peripheral blood CD4 Treg and
γδ
T cells show increased IL-17 transcription or stimulated production (respectively) that improves upon therapeutic blockade
of IL-1 (a known inducer of IL-17).
Macrophage Activation Syndrome
MAS is an uncommon but potentially fatal cytokine storm syndrome that can complicate JIA—almost exclusively SJIA—and
its immunophysiology may also hold clues to SJIA pathogenesis.
Classification criteria for MAS complicating known or highly
suspected SJIA were published in 2016 and require fever, high
ferritin, and any two of transaminitis, hypertriglyceridemia, relative thrombocytopenia, or relative hypofibrinogenemia.
These
criteria overlap conspicuously with those of the related disease
hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), and MAS is often
considered a form of secondary HLH occurring in rheumatic diseases.
Despite the recent classification criteria, diagnosis of MAS
remains challenging and requires recognizing the hyperinflammatory syndrome in the context of the level and dynamics of the
biomarkers that define it.

“Full-blown” MAS complicates roughly 10% of SJIA and
occurs most commonly at disease onset but can develop at any
point. Given significant overlap in diagnostic features and biomarkers, active SJIA may represent a subclinical MAS state,
implying a model that intimately relates the pathogenic factors
of MAS to those of SJIA itself.
Very high serum levels of IL-18
and detectable “free” IL-18 (that which is unbound by the natural antagonist IL-18–binding protein) are quite specific for SJIA
patients with active or “smoldering” MAS,
supporting IL-18
as the upstream driving cytokine of MAS-like inflammation in
SJIA.
It has been suggested that there is a high-risk group of
SJIA patients that develop MAS, on either the basis of chronically elevated IL-18 levels
or the presence of heterozygous or
hypomorphic genetic lesions in cytotoxic granule function.
Interferonγ
is a central pathogenic cytokine in animal models of MAS,
as well as in human disease. Several biomarkers
help distinguish MAS from active SJIA, and many overlap with
biomarkers of disease activity in HLH. These include ferritin
(particularly when normalized by erythrocyte sedimentation
rate [ESR]),
peripheral blood adenosine deaminase activity
(ADA)
, the soluble IL-2 receptor
α
, and the IFNγ
–responsive chemokine CXCL9.
Although total IL-18 levels are highly
elevated in the SJIA patients most prone to MAS, they are even
higher in active MAS and help distinguish MAS from other
forms of HLH.
Although there are no consensus guidelines,
the treatment of MAS typically involves the use of agents common in SJIA, like glucocorticoids (often at pulse doses) and
the IL-1 receptor antagonist anakinra). Treatment of severe or
refractory disease often involves agents used in HLH like cyclosporine or low doses of the chemotherapeutic etoposide.
IFNγ
and IL-18 blocking strategies are under investigation. The
pathogenic mediators of MAS, as well as the reason for their
dysregulation, continue to be an area of active study.
Multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MISC) is a
newly recognized postinfectious syndrome caused by the severe
acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) that
shares some phenotypic and serologic overlap with MAS. MISC
typically presents 3 to 4 weeks after infection with a constellation of symptoms including fever, abdominal pain, conjunctivitis,
thrombocytopenia, lymphopenia, rash, and myocardial dysfunction. These patients often present with elevated CXCL9 as in
MAS and often meet clinical criteria for MAS.
On the other
hand, MISC patients do not often develop the severe multiorgan
failure picture of MAS; rather the cardiac and gastrointestinal (GI)
limited manifestations are the major clinical features that require
management. Further work on the pathophysiology of MISC, and
how it may or may not related to MAS is likely to illuminate the
origins of both entities.

### Oligoarticular

Oligoarticular disease is defined by having four or fewer joints
affected at early stages of the disease course. Although synovial
fluid is typically dominated by neutrophils, the T cells present
show strong Th1 skewing.
It is well recognized that some patients
“extend” or develop a more polyarticular presentation later in the
disease course, while others have “persistent” disease, meaning it
remains oligoarticular. The etiologic factors that underlie this difference are of great interest because this might lead to our ability
to predict outcome and therefore alter therapy earlier to intervene.
Class I MHC polymorphisms,
IL-10,
and IFNγ
with activated macrophage signatures
have all been associated with the
distinction between extension and persistence. Proteomic analysis
of patients at a very early time point suggests that there may even
be measurable serum biomarkers that would allow for the prediction of disease course
; however, these remain to be validated for
clinical use.
Uveitis
The strong association of asymptomatic, vision-threatening uveitis with oligoarticular JIA is a particular distinguishing feature of
the disease. The causes of this uveitis remain unclear. It is associated with ANA positivity, but this may more likely be a useful
biomarker epiphenomenon rather than a direct pathogenic link.
Cytokines have been profiled from the tears of patients with JIA
uveitis that can distinguish them from healthy controls and uveitis
of other causes, with different sets of cytokines found to be predictive in different studies.
More work is needed to validate
these findings in larger cohorts. The remarkable efficacy of TNF
blockade for many patients with this condition suggests a central
role for this cytokine in uveitis pathogenesis. It is interesting to
note that this is true for the monoclonal antibody–based TNF
therapeutics but not for etanercept.
The mechanistic reasons for
this difference remain unknown. The studies of genetic associations with uveitis are completely mixed, especially for MHC allele
associations, and the lack of reports of familial occurrence perhaps
suggests only a weak genetic association. The major exception to
this is the association of HLA-B27 with acute anterior uveitis,
which is a distinct entity from the asymptomatic, chronic uveitis
of oligoarticular JIA.

### Polyarticular

RF
polyarticular arthritis shares a common genetic architecture
and pathophysiology with adult RF
disease. There is often a correlation with ACPAs, and the “shared epitope” hypothesis, in
which MHC alleles more capable of present citrullinated peptides
allow for T cell help B cells to produce these antibodies, holds as
much for RF
JIA as it does for adult disease. Thus the 16-yearsof-age cutoff is likely to be an artificial dichotomization. In RF
polyarticular JIA, SNPs in the TRAF1-C5
and PTPN22
loci
are associated with disease, although variants in these loci are also
reported in other subtypes, perhaps suggesting that the pathogenic link to the RF
polyarticular is less specific. Regardless of RF
status, peripheral T cells from treatment-naïve poly-JIA patients
showed enhanced IFNγ
signaling.
Due to the more heterogeneous nature of the polyarticular JIA subtype, a specific pathophysiologic model may be more difficult to develop.

### Enthesitis-Related and Psoriatic

As in adult spondyloarthropathy, there is a strong association with
HLA-B27 in the ERA subtype of JIA. There is a strong familial
occurrence of ERA, as well as related conditions of inflammatory
bowel disease and other HLA-B27–associated diseases. ERAP1
has also been associated with ERA.
Because ERAP1 encodes a
protein product that controls the unfolded protein response and
because HLA-B27 has been noted to be prone to misfolding,
the combinations of these genetic findings has suggested that an
altered unfolded protein response may be in part the mechanistic
cause for inflammation in ERA. Additionally, it has been suggested
that in areas of microtrauma, normal inflammatory tissue repair
becomes exaggerated, perhaps due to the aforementioned genetic
predispositions.
Th17-directed responses, perhaps arising from

aberrant resolution of microtrauma, may contribute to inflammation.
Th17 responses have also been liked to psoriatic arthritis
(PsA). IL-23R polymorphisms are associated with PsA,
suggesting that the Th17 axis may be involved in pathogenesis, much as
in adult disease.

## Conclusion

A unified model of JIA pathogenesis is unlikely to ever be defined.
The heterogeneity of the different subtypes both in clinical presentation and in molecular defects suggests that the different subtypes each have their own unique origins. Nonetheless, there are
also a number of similarities that can be leveraged for understanding. “Lumping” and “splitting” are each likely to reveal different
aspects of disease pathogenesis, and future use of complex, multidimensional data sets coupled with advances in machine learning
techniques are likely to illustrate the similarities and differences
in novel and currently unpredictable ways. A combination of the
systems biology approach paired with the traditional reductionist
mechanistic validation of hypothesis is likely to continue to fuel
the development of novel druggable targets. In combination with
advances in biomarker discovery, the challenge will be to move
beyond simple subtypes to a precise, individualized approach to
therapy grounded in pathogenic models of disease.
