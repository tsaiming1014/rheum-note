## Epidemiology of Arthritis and Other Rheumatic Diseases

### Prevalence Overall and by Selected Sociodemographic and Health Characteristics

is defined as answering “yes”
to the question, “Have you ever been told by a doctor or other
health professional that you have some form of arthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, gout, lupus, or fibromyalgia?” This standard question
was designed in 2002 to incorporate key elements of the 1994 public health definition of arthritis developed by the National Arthritis
Data Workgroup, which sought to capture conditions treated by
rheumatologists and other health care providers. The primary focus
of this chapter is on arthritis defined broadly, with a secondary
focus on some of the individual rheumatic conditions including
rheumatoid arthritis (RA), osteoarthritis (OA), lupus, and gout.

### Arthritis and Physical Activity

The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS)
recommends that adults take part in 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week to receive substantial health benefits.
The moderate to severe joint pain caused by arthritis
can be a major barrier to physical activity and interfere with
an individual’s desire to be active.
Adults with arthritis are
significantly (
p
< 0.01) less likely to meet the recommended
amount of aerobic physical activity compared with adults without arthritis (adults with arthritis: age-standardized prevalence
= 41.9%; adults without arthritis: age-standardized prevalence
= 52.2%).
Moreover, almost one in three adults with arthritis
is physically inactive (report 0 minutes of moderate-intensity
leisure-time aerobic physical activity per week).
Nonetheless, evidence-based physical activity and self-management
interventions have been developed to help adults with arthritis engage in safe and effective physical activity, and thus
improve their ability to meet DHHS recommendations.
These interventions are discussed in greater detail later in the
chapter.

### Arthritis Impact

This section illustrates some of the literature regarding arthritis
and its impacts on critical measures of quality of life, employment,
cost burden, health care burden, and morbidity and mortality.
Disability
A disability is any condition of the body or mind resulting in difficulty performing certain activities and in participation restrictions.
In the United States, arthritis is considered the most common cause
of disability (defined as reporting specific activity difficulties, selected
impairments, use of an assistive aid, household work limitations, and/
or paid work limitations), impacting 9.1 million adults or 19.2% of
all adults who reported a disability in 2010.
About 55% of adults
with a disability (either activities of daily living [ADL] disability or
instrumental activities of daily living [IADL] disability) report having
arthritis.
Among adults with arthritis, 11.1% and 5.7% report an
IADL and an ADL disability, respectively (
Fig. 32.3
).
Severe Joint Pain
Severe joint pain can be a debilitating condition for adults with arthritis that can limit physical function, result in poor mental health, and
reduce social participation.
In the United States, more than one
in four adults with arthritis report severe joint pain in the last 30 days
(
Fig. 32.3
).
The trend in prevalence of severe joint pain among
U.S. adults with arthritis has remained stable over the years; however,
the number of adults with arthritis reporting severe joint pain has
increased as the population size has risen over time.
Physical Function
Poor physical function is a common outcome among adults with
arthritis.
About one in four find stooping, bending, or kneeling very difficult (
Fig. 32.3
), and about 20% cannot or find it very
difficult to walk three blocks or push/pull large objects.
Adults
with arthritis are more likely to have poorer mobility, limitations
with ADL, and limitations with large-muscle tasks.
Mental Health
Poor mental health can impact all aspects of life. The link between
arthritis and poor mental health is well established.
The

age-standardized prevalence of anxiety symptoms is almost twofold higher among adults with arthritis, and the age-standardized
prevalence of depression symptoms is nearly 2.6 times higher for
adults with arthritis.
About 23% of adults with arthritis report
anxiety symptoms and 12% report depression symptoms (
Fig.
32.3
).
Among adults with arthritis, anxiety and depression
symptoms are more common among younger adults, adults with
chronic pain or comorbid chronic conditions, and those unable
to work or who are disabled.
Furthermore, about one in three
adults age 45 and older reports having anxiety or depression, of
whom only half sought help for their mental health condition in
the past year.
Employment
Working-age adults with arthritis have been shown to have lower
employment rates (7.2% lower) compared with adults without
arthritis in 2013.
There are several factors that likely play a
role in these lower employment rates, including mental health,
physical function, and pain. A recent study showed that adults
with arthritis who report symptoms of depression are half as likely
to be employed compared with adults with arthritis who do not
report symptoms of depression.
Another recent study showed
that the age-standardized prevalence of arthritis-attributable activity limitations (a potential marker of physical function and pain)
was significantly higher (42% higher) among unemployed versus
employed adults with arthritis.
Opioid Use
The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
Guideline for Prescribing Opioids for Chronic Pain states that
there are serious risks associated with long-term use of opioid
therapy to treat chronic pain and recommends the use of other
treatments (e.g., exercise therapy, weight loss, cognitive-behavioral therapy, certain interventional procedures, acetaminophen, and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) for arthritis.
Despite this, about 30% of adults with arthritis are prescribed
an opioid in the United States (55% of all adults prescribed an
opioid) to treat their pain.
Joint Replacement
About 1.3 million joint replacements occur each year (3.6% of
all inpatient procedures) with 95% of the joint replacements
performed on the knees (723,000) or hips (494,000).
Women
are more likely to have a joint replacement than men (60% vs.
40%).
About 98% of knee replacements are attributable to OA
and 87% of total hip replacements are attributable to OA.
Hospitalizations
Over 21% of all hospitalizations are associated with arthritis
and other rheumatic conditions.
Nearly half of the hospitalizations of those with arthritis and other rheumatic conditions
are associated with OA (46%).
About 59% were in women,
and women made up 75% of hospitalizations for RA.
Men
made up 67% of hospitalizations with gout.
Non-Hispanic
Blacks were disproportionately impacted by hospitalizations
due to gout, for which they comprised 18% of hospitalizations, compared with 12% of all hospitalizations associated
with arthritis and other rheumatic conditions.
OA is also the
seventh leading hospital discharge diagnosis according to the
Healthcare Cost and Utilization Project’s National Ambulatory
Medical Care Survey in 2020.
Falls
Falls are the leading cause of injury-related morbidity and
mortality in older adults (age 65 and older).
More than two
in five nonfatal emergency department visits and more than
five in six unintentional fall deaths occur in older adults.
Major consequences of falls among older adults include hip
fractures, brain injuries, decline in functional abilities, and
reductions in social activities.
Adults with arthritis are at risk
for falls and fall injuries,
potentially because of their reduced
physical function (e.g., walking speed and balance).
A CDC
report showed that adults with arthritis (age 45 and older) are
more than twice as likely to report an injury related to a fall
and about twice as likely to report two or more falls in the
past year.
Moreover, several cohort studies have shown an
increased risk of incident falls and fall injuries among adults
with knee OA.
A recent meta-analysis shows about a 35%
increased risk of falls among adults with knee OA compared
with adults without knee OA.

Mortality
Systematic reviews and meta-analyses have been published
that examine the association between individual rheumatic
conditions and mortality.
A meta-analysis of eight studies
showed a standard mortality ratio (SMR) of 1.66 (95% CI:
1.44, 1.92) in women and an SMR of 1.39 (95% CI: 1.11,
1.73) in men when comparing those with RA to the general
population.
Gout has been linked to an elevated risk of allcause and cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality in one systematic review (six studies) and to CVD mortality (HR: 1.29,
95% CI: 1.14–1.44) in a meta-analysis of four studies.
A
meta-analysis of seven studies showed an increased risk (HR:
1.21; 95% CI: 1.10, 1.34) of CVD mortality among those
with OA.
A meta-analysis of 12 studies showed a three-fold
elevated risk for all-cause mortality and CVD mortality and
an eight-fold increased risk of renal disease mortality among
individuals with lupus versus the general population.
A
more recent study examining lupus and race/ethnicity showed
that cardiovascular disease deaths were nearly four and six
times higher for Asian and Hispanic individuals with lupus,
respectively, compared with the general population.
From
these studies, it appears that although the risk of mortality is
increased for adults with OA and gout, mortality is highest in
individuals with lupus and RA.

## Arthritis-Attributable Costs

### Direct and Indirect Costs

In the United States, the total annual arthritis-attributable
costs were estimated to be over $303 billion in 2013.
The
direct costs (arthritis-attributable medical care costs)—estimated for all adults (over age 18)—were $139.8 billion (range:
$135.9–157.5 billion).
The indirect costs (arthritis-attributable earnings losses)—estimated for working-age adults (age
18–64)—were $163.7 billion (range: $163.7–170.0 billion).
Ambulatory care expenditures accounted for about half of
arthritis-attributable medical expenditures.
The large annual
arthritis-attributable earning losses was a result of the annual
employment rate being substantially lower among individuals
with arthritis.

## Public Health Approaches to Arthritis Self- Management

This section discusses public health approaches to arthritis selfmanagement such as physical activity and education interventions.

### Physical Activity

Although medications can help treat arthritis sequela, nonpharmaceutical approaches such as physical activity can help as well.
A meta-analysis of 33 studies showed that community-deliverable
physical activity interventions were associated with decreases
in pain and improvements in function, each by about 40%.
In addition to reducing arthritis symptoms, physical activity
programs can lower annual health care costs.
Participation in
EnhanceFitness (CDC-recognized, evidence-based physical activity intervention) was shown to reduce total health care costs by
$945 per person,
and produce improvements in function and
self-reported health at follow-up at 8 months.
Close to 70% of
adults with arthritis are counseled by health care providers to be
physically active (
Fig. 32.4
).
Only 40% of primary care providers always assess and recommend physical activity to their patients
with arthritis.
Though the number of adults with arthritis who
are encouraged by their health care providers to be physically
active is robust, more providers need to encourage these patients
to engage in physical activity to help bridge the differences in
physical activity among adults with and without arthritis.

### Self-Management Interventions

Self-management education interventions are designed to improve
an individual’s confidence and skills to manage a condition.
A meta-analysis of 23 studies showed that self-management

interventions for arthritis reduced pain, fatigue, and depression by
10 to 20%.
However, in 2014 only 11% of adults with arthritis
reported taking a self-management education course (
Fig. 32.4
).
A more recent report using 2019 state-specific data showed an
encouraging increase in self-management education course attendance (state-specific median = 15.7%; range:12.0% in Connecticut to 20.8% in Hawaii).
However, only about 10% of adults
with arthritis receive a health care provider recommendation to
take a self-management education class (
Fig. 32.4
).
A health care
provider’s recommendation to arthritis patients is essential, and
significantly increases the chances of attendance in a self-management education program.
In fact, 52% of adults with arthritis
who receive a recommendation attend a self-management education class.
Because of the relatively low rates of self-management
education course attendance in the United States, this remains a
major area in regard to arthritis management that can be targeted
for exponential growth and impact.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, the public health burden of arthritis in the United
States remains large for those who have it and the health care system that supports them. It continues to be a highly prevalent,
widespread, expensive, and debilitating condition. The risk of
arthritis can be mitigated by addressing modifiable risk factors
such as obesity, though many of the risk factors are nonmodifiable. Public health approaches to managing arthritis exist and are
currently underutilized. These approaches should be encouraged
as they are evidence based and have been shown to reduce pain
and improve function in adults with arthritis.
