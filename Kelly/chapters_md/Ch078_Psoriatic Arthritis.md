## Introduction

Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is a member of the spondyloarthritis disease spectrum and may be defined as an inflammatory arthropathy associated with psoriasis and usually negative for high-titer
rheumatoid factor (RF) or anti-citrullinated protein antibodies. Until the 1950s, an inflammatory arthritis that occurred in
the presence of psoriasis was thought to represent rheumatoid
arthritis (RA) occurring coincidentally with psoriasis. Based primarily on clinical and radiologic grounds and with use of the
RF, the distinction between RA and PsA was gradually accepted.
However, it was not until 1964 that PsA was recognized by the
American Association of Rheumatism as a distinct clinical entity
and Moll and Wright published their original classification criteria in 1973.

## Epidemiology

The reported prevalence of PsA in the general population ranges
from 0.01% to 0.67%.
A 2018 meta-analysis found a pooled
prevalence of 0.13%, but very high levels of heterogeneity among
studies exist,
likely due to both differing definitions of PsA and
geographic factors. While the prevalence in the United States is
0.25%,
estimates within Europe are quite divergent (0.05% to
0.67%).
Similarly, and although the affected population in
Asia has generally been very low (e.g., less than 0.01%), this could
be a reflection of underdiagnosis of PsA rather than true absence
of disease.
Importantly, the prevalence of PsA is on the rise,
with increases seen worldwide,
although this may be due in
part to increased awareness and consequent diagnosis of PsA.
There is no sex predilection and onset of PsA can occur at any
age, although progression from skin psoriasis to synovio-entheseal involvement usually takes between 5 and 10 years. The vast
majority of individuals with PsA develop skin psoriasis prior to
the development of arthritis, while the rest either develop psoriasis
and arthritis simultaneously or have arthritis prior to skin involvement, with the latter most commonly occurring in pediatric
PsA.
Importantly, the prevalence of synovio-entheseal involvement in patients with psoriasis is much higher than in the general
population, ranging from 6% to 41%.
Despite the high prevalence within this psoriasis population,
PsA remains underdiagnosed.
One study found that 29% of
patients with psoriasis attending a dermatology clinic with no
known diagnosis of inflammatory arthritis had undiagnosed
PsA.
This has led to the development of screening questionnaires
for PsA, including the Psoriatic Arthritis Screening Evaluation, the
Psoriasis Epidemiology Screening Tool, and the Toronto Psoriatic
Arthritis Screen. When compared head-to-head, all three tools
had similar sensitivities (74%–77%) but with low specificities
(30%–39%),
complicating their use in clinical and research
settings.

While this clinically driven taxonomy is the most commonly
cited, many alternative descriptions have been proposed. Variability in the definition of terms has led to differences in the reported
frequency of PsA in the different studies. Furthermore, the pattern
of joint involvement is not fixed.
The CASPAR (ClASsification for Psoriatic ARthritis) criteria
have included a breakdown of disease-pattern subtypes.
This
multicenter study incorporated data on 588 PsA cases and 536
controls. In contrast to the original Moll and Wright paper, but
similar to many subsequent publications, approximately 63%
of patients had polyarticular joint involvement (often difficult
to distinguish from RA), compared to 13% with oligoarticular
disease. The other patterns of joint involvement described by
Moll and Wright occurred much less commonly. Lone DIP disease was found in less than 5%, but DIP involvement can occur
in any of the subtypes.
Predominant spondyloarthritis is also uncommon (occurring
in less than 5%), although axial involvement, including sacroiliitis and syndesmophyte formation, may be found in 25% to
70% of PsA cases, depending on the definition used.
Risk
factors for spinal involvement include severe peripheral arthritis

Differentiating between PsA with axial involvement and axial
spondyloarthritis (AxSpA) can be difficult. (HLA)-B*27 is more
common in patients with AxSpA, and peripheral arthritis is
more frequent in those with axial PsA (AxPsA).
Patients with
AxSpA tend to have more severe radiographic findings, including
higher-grade sacroiliitis, and are more likely to have complete
sacroiliac joint ankylosis and bridging syndesmophytes.
The
predominant pattern of sacroiliitis in both is symmetric rather
than unilateral, but unilateral involvement is seen more commonly in AxPsA.
Additionally, in AxSpA, syndesmophytes
are more symmetrically distributed, progress caudal to cranial,
and are primarily marginal; in AxPsA, syndesmophytes typically
progress randomly and are equal in frequency of marginal and
paramarginal.
Despite these distinctions, individual patients
may be difficult to classify and may simultaneously satisfy criteria for both AxPsA and AxSpA.
Finally, arthritis mutilans, a destructive form of arthritis associated with digital shortening, is rare. The Group for Research
and Assessment of Psoriasis and Psoriatic Arthritis (GRAPPA) has
developed a broad consensus on the features of arthritis mutilans.
Clinical criteria include shortening digits, finger telescoping, and/
or flail joint not caused by subluxation. Radiographic findings
include erosion involving entire articular surfaces on both sides of
the joint and/or pencil-in-cup change; osteolysis is considered to
be the defining feature.

Dactylitis is characterized by a sausage-shaped swelling of the
fingers or toes (
Fig. 78.4B
) and may be found in up to a third of
patients with PsA at first presentation; 48% may have an episode of
dactylitis during follow-up.
Ultrasound and magnetic resonance
imaging (MRI) studies have shown that joint and tenosynovial
inflammation are prominent in involved digits with dactylitis.
Enthesitis, inflammation at the site of tendon and ligament
insertion into bone, is a feature of all of the spondyloarthropathies
and may be a presenting feature in PsA. Overall, enthesitis is found
in 38% of patients at presentation.
The most common entheseal
sites involved are the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia insertions.
Other sites include the insertions of the quadriceps and patellar
tendons, the iliac crest, the rotator cuff, and the epicondyles at the
elbow. Patients have reported pain at these sites, with tenderness
and swelling sometimes noted on examination. Entheseal involvement may be asymptomatic, and ultrasound is more sensitive than
clinical palpation. Enthesophytes, new bone formation at the area
of tendon and ligament insertion onto the bone, may be detected
on radiograph but are not always associated with symptoms.
Other systemic features of inflammation are common in
patients with PsA, including stiffness after rest and fatigue. Fatigue
is reported by up to 78% of patients and is independently associated with worse physical function, quality of life, and work
productivity.
There is a long-established relationship between the eye and psoriatic disease. PsA usually precedes the development of psoriatic eye
complications, but this is not necessarily the case. The incidence of
uveitis in patients with PsA is approximately 3.5 times higher than
that of the general population.
In PsA, uveitis usually affects the
anterior chamber, but posterior uveitis and pan-uveitis can occur,
and these may be more severe and threatening to vision. Other eye
complications associated with psoriatic disease include conjunctivitis, keratoconjunctivitis sicca, and episcleritis.
Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is more common in patients
with PsA than in either patients with psoriasis or the general population. Some studies have found an association between PsA and
both Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis; others have found an
association between PsA and Crohn’s disease only.
There is also
a significantly increased prevalence of subclinical bowel inflammation in patients with PsA with axial disease.

## Laboratory Features

There is currently no diagnostic laboratory test for PsA, and this
remains an area of unmet need. Although the absence of RF is
considered an important distinguishing feature from RA (i.e.,
included in the CASPAR classification criteria), low levels of RF
may be found in some patients (5% to 13%) with typical PsA
features.
Anti-citrullinated protein antibodies were initially
thought to be specific to RA but can also be seen in up to 12% of
patients with PsA

is associated with more joint involvement and erosive disease.

## Radiographic Features

Although substantial advances have been made in the application of both musculoskeletal ultrasound (MSKUS) and MRI in
patients with arthritis, plain radiographic imaging remains the
most widely utilized imaging modality for assessing bony changes
in peripheral joints in PsA.

### Plain Radiography

PsA is characterized by the presence of both osteodestructive
and osteoproliferative changes (
Fig. 78.5
).
Radiographs of the
peripheral joints can show co-occurrence of bony erosions and
joint space narrowing with new bone formation, sometimes
resulting in the classic “pencil-in-cup” deformity. New bone formation can be characterized by periostitis, bony ankylosis, and
enthesophyte formation and is part of the CASPAR classification
criteria (i.e., juxtaarticular new bone formation). Involvement is
often asymmetric.
Radiographic progression in PsA is slow in early stages of the
disorder, with a mean modified Sharp (to include DIP joints in
the hands) erosion score increasing from 1.2 at presentation to
3.0 at 2 years. However, within this time period, up to 47% of
patients with PsA develop erosive changes.
In PsA clinical trials,
radiographic progression is an important outcome measure; however, with the exception of the Ratingen score, no scoring system
(including the Sharp-van der Heijde modified scoring method,
which is the most frequently used in PsA trials) has been developed

MSKUS can be used to identify both structural changes and perturbations in perfusion of joints, entheses, and soft tissues (

also been used in objective monitoring of the response of synovitis

### Other Imaging Modalities

Use of other imaging modalities, such as computed tomography (CT) or scintigraphy, has largely been superseded by MRI.
However, micro-CT and high-resolution peripheral quantitative
CT are an excellent imaging platform for detecting morphologic
changes of juxtaarticular bone,
but CT is usually reserved
for patients for whom MRI is contraindicated or unavailable.
Positron emission tomography (PET) has shown promise for the
detection of early changes of PsA. In one study, PET/CT detected
asymptomatic enthesitis in one-third of patients with psoriasis.
Another exploratory study used high-resolution [
F] fluoride
PET to explore the pattern of DIP joint bone metabolism in PsA
compared with OA and healthy joints. There was a greater uptake
of [
F] fluoride at the enthesis and periosteum and at the tufts of
the distal phalange of PsA compared with OA.
More recently,
new PET tracers such as fibroblast activation protein inhibitors have been used to allow for identification of dynamic tissue
responses to inflammation.

## Clinical Course and Outcome

impacted the clinical course of PsA, studies performed in the
pre-targeted therapy era provide important information on the
natural history of the disease. Five such early cohorts were studied

was 6 to 12 months, the median patient age at onset of psoriasis was 27 to 31 years, and the median patient age at onset of
arthritis was 38 to 52 years. Overall, little relationship was noted

the hands and feet were most commonly involved; the DIP joints
were involved in one-third of patients, usually in association with

The “Psoriatic March” is a proposed concept whereby systemic
inflammation results in insulin resistance, which in turn triggers
endothelial cell dysfunction, leading to atherosclerosis and finally
myocardial infarction or stroke.
Consequently, individuals with
PsA have a higher prevalence of cardiovascular disease compared
with the general population and an increased risk of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality.
A recent meta-analysis comprising
over 30,000 patients with PsA found a 43% increased risk of cardiovascular disease compared with the general population.
Specifically, the risk of myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular
disease, and heart failure was increased by 68%, 22%, and 31%,
respectively. In addition, traditional risk factors of cardiovascular
disease, such as hypertension, hyperlipidemia, obesity, and diabetes, are all elevated in PsA.
An increased inflammatory burden may also impact the risk of
cardiovascular disease. Studies have shown that extent of psoriatic
skin involvement is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular
disease
and, in one cohort study, disease activity as measured
by number of dactylitic joints increased risk for major cardiovascular events.
This is also seen when studying surrogate markers
of atherosclerosis. One study assessing ultrasonic measurement of

carotid total plaque area (TPA) found a higher TPA in patients
with PsA compared with participants with skin psoriasis alone.
In those with PsA, a higher TPA was associated with duration of
PsA, skin severity, and ESR. Additionally, the use of coronary CT
angiography in individuals without any symptoms of coronary
artery disease found that 76% of individuals with PsA versus 44%
of controls demonstrated coronary plaques.
Elevated CRP, high
swollen joint count, and disease duration were all independent risk
factors of increased coronary plaque burden in PsA. Furthermore,
TNF inhibition (TNFi) in patients with psoriatic disease was associated with reduced carotid plaques in men and improvement in
vascular inflammation in both men and women.
Highlighting the impact of inflammation, the American College
of Cardiology/American Heart Association Guidelines on the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease now recognize chronic
inflammatory conditions (such as psoriasis) as a “risk-enhancing
factor.”
The European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(EULAR) has published evidence-based guidelines for cardiovascular risk management in patients with inflammatory arthritis.

### Diabetes Mellitus and Metabolic Syndrome

Insulin resistance, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and type 2 diabetes mellitus are all more prevalent in patients with PsA.
In one cohort of 283 individuals with PsA, 44% had metabolic
syndrome, and 16% had insulin resistance. A significant association was also noted between metabolic syndrome and more severe
PsA (odds ratio [OR] 4.47;
p
< 0.001).
In a study using a large
UK general practice database, the Health Improvement Network,
investigators found an increased risk of incident diabetes in PsA.
The age- and sex-matched hazard ratio (HR) for diabetes was 1.72
(95% confidence interval [CI], 1.46–2.02) and remained significant even after adjusting for body mass index, smoking, alcohol,
baseline glucocorticoid use, and comorbidities (HR 1.33, 95%
CI, 1.09–1.61).
Additionally, the risk of metabolic syndrome
may be higher in PsA compared with other types of inflammatory arthritis. In one study of 109 PsA patients compared with
699 RA and 122 AS controls, the adjusted OR for the metabolic
syndrome in PsA was 2.44 (95% CI, 1.48–4.01;
p
< 0.001) relative to RA or AS. The adjusted ORs for central obesity, impaired
fasting glucose, and low high-density lipoprotein cholesterol were
also significantly higher in patients with PsA.

### Other Comorbidities

The prevalence of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is
increased in patients with psoriasis and further elevated in patients
with PsA. In patients with psoriasis, an increased prevalence of
NAFLD is associated with metabolic syndrome, hypercholesterolemia, obesity, and the presence of PsA.
Liver disease can also
result from medications used to treat PsA, particularly methotrexate and leflunomide, and the presence of liver disease can complicate treatment selection.
Depression and anxiety are closely associated with psoriatic
disease, and the prevalence appears to be higher in PsA compared
with psoriasis alone.
In PsA, the prevalence of depression has
been estimated between 9% and 36% and that of anxiety between
15% and 20%.
Identification and management of mental
health comorbidities are essential as depression has been shown
to affect pain perception, quality of life, and ability to achieve
remission.
Presence of depression may also affect treatment
choice. Apremilast, for example, contains a label warning regarding
emergence or worsening of depression, suicidal thoughts, or other
mood changes.
Most studies of osteoporosis do not show higher prevalence
in PsA compared with the general population
; however,
patients treated with long-term glucocorticoids may be at higher
risk.
Additionally, hyperuricemia and gout have an increased
incidence in PsA,
which is of particular importance as gouty
arthropathy may be confused with PsA in certain circumstances.

## Pathogenesis

Many studies have explored key components of disease pathogenesis, including the contribution of genetic factors, the role of
infection or trauma, studies of animal models or involved sites
of disease, and the importance of components of the immune
system, such as cytokines. Despite much progress in identifying
factors that contribute to the development of PsA, overall the
pathogenesis of the disease remains incompletely understood.

### Genetic Factors

Familial clustering of psoriasis and PsA is well described. Twin
studies have shown a relatively high rate of concordance of psoriasis in monozygotic twins (up to one-third depending on the
study),
but a much lower concordance of PsA (i.e., 11%).
The genetic basis for this clustering has been the subject of extensive investigations in psoriasis but has been less studied in PsA.
Studies of PsA have often included patients as a subset of larger
psoriasis cohorts, and the diversity of clinical phenotypes has not
often been recognized. Earlier studies focused on the genetic contribution of the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) region
located on chromosome 6, and a number of more recent genomewide association studies (GWAS) have revealed genetic contributions from non-MHC loci.
A strong association between psoriasis and the HLA-C region
of the MHC has long been recognized. Whether this was HLAC*06 itself, found in approximately 60% of individuals with psoriasis, or a region telomeric to HLA-C has been the subject of
much controversy. Elder definitively showed that the HLA susceptibility region for psoriasis is HLA-C*06, which is often in
linkage disequilibrium with other HLA-B alleles such as HLAB*57, HLA-B*37, and HLA-B*13.
The presence of HLA-C*06
is associated with an earlier patient age of onset of psoriasis (type
1 disease, <40 years old) and with more extensive and severe disease.
Multiple studies have shown that in patients with PsA the
frequency of HLA-C*06 was lower than that in patients with psoriasis (28.7% vs. 57.5%;
p
< 0.001).
Additional associations
in PsA have been found with HLA-B*08, HLA-B*27, HLA-B*38,
and HLA-B*39. It was also observed that arthritis develops much
closer to the appearance of psoriasis in the HLA-B*27:05:02 or
B*39:01:01 subset than in the HLA-C*06:02 subset of patients
with PsA. Overall, patients with HLA-B*27:05:02 had similar
penetrance for musculoskeletal and skin disease, and they were
roughly contemporaneous in onset; however, nearly a third of
these patients actually had the onset of skin disease after the onset
of musculoskeletal disease (i.e.,
psoriatic arthritis sine psoriasis
).
Conversely for HLA-C*06:02 patients, the interval between skin
disease and musculoskeletal disease was more than a decade, and
the
HLA-C*06:02
gene appeared to specify an effect that included
highly penetrant severe cutaneous disease and delayed milder
musculoskeletal disease with greatly reduced penetrance.
In a further exploratory study, HLA-B*27:05 was also strongly
associated with symmetric sacroiliitis, enthesitis, and dactylitis. In contrast, HLA-B*08:01 was associated with predominant
asymmetric sacroiliitis, joint fusion, joint deformities, and dactylitis. HLA-C*06 was negatively associated with asymmetric
sacroiliitis.
These findings have been interpreted to suggest that the MHC
association with psoriasis lies close to the HLA-C region, whereas
the MHC association with the articular manifestations more likely
lies in or close to the HLA-B region. A study of a large cohort of
patients with PsA in the United Kingdom found HLA-C*06 to be
in linkage disequilibrium with HLA-DRBI*07 and that possession of both alleles was associated with fewer involved or damaged
joints (Pauline Ho, personal communication, 2007).
Other genes within the MHC region have been explored in
psoriasis and PsA. TNF promoter polymorphisms or a gene in
linkage disequilibrium with TNF may predispose the patient to, or
increase susceptibility to, psoriasis and PsA. One study has found
a further association between the TNF-308 A allele and disease
progression in early PsA.
Investigators have pointed to a class
I MHC chain-related A (MICA)-A9 polymorphism that confers
additional relative risk, in particular for polyarticular disease, in
patients with psoriasis who carry HLA-C*06:02.
MICA-A9
polymorphism was found in linkage disequilibrium with HLA-B
alleles (B*57:01, B*38:01). These results suggest that the
MICA
gene or other nearby genes may be involved in the development
of PsA.
Psoriasis whole-genome scans have identified additional nonMHC susceptibility regions, known as the
PSORS regions
on
chromosomes 4, 6, and 17. To date, no candidate genes from
these
PSORS regions
have been identified. GWAS studies have
confirmed associations of PsA susceptibility with HLA-C and
interleukin (IL)-12B. These efforts have identified another susceptibility region, TRAF3IP2 on chromosome 6p, which encodes a
protein involved in IL-17 signaling and interacts with members
of the Rel/nuclear factorκ
B (NFκ
B) transcription factor family.
Further studies have provided genome-wide significant
evidence for the involvement of the REL locus in PsA susceptibility and confirmed the previously reported association with psoriasis vulgaris.
A genome-wide significant association was found
between PsA and a single-nucleotide polymorphism (rs13017599),
detected at 2p16 near the REL locus encoding c-Rel (rs13017599;
p
= 1.18 × 10
; OR, 1.27; 95% CI, 1.18–1.35).
Multiple
other PsA-specific susceptibility loci with functional and pathophysiologic repercussions have been found, including IL-23R
(interleukin-23 receptor), TNFAIP3 (TNF alpha-induced protein
3; also known as A20), and PTPN22 (protein tyrosine phosphatase, nonreceptor type 22).
TRAF3IP2, which encodes for
Act1, an adapter protein critical for Th17-mediated inflammatory
responses, has also been associated with PsA.
Importantly, these
and other newly discovered genetic signatures have been computationally integrated to provide robust risk assessment of PsA development in psoriasis patients.

### Environmental Factors

The role of environmental factors in triggering skin or joint disease in patients with psoriasis or PsA has been supported largely by
clinical observations, although explanatory mechanisms remain
poorly understood. A strong association between guttate psoriasis
and preceding streptococcal infections in children has long been
recognized,
and the possibility of a streptococcal superantigen
underlying this association has been proposed. One study found
bacterial antigens in synovial tissue samples from PsA patients, but
this may be no different from those in non-inflammatory control
participants.
The microbiome—the collection of symbiotic, commensal,
and pathogenic microorganisms (and their genomes) residing in
the skin, mucosal surfaces, and other organs—is being extensively
studied as a potential trigger for psoriatic spectrum disease. A perturbation of the microbial community equilibrium (i.e., dysbiosis)
has been implicated in the initiation and perpetuation of several

immune-mediated inflammatory disorders, including psoriatic
disease.
In fact, studies in both animal models and humans
have demonstrated a mechanistic link between the microbiome,
gut inflammation, and both psoriasis and PsA. For example, multiple established susceptible murine models of psoriasiform disease and spondyolarthritis (SpA) features, do not demonstrate
the phenotype under germ-free conditions but exhibit an IL-23/
IL-17-driven, SpA-like inflammatory process when exposed
to (specific) microbes.
These observations strengthen the
hypothesis that intestinal dysbiosis is required to orchestrate
synovio-entheseal and cutaneous inflammatory disease.
In fact,
multiple studies in humans have demonstrated perturbations in
the intestinal (and skin) microbiota composition in psoriatic disease,
as well as worsening pathology after fecal microbial
transplantation.
In the currently prevailing hypothesis, this dysbiotic state leads to dysregulation of the gut (and/or perhaps skin)
epithelial barrier and the stimulation of IL-23-producing resident
immune cells.
IL-23R+ mucosal innate cells are then activated
and circulate away from the gut, migrating to the enthesis and
synovial tissues to cause inflammatory responses.
Multiple
groups are focusing efforts to further characterize the modulating
effects of the microbiome and its by-products in psoriatic disease
pathogenesis, including as a diagnostic and therapeutic target.
The Koebner phenomenon is the development of psoriasis
along the site of skin trauma and has been reported in 20%
to 25% of patients with psoriasis.
While less understood,
clinical observations indicate that mechanical stress may trigger episodes of joint inflammation, and the term
deep Koebner phenomenon
has been coined to describe this phenotypic
manifestation.
The association between smoking and psoriatic disease has been
a matter of debate. In the general population, smoking has been
linked to an increased risk of psoriasis and PsA.
Paradoxically, however, some studies have shown smoking to be negatively
associated with the development of PsA in patients with established psoriasis.
Further prospective studies are required to
elucidate the exact role of smoking in the progression of psoriatic
disease.
Finally, a link between stress and exacerbation of psoriasis has
been proposed, supported largely by clinical observational studies.
A similar association may exist in PsA, but this has not been
systematically examined.

### Animal Models

Although spondyloarthropathy has been detected in a variety
of primates, rodent models have proven helpful in deciphering
pathogenic pathways. In HLA-B*27 transgenic rats, skin, nail,
and joint features have been described that mimic select features
of the human phenotype.
HLA-B*27 transgenic rats raised in a
germ-free environment seemed to be protected from joint disease.
Mice genetically lacking class II MHC have developed skin and
joint disease, but the disease is confined to the distal phalanges,
with skin and nail disease also on the affected digits.
Involvement of the distal phalanges and nails was reported in aging male
DBA/1 mice from different litters that were caged together from
12 weeks.
In these animals, dactylitis, periostitis, and ankylosing enthesitis were observed. The SKG mice harbors a point mutation in the ZAP-70 gene yielding reduced T cell receptor signaling
that leads to multiorgan inflammation that mimics human SpA
disease–characterized enthesitis and axial and peripheral arthritis along with extra-articular manifestations, including psoriasis,
uveitis, and ileitis.
A20, the product of the
TNFAIP3
gene, is
a strong anti-inflammatory agent that regulates NFκ
B transcription and restricts its activation to suppress inflammatory responses
in normal homeostasis. Mice expressing distinct targeted mutations of A20 develop various phenotypic features characteristic
of PsA, most notably axial arthritis, enthesitis, DIP erosions, and
psoriasis.
Finally, the JunB protein is expressed in healthy and in clinically uninvolved psoriatic skin, but expression is considerably
reduced in involved psoriatic lesions.
Epidermal deletion of
JunB and c-Jun in a mouse model results in skin and joint disease
with 100% penetrance and a clinical and histologic phenotype
highly consistent with human psoriasis and PsA. In further experiments, the same authors showed that the joint disease, but not
the skin disease, required T and B cells and intact TNF receptor
1 signaling.

### Immunopathology

The key pathologic events in PsA occur in the skin, synovium,
entheseal sites, cartilage, and bone. Pathobiologic features in
the skin and synovium have been well described, and emerging
studies have focused on the enthesis. In relation to cartilage and
bone, studies have shown the presence of osteoclasts at the cartilage–pannus junction and high numbers of circulating osteoclast
precursors in the circulation of PsA patients. Detailed studies,
similar to those done in RA, on the synovial–cartilage–bone
interface could possibly yield valuable information regarding joint
destruction in PsA.

Skin
Psoriasis is a complex immune-mediated disease in which keratinocyte hyperproliferation associates with aberrant activation of
T lymphocytes, dendritic cells, and cytokines (primarily IL-23,
IL-17, and TNF) that lead to characteristic cutaneous plaques.
Involved psoriatic skin is characterized by epidermal hyperplasia,
mononuclear leukocytes in the papillary dermis, neutrophils in the
stratum corneum, and an increase in various subsets of dendritic
cells, which are major producers of TNF, IL-12, and IL-23.
CD8
T cells are the predominant T cell subset chiefly found in
the epidermis, whereas dermal T cells contain a mixture of CD4
and CD8
. In response to IL-23, naive CD4+ T cells differentiate into Th17 and Type 17 cells, which in turn produce IL-17A
synergizing with other cytokines (most notably TNF) to stimulate
keratinocyte proliferation and production of anti-microbial peptides and other pro-inflammatory cytokines.
Most T cells in
skin lesions express addressin, a cutaneous lymphocyte antigen, in
contrast to circulating T cells and T cells found in the inflamed
synovium in PsA.
Finally, vascular changes are prominent in
psoriasis, with impressive growth and dilation of superficial blood
vessels.
Synovium
Many early studies of synovial pathology in PsA highlighted the
presence of prominent and striking vascular changes. In the first
study that compared PsA and RA synovial tissues, quantitative
immunopathologic analysis confirmed these vascular changes and
found that vessel number was significantly increased in PsA (see
Chapter 28
).
Lining layer hyperplasia was less marked in PsA,
and fewer macrophages were seen trafficking into the synovium
and out to the lining layer. The numbers of T lymphocytes
and their subsets and the number of B cells were similar to the

frequency found in RA. Although neutrophil infiltration was not
assessed, this study examined adhesion molecule expression
further in the two patient subgroups and found E-selectin expression to be considerably reduced in PsA. Many of these observations have been confirmed by other authors.
In one study, the synovial immunopathologic features in
patients with SpA, including PsA, were compared with the features seen in RA.
Using a semiquantitative scoring system, the
authors identified many features characteristic of the SpA group
as a whole and in the PsA subgroup alone. Increased vascularity, higher neutrophil numbers (also seen in involved psoriatic
skin), and a higher number of infiltrating CD163
macrophages,
a marker of mature tissue macrophages, reliably distinguished
spondyloarthropathy from RA. No significant differences were
seen between oligoarticular versus polyarticular PsA.
The important role of the vasculature in PsA pathogenesis is
perhaps most elegantly shown by the large numbers of tortuous and dilated blood vessels observed through an arthroscopic
view of psoriatic joints.
An interaction of key growth factors is thought to regulate closely the new vessel formation or
angiogenic process. Growth factors—including TNF, transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
, platelet-derived growth factor, angiopoietins (ANG-1, ANG-2), and vascular endothelial
growth factor (VEGF)—have been described in skin and synovial tissue.
Because this expression is found at an early
stage of inflammation, it may represent a primary event in
PsA versus a reaction or response to hypoxia. One possibility
is that a genetic predisposition exists to endothelial activation,
which results in new vessel formation and increased cellular
trafficking.
Of interest, two studies have identified a change in synovial
CD3
T cell infiltration as correlating with clinical response in
patients commencing biologic therapies.
Changes in CD3
T cell infiltration also correlated with changes in MRI assessment of synovitis. Changes in CD3
T cell infiltration may well
prove to be a useful tissue biomarker of treatment response. In
another study, there was a reduction in the expression of CD4
regulatory T cells (Tregs) in the synovium of PsA patients treated
with abatacept.
Importantly, transcriptomic analyses comparing skin and joint
biopsies demonstrated a predominant Th17 profile in psoriatic
skin while PsA synovial tissues exhibited strong signals in TNF and
angiogenic pathways.
When the immunophenotype, molecular
profile, and function of synovial cell populations were studied, a
polyclonal subset of T resident memory cells were shown to be
polyfunctional, high pro-inflammatory mediator producers and
with increased CXCR6 expression.
Enthesis
Structural entheseal lesions are known to occur in a substantial proportion of psoriasis patients, even before clinically evident inflammation develops, and they are also associated with an increased risk
of PsA development.
These lesions develop extra-articularly
where tendons and ligaments insert into the periosteum. Inflammatory events at these anatomic sites are led by PGE2, IL-17A, and
IL-22, which activate mesenchymal cells to proliferate and differentiate into bone-forming osteoblasts
via expression of Wnt
proteins and bone morphogenic proteins (BMPs).
One group described the immunopathologic features of the
enthesis in patients undergoing joint replacement surgery with
SpA, including PsA, and compared them with RA.
The number of patients was small in this study, but a consistent increase in
CD8
T cell expression was observed at the enthesis in patients
with PsA compared with RA patients. These findings are consistent with the well-described association of PsA with class I HLA
antigens. They are also consistent with the previously described
dominance of activated and mature CD8
T cells in PsA synovial
fluid samples compared with RA.
In contrast, another study of
ultrasound-guided biopsies of five sites of acute enthesitis in early
SpA showed an inflammatory response with increased vascularity and cellular, predominantly macrophage infiltration.
These
studies have conflicting findings with respect to the predominant
infiltrating cell type and further work is needed in this area.
Cytokines
Synovial explant tissues obtained from PsA joints produce
higher levels of the T helper (Th) type 1 cytokines IL-2 and
interferonγ
protein than explants similarly cultured from OA
and RA patients.
This Th1 lymphocyte profile has also been
observed in psoriasis plaques.
The cytokines IL-1
β
and TNF were
released by psoriasis synovial explants in high concentrations.
In contrast, IL-4 and IL-5 were not identified, but IL-10 was
highly expressed in psoriatic synovium, although not in skin.
A similar pattern of cytokine production in PsA synovium was
shown by using immunohistochemical and gene expression
techniques.
Other innate cytokines, such as IL-18 and
IL-15, are present in PsA synovial tissue and are downregulated
by methotrexate therapy.
The role of Th17 cells has been explored, and an important
immunomodulatory role has been better established in psoriasis than in PsA. Comparison of lesional skin gene expression in
responders versus nonresponders to etanercept revealed rapid
downmodulation of innate IL-1
β
and IL-8 sepsis cascade cytokines in both groups, but only responders downregulated IL-17
pathway genes to baseline levels.
An increase in circulating Th17
cells has been found in PsA,
and an increase in IL-17 has been
found in skin, synovial tissue, and synovial fluid of PsA patients.
One study demonstrated that IL-17
CD4− T cells (predominantly
CD8
) are present at increased levels in PsA synovial fluid but not
in RA synovial fluid. In addition, the frequency of these synovial
fluid IL-17
CD4− T cells is correlated with serologic, clinical, and
imaging measures of PsA disease activity and erosive disease status.
Further supporting a key role for the Th17 pathway, genetic
polymorphisms in key cytokines involved in differentiation of T
cells to Th1 and Th17 subtypes, IL-12, and IL-23 have been found
to be associated with susceptibility to both psoriasis and PsA,
and inhibition of the common p40 subunit of IL-12/IL-23 has led
to clinical improvement in both skin and joint manifestations.
Importantly, inhibition of the p19 subunit of IL-23 has demonstrated the most robust clinical improvement in the skin yet,
with many patients achieving complete or near clearance of psoriasis.
Further observations by investigators revealed a higher
proportion of IL-17- and IL-22-producing CD4
cells within
the peripheral blood of PsA and psoriasis patients compared with
healthy controls.
There was no difference in the frequency of
IL-17
and IL-22
CD4
T cells between patients with psoriasis
alone and those with PsA. In addition to the genetic associations
described previously, there are now substantial data implicating the
IL17/IL-23 axis in mouse models of both psoriasis and SpA; investigators have shown that the anatomic sites that are inflamed in
SpA are primed to rapidly react to IL-23 by the presence of a previously unidentified population of IL-23R
resident cells.
They
also showed that in vivo exposure to IL-23 is sufficient to induce
highly specific entheseal inflammation in the absence of Th17 cells

and with rapid kinetics. These observations strengthen the case for
the central involvement of the IL17/IL-23 axis in both psoriasis
and PsA, as demonstrated by the clinical success of targeting these
pathways (see the “
Treatment
” section).
TNF levels are elevated in psoriatic skin, synovium, and synovial fluid of patients with PsA.
Several lines of evidence support the concept that TNF is an important cytokine in the psoriatic
joint. TNF transgenic mice exhibit extensive bone destruction
similar to that observed in some PsA patients. In a study of 129
patients with early PsA, patients with erosions were significantly
more likely to have the TNF-308 A allele, an allele associated
with high TNF production.
As mentioned earlier, immunohistochemical and gene expression studies have shown marked
upregulation of TNF in the psoriatic synovial membrane. Histopathologic analysis of synovial specimens from eight SpA patients,
four of whom had PsA, treated with the anti-TNF monoclonal
antibody infliximab revealed decreased vascularity, synovial lining
thickness, and mononuclear cell infiltration after therapy.

with PsA, infliximab therapy induced a significant reduction in

Matrix Metalloproteinases and Cartilage Destruction
Radiographs of psoriatic joints often reveal cartilage loss manifested as joint space narrowing. Similar to RA, matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and tissue inhibitors of MMPs (TIMPs) were
identified in PsA synovial lining and sublining layers.
In
particular, immunohistochemical studies revealed that MMP-9
localized to blood vessel walls, whereas MMP-1, MMP-2, MMP3, TIMP-1, and TIMP-2 showed a cellular and interstitial staining
pattern in the synovial lining. Serum levels of MMP-3 exhibited a
marked and rapid decrease after successful anti-TNF therapy, raising the possibility that this molecule may serve as an informative
biomarker in disease management. In another study, similar levels
of MMP-1 and MMP-3 messenger RNA were detected in RA
and PsA synovial tissues despite the fact that RA patients exhibited more erosions on plain radiographs.
The elevated ratio of
MMPs to TIMP-1 in synovial tissue favored cartilage degradation, although expression of MMPs was not significantly elevated
at the cartilage–pannus junction compared with other sites. These
reports indicate that MMPs are upregulated in PsA synovium, but
their precise functions remain to be defined.
Bone Remodeling
Radiographs of PsA joints can reveal markedly altered bone
remodeling in the form of bone resorption (tuft resorption or
osteolysis, large eccentric erosions, and pencil-in-cup deformities)
and new bone formation (periostitis, spur or enthesophyte formation, bony ankylosis). PsA joint biopsy specimens show large
multinucleated osteoclasts in deep resorption pits at the bone–
pannus junction.
These osteoclasts play an important role in
bone resorption.
Osteoclastogenesis (differentiation of monocytes into osteoclasts) is a contact-dependent process directed by osteoblasts and
stromal cells in the bone marrow. These cells release signals necessary for differentiation of an osteoclast precursor derived from the
CD14
monocyte population into an osteoclast. One of these signals is the receptor activator of NFκ
B ligand (RANKL), a member
of the TNF superfamily that binds to receptor activator of NFκ
B
(RANK) on the surface of osteoclast precursors and osteoclasts.
This ligand–receptor interaction stimulates proliferation and differentiation of osteoclast precursors and activation of osteoclasts.
It has been proposed that the relative expression of RANKL and
of its natural antagonist osteoprotegerin (OPG) ultimately controls osteoclastogenesis. Marked upregulation of RANKL and
low OPG expression were detected in the synovial lining layer of
patients with PsA compared with patients with OA.
Osteoclasts
strongly expressing RANK were also noted in cutting cones traversing the subchondral bone, supporting a bidirectional attack
on the bone in psoriatic joints. In addition, osteoclast precursors,
derived from circulating CD14
monocytes and known to express
dendritic cell-specific transmembrane protein (DC-STAMP),
were markedly elevated in the peripheral blood of patients with
PsA compared with healthy controls. In the arthritis prone transgenic Tg(hTNF) mice, genetic ablation of DC-STAMP abrogated
both bone resorption and inflammation.
Treatment of patients
with PsA with anti-TNF agents significantly decreased the level
of circulating osteoclast precursors, supporting a central role for
TNF in the generation of this precursor population.
The mechanisms responsible for new bone formation in the
psoriatic joint are poorly understood. Higher circulating levels of
bone formation markers, in particular bone alkaline phosphatase,
in patients with PsA compared with patients with RA both before
and after TNF inhibitor therapy have been demonstrated.
TGFβ
and VEGF may be pivotal in this process of new bone
formation, given that TGFβ
is strongly expressed in synovial tissues isolated from patients with AS and synergizes with VEGF to
induce bone formation in animal models.
Investigators showed
that BMP-2 and BMP-7 are upregulated in regions of pathologic
new bone formation.
These investigators showed that expression of phosphorylated Smad1 and Smad5, important signaling
molecules downstream of BMP, was markedly increased in regions
of new bone formation taken from the calcaneus in a patient with
Achilles tendinitis and periostitis. These studies provide evidence
that BMP molecules and possibly VEGF and TGFβ
are potential
mediators of ankylosis and periostitis in the psoriatic enthesis and
joint. Finally, the Wnt pathway and the balance between Wnt and
the Wnt antagonist dickkopf-1, which binds to the Wnt receptor complex on the surfaces of osteoblast lineage cells, may well
be of considerable importance in the disordered bone remodeling
found in PsA.

### Transition From PsO to PsA

Cross-sectional studies have identified several risk factors associated
with progression from skin psoriasis to PsA, including having a firstdegree relative with PsA, psoriasis phenotype, obesity, and other
comorbidities. By using the Icelandic genealogy database, risk ratios
(RRs) for the development of PsA in a sample that spanned five generations were estimated and results confirmed a strong and complex
genetic component with a significant RR detectable for individuals
with a positive family history extending to fourth-degree relatives
of PsA patients.
Additionally, a Canadian cohort found that the
RR of developing PsA in an individual with psoriasis who has a
first-degree relative with PsA compared with the risk in the general
population was over 30.
Intriguingly, both the extent and location of psoriasis may play a role in determining transition. When
comparing patients with a PASI score of greater than 20 versus less
than 10, one study found that severe psoriasis yielded a RR of 5.4
(95% CI, 1.6–21.7), a finding supported by other studies despite
differing definitions of psoriasis severity.
Additionally,
nail involvement, scalp, and inverse psoriasis (involving the body

fold; i.e., armpits, groin, inframammary regions) have all been associated with increased risk of progression.
Patients with comorbidities of uveitis, thyroid disease, depression, and obesity also have
increased risk of progression.

### Conventional Synthetic Disease-Modifying Anti- rheumatic Drugs

Based on limited evidence and expert opinion, nearly all conventional synthetic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (csDMARDs) appear to have small to moderate beneficial effects on
peripheral joints, enthesitis, and dactylitis.
Axial features
and nail disease, however, do not appear to respond to treatment
with these agents.
Good or moderate improvements in skin
disease have been reported with some of the older systemic agents,
such as methotrexate, cyclosporine, sulfasalazine, leflunomide,
and acitretin.
Methotrexate remains for many rheumatologists the csDMARD
choice for patients with PsA, but RCT evidence is limited. The
Methotrexate in Psoriatic Arthritis (MIPA) study randomized 221
participants to methotrexate or placebo and found no difference
in disease activity scores at 6 months in the treatment groups.
However, this study had a high dropout rate and many participants in the methotrexate group did not reach the target dose of
15 mg/week.
A small RCT of 35 patients with early PsA found
that participants receiving NSAIDs and methotrexate had more
improvement in joint counts and inflammatory markers than those
receiving NSAIDs alone.
The Study of Etanercept and Methotrexate in Subjects with Psoriatic Arthritis (SEAM-PsA) RCT
examined response to treatment with methotrexate, etanercept, or
combination therapy. This study found that 50.7% of participates
receiving methotrexate alone, at a median dose of 20 mg weekly,
achieved an ACR20 response at 24 weeks, although this was lower
than those receiving etanercept alone or in combination with methotrexate.
Those on methotrexate alone also had improvements in
enthesitis (43.1% had resolution) and psoriasis was clear or almost
clear in 66.3%. An open-label prospective trial of 73 patients on
methotrexate (average dose 17.5 mg/week) found that at 9 months,
MDA was achieved in 63% of patients
; however, this study and
the SEAM-PsA trial did not include placebo arms. The discrepancy
in results between these studies and the MIPA trial may be reflective
of the methotrexate dose. Additionally, multiple observation cohort
studies have also found methotrexate to be effective.
In a 24-week RCT of 190 patients, leflunomide was found to
be superior to placebo in improvement in the Psoriatic Arthritis Response Criteria (PsARC) scoring system (59% vs. 30%).
There were also significant, although small, improvements in
individual parameters, including joint scores, Health Assessment
Questionnaire results, PASI, and Dermatology Life Quality Index
scores. Observational studies have also shown improvement with
leflunomide.
Six RCTs have studied the use of sulfasalazine in PsA. The largest study included 221 patients. Using the PsARC scoring system,
59% of patients in one study achieved a therapeutic response but,
consistent with other studies, a high therapeutic response (42.7%)
was also noted in placebo-treated patients.
Cyclosporine has also been shown to be highly effective for
skin psoriasis,
but there are no RCTs comparing cyclosporine
with placebo in PsA. In a 12-month RCT, 72 patients with PsA
and an incomplete response to methotrexate were randomized to
either continue methotrexate alone or to add cyclosporine. The
combination group had significant improvements in synovitis
detected by ultrasound and PASI score.
In contrast to other DMARDs, anti-malarial agents (i.e.,
hydroxychloroquine) have shown no evidence of treatment benefit; although exacerbation of psoriasis has been reported, and these
agents are not recommended for the treatment of PsA.

### Biologic Disease-Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs

The approach to treatment in PsA has changed considerably with
the introduction of biologic therapies in the early 2000s.
TNF Inhibitors
As a result of numerous large, well-conducted RCTs, accumulating
evidence indicates that anti-TNF treatments are effective in controlling peripheral arthritis signs and symptoms, improving quality of life, and preventing radiographic progression.
Approved
anti-TNF agents include adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab,
golimumab, and certolizumab pegol. Although the anti-TNF
therapies have not been compared head-to-head, their efficacy on
peripheral arthritis seems to be similar. In clinical trials, infliximab
showed an ACR20 response rate of 59% (vs. 15% placebo), adalimumab 58% (vs. 14% placebo), certolizumab 58% (vs. 24% placebo), golimumab 51% (vs. 9% placebo), and infliximab 58% (vs.
11% placebo).
Evidence indicates that anti-TNF therapies
are also effective for other musculoskeletal disease features, such as
enthesitis, dactylitis, and axial disease.
Anti-TNF therapy also improves both skin and nail disease.
In patients on adalimumab, certolizumab, and infliximab, 59%,
62%, and 64% of patients, respectively, achieved an improvement in PASI-75 score compared with placebo rates of 1% to
3%.
Etanercept was the least effective on skin (23%
achieved PASI-75 vs. 3% placebo)
; however, better skin (but
not articular) responses have been demonstrated in etanercept
patients treated with 50 mg twice weekly compared with once
weekly.
The past 5 years have seen the emergence of a number of TNFi
biosimilar agents (bsDMARDs). These compounds clearly reduce
health care costs and increase patient access to biologic therapy
while maintaining the same benefits as the originator compounds.
Two RCTs demonstrated noninferiority of biosimilars compared
with their original counterparts (infliximab vs. CT-P13 and etanercept vs. CHS-0214).
The safety profiles of these agents are
also highly similar to the reference product.
Methotrexate is often prescribed as a comedication in TNFi
treatment for PsA. Its role in this setting was evaluated using the
Norwegian DMARDs registry. The study analyzed 440 patients,
170 on anti-TNF alone and 270 on combination therapy. Results
showed that anti-TNF drug survival in the group receiving combination therapy was superior to those not on TNFi alone. The
effect was mainly seen in those on infliximab, which leaves open
the possibility of a role for methotrexate in inhibiting development
of anti-drug antibodies when patients are treated with monoclonal
antibodies.
In contrast to these results, results from the SEAMPsA study showed little additional benefit from treatment with
combination methotrexate and etanercept compared with etanercept monotherapy at 24 weeks.
IL-17 Blockers
There is accumulating evidence that IL-17 is central to the pathogenesis of psoriasis and PsA. Two treatments targeting IL-17 are
currently U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)–approved
for treatment of PsA, secukinumab and ixekizumab. Secukinumab

is a fully human, high-affinity, monoclonal antibody that binds to
and neutralizes IL-17A. Secukinumab is approved for the treatment of psoriasis, PsA, and AS (and nonradiographic axial-SpA).
In a phase 3, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, a significantly
higher proportion of patients achieved an ACR20 response at
week 24 with secukinumab 300 mg (54%,
p
< 0.0001), 150 mg
(51%,
p
< 0.0001), and 75 mg (29%,
p
= 0.039) versus placebo
(15%) and 63% achieved a PASI-75 response. Moreover, 52-week
data from the same study suggests that efficacy of secukinumab is
sustained over time.
Ixekizumab is another humanized monoclonal antibody that
inhibits IL-17A and is approved for the treatment of PsA. Ixekizumab was tested against placebo and adalimumab in the phase
3 SPIRIT 1 open-label clinical trial.
Ixekizumab had significantly higher ACR20 response compared with placebo (62.1% vs.
30.2%) but similar to active control adalimumab (57.4%). Ixekizumab also showed improvement in physical function measures
and dactylitis and showed slowed progression of radiographic evidence of structural damage compared with placebo. For patients
with baseline BSA
≥
3, 75.3% achieved a PASI-75 response by
week 12 and had improvement in nail involvement.
Despite effectiveness for psoriasis and PsA, trials for IL-17
blockers in IBD have either been unsuccessful or stopped early
due to exacerbation of disease.
While trials of IL-17 blockers in psoriasis and PsA have not been associated with increased
incidence of IBD,
several case reports describe this phenomenon.
Therefore, these agents are usually avoided in patients
with concomitant IBD.
IL-23 Blockers
Agents targeting IL-23 have also been shown to be effective in
psoriasis and PsA. Ustekinumab is a fully human IgG1
κ
monoclonal antibody that binds to the common p40 subunit shared
by IL-12 and -23. Using doses of 45 mg and 90 mg, 42.4% and
49.5% of participants achieved an ACR20 response (vs. 22.8%
placebo). At 1 year, in those with BSA
≥
3% at baseline, 69.1%
of the ustekinumab-treated patients achieved at least a PASI-75
skin response and 49.5% had at least a 90% improvement in
skin psoriasis. Patients also demonstrated improvement in physical function, dactylitis, and enthesitis and showed slowed radiographic progression of joint damage.
Importantly, studies
on ustekinumab in axial SpA did not meet their primary endpoints
; therefore these agents should not be selected for those
with active axial disease.
Guselkumab and risankizumab are IL-23 inhibitors that act via
IL-23 p19 subunit binding. For guselkumab, at 24 weeks, 52%
of participants achieved an ACR20 response (vs. 22% placebo)
and 76% achieved a PASI-75 skin response.
For risankizumab,
57% of participants achieved ACR20 response (vs. 33% placebo)
and 52% achieved a PASI90 at 24 weeks.
Participants on both
guselkumab and rizankizumab also showed improvement in dactylitis, enthesitis, and physical function measures.
T Cell Inhibitor
Abatacept is a human fusion protein that inhibits T cell activation
through costimulatory blockade approved for the treatment of
PsA. Abatacept showed a significantly increased ACR20 response
versus placebo after 24 weeks (39.4% vs. 22.3%;
p
< 0.001). However, only 16.4% achieved a PASI-75 response (compared with
10.1% in placebo).
Given its modest efficacy compared with
other currently available biologics, this medication is rarely used
in psoriatic disease.

### Small Molecules or Targeted Synthetic Disease- Modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs

There are a number of small molecules that have been developed
for the treatment of PsA that have the advantage over biologic
therapies of being available as oral preparations.
Apremilast is a phosphodiesterase 4 inhibitor approved for the
treatment of psoriasis and PsA. At week 16, significantly more
patients receiving apremilast achieved ACR20 versus placebo
(40% [30 mg BID] vs. 19% [placebo];
p
< 0.001). In those with
baseline psoriasis affecting
≥
3% BSA, 21% achieved a PASI-75
response compared with 4.6% of the placebo group (
p
= 0.004).
Apremilast demonstrated an acceptable safety profile and was generally well tolerated.
Two Janus kinase inhibitors are approved for the treatment
of PsA. In a phase 3 trial of tofacitinib in patients who had an
inadequate response to csDMARDs, those receiving tofacitinib
had a significantly greater ACR20 response rate compared with
placebo (50% vs. 33% placebo) and greater PASI-75 score (43%
vs. 15%) after 3 months.
In a different phase 3 trial of upadacitinib, 70.6% of participants treated with upadacitinib achieved
an ACR20 response (vs. 36.2% for placebo) after 6 months. In
this same study, 62.6% achieved a PASI-75 response (vs. 21.3%
placebo).

### Treat to Target

In the TIght COntrol in Psoriatic Arthritis (TICOPA) study, 206
patients were randomly assigned to either tight control (step-up
regimen aiming for MDA by using methotrexate, combination
DMARDs, and anti-TNF) or to standard care. In the tight control group, 61.7% achieved ACR20 response at 48 weeks compared with 44.6% in the standard care group. However, there were
more adverse events in the tight control group.
This study highlights the possibility of adopting a treat-to-target approach in PsA
and accepting MDA as a potential treatment target in the future.

### Future Therapy Strategies

The therapeutic options for PsA continue to expand, and the
advent of biologic and small-molecule therapies mean that PsA
patients are reaching levels of disease control previously not
thought possible. However, it is noteworthy that while most
patients meet primary clinical endpoints of ACR20 in clinical trials, this indicates at best a partial response. To date, less than 35%
of patients meet clinically meaningful endpoints such as MDA.
Additionally, a significant proportion of patients do not respond
to biologics. Discordant responses in arthritis and skin outcomes
are not uncommon, and some treatments may lose efficacy with
time or patients may experience intolerable side effects. Therefore
highly effective treatment strategies for PsA remain a significant
unmet need and new strategies must be pursued including novel
therapeutic targets, combination therapy, and prevention.
New therapeutic targets are currently in development. Bimekizumab, a dual IL-17A and IL-17F inhibiting humanized monoclonal antibody, has been approved in Europe and Japan for the
treatment of psoriasis and is awaiting evaluation for PsA. In a phase
2b clinical trial, 51% to 73% of bimekizumab patients (depending on dosing regimen) achieved an ACR20 response at 12 weeks,
compared with 19% in the placebo group.
Deucravacitinib,
an oral, selective inhibitor of tyrosine kinase 2 (TYK2) acting via
binding to the TYK2 regulatory domain, was FDA approved for

skin psoriasis. A phase II trial in PsA, showed improvement in
ACR20 response at 16 weeks compared with placebo (52.9% [6
mg] vs. 62.7% [12 mg] vs. 31.8% [placebo]).
Another strategy for treatment may be a combination of targeted therapies. A bispecific TNF- and IL-17A-targeted variable
domain immunoglobulin (ABT-122) did not show any difference
in ACR20 response compared with adalimumab, but ACR50/70
and PASI75 were higher for the ABT-122 group at several timepoints.
Perhaps even more importantly, the safety profile was
similar to that of adalimumab alone. Several case reports have
reported improved efficacy with the use of combination TNFi and
anti-IL-23 therapies.
The VEGA trial in ulcerative colitis
examined guselkumab and golimumab in combination with preliminary results showing improvement in clinical response at 12
weeks without an increase in adverse events.
A similar clinical
trial is now underway in PsA.
Prevention may be a relevant strategy in PsA given that there is
a readily apparent pre-clinical marker (skin psoriasis) that generally
precedes synovio-entheseal involvement, creating a unique opportunity for intervention in a susceptible and readily identifiable population. Retrospective observational studies have sought to address the
question of whether treatment with biologic agents has an impact
on PsA development. However, these studies have reported disparate results and reached different conclusions.
Prospective
RCTs are currently underway to address this question.

### Lifestyle Modifications

Lifestyle modifications, such as weight loss and exercise, may
also be undertaken with the potential to lessen pain and improve
function. Elevated body mass index has been associated with
inadequate response to anti-TNF therapy
while weight loss
in conjunction with anti-TNF therapy has been associated with
improvements in PsA assessed by different measures.
Exercise has also demonstrated improvement for disease activity, wellbeing, and associated comorbidities.

## Conclusion and Future Research Agenda

PsA is a chronic, complex immune-mediated inflammatory condition that affects multiple domains and organ systems. Although
transformational efforts over the past 20 years have helped elucidate many aspects of pathogenesis and revolutionized therapeutics for both psoriasis and PsA, outcomes for synovio-entheseal
inflammation have not achieved those seen in psoriatic skin disease. Targeted therapies against TNF, followed by antibodies that
block the IL-23/IL-17 axis in addition to the availability of smallmolecule therapies, have dramatically improved the ability to treat
psoriasis, with a significant number of patients now achieving
total clearance of cutaneous disease.
However, this extraordinary degree of clinical response has
yet to be attained in PsA, where up to half the patients do not
experience clinically relevant improvement with blockade of the
same pathways. Multiple groups are working toward uncovering yet unidentified targets, implementing combination therapy
strategies with currently available modalities, and designing
interventional strategies to halt PsA before it becomes clinically evident. These efforts include private–public, multicenter,
and multipartner endeavors such as the National Institutes of
Health–driven Accelerating Medicines Partnership Autoimmune
and Immune Mediated Diseases and the European Innovative
Medicines Initiative HIPPOCRATES.
These endeavors, combined with programs that tackle multi
therapy interventional approaches (for a multidomain disease)
and preventive strategies, promise to propel the field forward by
studying the psoriasis-to-PsA continuum in an integrated and
comprehensive (both molecularly and phenotypically) manner.
