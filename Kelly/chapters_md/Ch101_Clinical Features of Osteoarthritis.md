## Introduction

Osteoarthritis (OA), the most common form of arthritis, affects
more than 300 million individuals worldwide
and is strongly
associated with aging (
Fig. 101.1
). Data from the 2010 to 2012
National Health Interview Survey provide an estimate of 50 million U.S. adults affected by arthritis, or nearly a quarter of the adult
population, with more than 20 million reporting arthritis-attributable activity limitations
; even this is likely an underestimate.
OA
of the knee and hip has a significant functional impact because of
effects on ambulation and mobility, is the cause of the majority of
joint replacement procedures, and is associated with considerable
medical costs. In the United States from 2000 to 2014, there was
a dramatic increase in procedure volume for primary knee and hip
arthroplasty, by 132% and 148%, respectively, to over 370,000
hip and 680,000 hip primary arthroplasties in 2014 (
Fig. 101.2
)
Given the aging of society and the obesity epidemic, we can only
expect the burden of OA to continue to increase.
OA affects all of the structures in and around a joint and should
be considered a failure of the total joint, with the disease process
affecting cartilage, subchondral bone, synovium, ligaments/tendons, meniscus, muscle, and nerve tissues. The clinically recognizable late- or end-stage joint with OA likely represents a final
common pathway of many different factors, including genetics,
environment, and biomechanical contributors.

site, the frequency or intensity of symptoms, and the time span
over which symptoms are assessed.

## Clinical Features

### General Signs and Symptoms

OA most commonly affects the knees, hands, feet, hips, and spine.
These joints may be symptomatic, or signs of OA may be identified only on radiographs. People with OA generally describe
pain in the joint(s) that is worse with activity, with limited morning stiffness (<30 minutes) and pain and stiffness with rest. This
stiffness after inactivity, or the “gelling” phenomenon, is often a
main feature, although morning stiffness is generally less severe
and of shorter duration than that seen in individuals with systemic inflammatory arthropathies. Affected joints in OA often
demonstrate bony enlargement and crepitus on examination, with
concomitant reductions in range of motion. Soft tissue swelling
or effusion may be present, although these symptoms tend to be
much less dramatic than in inflammatory arthritis. Reports of pain
may be more or less than expected on the basis of structural damThe effects of depression, disturbed sleep, and other psyage.
chosocial factors on the pain experience in individuals with OA
are increasingly being recognized.
Awareness is also increasing
of the contribution of central sensitization (i.e., enhanced neural
signaling leading to hypersensitivity) to chronic pain in OA, which
can result in pain remote to the affected joint site and loss of pain
inhibitory mechanisms. A systematic review and meta-analysis of
39 studies with self-reported questionnaire data on neuropathiclike pain and/or pain sensitization found a frequency of these pain
types of 20% to 40% among people with knee and 9% to 29%
among people with hip OA, regardless of instrument used or clinical setting.
Ongoing study is needed to determine optimal methods of identification and treatment of this issue.

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Knee

Knee OA is characterized by the insidious onset of knee pain with
gelling and limited range of motion. People with knee OA often
describe pain and limitation with walking, transferring (as from
seated to standing), and, especially, ascending or descending stairs.
These reports of pain and limitation are often associated with a
sensation of instability or “giving out” at the knee. A “locking”
sensation at the knee can be a consequence of stiffness, loose bodies in the joint space, or meniscal lesions and may be reproducible
using the McMurray test. Knees with OA often have demonstrable
crepitus (although this symptom is likely more indicative of patellofemoral than tibiofemoral disease
) and bony enlargement. Pain
may be elicited by palpation of the medial or lateral joint line, or
both. Effusions, when present, are often cool and generally without
erythema. They can be associated with popliteal bursa enlargement
(a Baker cyst) when large. Associated pain over the anserine bursa,
or even the greater trochanter, often occurs in individuals with
knee OA and may be related to altered biomechanics.
Appreciation of such soft tissue symptoms is important because they may be
amenable to corticosteroid injections with subsequent pain relief.
Malalignment, usually varus, is often seen in individuals with
severe disease but can be present even in individuals with mild or
early-stage disease. Clinically evident varus thrust is a risk factor for
knee OA progression.
Other signs of severe disease include flexion
deformities or joint instability. Quadriceps weakness represents an
early modifiable risk factor for knee OA progression, particularly in
women
and, in late stages of disease, it may be apparent as muscle atrophy.
Alterations in vibratory sense have been demonstrated
in association with knee OA, although the relation of these factors to
progression and pain is still unclear.
Patellofemoral OA may be
more common than tibiofemoral OA and can strongly contribute to
pain and disability at the knee, but it is often overlooked.
OA of
the patellofemoral joint is characterized by crepitus and pain when
ascending or descending stairs and is often located anteriorly. It can
be seen in isolation or in association with tibiofemoral OA. The relation between patellofemoral OA and commonly seen patellofemoral
pain disorders in younger individuals remains to be elucidated.

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Hip

OA of the hip can present with groin pain, which is fairly specific,
but it may be described more vaguely as pain in the thigh,
buttock, low back, and even in the ipsilateral knee. Therefore it is
important to assess for other causes of pain in the “hip,” including
back pain (e.g., lumbar disk degeneration, spinal stenosis, facet
joint OA, and sacroiliac pain); trochanteric bursitis; altered gait
due to knee disease; meralgia paresthetica (i.e., lateral femoral
cutaneous nerve entrapment); thigh claudication from vascular
causes; or even intrapelvic causes. It is also important to consider
other causes of hip and groin pain such as occult femoral neck
fracture or avascular necrosis. People affected by hip OA have
limitations in walking, bending, transferring, and stair climbing.
Internal rotation of the affected hip is often limited and can be
quite painful, even in early-stage disease; it is often evident to
the patient through difficulty in putting on socks, tying shoes,
or trimming toenails. Visible deformity, hip flexion contracture,
or severe limitations of range of motion are indicators of more
advanced disease, which may also be associated with shortening
of the affected limb because of superior migration of the femoral head. In young individuals presenting with groin pain that
is worsened by sitting and who have pain and limitation when
internally rotating and adducting the hip in the flexed position,
femoroacetabular impingement syndrome is a consideration.

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Hand

The hands often provide the first clue to a diagnosis of OA. As
previously mentioned, evident bony enlargement of the distal
interphalangeal (DIP) joints (Heberden’s nodes) and proximal
interphalangeal (PIP) joints (Bouchard’s nodes) can be appreciated
(
Fig. 101.3
). These nodes may be acutely inflamed with warmth
and tenderness or may be bland, hard enlargements and are often
more marked in the dominant hand. Some patients, most commonly older women, have erosive OA characterized by episodic
inflammation, pain, and swelling. It remains a matter of debate
whether this type of OA is part of the continuum or is a separate disease entity.
OA involvement of the first carpometacarpal
(CMC) joint is particularly problematic and can lead to significant pain, limitations in functionality of the hand, and reduced
grip strength.
CMC squaring, representing deformity of the
joint due to osteophyte formation and joint space narrowing, can
be seen on examination. Bilateral involvement of multiple joints

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Spine

Facet joint OA is prevalent among older adults. In one study, cervical spine facet joint OA and lumbar spine facet joint OA were
present in 57% and 89%, respectively, of community-living adults
older than 65 years.
The association between facet joint OA
and back pain has been controversial,
although a study using
CT assessments of lumbar facet joint OA identified associations
between severe facet joint OA and back pain, with an odds ratio
greater than 2; individuals with multiple affected facet joints were
also more likely to have back pain.
Facet joint OA is associated
with OA of other joint sites, including the knees and hands.
Osteophytosis of the spine is nearly ubiquitous in older individuals, although it is often asymptomatic.
Lumbar disk degeneration (LDD), characterized by disk space narrowing, end-plate
sclerosis, and herniation, is often seen in association with radiographic osteophytosis,
although the relationship between LDD
and OA of other joint sites remains controversial.
Symptomatically, these findings can be associated with radiculopathy, a pathologic process most commonly seen in older
patients as a consequence of hypertrophied facet joints or uncovertebral joints, disk protrusion, osteophytosis of the vertebral body,
or a combination of these features.
Cervical radiculopathy
most commonly involves the seventh cervical nerve root and is
caused by degenerative changes at the C6–C7 level.
Patients may
present with pain/numbness in the shoulders, triceps area, forearm,
and dorsal long finger, and triceps weakness or loss of reflex may
be present.
Lumbosacral radiculopathy, frequently due to disk
degeneration (particularly at lower lumbar levels), presents with
radiating back pain that is usually exacerbated by leaning forward,
sitting, or straining and is relieved by lying down.
Specific clinical
presentation varies by level, with the lower levels more commonly
affected. L4 radiculopathy can result in medial lower leg sensory
changes, weak knee extension and hip adduction, and potentially
reduced knee jerk.
At L5, foot drop and sensory symptoms of the
anterolateral leg and dorsal foot may be seen, whereas S1 radiculopathy is characterized by weakness of plantar flexion, knee flexion, and hip extension with reduced ankle jerk.
Cauda equina
syndrome, a medical emergency, occurs as a result of compression
of the lower lumbar and sacral nerve roots by a central disk herniation, commonly at L4–L5. This syndrome results in bowel and
bladder dysfunction, saddle anesthesia, and leg weakness.
Diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis, or DISH, is characterized by exuberant osteophytosis and calcification of spinal ligaments and entheses covering four or more spinal levels, leading to
an appearance likened to flowing candle wax.
Hyperostosis can
also involve peripheral sites such as the elbows and pelvis. DISH
is often asymptomatic and may be identified only by radiographic
appearance or can present in a fashion similar to OA or LDD.
Large anterior cervical spine osteophytes, which are more often
seen in DISH but are possible in LDD, can cause dysphagia as a
result of compression of the esophagus.

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Shoulder

The shoulder is a common source of pain in people with OA,
although the symptoms are more often due to osteophytosis and
narrowing of the acromioclavicular and/or sternoclavicular joints
than of the glenohumeral joint itself. Other maladies common in
older adults, such as subacromial bursitis, rotator cuff disease, and
adhesive capsulitis, should be assessed. Rotator cuff damage, in particular, can predispose to glenohumeral OA, which presents similarly to OA in other large joints with crepitus and reduced range of
motion with pain.
Disease at the cervical spine can present as pain
in the shoulder region, and thus the shoulder evaluation should
include an assessment of the cervical spine. Milwaukee shoulder
syndrome is a rare destructive arthropathy of the glenohumeral
joint associated with large effusions, which is bilateral in 60% of
cases and also affects the knees in 50% of cases.
Radiologically,
periarticular calcifications and cuff rupture may be seen, and synovial fluid examination often reveals high red blood cell content and
basic calcium crystals (particularly if Alizarin Red staining is used).

### Joint-Specific Signs and Symptoms: Other Joints

OA of the foot has been relatively neglected in the literature to
date.
The foot joints most commonly affected by OA include
the talocalcaneal joint, talonavicular joint, calcaneocuboid joint,
metatarsocuneiform joint, and first metatarsophalangeal (MTP)
joint; these joints can be affected by erosive OA as well.
OA
of the first MTP joint is characterized by pain and hallux valgus
(bunion) deformity and has radiographic features (e.g., joint space
narrowing and osteophytosis) similar to OA at other joints. Loss
of function at the first MTP joint as a result of ankylosis (hallux
rigidus) can lead to altered gait. Ankle OA likely occurs more frequently than previously thought
and may be due to biomechanical alterations from OA at other joints, in addition to accepted
post-traumatic and injury-related etiologies. Elbow OA is rare
and may be the result of trauma, vibration damage, or inflammatory entities such as gout or pseudogout. Temporomandibular
joint OA is largely ignored in the orthopedic and rheumatologic
literature but is well researched in the dental literature; it results in
pain, clicking, locking, decreased range of motion, and crepitus of
the joint, much like in other joints affected by OA.

### Polyarticular Osteoarthritis

Although the term
generalized OA
has been used to describe multiple joint involvement in OA, there is no universally understood or
accepted definition of what constitutes multiple joint OA (MJOA;
Fig. 101.4
).
It is clear that individuals with more than one joint
involved have poorer functional status.
In 1952 Kellgren and
Moore provided the first clinical description of MJOA, involving primarily Heberden’s nodes and the CMC joints, with the
spine, knees, hips, and feet involved in descending frequency.
Later studies defined MJOA in a variety of ways including numbers of affected joints (continuously or with thresholds) overall or
in the hand, or summed semiquantitative radiographic scores. It
remains unclear which joints should be included in such definitions, and particularly whether lumbar disk degeneration and hip
OA are part of the same disease entity as other joints. Clinically,
it is important to recognize that a patient with OA in one joint
is likely to have disease at other sites and to assess the impact of
that person’s overall OA burden on their overall level of pain and
functional status, rather than focusing on a single joint.

## Diagnostic Tests

OA is diagnosed clinically, and laboratory testing is rarely required.
Similarly, if the diagnosis is clear based on history and clinical
findings, radiographs are usually not required. The purpose of
additional diagnostic testing in OA is primarily to exclude potentially treatable underlying conditions. Depending on the patient’s
presentation, the diagnosis may be straightforward, or the differential diagnosis may include inflammatory arthropathies, such as
rheumatoid arthritis, crystalline arthropathies (particularly calcium pyrophosphate crystal deposition disease; see
Chapter 98
),

### Laboratory Tests

There is rarely an indication for testing rheumatoid factor or antinuclear antibodies or for performing other serologic studies in the
setting of clinically suspected OA; such tests should be reserved for
patients with findings suggestive of a more inflammatory arthropathy.
A complete blood cell count and chemistry panel, including glucose,
creatinine, and liver function tests, should be obtained before initiation of pharmacologic therapy for OA, especially in older individuals with comorbid medical conditions, because of an increased risk
of adverse events in this population (see
Chapter 102
). In cases in
which there is prominent involvement of the MCP joints, evaluation
for hypothyroidism and hemochromatosis may be warranted.
Synovial Fluid
The synovial fluid in people with OA is typically normal or mildly
inflammatory, appearing clear and colorless to slightly yellowish.
The leukocyte cell count is typically less than or equal to 2000
cells/mm
(<2 cells seen across 10 high-power fields).
Fluid is
often obtained in the course of an intra-articular injection for a
symptomatic joint, although diagnostic aspiration may also be
performed in the setting of an effusion. Concomitant calcium
pyrophosphate crystal disease can be identified, although other
calcium crystals, or hydroxyapatite, are not seen on routine preparations (see also
Chapter 53
).
Molecular Biomarkers
Quantitative determination of the products of cartilage, synovium,
and bone metabolism has provided several putative biomarkers
of OA pathophysiology. These biomarkers reflect processes such
as cartilage turnover, bone resorption/remodeling, and synovial
and other tissue inflammation.
Such biomarkers, obtained from
serum, urine, or synovial fluid, may eventually provide a method
for early diagnosis, monitoring of treatment effects, and objective trial endpoints, although currently they are used primarily in
research. Additional information is available in recent reviews
and in
Chapter 100
in this book.

### Imaging: Conventional Radiography and General Considerations

Conventional radiography is widely available in clinical settings
and is relatively inexpensive, making this modality useful to confirm the diagnosis of OA and exclude others, primarily when there
is clinical uncertainty regarding the diagnosis, when the joint
involved is atypical, or when evidence suggests other diagnoses
such as inflammatory arthritis, fractures, Paget’s disease, osteonecrosis, infection, or malignancy (see also
Chapter 58
). Radiographs of joints affected by OA typically show osteophytes, joint
space narrowing, sclerosis, and cysts of subchondral bone, which
will be described qualitatively on clinical radiographs.
For research purposes, semiquantitative and quantitative assessments are used. The semiquantitative Kellgren-Lawrence (KL)
grading system remains the most commonly used for research
purposes.
KL grades range from 0 (no osteophytes or joint
space narrowing) to 4 (severe joint space narrowing with subchondral sclerosis); a grade of 2 is generally considered diagnostic of
OA (
Fig. 101.5

unclear applicability of this system developed in the 1950s to current and diverse populations. Additionally, identification of joint
space narrowing on radiographs is not a specific finding indicative of cartilage loss but can also be reflective of meniscal disease.

view osteophytes and joint space narrowing separately and assign
grades for these specific OA features individually for various joint

### Imaging: Advanced Modalities

Several advanced imaging modalities such as MRI, ultrasound,
CT, and positron-emission tomography (PET) scans have been
used in OA research, although with the exception of ultrasound,
these specialized, expensive examinations are not indicated in routine clinical practice. A brief summary of these modalities in OA
is provided in the following sections.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
MRI is the most well-studied advanced imaging modality in
OA. Unlike radiography, it allows visualization of all joint tissues, including cartilage, menisci, ligament/tendon, synovium,
effusions, and bone marrow simultaneously, although arthroscopy remains the gold standard for joint tissue assessment.
An
MRI-based definition of tibiofemoral OA has been proposed that
requires either the presence of both definite osteophytes and fullthickness cartilage loss or one of these and one of the following
features: a subchondral bone marrow lesion or cyst, meniscal disease (subluxation, maceration, or horizontal tear), partial-thickness cartilage loss, or bone attrition.
There has been a push to
use MRI as a clinical trial endpoint, rather than radiographic joint
space loss, due to its greater responsiveness.
Semiquantitative scoring, which can be performed using conventional acquisition techniques, is frequently used to evaluate a number of OA features simultaneously, such as the articular cartilage,
meniscus, ligaments, bone marrow, synovitis, and joint effusion. Several scoring systems are publicly available. Whole Organ Magnetic
Resonance Imaging Score (WORMS), the oldest and most widely
used semiquantitative system for MRI scoring in OA, utilizes a subregional approach to scoring of features, including cartilage, bone
marrow lesions (BMLs), subchondral cysts, bone attrition, effusion,
synovitis, osteophytes, meniscal tears, and cruciate and collateral
ligaments.
Other systems include the Knee Osteoarthritis Scoring System (KOSS), the Boston Leeds Osteoarthritis Knee Score
(BLOKS), and the MRI Osteoarthritis Knee Score (MOAKS).
Overall, semiquantitative methods for knee OA are adequately
responsive to change and are reliable. Potential structural phenotypes of knee OA based on MRI include meniscal/cartilage based,
subchondral bone based, and those featuring inflammation.
A
2014 study using MOAKS and data from the Osteoarthritis Initiative found that, among 849 individuals with no radiographic
evidence of knee OA at baseline (bilateral KL grade = 0; one-third
of whom had baseline frequent knee symptoms), the majority had
cartilage damage (76%) and bone marrow lesions (61%) as identified by MRI, and these lesions were associated with prevalent and
incident knee symptoms.
Semiquantitative MRI scoring has also
been utilized at the hip, hand, shoulder, and spine, although to a
much lesser extent than for knee OA.
Quantitative assessment of MRI involves quantification of specific tissue characteristics such as thickness and volume of cartilage
or size and volume of BMLs. These techniques require specific
high-resolution sequences and are time and labor intensive but
provide greater sensitivity to change compared with semiquantitative scoring.
Quantitative MRI assessments can be used not only
for cartilage and bone but also for menisci and for assessment of
the infrapatellar fat pad.
Compositional imaging techniques, which allow visualization
of anatomy and biochemical properties as well, have been used in
the setting of very early-stage disease prior to obvious structural
alterations.
Techniques include delayed gadolinium-enhanced
MRI of cartilage and T1 rho, which can visualize changes in glycosaminoglycan concentrations, and T2 mapping, which reflects
variation in collagen orientation and cartilage hydration status.
Further study of these methods in human subjects, particularly
related to changes in healthy knees over time, is needed to fully
exploit their potential as outcome measures.
Ultrasound
Ultrasound is a point-of-care modality that can be used directly
by trained providers (e.g., rheumatologists, radiologists, and
orthopedists) to assess joint disease in patients.
Its relatively low
cost, safety (because it does not entail use of radiation or contrast agents), short examination duration, convenience (it can be
performed in the provider’s office), and noninvasive nature make
it an attractive OA assessment method (see also
Chapter 41
). It
has several advantages compared with conventional radiography,
including lack of radiation, ability to obtain numerous views of
a given joint, and ability to assess soft tissues and inflammation.
Limitations include the inability of sonography to penetrate the
bony cortex, which prevents assessment of BMLs or deeper aspects
of the joints. A study of 311 people age 61 to 63 years participating in the Newcastle Thousand Families birth cohort found
a high prevalence of ultrasound osteophytosis at the DIP (70%),
index PIP (23%), index MCP (10%), and thumb base CMC
joints (41%), as well as the knees (30%) and hips (41%), whereas
knee effusion was present in 20% to 24%.
OA features at various sites, such as osteophytes, effusions, cartilage thickness, and
synovitis, can be reliably assessed over time.
Ultrasound-guided
intra-articular injections can also be beneficial; a systematic review
of 23 publications found that knee injections at the superomedial patellar, medial midpatellar, superolateral patellar, and lateral
suprapatellar sites were more accurate with image guidance rather
than without it.
Ultrasound guidance also allows direct aspiration/injection of popliteal cysts for symptomatic relief.
Computed Tomography
CT is the best modality for assessment of cortical bone and soft
tissue mineralization. It is also the main technique used for assessment of facet joint OA. CT arthrography is the most accurate
method for the evaluation of articular cartilage surface damage
because of high contrast between cartilage, a contrast agent, and
bone using this modality; newer weight-bearing assessments may
further enhance these capabilities.
CT can be used to define tissue calcification such as chondrocalcinosis, and even type of crystal
deposition in the case of dual-energy CT. Expense, invasiveness,
and radiation exposure are limitations to the use of CT in OA.
Nuclear Medicine
Bone scans (technetium 99m hydroxymethane diphosphonate
scintigraphy) provide a full-body image in one test, allowing
location of the site of inflammation in patients with complex
symptomatology. Bone scans have been used as a measure of
whole-body OA burden for evaluation of systemic biomarkers.
PET, most often using 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose, can detect synovitis, BMLs, and other sources of increased metabolic activity
in aging and OA joints, and these findings have been associated
with pain.
Limitations include cost, limited availability, poor
anatomic resolution, use of ionizing radiation, and invasiveness
(because of the injection of the radiopharmaceutical agent). How
these various modalities are utilized in OA imaging is evolving

with newer imaging techniques, as well as new application of artificial intelligence methodologies.

Numerous validated tools for assessing functional status in OA
are available and are widely used in research settings. As the focus
on quality of care increases, these tools will likely be incorporated
more frequently into clinical practice.

Performance-Based Assessment
Performance-based measures (such as determination of walking
speed or ability to stand from sitting) capture different constructs
of physical function compared with self-reported assessments.
The Osteoarthritis Research Society International has provided
guidelines for appropriate performance-based tests for hip or
knee OA, which include a 30-second chair-stand test, 40-meter
fast-paced walk test, stair-climb test, timed up-and-go test, and
6-minute walk test, with the first three tests as a minimal core set
of measures.

### Quality of Life

OA is a chronic, progressive, and often debilitating condition,
and thus assessment of quality of life is an important outcome
for monitoring and treatment. Generic measures of quality of life,
such as the Medical Outcomes Study Short Forms (SF-36, SF-12,
and SF-6D) and Assessment of Quality of Life (AQoL), are
commonly used.
The HOOS and KOOS previously discussed
include quality-of-life domains specific to knee and hip OA.
The
Osteoarthritis Knee and Hip Quality of Life (OAKHQOL) has
been validated for this purpose.

### Time to Total Joint Replacement

Total joint replacement is a “hard” endpoint in OA, but its use as
an outcome measure is hampered by the prolonged time needed
for assessment, the large number of patients and events required,
and a variety of factors affecting an individual patient’s decision to
undergo this procedure, including health care system issues, procedure availability, comorbid conditions, ability to rehabilitate the
joint, and patient/provider preference.
To date, efforts have not
been successful to identify a composite index that would consider
pain, functional disability, and structural degradation; that would
define the “need for total joint replacement”; and that could be
used as an outcome measure.

### Responder Criteria

In some cases, it is more useful to categorize individuals as
responders or nonresponders to treatments, given the shortcomings of outcomes based solely on imaging or self-report, particularly for clinical trials. The Outcome Measures in Rheumatology
(OMERACT)-OARSI responder criteria provide a composite
assessment, including threshold levels of percentage improvements in pain and function, and required thresholds of absolute
change to define response and no response that are applicable in a
variety of settings.

### Mortality in Osteoarthritis

The association between OA and increased mortality has received
substantial attention in the last decade. A 2008 review indicated
a modestly increased risk of mortality, primarily due to cardiovascular and gastrointestinal causes.
A 2016 systematic review
and meta-analysis of 7 studies and nearly 29,000 participants did
not reveal excess mortality in OA overall but did find higher risk
of cardiovascular death among participants with OA.
Recent
work has demonstrated that the association with excess mortality
is primarily due to joint pain, rather than radiographic changes of
OA. A large individual participant-level data analysis of more than
10,000 participants from six community-based cohorts showed
35% to 37% reduced time-to-mortality among participants with
knee OA only when the OA was painful, regardless of radiographic
findings.
This remains an area of active study.

## Conclusion

OA is a highly prevalent and debilitating condition with increasing prevalence as a result of the aging of our society and the
ongoing obesity epidemic. OA is readily recognized on clinical
grounds, although further diagnostic testing and imaging are indicated in some cases to assess for potentially treatable underlying
conditions. Pain is the most common reason for a person with OA
to seek medical advice, and examination often reveals loss of function. Assessment of the patient’s pain symptoms and functional
limitations, in the framework of that person’s goals, is necessary
to formulate an appropriate plan of care in the absence of diseasemodifying medications. Current research in OA, in addition to
ongoing efforts in both pharmacologic and nonpharmacologic
therapeutic development, is focused on optimizing incident case
and progression definitions and understanding the whole-body
burden of OA.
