## Juvenile Dermatomyositis

KEY POINTS

> Juvenile dermatomyositis (JDM) is a systemic immune-mediated

> vasculopathy affecting predominantly skin and muscle but can affect

> any organ system.

> JDM is the most common inflammatory myopathy of children, and

> without appropriate immunomodulatory treatment, there is high

> potential for morbidity and potential for mortality.

> Although JDM and adult dermatomyositis (DM) share many features,

> there are differences in pathogenesis, presentation, and outcome.

> Malignancy is seldom associated with JDM, as opposed to adult DM.

> Children with JDM are at higher risk for calcinosis and lipodystrophy

> compared with adults with DM.

> With treatment, children with JDM have a better long-term prognosis

> with improved survival compared with adults with DM.

### Introduction

Juvenile dermatomyositis (JDM) is the most common acquired
idiopathic inflammatory myopathy (IIM) of children, accounting for over 80% of cases.
Historically, the diagnosis of JDM
was based on the criteria published in 1975,
which included
the pathognomic rashes of dermatomyositis (DM) (i.e., heliotrope
rash and Gottron’s sign), in addition to predominantly symmetric
weakness of the limb girdle muscles, elevation of muscle enzymes,
characteristic electromyographic findings, and muscle biopsy
changes consistent with inflammatory myositis.

### Classification

Historically, the 1975 criteria were empirically derived to standardize how DM and polymyositis (PM) were diagnosed and to
distinguish them from other inflammatory and noninflammatory
conditions that can cause weakness. These standardized criteria
also helped to identify more homogenous patient populations,
which in turn helped to improve the research in this field.
Updated classification criteria for adult and pediatric IIM
subtypes have been published by the International Myositis Classification Criteria Project (IMCCP) in 2017, referred to as the
European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR)/
American College of Rheumatology (ACR) classification criteria.
These criteria were data driven, developed by consensus, and are
able to identify different subgroups of idiopathic inflammatory
myopathies with greater sensitivity, specificity, and classification
accuracy. These new classification criteria incorporate myositis
antibodies, muscle enzymes, and specific histologic features, using
a scoring system based on age of onset, muscle weakness, rashes,
and other clinical and diagnostic results, including muscle biopsy
if performed. Definite JDM corresponds to a 90% or greater
probability of JDM, with scores of
≥
8.7 and
≥
7.5 with and without a muscle biopsy, respectively. Lower scores would be assigned
to probable or possible JDM. There is a useful website calculator
link to aid to use these improved classification criteria in clinical
practice (
http://www.imm.ki.se/biostatistics/calculators/iim/
).

### Epidemiology

JDM is an inflammatory vasculopathy characterized by characteristic skin rashes and proximal muscle weakness and the most
common IIM in children with an incidence of about 2 to 3
cases per million children annually in the United States and the
United Kingdom.
The mean age of onset is 7 years, with typically a female predominance (1.5:1 to 2:1). In the United States
and United Kingdom, according to race and ethnicity, patients
are most often white patients (65% and 83%, respectively), followed by Hispanic patients (14% in United States), Black patients
(11% and 8%, respectively), and Asian patients (1% and 7%,
respectively).
Although JDM has many clinical features that overlap with
adult DM, JDM is not simply adult DM in pediatric age patients
(
Table 111.1
). There are several features unique to JDM highlighting the differences based on the age of disease onset and disease
pathogenesis. Myositis-specific antibodies (MSAs) and myositisassociated antibodies (MAAs) are found in both DM and JDM,
in variable frequencies, and although malignancy is commonly
associated DM, it is extremely rare in JDM, while calcinosis, cutaneous ulceration, and vasculitis are common in JDM compared
with adult cohorts.

### Pathogenesis

As with DM, the pathogenesis of JDM is not entirely known and
remains the topic of intensive research. As with other autoimmune conditions, JDM appears to occur in genetically susceptible individuals, and environmental factors also appear to play
a role.

### Clinical Features

The classic presentation of a child with JDM includes pathognomic rashes and proximal muscle weakness. However, JDM is a
systemic condition, and any organ system can be involved.

Cutaneous Manifestations
The characteristic rashes of JDM includes Gottron’s sign and
heliotrope rash, but the cutaneous manifestations may vary widely
in JDM.
Gottron’s papules are papular lesions, often associated with erythema and scale and found over the dorsal aspect
of the hands and feet (
Fig. 111.1A and B
) or classically overlying the metacarpophalangeal and interphalangeal joints, elbows,
and knees. The heliotrope rash of JDM is periorbital, pink to
violaceous, and may be associated with scaling and edema (
Fig.
111.1C
). The small-vessel vasculopathy associated with JDM
is well demonstrated with the nail bed capillary changes commonly seen in this condition. These are abnormally large, dilated,
shaped capillaries, best seen under magnification (
Fig. 111.1D
and E
). Similar capillary changes can be seen in the upper eye
lid and gingival margins (
Fig. 111.1C
). Nail bed erythema and
cuticular overgrowth are also commonly seen (
Fig. 111.1E
).
Close monitoring of nailfold capillaries is recommended, since
abnormal nailfold capillaries have been associated with longer
disease duration of untreated disease.
In addition, persistent
changes in the nailbed capillary loops at 6 months and Gottron’s
sign at 3 months have been correlated with increased disease severity and longer time to remission.
Similarly, lower nailfold capillary density has been correlated with smaller lung volumes, lower
gas diffusion, and CT-detected airway disease.
Children with JDM are often extremely photosensitive,
and other common cutaneous findings include malar rash,

erythroderma, V sign, Shawl sign, livedo reticularis, and others.
The rashes found in JDM phenotypically similar to the
rashes found in adult DM.
Interestingly, rashes including ulceration, calcinosis, and lipodystrophy are reported more commonly
in JDM compared with adult DM, whereas mechanic’s hands are
uncommon in children. The ulcerative skin lesions (
Fig. 111.2A
to C
) that can occur with JDM herald a more severe disease
course, potentially life threatening systemic vasculopathy, and a
more aggressive disease course that requires closer monitoring and
more intensive therapy.
Calcinosis can be part of the acute inflammatory process of
JDM and later in the disease represents disease chronicity and
skin damage. Calcinosis is often found in the skin, subcutaneous
tissue, or muscle of patients with JDM and can be localized or
extensive (
Fig. 111.3A to D
). Calcinosis has a tendency to develop
in areas of pressure or repeated trauma, is found in 40% or more
of patients with JDM, can be cosmetically distressing, can cause
pain, and can be associated with functional disability, joint contractures, ulceration, and infection. Increased rates of calcinosis
tend to be associated with delays in treatment
and improve
with early and aggressive treatment of the inflammation associated
with JDM.
Lipodystrophy, typically a late complication of JDM, is
reported in up to 20% of patients or more in some cohorts; can be
generalized, partial, or focal; and can be disfiguring.
It is commonly associated with calcinosis and with more severe, chronic
JDM. Interestingly, patients with lipodystrophy have features of
metabolic syndrome and are at higher risk of insulin resistance
and hypertriglyceridemia.

Muscle Weakness
Classically the weakness of JDM affects the proximal muscles
with symmetric involvement. The weakness of JDM can range
from negligible to severe: it is not uncommon for the weakness
of JDM to be insidious. Some patients may have no detectable
weakness, while others may present with fatigue or myalgia alone.
More severe weakness is also possible and can be severe enough
that patients are unable to get up out of bed or a chair, walk up the
stairs, or raise their arms above their head. The weakness of JDM
can affect muscles of speech and deglutination and the muscles of
the esophagus and diaphragm, causing trouble with swallowing,
speaking, and/or breathing. Patients may present with muscular
atrophy and contractures around joints due to muscle disuse that
lead to irreversible, fixed contracture and physical disability if left
untreated.
Head lag, Gower’s sign, and Trendelenburg gait may be found
in children with muscle inflammation and weakness due to
JDM. Strength testing is often challenging, especially in children
younger than 4 years of age due to many factors that may include
fear of the examiner and the inability to follow directions during the assessment. Repeated standardized testing using manual
muscle testing or the childhood myositis assessment scale is recommended to track changes in disease activity over time with a
standardized approach.
In addition to blood muscle enzyme assessments, electromyography (EMG) and muscle biopsies can help support the diagnosis
of JDM, but MRI is being used more broadly to assess for myositis
in lieu of more invasive diagnostic modalities.

Systemic Manifestations
Virtually any organ system can be involved in JDM and may
include the central and peripheral nervous systems gastrointestinal tract, joints, heart, and lungs. These manifestations can
be a significant source of morbidity and mortality and should
be monitored throughout the disease course. Systemic features
can include cardiac conduction abnormalities, diminished
respiratory and lung diffusion capacity, esophageal, gastric,
and gastrointestinal dysmotility, ulcers, hemorrhage, and perforation. More than half of children with JDM have associated
arthritis.

Cardiopulmonary Disease
Cardiopulmonary disease is uncommon at disease onset in JDM,
but when it occurs, it can carry high morbidity and mortality.
Severe and acute cardiac involvement and cardiovascular complications are uncommon in JDM but pericarditis, myocarditis,
conduction defects, and heart block have been reported. Abnormal electrocardiograms and echocardiograms were found in 9%
and 48% of children at presentation from a Canadian cohort of
patients, but findings tended to be mild and typically unrelated
to JDM (i.e., prolonged heart-rate corrected QT interval [QTc],
mild tricuspid regurgitation, or pulmonary insufficiency).
A
long-term follow-up of patients with JDM from Norway, for
over a decade following disease presentation, suggests that there
is decreased heart rate variability and long axis strain in JDM
patients that correlated with skin disease activity at 1 year.

Amyopathic Juvenile Dermatomyositis
A small subset of patients with JDM are described as having amyopathic JDM (aJDM). These children present with the cutaneous
manifestations of JDM but have no evidence of muscle weakness.
This terminology is controversial, since patients may have undetected muscle disease, and comprehensive assessments for myositis, such as EMG, MRI, or muscle biopsies, are seldom performed
when patients are not weak.
Other terminology to define this
subset of patients has also included
clinically amyopathic, hypomyopathic,
or
skin-predominant JDM
. Long-term follow-up and treatment of this patient subset is needed, since resistant skin disease
is common, and almost 20% of children may ultimately develop
weakness.
Malignancy
Adult dermatomyositis has an association with malignancy, which
typically presents as a paraneoplastic phenomenon within 2 years
of diagnosis, and most commonly includes adenocarcinomas of
the cervix, lung, ovaries, pancreas, bladder, or stomach. The risk of
malignancy is higher in DM in association with MSAs, especially
anti–TIF1-
γ
antibodies in up to 20% of adults.
In children with JDM compared with adults with DM, the
association with malignancy is uncommon (
Table 111.1
). However, leukemia and lymphoma are the most frequently reported
malignancies in this context.
Hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and physical examination findings atypical for JDM should
prompt additional work-up for malignancy.

### Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests

Diagnostic tests for JDM should include a complete cell count
with differential and a complete set of muscle enzymes including creatine kinase, aldolase, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine
transaminase, and lactate dehydrogenase. With longer duration
of untreated disease, muscle enzymes may become unreliable
markers of disease activity.
Other possible biomarkers of disease
include neopterin and von Willebrand factor antigen, which are
elevated in a subset of JDM patients.
Interferon chemokine
scores vary significantly with disease activity in JDM cohorts as
assessed by the global visual analog scale (VAS),
but these are not
presently commercially available.
MSAs (see below) are becoming increasingly important in the
assessment of JDM. MSAs can be found in up to 70% of JDM
patients and appear to define specific subsets of JDM patients that
may help predict disease severity, end organ involvement, prognosis, and help inform medication choices. Immunoprecipitation is
generally considered to be the gold standard approach to testing
and confirming MSAs, and research continues to optimize testing.
Due to the growing importance of MSA and prognosis,
more research is needed to determine the most sensitive and specific approaches to testing for MSAs.
Myositis-Specific Antibodies
MSAs have been found to be specific for dermatomyositis subtypes
and are thought to be found exclusively in patients with
myositis. For JDM, the most commonly described include antitranscriptional intermediary factor 1 (anti-p155/140, also known
as TIF1-
γ
), anti-nuclear matrix protein 2 (anti-NXP2, also known
and MJ), and anti–melanoma differentiation–associated gene 5

(anti-MDA5, also known as CADM-140) and have been found
to have distinct clinical features and outcomes.
Other common MSAs in adult DM include anti-synthetase
antibodies, anti–signal recognition particle (anti-SRP) autoantibodies, and anti–Mi-2 autoantibodies. Anti-synthetase antibodies include anti–Jo-1, anti–PL-7, anti–PL-7, anti-PL-12, and
other aminoacyl tRNA synthetases. These antibodies have been
associated with anti-synthetase syndrome, consisting of mechanic’s hands, Raynaud’s syndrome, interstitial lung disease, arthritis, lipoatrophy, and fever; patients are typically older at age of
onset.
Patients with JDM and anti–TIF1-
γ
antibodies, also known
as anti-p155/140 antibodies, have been described in up to 35%
of some patient cohorts and are associated with photosensitivity,
Gottron’s papules, malar rash, V-sign, and cuticular overgrowth.
Patients may have a chronic or polycyclic disease course, and cutaneous ulceration is more common in JDM.
Anti-MDA5 antibodies are reported in up to 20% of some
cohorts and more commonly have amyopathic disease associated with oral and skin ulceration, fever, interstitial lung disease
that can sometimes be rapidly progressing, arthralgia and arthritis, and older age of onset. Rapidly progressive lung disease has
been reported more frequently in Japanese cohorts.
In adults
anti–TIF1-
γ
antibodies and anti-MDA autoantibodies may be
associated with malignancy, which is extremely uncommon in
children.
Anti-MJ antibodies are also reported in up to 20% of some
cohorts and are more commonly associated with muscle cramping and weakness requiring hospitalization, dysphonia, calcinosis, younger age of onset, and less frequent remission. Anti-MJ
antibodies have been associated with markedly increased odds of
calcinosis (odds ratio [OR], 7.0 [3.0 to 16.1]).
Myositis-Associated Antibodies
Positive anti-nuclear antibody (ANA) testing is common in JDM,
but the additional autoantibodies are typically negative. MAAs
are autoantibodies that are also described with other autoimmune
conditions. Notably, although the same autoantibodies are found
in both adults and children, they are not always associated with
the same clinical features.
A more recently described MAA, antibodies to cytosolic
5
′
-nucleotidase 1A (anti-NT5C1A) identified in adults with
inclusion body myositis, has also been found more commonly in
a subset of JDM to be associated with more pulmonary symptoms
at diagnosis, requiring more hospitalizations and immunomodulatory therapy. However, these are not specific to JDM and are
also found in juvenile arthritis patients and in healthy children
(27%, 27%, and 12% respectively).
Anti-Ro52 is another
MAA reported in 14% of a North American cohort, is more frequent with anti-synthetase and anti-MDA5 MSA groups,
and
has been associated with ILD, chronic disease course, and more
medications.
Muscle Pathology
When children have atypical features and no rash, muscle biopsies are an essential part of the diagnostic work-up to confirm
the diagnosis of JDM. Muscle biopsies from adult and juvenile
DM are characterized by perifascicular atrophy, vasculopathy,
perivascular inflammation, fiber degeneration and regeneration,
and swelling of the endothelial cells, and tubuloreticular inclusion bodies can be seen within endothelial cells with electron
microscopy. The inflammatory infiltrate is composed on B cells,
CD4
T cells, macrophages, and plasmcytoid dendritic cells. The
vasculopathy of DM is characterized by deposition of complement and the membrane attack complex on capillaries, capillary thrombosis, and reduction in number of capillaries, which
is typically perifascicular. Although there the pathology appears
similar in both adult and juvenile DM, there appear to be differences in the molecular pathways between adult and juvenile DM.
For example, increased expression of hypoxia-inducible factor 1
α
(HIF-1
α
) and macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF)
genes has been described in JDM but not found in adult DM.
In adult DM the perifascicular atrophy was more associated with
IFN-associated genes, suggesting a greater role of innate immunity rather than the hypoxia-related mechanisms seen in JDM.
Muscle biopsies from JDM appear to have more prominent vasculopathy compared with adult DM.
There is increased expression of MHC class I and myxovirus
resistance A (MxA), which is an IFNα
/
β
–inducible protein.
Pathology scoring tools have been developed and validated to
standardize histologic estimates of disease severity in JDM and
also have good correlation with measures of disease activity.
Histopathologic severity and MSA subtype together are also able
to predict duration of treatment in JDM.
Imaging
Baseline chest radiographs should be performed in patients with
JDM and can help screen for occult lung disease. Chest CT is
essential in the assessment in any patient with suspected interstitial lung disease. MRI helps to identify areas of inflammation
or edema and in some settings has replaced the need for invasive
muscle biopsies, and should include either STIR images or T2-fat
suppressed images (
Fig. 111.4
).
Other imaging modalities
are being studied for JDM, including ultrasound, which is not yet
standardized to assess muscle inflammation.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is important to consider other diagnoses when considering
JDM. Other autoimmune conditions that cause myositis include
systemic lupus erythematosus, mixed connective tissue disease,
scleroderma, and sarcoidosis.
Other primary IIMs are extremely rare in children, including
polymyositis. Patients without rashes are typically not be diagnosed with JDM, and muscle biopsies should be strongly considered in those cases, since treatment and prognosis may vary based
on the primary diagnosis.
Congenital myopathies and metabolic myopathies should be
considered in any patient who presents with weakness, but typically features of the history, family history and exam help distinguish these from JDM. Working closely with neurology and
considering other diagnostic testing such as genetic testing, EMG
or muscle biopsy should be considered in atypical cases.
Infectious myositis can include rashes and can be associated
with various infections including bacteria, viruses, and parasites
and need to be considered based on the appropriate clinical circumstances, including exposure and travel history.
Prominent
eosinophilia may prompt a work-up for parasitic infections. Infections most often associated with myositis include influenza B,
which can present with acute muscle pain, weakness, and elevated
muscle enzyme levels that typically involve the gastrocnemius
muscles. Other common viral infectious associations include

adenovirus, coxsackievirus, dengue virus, human immunodeficiency, and parvovirus B19. Myositis following COVID-19 infections has also been reported and could be due to viral myositis or
induction of autoimmunity.

### Treatment

Standard Initial Treatment Approaches
Prior to the use of corticosteroids for JDM in the 1960s, mortality
was up to one of three children with JDM. A variety of immunomodulatory treatments have been used to treat JDM in addition to steroids and are essential for all patients with JDM due to
the significant morbidity due to long-term steroid use, including
cushingoid features, osteoporosis, glaucoma, cataracts, diabetes,
avascular necrosis of bones, etc. Panels of experts worldwide have
developed treatment approaches to help guide and standardize the
treatment of JDM, and the mainstay of initial therapy includes
steroids and methotrexate (MTX).
There are no U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)–
approved medications for JDM. Conventional, large-scale, blinded
clinical trials are nearly impossible to perform for JDM, and only
two randomized controlled trials have been performed.
Thus, by necessity, current standard treatment recommendations
are based on anecdotal experience, but more recently the treatment approaches for patients with JDM have become increasingly
standardized by international panels of experts largely based on
developed consensus.
Specific consensus treatment plans have been developed for
JDM patients with skin-predominant disease, moderately severe
disease, and resistant skin disease and represent typical, reasonable treatment approaches for the initial treatment of JDM, with
options to choose from based on the case severity and the experience of the treating clinician (
Fig. 111.5
).
Medications used for JDM typically include oral and parenteral corticosteroids in combination with steroid-sparing immunomodulatory treatment, most commonly methotrexate. To
prevent the complications associated with long-term steroid use,
it is imperative that steroid-sparing therapy is initiated promptly
at the time of diagnosis.
Methotrexate is typically administered weekly, and recommended doses are 1 mg/kg/dose, typically not exceeding 25 mg/
dose. Oral steroid dosing is typically initiated 2 mg/kg/day, typically not exceeding 60 mg/day. High-pulse dose corticosteroids
are often given at the start of therapy over 3 days (30 mg/kg/dose
of methylprednisolone, typically not exceeding 1000 mg). Aggressive treatment of persistent disease activity improves outcome in

### Outcomes

The long-term prognosis of children with JDM is improved
with the use of immunomodulatory therapies. Prior to the use
of immunosuppressive medications for JDM in the 1960s, nearly
one-third of children succumbed to the disease. Presently, survival
is greatly improved for JDM, and the survival rate is typically
>95% in most regions of the world.
About 30% to 40% of patients have monocyclic disease, but
the remaining patients tend to have polycyclic or chronic disease,
so treatment may be necessary long-term, well into adulthood.

Undertreatment and/or delayed diagnosis and treatment are associated with increased long-term morbidities including calcinosis
and lipodystropy, so aggressive treatment at the time of diagnosis
is essential to limit these complications.

### Conclusions

Juvenile dermatomyositis is rare and has many features that distinguish it from adult DM. Aggressive treatment is needed for JDM
compared with approaches more commonly used in adult DM,
because of the impact of the disease on the growth and development of children, as well as the high risk of morbidity from
features of disease more common in JDM including ulceration,
lipodystrophy, and calcinosis. Early recognition and treatment of
disease manifestations is essential to improve outcomes. Recent
developments in JDM and DM research, including the prominent
interferon signature associated with disease, as well as the contribution of MSAs and distinct disease subsets, have shed more light
on the pathogenesis and possible changes to treatment approaches
for this rare condition.

### Introduction

The scleroderma disorders in children encompass both systemic
sclerosis (SSc) and localized scleroderma (LS), also termed
morphea
. SSc is much less common than LS in childhood and comprises less than 10% of all cases.
Both share the general
features of an earlier inflammatory state, followed by collagen
deposition and atrophy. Skin biopsies of these patients are typically indistinguishable, and they may share some common pathophysiology; however, their clinical manifestations are markedly
different with unique morbidities and prognoses. LS is typically
confined to the skin and underlying tissue, and although lacking fatal potential of SSc, as many as a one-half of the patients
may have extracutaneous disease manifestations, such as joint
contractures and seizures.
Although any organ may be
affected in SSc, vascular (relapsing polychondritis [RP]), cutaneous (skin thickening), gastrointestinal (GI), pulmonary, and musculoskeletal involvement are most commonly seen in children. A
recent cohort study shows that interstitial lung disease (ILD) is
just as prevalent in juvenile SSc (40%) compared with adult SSc,
and ILD is the leading cause of SSc-associated death in adults.
Both SSc and LS can be further classified by subtype (

### Epidemiology

Both LS and SSc are uncommon in children; the estimated
annual incidence of LS is 1 to 3.4 per 100,000 children,
and
that of SSc is 0.27 to 1 per million.
Recent studies examining administrative claims data in the United States support a
prevalence of 3 per 10,000 children for pediatric LS and 3 per
1,000,000 for pediatric SSc. The mean age of onset for pediatric
LS is 6.5 to 8.5 years old and is slightly older for pediatric SSc at
8 to 10.5 years old. Unfortunately, there is significant delay in
diagnosis, with an average of 0.7 to 2.8 years for SSc.
and 0.9 to 1.6 years for LS.
In rare cases of congenital LS,
the mean time to diagnosis is longer, 2.9 to 3.9 years.
The
approximate female to male ratio of pediatric SSc is 4:1,
and for pediatric LS, it is 2:1. There is no clear evidence for racial
predilection for either form of pediatric scleroderma, although
Caucasian race is the most prevalent in published cohorts.

### Pathogenesis

Genetics
HLA studies in pediatric-onset SSc and LS have shown unique
HLA-DR and HLA-DQ findings compared with those found in
adult-onset SSc. This includes a lack of association with adult SSc

*11:04. Although subanalyses showed an association of childhoodonset SSc to adult-onset SSc DRB1*01, it was only in children
with older onset (>11 years old) with centromere antibody positivity. A novel association with DRB1*10 was observed in childhood-onset SSc (OR, 7.48;
P
= 0.002), which remained significant
regardless of pediatric SSc subtype (limited or diffuse) or antibody
positivity (topoisomerase, centromere antibody).
HLA class I
and II studies in the Morphea in Adults and Children Cohort,

demonstrated the strongest associations of LS with DRB1*04:04

When evaluating the association of different organ
manifestations in the CARRA jSSc cohort, it became apparent
that those with GI involvement had more impact on quality-oflife measures, such as the Child Health Assessment Questionnaire (CHAQ), pain scale, and global well-being; therefore the
importance of GI manifestations in children with jSSc should not
be overlooked.
Pulmonary Manifestations.
Pulmonary involvement in jSSc
ranges from 30% to 55% and includes ILD, PAH, and abnormal
pulmonary function tests (PFT). PFT abnormalities especially
of concern are decreased forced vital capacity (FVC) and diffusing capacity of the lungs for carbon monoxide (DLCO).
The
clinical presentation of ILD with slowly progressive dyspnea on
exertion (DOE) with dry hacking cough, and that of PAH with a
more rapid progression of DOE is similar between adults and children. ILD in children is often asymptomatic and only identified
with PFT and/or chest imaging due to the child’s response to selflimit more rigorous physical activities if experiencing DOE and to
not verbalize or complain. A recent international cohort study of
150 jSSc patients identified ILD in 44%, with a surprising finding
of 60% of those with ILD determined by chest CT to have normal
PFT (FVC
≥
80 and DLCO
≥
80).
This supports the SHARE
recommendations and more recent guidelines of obtaining a
baseline high resolution chest CT for all jSSc subjects in tandem
with PFT.
Similar to adult SSc, pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is uncommon in jSSc with a recent international
cohort study showing 6% (9 of 150) of patients with jSSc having
PAH.
Typically this is detected by echocardiogram in children,
supported by serum pro–brain natriuretic peptide (pro-BNP) and
the 6-minute walk test and verified by right heart catheterization
(see “Evaluation” below).
Cardiac Manifestations.
Cardiac involvement, although infrequent (see
Table 111.2
), is the major cause of scleroderma-related
mortality in children, as evidenced by the deaths of 10 of 15 in one
cohort
and of 5 of 32 in another cohort.
Cardiac manifestations result from the combination of myocardial fibrosis, vascular
insufficiency, and inflammation and include heart block, bundle
branch block, premature atrial and ventricular contractions,
nonspecific T-wave changes, arrhythmia (supraventricular more
common than ventricular), congestive heart failure, cardiomyopathy, and pericarditis.
Cardiomyopathy is the major cause
of morbidity and mortality for the few jSSc patients with sine
scleroderma.
In contrast to pediatric SSc, scleroderma-related
mortality in adults is primarily caused by pulmonary involvement
(both ILD and PAH).
Musculoskeletal Manifestations.
Compared with adult-onset
SSc, musculoskeletal involvement is more common in pediatric
SSc. Approximately one-third of patients with jSSc experience
inflammatory arthritis (joint effusions) in addition to the typical dry synovitis of scleroderma, reflected by the fibrosis of tendons traversing the joints, which limits their range of motion
(ROM).
Early in the disease process, especially in those
with dcSSc, tenosynovitis/bursitis causes palpable tendon friction
rubs (a “leathery crepitus”) when the joint is extended or flexed,
demonstrated in approximately 10% of patients with jSSc.
Myopathy typically results in symmetric proximal weakness and
muscular atrophy and is typically described as
bland
, reflecting
the relative lack of elevated muscle enzymes, despite weakness on
examination and myositis on MRI.
Muscle weakness ranged
between 20% and 32% in jSSc cohorts (see
Table 111.2
)
and was markedly higher than the adult comparison cohort in one
of the studies.
Myositis also occurs, more commonly in the overlap SSc subtype, and presents similarly to juvenile DM. Children
with dcSSc and myositis are particularly at risk for severe cardiac
involvement, including myocardial perfusion deficits and dilated
cardiomyopathy.
Often overlooked is the effect of myopathy
on the respiratory muscles, intercostal muscles, truncal muscles,

and diaphragm, which can severely affect the respiratory reserve
reflected by restrictive defects on FVC and total lung capacity, and
in moderate to severe cases affecting the DLCO.
Renal Manifestations.
Mild renal dysfunction is not uncommon in SSc and is secondary to vasculopathy rather than glomerulonephritis. It occurs with similar frequency in jSSc and SSc.
In contrast, the more severe and morbid form of renal disease,
scleroderma renal crisis (SRC), occurs in approximately 15% of
adult patients with SSc but seldom in children (<5%, only five
patients among the six cohorts).
SRC is characterized
by accelerated arterial hypertension, renal insufficiency, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, and thrombocytopenia. SRC is
most commonly seen early in the disease course of dcSSc in RNA
polymerase III–positive patients, which is uncommon in jSSc (see
eTable 111.2
), and may account for the lower frequency of SRC
in jSSc.
Neurologic Manifestations.
Neurologic involvement in jSSc
is rare (<5% in all studies) and can include CNS changes, such
as seizure, abnormal brain MRI findings, and peripheral neuropathy.
Peripheral neuropathy is typically secondary to nerve
entrapment from bound down thick skin of the extremities, such
as carpal tunnel syndrome.

The diagnosis of jSSc relies on distinctive clinical features (

which predict organ involvement in both adult- and childhoodonset SSc (see

).

### Disease Monitoring

For most children with SSc or suspected SSc, in addition to complete physical examination, including evaluation of skin thickness
with the mRSS and nailfold capillary examination, initial cardiopulmonary assessment, especially evaluating for evidence of ILD
and PAH, includes chest radiograph, PFTs with DLCO, ECG,
echocardiogram, and more recently, high-resolution CT (HRCT)
of the chest.
Expanding further, following the experience
of adult SSc population in detecting subclinical cardiac disease,
and given the high cardiac mortality potential in jSSc, additional
cardiac screening is becoming more routine, including 24-hour
Holter and loop recorder monitoring for arrhythmias, exercise
stress echocardiogram to capture cardiopulmonary dynamic testing, cardiac MRI, and two-dimensional/three-dimensional speckle
tracking echocardiograms.
It is important to keep in mind
normal parameters for pediatric patient testing, especially the
lung function testing, including DLCO, which should be calculated using standardized prediction equations which account for
weight, height, age, sex, and race.
This will avoid the unwanted
anxiety of a falsely low reading or false confidence in absent
pathology if a “normal” reading is documented when unmatched
norms are used. Additional cardiopulmonary testing is pursued if
abnormalities are detected or the patient is too young to appropriately complete testing (i.e., PFTs). Abnormal PFTs typically
prompt evaluation with HRCT of the chest, with many pediatric
centers including HRCT as first-line evaluation in jSSc given the
variable effort and interpretation of PFTs, the relatively common
observation of a normal chest radiograph despite the presence of
ILD, and the updated SHARE recommendations.
Rarely a
bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) is performed because inflammation
(aveolitis) can be detected as ground glass opacities and subpleural
micronodules on HRCT, and is utilized only in instances where
evaluation of infection if needed before starting therapy.
PAH is typically screened by transthoracic echocardiogram with
elevated right-heart pressures, providing evidence for PAH. The
DLCO can also be used as an indirect measure to screen for PAH,
especially if there is a decrease in the DLCO without evidence of
obstructive or restrictive lung disease (FVC), suggesting a defect in
gas exchange as a result of pulmonary vascular disease. More formal PAH evaluation with direct measurements is obtained by performing right-heart catheterization as in adults. A 6-minute walk
(6MW) test may also be used to monitor lung disease (ILD and/
or PAH), but the standardized values in children are still being
determined and depend on ethnicity, weight, height, and age, and
nonpulmonary issues in jSSc, such as musculoskeletal involvement and fatigue, may contribute to decreased values.
After initial organ assessment, ongoing cardiopulmonary
screening with ECG and echocardiogram every 6 to 12 months
and PFTs with DLCO every 3 to 6 months are recommended
by most pediatric rheumatologists and recently endorsed by
the SHARE guidelines, along with every 6 months mRSS and
renal function assessment.
There is a lack of consensus
regarding screening for GI involvement in jSSc. Most clinicians
will screen at some point in time for evidence of silent aspiration
and dysmotility with a swallow study and upper GI with SBFT;
additional imaging is more dependent on symptoms (see previous
discussion under “Gastrointestinal Manifestations”). A pediatric
multidimensional severity score, the Juvenile Systemic Sclerosis
Severity Score (J4S), encompasses several clinical variables, such
as growth parameters and skin and internal organ assessment,
developed under the framework of the Medsger Systemic Sclerosis Severity Score
(see
Chapter 85
;
Table 85.2
), and could
be useful to monitor disease severity over time; the J4S is being
studied in clinical practice and is supported by the recent SHARE
guidelines.
General laboratory assessment is encouraged, including muscle
enzymes, vitamin levels for GI absorption, pro-BNP for cardiac
dysfunction, and urinalysis for proteinuria.
There are no
standardized biomarkers available, although a study has suggested
serum KL-6, a mucin-like protein strongly expressed in type II
pneumocytes, as a promising noninvasive pulmonary marker of
fibrosis in jSSc.

### Treatment

Treatment of SSc is dependent on organ involvement, but general
measures such as antacid medication for GI protection, prokinetic
agents for dysmotility, rotating antibiotics for malabsorption, as
well as vasodilators for RP and digital ulcers, nonsteroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and physical therapy (PT) for
arthritis or tendonitis are generally recommended. These pharmacologic and supportive measures are included in a recent review of
SSc treatment applicability to pediatric scleroderma.
Iloprost
(not available in the United States) and bosentan were specifically recommended for the treatment of ischemic digital ulcerations in the jSSc SHARE statement.
Corticosteroids (CSs) for
myositis, arthritis, and other inflammatory clinical features are

more mainstream in jSSc and recommended in combination with
DMARD therapy in early active disease,
although they should
be used with caution because they can prompt SRC. MTX and
MMF are now used routinely at the time of diagnosis of SSc, especially for skin, subcutaneous, and articular involvement, and are
part of the SHARE recommendations.
More specific treatment recommendations, such as CYC and rituximab, for different organ manifestations have been agreed upon by a consensus
conference of international scleroderma experts, should be considered for all patients with SSc (see
Chapter 85
for additional
information on treatment), and are supported by more recent jSSc
SHARE recommendations.
Treatment for SSc-associated
ILD is making advancements with two agents recently receiving
FDA approval: tocilizumab for more inflammatory ground-glass
ILD and nintedanib for more fibrotic (reticular) ILD.
As in
adult SSc, autologous stem cell transplant (ASCT) is an option
and may be the best opportunity for survival in selected severe
patients, with a number of successful outcomes reported.
ASCT was also agreed on as a good treatment option for cases
of jSSc with progressive disease refractory to immunosuppressive
therapy by the SHARE committee.

### Outcomes

Despite all the potential organ involvement in pediatric SSc, it has
a far more favorable prognosis at 5, 10, and 15 years compared
with adult SSc. Investigators recently reported survival rates at 10
years of 98% in children compared with 75% in adults.
In jSSc
cohorts, the most common cause of death was cardiac (including PAH) and respiratory failure.
Death within a few years
of onset of disease (majority
≤
5 years) was reported in two jSSc
cohorts.
One study proposed a model for prediction of poor
outcome in jSSc that included fibrosis on chest radiograph, raised
creatinine levels, and pericarditis.
The research group at Padua
University coordinated a study that developed and validated the
J4S, to objectively establish the degree of jSSc severity in daily
practice using a comprehensive score.
This is not a mortality
predictor but a gauge to possibly use longitudinally to follow the
patients’ disease course and trajectory and will enable comparisons
of patients across observational cohorts and assist in the stratification of patients for clinical trials. It is mirrored from the Medsger
Severity Score,
ranging from normal, mild, moderate, to severe
involvement of different organ manifestations and general health
parameters. The J4S is a promising measure and should be validated in prospective studies.

### Localized Scleroderma

Classification
LS, also termed
morphea
, has a different pattern of skin involvement than SSc and encompasses several subtypes classified by
depth and pattern of the lesion distribution. These disorders are
characterized by inflammation-driven fibrosis and atrophy that
are mainly confined to the skin and subcutaneous tissue in more
superficial disease; however, deeper forms also involve the fascia,
muscle, tendon, and joint capsule. Certain subtypes of LS have
unique associated extracutaneous manifestations (ECMs). The
Padua criteria are the most commonly used to identify LS subtype and have been recently validated to correctly classify 95%
of a large adult and pediatric LS cohort (944 subjects).
The
criteria include a “mixed morphea” subtype, which includes more
than one type, the prevalence of this category is 15% in juvenile
LS (jLS) (
Table 111.4
).
The most common subtype in juvenile
LS (jLS) is linear scleroderma (LiScl) (50% to 60%), which is
characterized by a linear streak or band involving the dermis, subcutis, and often the underlying muscle, tendons, and bone. LiScl
typically occurs as a single, linear, unilateral band on the extremities, trunk, or head.
Linear lesions crossing the joint may
cause joint contracture and limb dysfunction (
Fig. 111.10A
). Of
particular concern in with LiScl is involvement of the underlying
tissues, including muscle, bones, growth plates, and organs, which
can lead to permanent shortening or atrophy of the limb in children (
Fig. 111.10B
). Orthopedic complications are reported in
approximately 30% to 50% of patients with LiScl of the limb(s),
causing mild to moderate disability.
Linear lesions affecting the head affect approximately 25% of
patients with jLS.
Those with more obvious cutaneous findings sometimes have a dyspigmented linear streak or depression
of the scalp and forehead termed
en coup de sabre
(ECDS), resembling a saber wound scar (
Fig. 111.11
). Those with more atrophy
of the subcutis, with associated hemifacial atrophy of the mandible, maxilla, tongue, and muscle tissue of the face, are classified
with Parry–Romberg syndrome (PRS) or progressive hemifacial
atrophy. These disorders can occur together, either at the same
location (side of face) or on opposing sides (
Fig. 111.12
), and
are thought to be on a spectrum of the same process, collectively
termed Craniofacial scleroderma (CfS).
PRS and ECDS are
associated with neurologic, ophthalmologic, and dental ECMs.
Neurologic involvement is demonstrated in 8% to 20% of the
patients with these conditions, including CNS involvement
expressed as seizures, chronic headaches, optic neuritis, and, less
frequently, neuropsychiatric changes or ischemic stroke.
Brain
MRI abnormalities associated with the CNS can be found in up to
34% of patients and include subcortical white matter lesions (typically ipsilateral to the cutaneous lesion), calcifications or blood
products (areas of susceptibility), vascular malformations, and
cysts. Orthodontic involvement in CfS includes malocclusion,
atrophic roots, gingival recession, loss of alveolar bone, and tooth
mobility. Ophthalmologic involvement in CfS includes episcleritis, optic neuritis, uveitis, enophthalmos, and fibrotic changes to
the lacrimal gland or eyelid.
Guidelines for screening and
monitoring these unique ECMs for CfS have been developed (see
“Disease Monitoring” below).
The less common subtypes of jLS are superficial and deep circumscribed LS, generalized morphea, and pansclerotic morphea.
Most superficial circumscribed lesions (epidermis and upper dermal involvement) affect the trunk, are more common in adults,
and are usually treated topically. Deep circumscribed morphea
affect the dermis and subcutis with variable penetration into
the fascia and muscle. Typically, there are only subtle cutaneous
changes, such as slight erythema and thinning of the dermis, but
secondary to deep infiltrate causes a “scooped-out” appearance
of the subcutis. Generalized morphea (GM) is more typical in
the adult LS population, occurring in less than 10% of patients
with jLS,
and is associated with systemic symptoms such
as myalgia, arthralgia, and fatigue.
Lesions typically are
widespread throughout the trunk and coalesce. This subtype is
often associated with several autoimmune phenomena, including
positive antibodies and concomitant and family history of autoimmune disorders.

Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests
LS remains a clinical diagnosis by physical examination features,
and when there is uncertainty, a skin biopsy is supportive (findings
described above). There are no diagnostic laboratory tests for LS,
but there is a high prevalence of autoantibodies, including ANA,
anti-histone antibody (AHA), and anti–single-stranded DNA
antibody (ssDNA Ab). A positive ANA is found in 42% to 73%
of patients with LS and has been associated with an increased risk
for ECMs and disease recurrence.
The prevalence of ssDNA
and AHA in LS is approximately 50%, and both are associated
with disease severity features, such as deep muscle involvement,
joint contractures, and increased number of lesions.
In addition, rheumatoid factor (RF) is present in one-third of patients,
particularly those who have GM, and also correlates to disease
severity (greater number of lesions and deeper joint involvement).
These antibodies (ssDNA and AHA) and RF may
help in stratifying patients during the earlier stages of disease,
suggesting the possible initiation of more aggressive therapy and
increased surveillance.
General markers of inflammation, including erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and serum immunoglobulin levels (immunoglobulin [Ig]G and IgE), may be useful markers of disease
activity in select patients with LS, particularly those with deep or
eosinophilic-like variants. However, the majority of patients seen
with active LS lesions do not have elevation of these markers. A
recent review of the CARRA Registry data of 259 patients with
jLS supports elevated creatine phosphokinase (CPK) or aldolase to
reflect disease activity through their association with new lesions.
CPK elevation was also associated with muscle atrophy and limb
shortening and may predict muscle involvement.
Disease Monitoring
Several tools have been used for the assessment of skin and subcutaneous involvement in LS, such as clinical skin scores, measurement tools, and imaging. The Localized Scleroderma Cutaneous
Assessment Tool (LoSCAT) is a clinical skin scoring system that
assesses disease activity and damage parameters and is the most
widely validated and used cutaneous tool.
A computerized
skin score (CSS) can be used to determine the size of lesion in an
objective manner.
Measurement tools, including a durometer,
which measures the degree of skin hardness,
and a cutometer,
which measures skin elasticity.
have also been proposed as useful.
In addition, several types of imaging can be used for diagnostic or
assessment and monitoring purposes, in tandem with the cutaneous examination such as thermography, laser Doppler flowmetry,
ultrasound, and MRI.
Musculoskeletal MRI to evaluate and
monitor inflammation in the deeper structures (subcutis, fascia,
muscle, tendon, bones and joints) has become more mainstream
in linear scleroderma and generalized morphea subtype assessment,
with fascial thickening and enhancement and subcutaneous thickness being useful outcomes to monitor in regards to medication
response.
Recent recommendations regarding the evaluation and
treatment of jLS have been published including SHARE consensus-based recommendations from PReS,
an international collaboration of pediatric rheumatology and dermatology LS experts
outlining minimal standards,
CARRA LS experts’ opinion,
and pediatric dermatology morphea expert opinion,
reaching
several shared core recommendations. Some of tools included in
the recommendations have been validated in LS, including the
LoSCAT, ultrasound using higher frequencies (10 to 25 MHz), and
MRI. Specific minimal standards of care for jLS evaluation mentioned across all recent recommendations include cutaneous monitoring for all patients with the LoSCAT and for those with linear
scleroderma of the head, brain imaging with MRI, dental, and eye
screening.
In general, internal organ screening with echocardiogram, PFTs, etc., for a patient with LS is not recommended
because they tend not to have typical SSc features.

### Differential Diagnosis

Differential diagnosis is limited and includes lichen sclerosus et
atrophicus and atrophoderma of Pasini and Pierini; superficial dermal and epidermal inflammatory conditions that are thought by
some to be on the milder spectrum of LS; cutaneous T cell lymphoma, especially if there is a deeper purple skin discoloration; and
collagenoma, usually presenting with induration of the skin and
subcutis without pigmentary changes. Linear lesions of LS tend to
follow the lines of Blaschko; therefore other childhood skin conditions following these lines need to be discerned, such as linear
epidermal nevi, lichen striatus, and linear lichen planus, most of
which will have more superficial changes, such as being raised,
with slight scale or velvety/warty texture. Other possible conditions are associated with certain exposures and generally have more
widespread lesions, such as GVHD (status, post-transplant), nephrogenic fibrosing dermatopathy (gadolinium administered in renal
compromised state), and porphyria cutanea tarda (sun exposure).

### Treatment

The approach to the treatment of LS initially depends on the
severity of the disease, which is characterized by the subtype of
LS, potential involvement of deeper tissue and structures (such
as joints), location of LS (crossing joints or involving the head),
and disease activity state. The vast majority of jLS patients are
treated with systemic therapy. The exceptions are very superficial
circumscribed plaque morphea lesion(s), typically on the trunk,
which have no progression for several years. jLS is a slow, smoldering disease, and the goal is to treat earlier in the disease to reduce
inflammation, prevent damage, and preserve function.
There is growing consensus among both pediatric rheumatologists and dermatologists that systemic therapy is warranted for
more moderate to severe jLS, which is defined as lesions with deep
tissue involvement (subcutis, fascia, and muscle) that transverse a
joint and potentially cause functional impairment, affect the face
or scalp, are rapidly progressive or widespread active disease, and
for those who have failed topical or UV therapy.
The
most commonly used systemic therapy is MTX in conjunction
with CS.
The only double-blind randomized control trial in
jLS with objective measures comparing MTX and oral CS versus

placebo and oral CS found MTX to be effective and safe, and
those treated with MTX significantly improved and had significantly fewer relapses compared with those on CS alone.
An
open-label extension (average follow-up, 40 months) supported
continued MTX efficacy: 74% had a good response to methotrexate, and 73% of those responders remained in clinical remission
for a mean of 25 or more months after stopping methotrexate.
Relapses were documented during this longitudinal study, and the
authors concluded that it is beneficial to continue MTX treatment
for at least 24 months to ensure a prolonged and sustained disease
remission. This was supported in two other cohort studies, reporting higher relapse rates in those with shorter MTX treatment
duration and recent consensus recommendations.
A subcommittee of CARRA derived four consensus treatment
plans (CTPs) for the first 12 months of induction of therapy for
those with moderate to severe jLS. Three protocols involve subcutaneous MTX (1 mg/kg, with a maximum dose of 25 mg/week),
and the fourth was derived for those who either fail to respond to
MTX or are intolerant to MTX. The protocols are divided into
MTX alone, MTX with oral CS and taper, MTX with intravenous
(IV) CS, and MMF in addition to either oral or IV CS.
MMF
was included as an alternative because it has been effective in severe
MTX-resistant jLS (pansclerotic, GM, LiSCl subtypes).
These
regimens have been found to be equally effective and are also
endorsed through PRES consensus.
Therapy in non-MTX
responders typically involves the addition of monotherapy with
MMF, followed by the use of various biologics that have been published in case reports with success in treating LS, including abatacept, tocilizumab, infliximab, and tofacitinib.
Other Therapies
For a single superficial circumscribed lesion in a noncosmetic location, topical treatment with CS, calcineurin inhibitors, imiquimod, vitamin D, or recurrent phototherapy with UV light is
acceptable.
All modalities have had clinical success in case
series or uncontrolled trials exhibited by lightening of hyperpigmentation and decreased skin thickness. UVA-1 was successful in
two randomized control trials, especially in regard to early inflammatory and superficial dermal lesions.
A meta-analysis of
19 studies including MTX or UVA therapy was conducted that
included 193 children, 48 of whom were treated with UVA, and
reported both MTX and UVA to have a positive effect in lesions
of jLS; however, MTX appeared to be superior.
Intensive physical and occupational therapy in conjunction with
systemic immunosuppressive therapy is recommended for those
with linear or deep scleroderma of the extremity to help minimize
joint contractures. Also, for those with ECDS and/or PRS, plastic
surgery intervention is felt to be reasonable after the disease has
been in remission without any signs of clinical activity. Augmentation includes fat transfer and grafting with microdroplets from the
patient’s abdominal subcutis to assist in filling in some of the subcutaneous atrophy to yield a more symmetric facial contour.
For more severe disease, fasciocutaneous flaps and facial bone
reconstruction are also options, although they are typically reserved
for the late teenage years after the skeleton has matured.
Outcomes
The largest obstacle to good outcomes is awareness of LS among
primary care physicians and timely referral to academic dermatology or rheumatology to enable correct diagnosis, evaluation, and
treatment, especially while the disease is still in the active phase.
When fibrosis and atrophy dominate, available therapies that target
inflammation are less effective. Two recent European studies demonstrated an average 11- to 13-month delay from onset to correct diagnosis of LS.
In one of the studies, all 50 cases were originally
misdiagnosed or undiagnosed at first provider visit.
The CARRA
jLS cohort (
n
= 381) reported 17% of the patients having a 2-year
or greater delay to their first pediatric rheumatology visit.
North
American dermatology and Italian pediatric rheumatology longitudinal LS cohort confirmed that a prolonged interval between onset
and definitive diagnosis adversely affects the final outcome.
Once systemic immunosuppressive therapy is initiated in
patients with LS, the signs of disease activity typically resolve
within months; damage features may improve but typically do not
resolve.
After this initial improvement, it is important not
to discontinue immunosuppression too early, potentially resulting
in relapse.
In general, it is recommended to treat for 2
to 3 years with MTX with sustained remission before discontinuation of systemic therapy. After this initial course, it is not uncommon for the patient to relapse, and despite prior notions that LS
“burns out,” approximately 30% to 50% of patients will relapse,
requiring reinitiation of systemic therapy.
In the authors’ opinion, these relapses typically occur in the same region as the original
lesion, and most respond to reinitiation of systemic therapy.

### Conclusions: Localized Scleroderma and Systemic Sclerosis

Although the underlying pathogenesis of jLS and jSSc remains
understudied and mostly relies on adult SSc studies, newer data
are available suggesting a shared immunophenotype and associated cytokine profiles between juvenile and adult patients and
between LS and SSc. Treatment is most effective during the active
inflammatory state in both jLS and jSSc; therefore it is imperative
to raise awareness in general providers for prompt referral, diagnosis, complete evaluation, and management. CARRA, PRES,
the Pediatric Dermatology Research Alliance (PeDRA), and other
international efforts are further refining disease activity, damage,
and responsiveness measures of LS through CTPs to offer more
defined guidelines for therapy management and to be used in
future clinical trials of LS. The J4S is a promising tool for similar
purposes in jSSc

### Introduction

Childhood vasculitis encompasses a group of disorders characterized by the presence of inflammation in the blood vessel wall.
Similar to adults, clinical symptoms vary depending on the types

and location of the vessels involved. Apart from Kawasaki’s disease
(KD) and immunoglobulin A vasculitis (IgAV), these diseases are
relatively rare in childhood. KD and IgAV are usually acute and
self-limited; however, the other forms can be organ or life-threatening, especially if not recognized and treated in a timely manner.
Compared with adults, children may have a higher potential for
significant morbidity due to the longer duration of disease or effects
of treatment over the course of their lives. This section will focus
on the types of vasculitis most commonly seen in the pediatric age
group and highlight the unique features of childhood-onset disease.

### Classification

The framework for classification of childhood vasculitis is based
upon the predominant size (large, medium, or small) of the
involved vessels. Additional factors that have been incorporated
into classification include clinical phenotype, histopathology, and
the presumed underlying disease pathogenesis.
The most
widely used classification systems in adults are the ACR criteria
that were proposed in 1990
and the Chapel Hill Consensus
Conference (CHCC) framework for nomenclature and disease
definitions that were proposed in 1994 and updated in 2012
(CHCC 2012).
The CCHC system provides comprehensive
descriptions that can help define diseases, but it does not provide
classification criteria. The CCHC framework and ACR classification criteria were based on adult data and have limitations for use
in pediatrics. For example, the ACR criteria do not include IgAV
and KD. In pediatrics, the most commonly used classification system is the European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology
(formerly known as the EULAR)/Paediatric Rheumatology International Trials Organisation/Paediatric Rheumatology European
Society (EULAR/PRINTO/PRES) classification scheme (
Table
111.5
).
The EULAR/PRINTO/PRES classification is based
on vessel size like the ACR and CCHC systems, but it also provides pediatric specific classification criteria for IgAV, polyarteritis nodosa (PAN), granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), and
Takayasu arteritis (TA). In 2022 new ACR/EULAR criteria for
the classification of GPA,
microscopic polyangiitis (MPA),
and eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA)
were proposed for adults. The utility of these criteria in the pediatric population has not been evaluated; however, they do provide
the first-ever classification criteria for MPA.

### Immunoglobulin A Vasculitis

Definition and Classification
IgAV, formerly known as Henoch–Schönlein purpura,
is the
most common vasculitis in childhood. It is a small vessel vasculitis
that is characterized by IgA immune deposition and involvement
of the skin, gastrointestinal, musculoskeletal, and renal systems.
IgAV typically presents acutely with a self-limited disease course;
however, a minority of patients will have a more chronic course.
The EULAR/PRINTO/PRES criteria for IgAV include a mandatory finding of purpura or petechiae (without thrombocytopenia) with lower limb predominance and one or more of the
following.
• Diffuse abdominal colicky pain (may include intussusception and gastrointestinal bleeding)
• Arthritis or arthralgia
• Histopathology showing leukocytoclastic vasculitis or proliferative glomerulonephritis with predominant IgA deposition

Epidemiology
IgAV has an incidence of 6 to 30 cases per 100,000 children,
with a peak incidence between ages 4 to 6 years.
Most studies suggest a slight male predominance at a ratio of 1.2
to 1.5:1.
IgAV is much less common in adults, but adults may have
worse renal outcomes.
IgAV occurs throughout the year but
less frequently in the summer months.
Infectious triggers
have long been recognized, with more than half of affected children having a preceding upper respiratory infection.
Etiology and Pathogenesis
The underlying cause of IgAV remains unknown; however, a combination of genetic, environmental, and immunologic factors
likely plays a role.
Associations with certain HLA alleles,
as well as polymorphisms in other genes, have been reported.
The presence of IgA deposits in vessel walls of affected organs is
characteristic of IgAV, and both aberrant and increased levels of

IgA and IgA complexes are considered to play a central role in the
pathogenesis of IgAV.
Clinical Features
The classic tetrad of IgAV includes palpable purpura, joint pain,
gastrointestinal complaints, and renal involvement. The signs and
symptoms may develop over days to weeks. Nonthrombocytopenic purpuric skin lesions are the hallmark of IgAV, and their
presence is required for the diagnosis.
Palpable purpura usually
appear in dependent areas; however, the trunk, arms, and face can
also be affected. The rash typically appears in crops and may be
preceded by a maculopapular or urticarial rash before evolving into
the more classic purpura. Joint involvement is the second most
common manifestation seen after rash, with arthritis or arthralgias
occurring in approximately three-quarters of children.
The
arthritis is usually oligoarticular, with the knees and ankles most
commonly affected.
Joint involvement is the presenting manifestation in 15% of children.
Subcutaneous edema, including
periarticular swelling, is common.
Gastrointestinal involvement occurs in up to two-thirds of children, usually manifesting
as abdominal pain.
Other symptoms include nausea, vomiting, and gastrointestinal bleeding. Surgical complications occur
in approximately 5% of children and include intussusception and
bowel wall perforation.
Renal disease is present in up to half of
children and usually presents with hematuria and/or proteinuria
and within 6 weeks of diagnosis.
Renal involvement is usually mild and self-limited; however, a minority of children will
develop nephritic or nephrotic syndrome, impaired renal function, or renal failure.
Less than 2% of children will develop
long-term renal impairment.
The risk is higher in older children and adults and in patients who have more severe renal disease
at presentation
Other less common clinical manifestations
of IgAV include pulmonary involvement, nervous system involvement, and urologic involvement (scrotal pain and swelling).
Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests
The diagnosis of IgAV is based on clinical manifestations. Laboratory tests are helpful for identifying renal involvement and for
excluding other diseases (for example, ruling out thrombocytopenia or coagulopathy as a cause for purpura). Markers of inflammation (ESR or C-reactive protein [CRP]) levels may be elevated
but are nonspecific. Serum IgA levels are increased in approximately half of patients.
If the diagnosis remains uncertain, a
skin biopsy demonstrating leukocytoclastic vasculitis with deposition of IgA-containing immune complexes supports the diagnosis.
In rare cases, renal biopsies are performed, particularly if the renal
disease is severe and progressive. Imaging studies such as abdominal radiography and ultrasound are useful in patients with significant abdominal symptoms.
Treatment
Most children with IgAV have self-limited disease that can be
managed with supportive care, including hydration and pain
management. For more severe disease, there may be a role for glucocorticoids and, in some cases, immunosuppressive treatment.
In 2019 the European initiative SHARE (Single Hub and Access
point for pediatric Rheumatology in Europe) published the firstever evidence-based consensus recommendations for the diagnosis and management of pediatric IgAV.
SHARE recommends
that in patients with severe gastrointestinal involvement, corticosteroid treatment could be considered based on some studies
showing that corticosteroids shorten the intensity and duration of
abdominal pain.
For the treatment of IgAV nephritis, SHARE
recommends the use of corticosteroids (dosing dependent on
severity) along with immunosuppressive treatment such as azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, or intravenous cyclophosphamide in some cases.
Prophylactic corticosteroid treatment to
prevent the development of IgAV nephritis is not recommended
since controlled studies have shown that corticosteroids used in
this way do not change renal outcome.
Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors should be considered in IgAV
nephritis to prevent/limit secondary glomerular injury for patients
with persistent proteinuria.
Outcomes
Overall, the majority of children with IgAV have a self-limited
disease resolving within 4 to 6 weeks. Early morbidity is related
to gastrointestinal involvement, and late morbidity is related to
long-term renal impairment, which occurs in less than 2% of
children.
The risk of chronic renal disease is higher in older
children and adults and those with more severe initial renal
involvement.
Recurrence of IgAV occurs in up to one-third
of patients, typically within 4 months of initial presentation.

### Kawasaki’s Disease

Definition and Classification
KD is the second most prevalent form of pediatric vasculitis.
It
was first described in 1967 by the Japanese physician Dr. Kawasaki as a mucocutaneous lymph node syndrome.
It is typically
an acute and self-limited vasculitis that mainly affects mediumsized arteries. It predominately affects infants and young children
and is rare in adults. Timely and appropriate treatment of KD
is highly effective and significantly reduces the risk of coronary
artery aneurysms, which is the most concerning complication of
KD.
Classification criteria for KD were proposed by EULAR/PRES
in 2006; however, the criteria have never been validated.
The
criteria are almost identical to the diagnostic criteria set forth
by the American Heart Association (AHA), which are the most
widely used criteria for diagnosing KD.
The AHA criteria for diagnosing KD
include a mandatory
finding of fever for 5 or more days and at least four of the five following features:
• Bilateral bulbar conjunctival injection without exudate
• Erythema and cracking of lips, strawberry tongue, and/or
erythema of oral and pharyngeal mucosa
• Erythema and edema of the hands and feet in acute phase
and/or periungual desquamation in subacute phase
• Rash: maculopapular, diffuse erythroderma, or erythema
multiforme-like
• Cervical lymphadenopathy (
≥
1.5 cm diameter), usually
unilateral
Patients with fever and fewer than four of the above criteria can
also be diagnosed with KD if coronary artery abnormalities are
detected either by two-dimensional echocardiography or coronary
angiography.
Epidemiology
KD has a worldwide distribution; however, the disease is significantly more prevalent in Asian countries, especially Japan.
Annual incidence rates as high as 260 per 100,000 children <5 years
of age have been reported in Japan compared with 20 per 100,000
children <5 years of age in the continental United States.

The majority of children are diagnosed before the age of 5 years,
and boys are affected more commonly than girls.
Etiology and Pathogenesis
The etiology of KD remains unknown. The seasonality of KD,
with peaks in the winter and spring and occurrences of epidemics,
have suggested an infectious trigger; however, no specific infectious agent has been identified.
In the absence of identifying a
single infectious trigger, KD may result from an abnormal immunologic response to a more widely distributed infectious agent(s) in
genetically susceptible individuals.
The increased frequency
of KD in Asian populations and in children of Asian descent residing in different countries, and the increased occurrence rates of
KD among siblings and other family members of index cases supports a genetic component to KD susceptibility.
Clinical Features
KD progresses through three phases: acute (as long as 14 days),
subacute (2 to 4 weeks), and convalescent (4 to 8 weeks).
The
acute phase is characterized by high fever along with other clinical features that characterize KD: rash, bilateral limbic-sparing
nonexudative conjunctivitis, unilateral cervical lymphadenopathy, mucous membrane changes (strawberry tongue, red cracked
lips), and extremity changes (swelling and erythema of the hands
and feet). The fever is often high spiking (above 102°F [39°C])
and minimally responsive to antipyretic agents. The subacute
phase follows the acute febrile phase and is largely asymptomatic
other than the desquamation of the hands and feet that is seen
in some children. During this phase, coronary artery aneurysms
may develop or become apparent as a result of the preceding acute
phase. The final convalescent phase is asymptomatic.

### Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody- Associated Vasculitis, Polyarteritis Nodosa, and Takayasu’s Arteritis

Other forms of pediatric vasculitis are rare and present with
similar manifestations as adult-onset disease.
Given the
rarity of these vasculitides in childhood, much of the information on pathophysiology, clinical manifestations, and treatment
is based on adult studies. This section will highlight some of
the differences between adult and pediatric disease. Please refer
to the individual chapters on each disease for more in-depth
review.
Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody-Associated
Vasculitis
Definition, Classification, and Epidemiology.
Anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–associated vasculitis (AAV)
includes GPA, MPA, EGPA, and renal-limited pauci-immune
glomerulonephritis. These diseases are collectively characterized
by predominantly small vessel necrotizing vasculitis associated
with the presence of proteinase 3 (PR3)-ANCA or myeloperoxidase (MPO)-ANCA. These diseases are often grouped together
because the definitions and classification characteristics of each
disorder are not mutually exclusive. AAV is rare in children, with
an overall incidence that varies depending on geographic location, but ranges between 0.1 to 6.4 cases per million children per
year.
In contrast to adult-onset disease, pediatric-onset AAV is
more common in girls.
EULAR/PRINTO/PRES proposed classification criteria for
GPA, but not for MPA or EGPA. The criteria for GPA require
three of the following six features
:
• Renal involvement (hematuria, proteinuria, or necrotizing
pauci-immune glomerulonephritis)
• Upper airway involvement
• Laryngo-tracheo-bronchial involvement
• Pulmonary involvement
• ANCA positivity
• Granulomatous inflammation within the wall of an artery
or in the perivascular or extravascular area on biopsy
There are no pediatric-specific classification criteria for MPA
and EGPA; therefore classification of these diseases is largely based
on the adult ACR and CHCC systems.
The 2022 newly
proposed adult ACR/EULAR criteria for the classification of AAV
includes updated criteria for GPA and EGPA and the first-ever
classification criteria for MPA
; however, the utility of these criteria in children has yet to be evaluated.
Clinical Features.
The clinical features of the various AAV
conditions overlap significantly and predominantly involve the
kidneys, lungs, and upper airways. Constitutional symptoms
such as fever, weight loss, and fatigue are seen in the majority of
patients.
Similar to adults, GPA is characterized by the triad of upper
respiratory tract/ear–nose–throat (ENT) involvement, lower
respiratory tract involvement, and renal disease. Cutaneous, musculoskeletal, gastrointestinal, and ocular manifestations are also
seen.
Compared with adults, children with GPA have higher frequencies of constitutional symptoms and respiratory involvement
but a lower frequency of conductive hearing loss.
Some
studies suggest a higher frequency of ENT involvement in children, especially over the longer term.

MPA is characterized by renal disease (often rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis) that may be accompanied by cutaneous,
musculoskeletal, renal, and/or gastrointestinal involvement.
EGPA is exceedingly rare in children but similar to adults is characterized by chronic rhinosinusitis, asthma, and peripheral blood
eosinophilia. The vasculitis manifestations of EGPA are often not
apparent until later in the disease course.
Diagnosis and Diagnostic Testing.
Laboratory studies are
similar to adults. ANCA positivity is common; however, the
absence of ANCA does not preclude the diagnosis. The presence
of PR3-ANCA is usually associated with GPA, whereas MPOANCA is more common in MPA.
Other laboratory findings are nonspecific and usually reflect
systemic inflammation (anemia, high acute-phase reactants) or
indicate other organ involvement (e.g., abnormal urinalysis, elevated liver enzymes). Other investigations such as imaging of the
lungs or sinuses may help determine extent and severity of disease,
and tissue biopsies (skin, kidney) may be necessary to confirm the
diagnosis.
Treatment and Outcome.
The rarity of pediatric AAV has
limited the ability to obtain pediatric-specific data on drug efficacy and toxicity; hence, treatment recommendations are largely
extrapolated from adult randomized trial data. Treatment of AAV
is based on a remission-induction model followed by a remissionmaintenance model. Glucocorticoids continue to be a mainstay of
initial treatment. Choice of primary immunosuppressive agent is
dependent on disease severity and type of organ involvement. For
severe organ or life-threatening disease, intravenous cyclophosphamide or B cell–depleting agents are primarily used; whereas for
milder disease, methotrexate and azathioprine are favored.
Different from adults, current consensus-based guidance in children recommends the use of B cell–depleting agents be reserved
for children not responding to conventional treatments or those
with relapsing disease. In an effort to obtain more pediatric-specific data regarding the effectiveness and safety of cyclophosphamide versus rituximab in childhood-onset disease, CTPs for severe
pediatric AAV were published in 2022 with the goal of prospectively comparing patients treated with the different protocols.
For remission maintenance, methotrexate, azathioprine, or rituximab are recommended.
Similar to adults, pediatric AAV is associated with a chronic
relapsing disease course and high rates of damage.
Direct
comparisons between adults and children are difficult due to variability in how remission and relapse are defined, as well as duration of follow-up.
Polyarteritis Nodosa
Definition, Classification, and Epidemiology.
Polyarteritis
nodosa (PAN) is a systemic necrotizing vasculitis affecting smalland/or medium-sized arteries. It frequently presents with systemic
symptoms (e.g., fever, malaise, and weight loss) and involvement
of the skin, musculoskeletal, and renal systems.
The EULAR/PRINTO/PRES classification criteria for childhood PAN include a mandatory finding of either necrotizing
vasculitis on biopsy or angiographic abnormalities of small- or
medium-sized arteries plus one of the following
:
• Skin involvement (livedo reticularis, subcutaneous nodules,
skin infarctions)
• Myalgia or muscle tenderness
• Hypertension
• Peripheral neuropathy
• Kidney involvement (proteinuria, hematuria, or impaired
renal function)
The estimated annual incidence of PAN in adults is 1 to 8
cases/million.
It is rarer in childhood; however, epidemiologic
data in the pediatric population is scarce. In children, the peak age
of onset is
∼
9 to 10 years of age, and males and females are equally
affected.
Clinical Features.
PAN varies in its presentation from a relatively mild primarily cutaneous form to a more severe systemic
form.
The cutaneous form of the disease presents similarly in
children and adults with rash, arthritis/arthralgias, and fever being
the most common features.
Children with the systemic form of
the disease typically present with constitutional symptoms, rash,
and other major organ involvement, primarily renal, gastrointestinal, and neurologic systems.
Two studies comparing adultonset PAN to childhood-onset PAN had varying conclusions;
both demonstrated less neurologic involvement in children; however, one study suggested that childhood PAN had a more benign
course,
whereas the other study suggested that childhood-onset
disease was more severe and carried a higher risk of relapse.
In childhood, the possibility of a more recently described
monogenic vasculitis called deficiency of adenosine deaminase 2
(DADA2) should also be considered in any child being evaluated
for PAN. Children with DADA2 have mutations in the CECR1
(cat eye syndrome chromosome region, candidate 1) gene encoding adenosine deaminase 2 (ADA2) and usually present at a young
age with signs of systemic inflammation, prominent vasculitic rash
(livedo racemosa, subcutaneous nodules), early onset stroke, musculoskeletal involvement and immunologic abnormalities.
Stimulator of interferon genes (STING)–associated vasculopathy
of infancy (SAVI) is another recently described condition that is
characterized by severe vasculopathy and interstitial pulmonary
disease.
Diagnosis and Diagnostic Testing.
The diagnosis of PAN is
based upon the presence of characteristic signs and symptoms and
evidence of necrotizing vasculitis affecting small or medium vessels on biopsy or angiographic abnormalities of such vessels. Laboratory tests help identify the extent of other organ involvement
and can aid in ruling out other secondary causes of vasculitis.
Elevation of acute phase reactants is supportive but nonspecific.
Biopsies and vascular imaging are guided by what organ systems
are involved.
Treatment and Outcome.
Treatment of PAN is mainly based
on data from adults and is guided by the extent and severity of
the disease. Corticosteroids are needed by most patients; however,
more aggressive steroid-sparing treatments are usually required
when the disease is not limited to the skin. As with other forms of
chronic systemic vasculitis, the recommended approach is to start
with induction treatment followed by less intense maintenance
treatment for 12 to 36 months depending on the clinical course.
The current adult-based guidance is to use intravenous cyclophosphamide and corticosteroids for remission induction of severe
PAN, followed by maintenance with less toxic agents such as azathioprine or methotrexate.
A recently published small pediatric randomized controlled study evaluated mycophenolate mofetil
versus intravenous cyclophosphamide for remission induction in
11 children with PAN. Rates of remission were similar in both
groups, but MMF was associated with a shorter time to remission
and better health-related quality of life measures at follow-up.
Compared with adults, overall survival is better, and the relapse
risk is lower in children.
The overall mortality is about 1% to

4%, and relapse risk is about 10% to 35% in large pediatric PAN
cohorts.
Takayasu’s Arteritis
Definition, Classification, and Epidemiology.
TA is a granulomatous large-vessel vasculitis that affects the aorta and its major
branches. Incidence in all ages is estimated to be between 0.8 and
2.6 per million persons/year.
The incidence in children is not
known; however, it is estimated that up to 30% of patients with
TA present in childhood.
Median age of onset is between 12
to 14 years, but it has also been reported in infancy.
The EULAR/PRINTO/PRES classification criteria for
Takayasu’s arteritis in childhood require a mandatory finding of
angiographic abnormalities of the aorta or its main branches and
pulmonary arteries (aneurysm/dilatation, narrowing, occlusion,
or arterial wall thickening not due to fibromuscular dysplasia) and
at least one of the following five features
:
• Pulse deficit (lost/decreased/unequal peripheral artery
pulse[s]) and/or claudication induced by activity
• Systolic blood pressure difference >10 mm Hg between any
limb
• Bruits or thrills over the aorta and/or its major branches
• Hypertension (systolic blood pressure greater than the 95th
percentile for height)
• Elevated acute-phase reactants
Angiographic confirmation may be performed by conventional radiography, computed tomography, or magnetic resonance
imaging.
Clinical Features.
The early symptoms of TA are nonspecific,
and diagnostic delays are common.
Similar to adults, constitutional symptoms, hypertension, and headache are common at
presentation.
Other symptoms include abdominal pain,
vomiting, dyspnea, and musculoskeletal complaints. Claudication
is more frequently observed in adults.
Bruits, diminished
pulses, and blood pressure discrepancies between limbs are signs
seen on exam.
Diagnosis and Diagnostic Testing.
The diagnosis of TA
is based upon the presence of characteristic clinical findings
and angiographic evidence of abnormalities (aneurysm, dilatation, narrowing, occlusion, or thickening) of the aorta, its main
branches, or pulmonary arteries. Abnormalities may be demonstrated by conventional angiography, magnetic resonance angiography (MRA) or computed tomography angiography (CTA).
Other vascular imaging modalities that may also be useful include
Doppler ultrasound, as well as more specific vessel wall imaging
modalities such as gadolinium-enhanced MRA and fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography (PET).
Laboratory tests
usually demonstrate nonspecific elevation of inflammatory markers. Additional investigations to rule out secondary causes of large
vessel vasculitis, such as infections like tuberculosis, may also be
required.
Treatment and Outcome.
The approach to treatment of
childhood TA is similar to adults. The primary goal is to induce
remission as promptly as possible to prevent irreversible vessel damage and resultant organ damage from vascular insufficiency. Corticosteroids are the mainstay of treatment but due
to the chronic relapsing nature of the disease, the addition of a
corticosteroid-sparing agent is recommended. The pediatric-based
guidance from SHARE is to use cyclophosphamide or MTX as
the preferred initial choices.
Other agents that may be considered on a case-by-case basis are TNF inhibitors, anti–IL-6
therapy, and rituximab.
For maintenance, MTX, azathioprine,
or mycophenolate mofetil is recommended.
Treatment of TA
also includes careful monitoring and management of hypertension along with endovascular and or revascularization procedures
in some patients.
Recent advances in treatment and disease recognition in TA
have resulted in decreased morbidity and mortality. Reported
mortality rates in childhood-onset disease range between
0 and 27%.
Some studies suggest a more systemic and
widespread disease presentation in childhood-onset disease
with lower rates of remission and increased need for biologic
treatments.
