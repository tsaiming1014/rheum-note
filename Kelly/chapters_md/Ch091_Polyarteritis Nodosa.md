# Polyarteritis Nodosa

NURAY AKTAY AYAZ AND SEZA OZEN

KEY POINTS

> Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) is characterized by a necrotizing vasculitis of

> predominantly medium-sized arteries, with few or no deposits, and

> is a relatively rare disease.

> Presenting features of PAN often include an insidious onset of

> constitutional findings, skin vasculitis, mononeuritis multiplex, and

> symptoms related to the ischemia of the affected organ system,

> such as myalgia or abdominal pain. Acute phase reactants are

> increased.

> Glomerular renal disease is absent in PAN and should prompt

> consideration of other diagnosis, especially small vessel vasculitis.

> However, hematuria and mild proteinuria may occur as a result of

> renal infarction.

> Cutaneous PAN is a skin-limited form of vasculitis and is not expected to

> evolve into a systemic disease.

> Deficiency of ADA2 is a monogenic disease mimicking PAN and

> mutations in the associated gene should be sought for in young

> children presenting with PAN. On the other hand hepatitis B related

> PAN-like disease is not seen in children anymore (due to effective

> vaccination policies) and is now classified as a vasculitis with known

> etiology.

## Definition and Classification

Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) is a multisystem disease characterized by necrotizing vasculitis predominantly in the medium-size
arteries. Historically, the term
periarteritis nodosa
, first introduced by Kussmaul and Maier in 1866,
has evolved over time
into the more pathologically correct term:
polyarteritis nodosa
. In
1908 Dickson
proposed the name
polyarteritis nodosa
as a definition for explaining destructive and proliferative changes of the
vessel wall due to inflammation involving all the layers transmurally. PAN has been formally defined in the 2012 revised Chapel
Hill initiative as necrotizing arteritis of medium and small-size
arteries without glomerulonephritis or vasculitis in arterioles,
capillaries, or venules and not associated with anti-neutrophil
cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA).
Herein, diseases involving
small vessels like eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis
(EGPA), granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), and microscopic polyangiitis (MPA) came out of the umbrella term
polyarteritis nodosa
. In addition, hepatitis B–related PAN is clearly
separated from PAN and classified as “vasculitis associated with
the probable etiology.” The lack of association with ANCA has
also been emphasized.
Not only the definition but also the classification of vasculitides is a matter of issue for correctly placing the patient to proper
diagnosis, especially when selecting the patients for clinical trials.
The initial classification for PAN was introduced by the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) in 1990; this classification
required the presence of at least 3 of the 10 following items: (1)
granulocyte or mixed leukocyte infiltrate in an arterial wall on
biopsy; (2) arteriographic abnormalities; (3) livedo reticularis;
(4) myalgia; (5) diastolic blood pressure (BP) of >90 mm Hg;
(6) mono- or polyneuropathy; (7) elevated blood urea, nitrogen,
or creatinine; (8) testicular pain/tenderness; (9) hepatitis B reactants; and (10) weight loss of >4 kg (sensitivity, 82.2%; specificity, 86.6%). No differentiation was made between PAN and
MPA.
A European Medicines Agency (EMA) study group aimed
to develop a consensus methodology for the classification of
the ANCA-associated vasculitides and PAN.
They proposed
an algorithm based on exclusion of GPA (formerly known as
Wegener’s granulomatosis), EGPA, and MPA. The presence of
histologically proven small-vessel vasculitis or glomerulonephritis and the presence of ANCA were considered to be features of
MPA, not PAN.
In 2006 the European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology (EULAR)/Paediatric Rheumatology International Trials
Organisation (PRINTO)/Paediatric Rheumatology European
Society (PReS) endorsed the Ankara 2006 consensus criteria for
the classification of childhood vasculitides including PAN.
Classification of the patient as having cutaneous PAN (cPAN) requires
the presence of necrotizing arteritis at histopathology or typical
changes in angiographic abnormalities reflecting vasculitis in
addition to the presence of one of the five following features: skin
involvement, myalgia/muscle tenderness, hypertension, peripheral neuropathy, and renal involvement. It should be emphasized
that the renal findings in PAN should be solely due to the involvement of the medium- and small-size arteries within the kidney
tissue but not the glomeruli. Revised classification attempts may
further address this issue.
In the past decade, we have become aware that necrotizing
arteritis as defined for PAN may be due to other defined causes
as well.
A striking example was the monogenic disease caused by
mutations in the
ADA2
gene, formally the
CECR1
(cat eye syndrome chromosome region candidate 1) gene mutations and deficiency of adenosine deaminase 2 (DADA2), described in 2014.
DADA2 may present with classical features of PAN, both clinically and histologically.
We were then introduced to another

autoinflammatory disease, VEXAS, which also may present with
features of necrotizing vasculitis in certain organ systems.
Last
but not least, we observed PAN mimics during the COVID pandemic.
These patients should now be classified under the category of “vasculitis associated with probable etiology” and not
as PAN. Better understanding of the underlying pathogenetic
mechanisms will surely enable us to better define and diagnose
these patients.

## Epidemiology

We now know that with the evolving changes in its classification, by placing hepatitis B virus (HBV)–associated vasculitis in a
group with vasculitis associated with probable etiology and placing MPA, GPA, and EGPA in a group with small vessel vasculitis,
PAN has become a quite rare disease. However, it is very challenging to define the real frequency, as a satisfying classification system
is not yet in use in clinical practice.
The estimated annual incidence of PAN according to ACR criteria was reported as 2.0 to 9.0 per million in adults in Europe
and the United States.
The prevalence was reported as 31 cases
per million.
While PAN is considered an adult disease with
the highest incidence observed in the fourth to fifth decades of
life, childhood-onset PAN is increasingly recognized with some
variation in clinical manifestations compared with adult disease.
Previously, it was confirmed that childhood PAN had better outcomes than adults, and the age at disease onset is around 9 to
10 years of age.
Previous epidemiologic studies found that
PAN is slightly more common among males, with the male-tofemale ratio of approximately 1.5:1.
It may affect all ethnicities.
A recent study investigated the inpatient prevalence of PAN from
the Nationwide Inpatient Sample in the United States and found
higher frequencies than what would be expected from the overall
prevalence. The inpatient prevalence of PAN was 11.6 cases per
100,000 hospital discharges.
The mortality rate was reported as
1% to 4% in children, while it was 14.5% to 24.6% in adult PAN
patients.

## Etiology and Pathogenesis

The etiology of PAN is still unknown; however, both innate and
adaptive immunity are thought to play a role in the pathogenesis
of necrotizing inflammation of the vessels. In the active phase of
vasculitis, the main cells migrating to the vessel wall are neutrophils, but the T cell–mediated immune mechanism is considered
to play a significant role in the pathogenesis of the disease due to
the subsequent predominant infiltration of T cells.
The frequencies of T helper (Th) cells were studied in patients with PAN and
compared with healthy controls and patients with GPA, and an
imbalanced expression of excessive Th cells and defective regulatory T cells (Tregs) in PAN was demonstrated.
Th cells were
found to be significantly higher in number than in healthy controls, and in comparison with GPA, the expression of Th1 cells
was higher but that of Th17 cells was lower in PAN. Additionally, a significant increase in Tregs was observed in PAN. However, defects in suppressive ability and CTLA-4 expression were
observed as well.
The skin lesions of systemic vasculitis are predominantly CD8
T cells that infiltrate the intima and that CD8
T cells in the
intima are activated in an antigen-independent manner. These
cells cause vasculitis by inducing inflammation with neutrophil
infiltration. Activated CD8
T cells produce interferon (IFN)
γ
that plays a critical role in activation of macrophages and perpetuating the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines. CD8
T cells
play a crucial role in both, leading to vessel wall injury and triggering inflammatory reactions.
One study identified the expression of adhesion molecules on the luminal endothelium in the
early lesions of PAN.
They also concluded that initial luminal
endothelium damage in the vasculitis process diminishes before
the chronic phase.

## Clinical Features and Diagnostic Tests

The clinical spectrum of PAN is characterized by constitutional
features and protean organ-specific manifestations overwhelmed
by the extent of inflammation. The occlusion of the inflamed
arteries may result in tissue ischemia and necrosis of the affected
sites.
Constitutional symptoms like fever, weight loss, arthralgia,
and myalgia are very common among patients.
Purpura, nodules, and livedo reticularis are the main cutaneous findings; in
a limited number of patients, digital necrosis may accompany
the disease (
Fig. 91.1
). In 50% to 75% of the patients, the
arteries of the peripheral nervous system are affected, presenting in the form of mononeuritis multiplex and rarely peripheral
neuropathy.
Central nervous system (CNS) involvement may
occur due to vasculitis, but posterior leukoencephalopathy due
to malignant hypertension may also be present and is usually
associated with poor prognosis.
Renal involvement is due to
involvement of the intrarenal arteries without the presence of
glomerulonephritis.
Proteinuria, hematuria, and recent-onset
hypertension have been reported due to this involvement. The
gastrointestinal system is one of the major affected sites of PAN,

usually presenting with severe manifestations and poor outcome.
There may be severe abdominal pain, gastrointestinal bleeding
and perforation of the intestines. Although rare, cholecystitis,
pancreatitis, and appendicitis may occur in patients with PAN.
Cardiovascular involvement was reported in 22% of the patients,
mainly as vasculitis-related cardiomyopathy.
Albeit rare, testicular involvement is one of the significant features of PAN;
patients may present with severe testicular pain and orchitis.
Investigators have reported involvement of the gastrointestinal
tract and skin at a rate of 50%, skeletal muscles and mesentery
at a rate of 30%, and the CNS at a rate of 10%, as well as a high
rate of involvement of the renal arteries.
Compared with adults, children and adolescents have higher
frequency of cutaneous involvement and constitutional features,
but lower frequency of neurologic manifestations.
In the
retrospective study of the French Vasculitis Study Group, patients
with HBV infection–related PAN have a more severe course but
fewer relapses compared with patients with non–HBV-related
PAN.

### Laboratory Tests

There is no single diagnostic laboratory test for PAN. However,
due to the presence of inflammation, acute phase reactants including C-reactive proteins and erythrocyte sedimentation rate are
elevated. Basic evaluation of the complete blood count may show
anemia and thrombocytosis with accompanying mild to moderate
neutrophilic leukocytosis.
Chest radiography should be obtained for differential diagnosis. Autoantibodies like ANCA and, if required, antinuclear antibody (ANA), complements, and cryoglobulin levels may help in
the differential diagnosis of PAN as well.

### Histopathology and Imaging

PAN has been described for over 150 years. However, as there are
no specific serologic tests, the diagnosis is still challenging. The
EULAR/PReS/PRINTO–endorsed Ankara 2006 classification
criteria require the demonstration of necrotizing arteritis either
in a biopsy specimen or through the aneurysms in medium-size
arteries that reflect the pathologic process (
Fig. 91.2
).
When a suitable biopsy site is present, histopathology-based
diagnosis is a reliable method for differentiating PAN from other
vasculitis syndromes. For imaging, a spectrum of angiographic
findings have been defined to suggest the diagnosis of PAN.
The affected site should be chosen for biopsy, but skin, muscles,
and sural nerves are common sites for the specimen. Renal biopsy
is not the preferred method for the histopathological diagnosis
since glomerulonephritis is not present and rupture of microaneurysms may lead to severe bleeding. In biopsy specimens from
PAN patients, inflammatory lesions of medium-sized or small
arteries are characteristically segmental and more pronounced
in branching points.
The propensity toward bifurcation sites
had been justified by the influence of hydrostatic forces or the
adhesion molecules secreted from the macrophages. However,
arterioles, capillaries, and venules are generally not affected. The
inflammation site in the vessel wall is rich in lymphocytes, neutrophils, and macrophages, and fibrinoid necrosis is present.
At the chronic stages, lymphocyte and macrophage infiltration
accompany the process of neoangiogenesis.
With the initiation
of remodeling, intimal proliferation and fibrosis ensue and then
lead to occurrence of stenotic lesions, thrombosis, microaneurysm
formation, and rupture of the vessel walls.
One of the most important characteristics of PAN biopsy
is coexistence of different stages of the inflammation such as
active lesions, fibrosis, and healing lesions in the same specimen.
Microaneurysms or arterial stenoses of the visceral arteries have been reported in 40% to 90% of patients with PAN at
presentation.
Conventional angiography is the gold-standard technique
for identifying vascular abnormalities with a sensitivity and
specificity of
≈
90%.
However, with improving technology,
now CT-angiography also has a high yield for demonstrating
the aneurysms of PAN.
The major angiographic finding is
microaneurysms. Arterial steno-occlusive lesions are less common findings. Early diagnosis of PAN has been reported by
a few investigators based on CT and MRI findings.
One
group reported positive CT-angiography findings in 15 of the
27 patients with PAN.
The most common CT-angiography
finding was aneurysm, followed by stenosis/occlusion. The most
commonly involved arteries were the renal arteries, followed
by the hepatic and splenic arteries. The most common visceral
abnormality was splenic and renal infarct followed by bowel
wall thickening and, rarely, perinephric hematoma. Although
not assessed adequately,
F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) positron
emission tomography (PET) may show the increased uptake in
the affected arteries.

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis includes infectious diseases and lesions
affecting small- and medium-sized arteries. Vasculitis has been
associated with HBV, hepatitis C virus, human immunodeficiency
virus, parvovirus B19, cytomegalovirus, Epstein–Barr virus, streptococcal infections, and finally COVID.
The presence of eosinophilia may be a marker for ruling out
EGPA (Churg–Strauss disease). The presence of ANCA suggests
an ANCA-associated vasculitis such as MPA, GPA (Wegener
granulomatosis), or EGPA.
Kawasaki disease is the second
most common vasculitis of childhood; it should be differentiated

If there is a family history or atypical features, monogenic diseases should be sought. Many experts suggest that screening for

gene should be done in all childhood onset
patients since it is being increasingly recognized. DADA2 should
be suspected, especially with the presence of early-onset strokes or
hematologic or immunologic abnormalities.
Researchers have
suggested that DADA2 patients do not have the typical thrombocytosis seen in classic PAN.

## Treatment

PAN is a rare and life-threatening disease with heterogeneous
clinical presentations. The major goal of therapy is suppressing inflammation and inducing long-term remission. In 2008
EULAR recommendations for the management of primary small
and medium vessel vasculitis (in adults) were published. A combination of cyclophosphamide (intravenous [IV] or oral) and glucocorticoids for remission induction was recommended. High-dose
glucocorticoids were advocated as an important part of induction
therapy, and dose reduction to
≤
10 mg/day was favored after 3
months of remission. This can be reduced gradually after 6 to 18
months according to patient response with the aim of discontinuing therapy.
Cyclophosphamide doses of 2 mg/kg/day for oral
use or 600 mg/m
given at 2- to 4-week intervals for IV pulse
therapy were accepted as alternative regimens with similar effects,
although IV application has fewer side effects.
The authors
strongly recommend lower doses in children, such as that used in
the Euro-Lupus protocol.
For mild cases 1 mg/kg/day of prednisolone alone and tapering the dose after the control of manifestations is advised.
For refractory or difficult to treat cases, there are rare
reports suggesting the use of rituximab, anti-TNF agents, or
tocilizumab.
In 2021, the ACR/Vasculitis Foundation proposed guidelines
for the management of PAN.
Accordingly, for patients with
newly diagnosed active, severe PAN, initiating treatment with
IV pulse glucocorticoids over high-dose oral glucocorticoids
were conditionally recommended.
For pediatric patients, pulse
glucocorticoid therapy seems to be safe with tolerable infection
rate and morbidities compared with oral glucocorticoids.
Initiating treatment with cyclophosphamide and high-dose glucocorticoids over high-dose glucocorticoids alone is recommended
for patients with severe and active PAN.
For patients with
newly diagnosed active, severe PAN, initiating treatment with
cyclophosphamide and glucocorticoids over rituximab and glucocorticoids was recommended.
The efficacy of rituximab in
PAN is uncertain as there is lack of large comparative studies in
this disease.
For patients unable to tolerate cyclophosphamide,
agents like azathioprine or methotrexate are recommended over
glucocorticoids alone.
Although the benefit of plasmapheresis
was not shown in PAN, it may be considered in catastrophic
cases unresponsive to aggressive immunosuppressive therapies
and may have a role in the management of HBV-related PAN.
The duration of glucocorticoid therapy for PAN is not well
entrenched, so in line with these recommendations, the duration of therapy should be guided with respect to the patient’s
clinical condition, values, and preferences.
In the maintenance
therapy of patients with newly diagnosed PAN, switching to
another nonglucocorticoid immunosuppressive agent (azathioprine) is recommended.
In an international, open-label, randomized controlled trial
to investigate the relative effectiveness of cyclophosphamide and
mycophenolate mofetil for remission induction in childhood
PAN, remission rates were similar with MMF treatment and CYC
treatment.
This study was conducted among a small group of
pediatric PAN patients; however, it was the first randomized trial
in childhood PAN.

## Outcome

If left untreated, PAN has a devastating course and a survival rate
of 13% in 5 years.
Patients with limited organ involvement
and cutaneous disease have a better prognosis than patients with
severe disease, but survival rates have increased with the use of
cyclophosphamide in remission induction.
Early diagnosis and
prompt initiation of treatment have dramatically increased the
5-year survival rate. However, the relapse rate is about 20%.
The Paediatric Vasculitis Activity Score (PVAS) can be used to
assess the activity of the disease.
The French Vasculitis Study
Group established a five-factor score (FFS) as a simple prognostic tool for evaluating patients with various forms of vasculitis,
including PAN.
Studies focusing on biomarkers for predicting
disease activity in PAN have suggested that biomarkers such as
d-dimer, anti-moesin antibody, and anti-endothelial antibodies
are valuable markers, but none of these are currently used in routine clinical practice.

## Cutaneous Polyarteritis Nodosa

cPAN is a rare subset of PAN confined to the skin and muscles
in the absence of any systemic involvement.
Differentiation
of cPAN from systemic PAN may be challenging, especially in
the presence of peripheral neuropathy and raised CRP. In cPAN
livedo reticularis, painful subcutaneous nodules, or cutaneous
ulcerations may be present.
Rarely, petechia, purpura, and
necrosis may occur during the course of the disease. Lesions are
usually confined to extremities and then the trunk.
Steroids
are the mainstay of treatment, but their side effects limit their
longer use.
Patients may relapse when the steroid dose is
reduced; therefore disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs like
azathiopurine, hydroxychloroquine, methotrexate, mycophenolate, and tacrolimus may be preferred to maintain remission.
cPAN has a more favorable disease course than systemic PAN,
but nonspecific constitutional features still may accompany the
disease.

## Monogenic Mimic of Polyarteritis Nodosa: Deficiency of Adenosine Deaminase 2

DADA2 is an autosomal recessive disease associated with lossof-function mutations in ADA2 gene.
It is a monogenic
autoinflammatory disease presenting with necrotizing vasculitis
and displaying clinical features like livedo racemosa, neurologic
involvement, lacunar infarcts and stroke, peripheral neuropathy,
digital ischemia, cutaneous ulcerations, and target organ damage.
These patients may also have features of immune dysregulation or hematologic abnormalities.
ADA2 is a growth factor for leukocytes, is critical for endothelial stability, and has a role in immune development.
Autoantibodies, particularly antinuclear antibodies, can be
detected, and macrophages evolve to the proinflammatory

phenotype M1 rather than the M2 anti-inflammatory phenotype in patients with ADA2 deficiency.
A recent study
comparing the inflammatory markers in PAN with DADA2
has shown that DADA2 patients with PAN-like presentation
had higher concentrations of sRAGE and TNF, compared with
classical PAN patients.
After definition of DADA2 in 2014, awareness for this PANmimic disease has increased. According to the literature review
with systematic evaluation of childhood PAN and DADA2, 613
childhood PAN and 207 pediatric DADA2 were compared:
neurologic, gastrointestinal, and cardiac manifestations were frequently seen in DADA2 patients, while constitutional symptoms
and testis involvement were more common among pediatric PAN
patients.
Cutaneous findings such as livedo, purpura, ulceration,
subcutaneous nodule, necrosis, myalgia, and fatigue increased
inflammatory markers during active disease, and vasculitis is frequently seen in both PAN and DADA2.
During follow-up, the
relapse rate was higher in PAN patients, while the mortality rate
was higher among DADA2 patients. Anti-TNF drugs were the
mainstay of DADA2 treatment; however, corticosteroids and
immunosuppressive drugs were preferred in PAN.
Diagnosis
of DADA2 is made by analyzing the
ADA2
gene mutations and
measuring the levels of ADA2 enzyme.
Patients carrying biallelic mutations and patients carrying heterozygote mutations with
clinical manifestations have very low levels of ADA2 enzyme.
Asymptomatic carriers may have low levels of ADA2, so knowing the enzyme levels is pivotal when planning the treatment. An
increased risk of intracranial hemorrhage and lacunar infarction in
DADA2 patients has often been confirmed, possibly due to endothelial involvement of cerebral vessels.
Anti-TNF agents are
the first choice in the treatment of DADA2 cases.
Hematologic
signs such as anemia, neutropenia, lymphopenia, thrombocytopenia, and low immunoglobulin levels may be demonstrated,
and stem cell transplantation may be required in DADA2
patients.
In conclusion, the diagnosis and treatment of PAN continues to be a challenge for physicians. Better understanding of the
pathogenetic pathways and multiomic studies will enable us to
better manage these patients with precision medicine.
