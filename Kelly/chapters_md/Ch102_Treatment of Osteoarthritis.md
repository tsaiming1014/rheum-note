## Introduction

Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common form of arthritis, estimated to affect 528 million adults worldwide.
Updated estimates
of arthritis prevalence in the United States indicate that 91 million
adults were affected by arthritis at any site in 2015.
It is thus a
substantial public health problem, compounded by the fact that
musculoskeletal conditions are the second leading cause of years
lived with disability.
Increasing age, obesity, and knee injuries are
among the common risk factors for knee OA, the most frequent
joint affected by symptomatic OA.
Pain is the primary symptom of OA and the main contributor to
functional limitations and disability. In the early stages of disease,
the pain is primarily activity related, whereas later in the course
of disease, the pain can become more constant, and this constant
pain can be punctuated by episodes of often unpredictable pain.
To date, there are no approved treatments proven to alter structural progression. Thus pain relief remains the main focus for OA
management, in addition to maintenance of function.

## Therapeutic Pain Targets in Osteoarthritis

To develop a rational approach to pain management as a therapeutic goal, the contributions to pain in OA need to be better
understood to enable targeting appropriate pain mechanisms.
Under normal circumstances, pain is a warning signal, such as
when touching a hot stove or chest pain due to ischemia. In these
instances, pain plays a protective role, signaling to the individual
to withdraw from the threat, rest to allow tissue healing, or seek
help. Once its warning role is over, persistence or continued pain
(i.e., chronic pain) is considered maladaptive. However, unlike
other conditions, OA does not heal or resolve and thus is often
accompanied by chronic pain.
The definitive therapy for late-stage OA remains joint replacement, which is highly effective in improving both pain and function, providing evidence that structure does indeed contribute to
symptoms in OA. In further support of the important contribution of articular structures to pain in OA, a randomized trial demonstrated intra-articular lidocaine to effectively decrease knee pain

individual, patients with OA will likely need to be managed with
a variety of different approaches over the course of their disease,
often with combinations of interventions. Even after a surgical
procedure, such as joint replacement, patients often need to have
other interventions added or continued.
In addition to taking into consideration patient preferences
and medical status or comorbidities, many of the interventions

also require consideration of accessibility, physical ability, and
cost; many options may not be readily available within the context
of traditional health care centers or, on the other hand, within the
context of low- and middle-income countries. In general, when
choosing among therapies, one may give preference to starting
with treatments that offer the lowest systemic exposure or toxicity, such as exercise and local pharmacologic therapy directed
at the affected joint. Referral to appropriate providers should be
considered for individuals who may be experiencing negative psychological behaviors (e.g., depressed mood, catastrophizing, fear
avoidance) or sleep difficulties. With disease progression, individuals with OA may have greater risk of falls, which can have detrimental downstream effects, including fracture.
Also, stepped
approaches may be considered where patients are initially provided
low-cost, low-burden, and safer interventions (e.g., education and
home exercise program) and individuals who may not respond are
progressed to other interventions as needed (e.g., pharmacologic,
cognitive behavioral therapy). However, research on such stepped
approaches is still in its infancy.

### Education and Physical Interventions

All patients should be provided education related to the disease
and self-management strategies, counseled about relevant physical interventions, with weight loss and physical activity (including
structured exercise) being the most important aspects of managing all patients with lower extremity OA. Importantly, appropriate participatory language should be used to communicate about
OA and management options rather than fatalistic language to
encourage patients’ engagement in their OA care.
Patient Education and Self-Management
Patient education and self-management strategies are recommended as core intervention approaches by all clinical guidelines for management of OA. Patients can be educated using
verbal and written information to improve their understanding
of the disease, allay fears and misconceptions, and help them
make informed decisions. Patients should be encouraged to seek
information from reliable public sources on the Internet including
the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC),
the Arthritis Foundation, and the Osteoarthritis Action Alliance. This should be an ongoing process rather than something
that occurs once. It is also important to consider the language
used during patient interactions. Use of biomedical language
to explain health conditions can often instill fear, perpetuate
maladaptive behaviors, and reduce active patient involvement
in their care.
Simple changes to words toward reduced
usage of medical or precise terms, a health- rather than diseasefocused approach, and de-emphasizing structural pathology
are some strategies that could be considered during patient
encounters.
Self-management strategies should be tailored to individuals
and should focus on behavioral and lifestyle modifications that
could help the patients improve their self-efficacy and reduce
reliance on health care providers. These can include exercise and
physical activity programs, weight-management strategies, use of
assistive devices, and activity modifications. These programs can
be delivered in-person for individuals or groups, via videos and
self-management mobile health applications, or through telehealth approaches.
Meta-analyses of published studies report
moderate benefits of self-management programs for chronic musculoskeletal pain and for arthritis.
Weight Management
Obesity is both a risk factor and a consequence of lower-extremity
OA. Hence, weight management strategies for overweight and
obese patients with OA is extremely important. Being overweight
or obese increases loading over the lower-extremity joints, leading
to worsening OA; there may be a contribution of low-grade systemic inflammation related to adipose tissue to the symptomatic
experience as well. Overweight and obese patients with knee OA
should be provided personalized weight management strategies. In
general, strategies that include both diet and exercise components
to ensure that lean muscle mass is maintained and prevention of
sarcopenia are more effective than diet alone.
Ideally such interventions should be delivered using a team approach that includes
nutritional counseling, an exercise program, and behavioral strategies. As for any chronic condition in which obesity is an important factor, achieving weight loss is challenging, and therefore a
multimodal approach with professional support should be considered. Some patients may even require bariatric surgery. Significant
improvements in pain and function have been reported with both
diet- and exercised-based weight-loss interventions and bariatric
surgery for knee OA.
There is a significant dose–response relationship between the magnitude of weight loss and the observed
improvements in OA-related outcomes.
While any amount of
weight loss is likely to provide benefits, weight loss of 20% or
more provides the most benefits.
The 2019 American College of
Rheumatology (ACR) OA guidelines strongly recommend weight
loss for those who are obese or overweight.
Physical Activity
Physical activity refers to any bodily movement produced by
skeletal muscles that requires energy expenditure. Physical activity includes exercise as well as other activities that involve bodily
movement and that are done as part of playing, working, active
transportation, house chores, and recreational activities (
Fig.
102.3
). The 2019 ACR OA guidelines strongly recommend exercise and physical activity.
Over 50% of men and up to 80% of
women with knee OA fail to meet physical activity guidelines.
Reduced walking activity could increase the risk of cardiovascular
disease, depression, and early mortality in these patients.
Hence,
behavioral strategies for sustained improvements in physical activity are essential for patients with OA. Greater physical activity is
associated with decreased pain and improved physical function
in adults with hip or knee OA. Some patients may have a concern that increasing activity might make their OA worse; however,
currently there is no evidence to suggest that increasing physical
activity contributes to disease worsening. Furthermore, the overall benefits of increased physical activity far outweigh any potential risks. Any symptomatic exacerbation is typically short lived,
with symptomatic improvement achieved over the longer term.
Fatigue in OA can also be an issue, which may require counseling
regarding activity pacing. Thus patients should be encouraged to
engage and maintain engagement in physical activity as there is
no risk of worsening their OA, and over the long term, they will
have improved pain and function. As mentioned earlier, use of
participatory language that focuses on what the individuals can
do versus what they cannot do may encourage healthier behaviors.

Exercise
Closely linked to physical activity is exercise, which is planned,
structured, repetitive, and purposeful in the sense that the improvement or maintenance of one or more components of physical fitness is the objective. Exercises for OA can include strengthening
or power, aerobic, neuromuscular, or range-of-motion and flexibility programs delivered on land or in aquatic environments
(
Fig. 102.3
). In general, exercise programs are recommended as
a core and first-line intervention for OA and have shown efficacy
similar or better than pharmacologic therapies without the risk of
adverse effects.
Patients do best when provided with specific recommendations rather than simply being told to exercise. To date,
no single type of exercise has been shown to be more beneficial for
OA than other types, and thus the 2019 ACR OA guidelines do
not recommend one type of exercise over another.
Thus, providers should counsel every patient about various types of exercise
options and provide them with an exercise program prescription
that is tailored to their preferences, abilities, and comorbidities.
Physical therapists are best suited to prescribe tailored exercise
programs, and referral to physical therapy should be considered
as part of the core treatment plan for patients with osteoarthritis.
The benefits of exercise are sustained as long as the patients continue to adhere to the intervention and can extend beyond OA to

Strength training
refers to exercise programs that focus on
improving the force generation or load-carrying ability of individual muscles or muscle groups. Muscle weakness is a ubiquitous
finding in patients with OA. Muscle weakness can be multifactorial, resulting directly from muscle involvement in OA pathogenesis (e.g., fatty infiltration of the muscle, inflammation) and
indirectly from reduced activity, injury, or muscle inhibition due
to pain. Addressing reductions in muscle function, i.e., the ability
of the muscles to generate force and power for daily activities, is
extremely important in patients with OA. Strength gains can be
achieved by individuals irrespective of their age. These exercises
include high-resistance, low-repetition protocols with progressive increase in resistance over a period of time. Resistance can
be provided by free weights, elastic resistance bands, or resistance
machines. For patients with OA, strengthening programs should
include both functional movements that target multiple muscle
groups (e.g., stair or rowing exercises and movements that specifically target muscle groups around the affected joint and those
immediately proximal and distal such as knee extensors and flexors,
hip abductors, and calf muscles in people with knee OA). Progressive resistance-based strength training programs show significant
improvements in pain and function for patients with OA.
For
hand OA, occupational therapists are best suited to provide specific hand exercises based upon the joints involved. Care should be
taken to tailor the programs to individual’s abilities, and the interventions should be delivered by expert professionals (e.g., physical
therapists, strength and conditioning specialists, etc.).

Neuromuscular training
refers to exercise programs that are
designed to improve control of functional movements. These
exercises rely on perturbing the ability of the body to maintain
a position or perform a movement to train improved control.
Patients with OA often exhibit alterations in neuromuscular control including reduced joint proprioception, reduced control of
muscle activity during submaximal contractions, and abnormal
movement and muscle activation patterns during daily activities
like walking. The exercises commonly included in neuromuscular
training protocols are balance and stability exercises, movement

Therapies to Modulate Joint Loading
Patients with lower extremity OA, particularly knee OA, exhibit
movement patterns that cause abnormal or increased loading
over the joint during weight-bearing activities like walking. An
example of an abnormal walking patterns that increases loading
over the medial tibiofemoral compartment is called varus thrust.
Patients who walk with a varus thrust have a transient lateral shift
of the knee when they take a step. Being overweight or obese also
increases the loading over the joints. Thus weight loss is recommended as an intervention strategy for these patients. To counter abnormal joint loading due to movement patterns, there are
a number of intervention approaches that have been investigated
or are being developed. These include gait-retraining strategies,
orthotics (lateral heel wedge insoles, knee braces), aids/assistive
devices (e.g., canes), and different types of footwear. For patients
with predominantly patellofemoral OA, patellofemoral taping is
commonly used in physical therapy clinics to improve the biomechanical function of the joint; patellofemoral bracing may also
be beneficial. However, studies to date have used different techniques, making it difficult to discern true efficacy and clouding
which approach to recommend.
Gait retraining
refers to behavioral strategies to induce changes
in walking patterns in an attempt to reduce loading over the medial
tibiofemoral joint. Examples of gait-retraining strategies include
walking with increased toe-out, walking with increased toe-in,
and biofeedback to reduce knee loading in real time. While these
intervention strategies appear to reduce knee loading and may
have beneficial effects on pain,
widespread implementation

has been hampered by technological challenges and patient compliance. Most previous studies were conducted using expensive
gait laboratories that require individual patients to make multiple
visits to learn the new gait patterns. This approach is not scalable for wider dissemination. Furthermore, implementing the gait
modification in free-living conditions requires use of technology
that could monitor specific attributes of gait patterns (e.g., degree
of toe-out) and provide feedback to the patient on when and how
much to change their gait. While there have been a few promising
advances in this area, more work is needed before these techniques
are widely available and recommended.
Lateral wedge insoles
are orthotics that can be worn inside the
shoe to offload the medial tibiofemoral compartment during
walking for patients with predominantly medial knee OA. These
insoles include a heel wedge that is higher on the lateral side in an
attempt to realign the leg by moving the lower extremity closer
to the midline of the body. Similarly, medial wedge insoles could
be used for patients with lateral knee OA. These insoles offer a
potentially attractive option since they are extremely inexpensive and widely available. While lateral wedge insoles can lead to
small reductions in medial knee loading,
their effects on pain
and function have not been encouraging in patients with medial
knee OA.
However, some recent work has suggested that when
patients are screened for an initial response of reduced knee loading to lateral wedges, small but significant improvements in pain
can be attained.
Strategies to identify patients who show a
response, i.e., a reduction in knee loading, need to be developed
that do not require expensive and infeasible gait laboratory assessments. Results have been more promising with the use of medial
wedge insoles for patients with lateral knee OA, but there also
have been far fewer studies assessing the efficacy of medial wedge
insoles.
If these insoles are prescribed, it is important to ensure
that the functional foot anatomy and any foot symptoms be taken
into consideration and appropriate adjustments included. For
example, patients who present with pronated feet should be provided an insole with medial arch support.
However, long-term
compliance with these devices is unclear. As such, the 2019 ACR
OA guidelines conditionally recommended against lateral wedge
insoles.
Footwear that could reduce knee loading has also been of
significant interest for people with knee OA. Such footwear can
include minimal shoes (flat, flexible, nonheeled), variable stiffness
shoes (greater lateral stiffness than medial or a combination of
lateral wedge and variable stiffness), unstable shoes, and biomechanical shoes (e.g., Apos Therapy, Apos US Management Inc,
USA). Overall, the minimal shoes, variable stiffness shoes, and
unstable shoes do not appear to reduce pain or improve function
in people with knee OA.
The biomechanical shoe combines a
shoe with semicircular pods on the sole to modulate knee loading
and induce neuromuscular training and thus reduces knee loading
and pain, but further research is needed before making clinical
recommendations.
While the biomechanical shoe system can
be relatively expensive, some of the other shoes, including minimal shoes and variable stiffness shoes, are commercially available
at a fairly low cost. Hence, patients interested in trying such footwear should not be discouraged from doing so, although specific
recommendations are difficult to make at present due to the paucity of data. Because of the lack of high-quality data, the 2019
ACR OA guidelines conditionally recommend against modified
shoes.
Offloading knee braces
are devices that are worn over the knee
joint in an attempt to offload the medial knee compartment.
These braces attempt to realign the leg by applying pressure proximal and distal to the knee and at the knee. While offloading braces
are effective at reducing medial knee loading compared with no
brace, it is not clear whether they are effective compared with a
regular brace.
The changes in knee loading inconsistently translate to improvements in pain or function, with most studies being
small with concerns for bias.
Because they can be bulky and
may be difficult to wear under trousers and/or are visible if wearing a skirt/dress, most patients do not prefer to use these devices
long-term, leading to poor adherence and limiting their utility.
Minor adverse events including slipping or skin irritation and
blisters are possible. Hence, offloading knee braces could be an
adjunctive therapy for patients who predominantly have medial
knee OA and are willing to wear the devices. Patellofemoral braces
are also available, and one trial has demonstrated a reduction in
bone marrow lesion volume and pain over a short 6-week period,
providing proof of concept of efficacy for patellofemoral OA bracing as well.
The 2019 ACR OA guidelines strongly recommend
tibiofemoral bracing and conditionally recommends patellofemoral bracing.
A walking cane used by the hand contralateral to the lower
extremity joint with painful OA (e.g., holding in right hand for
left knee or hip OA,

people with advanced knee OA. Their use in individuals who are

One unblinded clinical trial reported improved pain with the use
of cane for 2 months in people with knee OA.
The risks and
costs of cane use are minimal and therefore can be reasonably

Manual Therapy
Manual therapy includes a wide variety of techniques in which clinicians or patients use manual contact for a desired outcome like
pain relief, regaining movement, or releasing adhesions. Examples
of manual therapy techniques include joint manipulation and
mobilization, muscle stretching, and soft tissue massage. These
should be delivered by experienced professionals (e.g., physical
therapists, osteopaths, chiropractors). However, the benefits of
these therapies when used in isolation are unclear. Recent evidence
also raises questions about their utility when provided in combination with exercise over exercise alone.

Physical and Electrotherapeutic Modalities
The phrase
physical agents and electrotherapeutic modalities
refers
to application of therapeutic heat (moist heat) or cold, electrical stimulation (e.g., transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation
[TENS] and neuromuscular electrical stimulation [NMES]),
radiofrequency ablation, or therapeutic ultrasound. These agents

Nerve ablation has recently been of significant clinical interest. It refers to focused application of a small current of thermal energy that generates heat (thermal radiofrequency ablation;
cooled radiofrequency ablation, which includes a cooling component; or pulsed radiofrequency ablation) or cold (cryoneurolysis)
to sensory nerves to impair their ability to transmit nociceptive
signals. Evidence suggests that radiofrequency ablation may be
effective at reducing pain and improving function, and cooled
procedures may yield better outcomes than conventional or
pulsed modalities with benefits persisting for up to 2 years.
In clinical practice, it is common to use a prognostic nerve
block prior to ablation; however, the utility of the nerve block is
unclear in OA.
The superomedial, superolateral, and inferomedial branches of the genicular nerve are commonly targeted in
radiofrequency ablation therapies for knee OA. Cryoneurolysis
refers to percutaneous application of cold to peripheral nerves to
disrupt nerve conduction. For knee OA, the infrapatellar branch
of the saphenous nerve has been targeted.
In a double-blind,
sham-controlled trial, cryoneurolysis resulted in significant
improvements in pain that lasted up to 5 months. Adverse events
with these therapies seem to be infrequent and the U.S. Food
and Drug Administration (FDA) has recently approved a cooled
radiofrequency ablation device (Coolief®, Avanos Medical, USA)
and a cryoneurolysis device (Iovera®, Myoscience, USA) for use
in patients with OA. The 2019 ACR OA guidelines conditionally recommended radiofrequency ablation due to heterogeneity
of techniques and the lack of long-term safety data at that time.
While more studies have been published since then with promising longer-term efficacy and safety results, there is still substantial
heterogeneity in techniques and comparators.

### Psychological Interventions and Complementary and Integrative Therapies

Individuals with painful osteoarthritis may have comorbid
depressive symptoms, sleep problems, and other psychological
impairments like reduced self-efficacy, poor coping skills, pain
catastrophizing, and fear-avoidance behaviors that may exacerbate
the pain experience. These comorbidities can increase reliance
on analgesics, interfere with adherence to treatment programs,
and reduce efficacy of treatments. Interventions to address these
impairments should be considered for relevant patients.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is a behavioral therapy intervention delivered by psychologists or other trained clinicians and is
recommended as a first-line treatment for individuals with depressive symptoms. CBT is usually delivered via in-person sessions,
but recently a number of internet- and/or telephone-based CBT
interventions have been developed for many chronic conditions.
For people with knee OA, CBT by itself is not any better than
usual care for pain management but could help with managing
depressive symptoms and sleep
and is helpful in other pain
conditions. Hence, CBT could be considered as an adjunctive
therapy for individuals with knee OA who present with depressive
symptoms and/or sleep disturbances.
Pain Coping Skills Training
Pain coping skills training
refers to behavioral interventions that
target catastrophizing, maladaptive coping strategies, etc., in
patients with OA. Pain coping skills training alone or in combination with CBT can lead to improved self-management of pain in
people with osteoarthritis.
Such interventions can be provided by trained physical therapists, or patients can be referred to
online programs.
Complementary and Integrative Therapies
Complementary and integrative therapies include a variety
of approaches that are not considered part of Western medical practice and that could be used together with conventional
approaches. Integrative therapies refer to using conventional and
complementary approaches in a coordinated patient-focused way
while addressing mental, emotional, functional, spiritual, social,
and community aspects. Integrative health care approaches are
growing, for example with the Veteran’s Affairs (VA) health care
system pioneering work in this area for chronic pain management
in military and veteran personnel, as well as other health care systems providing integrative medicine services within the medical
clinic setting.
Mind–body practices
refers to a variety of complementary and
alternative approaches delivered by a trained clinician or teacher
and can include yoga, meditation, acupuncture, tai chi, relaxation
techniques, etc. While acupuncture has been gaining popularity,
the efficacy of acupuncture for pain management in OA remains
unclear. Studies comparing acupuncture with usual care report
improved pain and function,
but compared with sham acupuncture, the benefits are not consistently evident.
There
are also different protocols for “sham” acupuncture, including
penetrating versus nonpenetrating needles or non-needle sham
and use of “true” acupuncture points versus other nonacupuncture points; generally, penetrating-needle sham-controlled studies
have smaller effect sizes (e.g., 0.16) than nonpenetrating-needle
sham-controlled studies (e.g., 0.50).
It is also a possibility that
the clinician’s optimism and communication about positive outcomes could influence patient’s perception of pain.
While the
evidence supporting acupuncture for OA is unclear, considering
the relative safety of the intervention, it could be considered as
adjunctive therapy for patients who have positive beliefs about
acupuncture and is conditionally recommended by the 2019 ACR
OA guidelines.
Tai chi is a Chinese mind–body practice that integrates meditation with breathing, relaxation, and slow, gentle movements. Tai
chi improves pain and physical function in people with knee OA,
including in a randomized controlled trial.
As such, the
2019 ACR OA guidelines strongly recommend tai chi for knee
and hip OA.
Yoga is an Indian mind–body practice that incorporates meditation, breathing, relaxation, and exercise. While
there have not been many studies on yoga for OA, current research
suggests beneficial effects with improvements in pain and physical
function.
Therefore for patients who are interested in tai chi
or yoga and have access to an instructor, tai chi or yoga should be
offered as a treatment intervention for people with OA, especially
knee OA. However, yoga is likely not advisable for individuals
with hip OA due to concerns about certain hip abduction movements that may be painful and/or harmful; the 2019 ACR OA
guidelines conditionally recommend yoga for knee OA.
To date,
there are insufficient data to specifically recommend meditation
for OA-related symptoms. However, mindfulness-based stress
reduction (MBSR) has been demonstrated to be efficacious for
chronic low back pain and is recommended by the American College of Physicians for management of chronic low back pain.
It
is likely the MBSR would have similar symptom benefits in OA,
and given the lack of adverse effects and low cost, with several
free smartphone apps, it is reasonable to recommend MBSR as an
adjunct for patients’ pain management strategies.

### Pharmacologic Management: Knee, Hip, Hand

Many patients will have tried acetaminophen and/or over-thecounter nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory therapy by the time they
seek medical attention. Before escalating pharmacologic therapy,
the first step should be to optimize patients’ use of the physical modalities listed above and consider adjunctive mind–body
approaches. Setting expectations is also important. While most
patients expect and want a medication, they need to be counseled that much of OA symptom management requires their
active engagement and participation through weight loss (where
indicated) and physical activity. Further, a goal of “zero pain” is
not realistic; setting reasonable expectations about “pain management” as focusing on making “pain manageable” so that they can
carry out their activities with minimal limitations or restrictions
from their symptoms is necessary to avoid disappointment.
Decisions regarding pharmacologic therapy should be guided
by the severity and frequency of symptoms (

intermittent symptoms may be managed by the patient with asneeded analgesics. A single affected joint may be best managed by
local therapy, such as topical therapy or intra-articular injection.
More frequent and limiting pain may require multiple therapeutic
options, including physical modalities and systemic as well as local
therapies. When reasonable use of the various available and appropriate options have been tried without adequate symptom control,
it is reasonable to consider referral to an orthopedic surgeon for
consideration of joint replacement surgery.

Topical NSAIDs likely produce their anti-inflammatory and

NSAIDs in topical

small.

oral formulations. In meta-analyses of placebo-controlled trials,
the diclofenac patch was found to have the greatest efficacy for
pain and piroxicam gel for function.

been reported, although the anemia and renal and hepatic abnormalities noted with oral NSAIDs are much less common with
topical NSAIDs use.
On balance, the adverse effects associated

and therefore topical NSAIDs may be considered the first line
of treatment for knee and hand OA pain, particularly in elderly
patients due to their safety and efficacy profile. Topical NSAIDs
are strongly recommended for knee OA by the 2019 ACR OA
guidelines.

afferent pain fibers. This results in a period of enhanced neurostimulation (irritation, burning, vascular dilation) followed by
decreased sensitivity due to exhaustion of substance P with regular
use. Topical capsaicin thus provides analgesia through its effect
on peripheral nerves around the joint tissue. Topical capsaicin has
shown efficacy for a variety of painful conditions such as postherpetic pain, diabetic neuropathy, and musculoskeletal conditions,
but only a few trials have been conducted in OA. Two older metaanalyses of trials of capsaicin versus placebo demonstrated pain

Lidocaine.
Inflammatory joint tissue is hypothesized to induce
increased afferent input from the surrounding peripheral nerves.
Topical anesthetics are used to block sodium channels in sensory
nerves surrounding the affected tissue. A 2-week open label study
demonstrated significant improvement in pain and quality of life
in OA patients who had suboptimal pain control with other analgesics.
Post-hoc analysis of a trial of lidocaine 5% patch versus
celecoxib 200 mg/day showed no difference in knee OA pain or
skin reactions.
Thus there are insufficient data to support the

celecoxib noted in that trial may suggest some potential for pain
relief in OA.
In summary, topical agents are well tolerated and can be considered throughout the course of management for knee and hand

guidelines provide a strong recommendation for topical NSAIDs
for knee OA (conditional for hand OA) and conditional recommendations for capsaicin in knee OA while they conditionally recommend against capsaicin for hand OA.

Intra-articular Agents
Certain formulations of pharmacologic agents can be delivered
directly into the joint. This option is particularly attractive when a
single joint is affected. There are data to support greater accuracy
using ultrasound or other image guidance with joint injections.
However, for the most part, hand and knee joints can be injected
in the clinic without image guidance. The hip, being a deep joint,
is recommended to be injected under image guidance. The agents
that are injected intra-articularly for OA include glucocorticoids
and viscosupplementation, although there is controversy about
efficacy for the latter class of agents. Platelet-rich plasma, prolotherapy, mesenchymal stem cell therapy, and botulinum toxin
have inadequate high quality data to support their use.
Glucocorticoids.
Synovitis is a key pathologic entity in OA,
and there is evidence implicating it in OA progression.
Evidence from a Cochrane review of randomized or quasirandomized
controlled trials of intra-articular steroid versus sham or no treatment suggested that pain relief from steroid injections was short
term, i.e., moderate effect 1 to 2 weeks after end of treatment,
small to moderate effect at 4 to 6 weeks, small effect at 13 weeks,
and no clear evidence of an effect at 26 weeks.
There is also evidence to support small improvement in function in OA patients
receiving intra-articular corticosteroid therapy. While there has
been some thought that joints with effusions, indicative of inflammation, may benefit more from intra-articular glucocorticoid
injections, a randomized trial of triamcinolone versus saline intraarticular injections every 12 weeks over 2 years in a sample of individuals with knee OA who had evidence of effusion or synovitis
on ultrasound demonstrated no difference in pain relief.
Thus,
this study has called into question the theory that knees with signs
of inflammation benefit from steroid injections. A longer-acting
formulation, triamcinolone acetonide extended release, has been
approved by the FDA, although pain efficacy is not clearly of longer duration than conventional triamcinolone acetonide based on
the primary endpoint of average daily pain.
Nonetheless, this
formulation has less systemic blood glucose effects.
Intra-articular glucocorticoids are generally considered safe. A
Cochrane review found that those receiving intra-articular glucocorticoids were 27% less likely to experience any serious adverse
event than other treatment arms, although results did not reach
statistical significance.
In the randomized trial of triamcinolone
injections versus saline injections cited above, a nonsignificant
greater risk of cartilage loss at 2 years in the triamcinolone group
compared with the saline group.
However, the clinical significance of the small differential change in cartilage over 2 years is
not clear; indeed, the between-group difference of −0.11 mm over
2 years was less than the size of a voxel (0.3 × 0.3 × 1 mm). The
2019 ACR OA guidelines strongly recommended the use of intraarticular glucocorticoids for hip and knee OA, as did the 2019
Osteoarthritis Research Society International (OARSI) guidelines.
However, since then, controversy has arisen regarding
the potential for rapidly progressive OA related to intra-articular
glucocorticoid injections from a case series, although interpretation is difficult due to the observational nature of the report.
Data from a large observational cohort also suggested increased
risk of OA progression with intra-articular steroid use compared
with no use; however, this association was not seen in another
study using intra-articular hyaluronic acid as a comparator to better account for confounding by indication.
Rare adverse
effects have also been described.
Viscosupplementation.
Hyaluronic acid is a polysaccharide
present in synovial fluid that, together with other molecules, aids
joint lubrication. In OA, the concentration of hyaluronic acid is
decreased due to depolymerization and increased clearance from
the joint. Various hyaluronic acid preparations, known as viscosupplements, have been synthesized and injected intra-articularly
to replete hyaluronic acid in the synovial fluid to improve lubrication, mainly for knee OA. The preponderance of data from published and unpublished trials of intra-articular viscosupplements
overall have demonstrated either no efficacy or small and clinically
irrelevant effects on pain and no effect on function in OA, and
meta-analyses raise concerns about publication bias.
There
is stronger evidence regarding the lack of benefit in hip OA. Several society guidelines including the ACR, the American Academy
of Orthopedic Surgeons (AAOS), and OARSI recommend against
their use, either strongly or conditionally, or have weak recommendations for their use.

For prolotherapy, only a limited number of small studies with variable regimens have been conducted

Due to the heterogeneity of study

inadequate long-term data, ACR and OARSI both recommend
against the use of these agents.
In summary, intra-articular glucocorticoids remain the mainstay as the preferred therapy for local intra-articular joint injections. While the use of hyaluronic acid is not recommended by
most society guidelines, clinicians may attempt a trial if other
approaches, including intra-articular glucocorticoids, have failed,
particularly as a trial to attempt to delay joint replacement surgery
or provide some alternative symptom management while awaiting
joint replacement surgery.

Systemic Therapies
The vast majority of patients with OA will have tried over-thecounter oral medications before seeking medical attention for
OA-related pain. The mainstays of oral therapy remain limited,
with only minimal or moderate efficacy at best and often complicated by adverse events and/or contraindications. By the nature of
their systemic exposure, therapies given orally or parenterally have
greater concerns for adverse effects. A general guiding principle is
to tailor the dose and frequency to the degree of symptoms, ideally choosing the lowest dose and shortest duration of therapy as
possible, including use on an as-needed basis if appropriate rather
than chronically.
Acetaminophen (paracetamol) is considered a weak analgesic through weak prostaglandin inhibition, with greater COX-2
inhibition than COX-1.
It has traditionally been considered “first-line” therapy for pharmacologic OA management
but is no longer considered as such. A meta-analysis of trials of

acetaminophen showed a only small benefit for improving OA
pain compared with placebo and less efficacy than oral NSAIDs.
A 2017 network meta-analysis demonstrated that acetaminophen
had a nearly null effect on pain symptoms at various doses, with
none of the doses reaching a minimum clinically important difference (effect size of −0.18, corresponding to a 4.5-mm difference
on a 100-mm visual analogue scale).
Thus, from an efficacy
standpoint, acetaminophen is no longer considered appropriate
as a single agent for managing OA pain. Despite its lower efficacy,
some patients do use acetaminophen regularly or on a short-term
basis, often due to contraindications for NSAID use. For regular
users of acetaminophen, a total daily dose of 3 grams and under is
considered the maximal safe dose, although hepatotoxicity needs
to be monitored, particularly in older adults. For patients using
long-term acetaminophen, monitoring should also be performed
for gastrointestinal toxicity. Gastrointestinal blood loss/perforation can be associated with regular use of acetaminophen at doses
greater than 2 gm/day.
The ongoing use of acetaminophen
despite limited efficacy reflects in large part the lack of sufficient
effective and safe alternatives.
Oral NSAIDs, Including COX-2 Inhibitors.
Oral NSAIDs are
the most commonly used and prescribed pharmacologic treatments for OA pain. Both selective and nonselective NSAIDs have
shown moderate efficacy in improving pain in OA in randomized
trials compared with acetaminophen. Risk stratification is needed
for initiating and maintaining treatment with oral NSAIDs due
to a multitude of adverse effects associated with them. Lower
doses are considered safer and better tolerated, but higher doses
are more efficacious. Thus the dose should be individualized for a
particular patient based on their risk profile. While on NSAIDs,
patients should be monitored for adverse effects, such as gastrointestinal bleed, renal insufficiency, and cardiovascular toxicity. In a
large randomized trial of patients with OA or rheumatoid arthritis
who had moderate or high cardiovascular risk (PRECISION), all
of whom were on proton pump inhibitors, approximately 4% to
5% experienced a major NSAID toxicity (major adverse cardiovascular event, important gastrointestinal events, renal events, and
all-cause mortality) over 1 to 2 years,
highlighting the common
occurrence of these adverse events. As much as is feasible, oral
NSAIDs should be used for as short a time as possible and on an
as-needed basis rather than long-term regular use.
Opioids (Tramadol, Nontramadol).
Opioids provide analgesia
through their action on M (predominantly), D, or K receptors
in the central and peripheral nervous system. Since the primary
presentation of OA is pain, opioids have been evaluated for the
management of OA pain. However, neither traditional (nontramadol) opioids nor tramadol have shown benefit for OA pain,
and there is only a small benefit in pain and physical function
demonstrated for the management of noncancer pain.
The
opioid epidemic in the United States and the rise in drug overdoses highlights the addiction potential of opioids. Osteoarthritis is among the most common conditions for which opioids are
prescribed in the United States for people with obesity, which is
a major risk factor for knee OA.
There are also many other
adverse consequences of nontramadol opioids and tramadol, such
as sedation, increased fall risk, constipation, respiratory depression, altered cognitive status, etc. Overall, opioids should not play
a role in the routine management of OA, but their limited use in
certain circumstances may be appropriate when other approaches
are ineffective or contraindicated. For example, for patients in
whom NSAIDs are contraindicated, not tolerated, or ineffective
or for patients in whom other approaches are ineffective, tramadol
may be an option to consider. Use of nontramadol opioids would
be less advisable due to even higher risk of adverse events. Use of
the lowest possible dose with the shortest duration possible should
be implemented if tramadol (or nontramadol opioids) are being
considered.
Duloxetine and Other Centrally Acting Agents.
Duloxetine
is a serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI)
with central effects that is purported to target central pain processing. A meta-analysis of three trials demonstrated improvement of pain and function in OA.
Since that meta-analysis,
two randomized trials of duloxetine for hip or knee OA have had
conflicting results. In an open-label, cluster randomized trial of
duloxetine delivered in a primary care setting, there was no difference compared with placebo on the primary outcome of pain at 3
months.
However in a pragmatic, open-label randomized trial,
duloxetine had significantly better change in pain scores compared with placebo.
Nonetheless, for patients with widespread
pain and/or pain hypersensitivity, duloxetine may be considered.
A cost-effectiveness analysis demonstrated the benefit for adding duloxetine to usual care for knee OA, even in the absence
of depressive symptoms.
The 2019 ACR OA guidelines conditionally recommend the use of duloxetine. Adverse effects
are more common in duloxetine users compared with placebo,
including nausea, constipation, dry mouth, diarrhea, fatigue,
dizziness, somnolence, and insomnia. Because of tolerability and
side effect issues, the use of duloxetine may be limited. Other
centrally acting agents, such as gabapentin, pregabalin, or other
anti-depressants (other SNRIs, tricyclic antidepressants) have not
been directly studied for symptoms of OA.

Supplements/Nutraceuticals
Use of dietary supplements in the United States is on the rise.
Approximately three-quarters of adults consumed dietary supplements in 2017. A number of supplements or nutraceuticals are
purported to have benefits for OA, with controversies regarding
actual efficacy, as will be outlined below. Unlike pharmacologic
agents that are FDA-approved, supplements or nutraceuticals do
not have the same level of oversight, with wide variation in the
quantity and true ingredients present in a product.
Glucosamine and Chondroitin.
Both glucosamine and chondroitin are glycosaminoglycans and constituents of the macromolecule
aggrecan in cartilage. These molecules contribute to the healthy functioning of cartilage. However, it is not clear whether ingested glucosamine or chondroitin are actually incorporated into cartilage. There
has been much controversy in the literature about the efficacy of these
compounds, with some differentiation between glucosamine hydrochloride versus glucosamine sulfate. However, the dissociation of glucosamine from its salt when digested would argue against biologically
meaningful differences between the two salts.
The National Institutes of Health (NIH)–funded Glucosamine/Chondroitin Arthritis
Intervention Trial (GAIT) study demonstrated no efficacy of glucosamine hydrochloride or chondroitin sulfate, alone or in combination,
compared with placebo.
However, in a prespecified subgroup of
those with moderate to severe pain, the combination of glucosamine
and chondroitin did have a higher rate of response than placebo. Further, meta-analyses have highlighted concerns regarding publication
bias and efficacy only being noted in industry-sponsored trials, with
no efficacy demonstrated in non–industry-sponsored trials (e.g., the
NIH-funded GAIT trial).
Treatment guidelines do not recommend these agents.
Omega-3 Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids.
The anti-inflammatory
properties of fish oil, thought to be largely related to omega-3
polyunsaturated fatty acids, have been studied in relation to various types of arthritis, without clear evidence of efficacy to date.
In a randomized trial of high-dose fish oil versus low-dose fish oil
(a blend of fish oil with sunola oil), there was an unanticipated
greater improvement in pain and function in the comparator lowdose fish oil arm than the intended treatment arm of high-dose
fish oil; why this may be is not clear.
More recently, a randomized trial of krill oil, which contains different concentrations of
anti-inflammatory long-chain omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty
acids than fish oil, demonstrated benefits in pain improvement
but not structural benefit.
At present, there are insufficient
data to recommend fish oil or omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acid
supplements for OA.
Curcuminoid Formulations.
Curcuma longa
extract has antiinflammatory properties and has been investigated in multiple
small trials for use in OA. In a 12-week, placebo-controlled trial
for knee OA,
C. longa
extract showed benefits for pain but not
structure.
Currently OARSI provides a conditional recommendation for its use.

### Surgical Management of OA

When physical modalities, pharmacologic approaches, and mind–
body approaches in various combinations are insufficient for
managing OA symptoms, patients and providers often consider
surgical options. Arthroscopic meniscal surgeries are the most
common orthopedic procedure, estimated to number 700,000/
year in the United States alone. Similarly, there are an estimated
790,000 knee arthroplasties and an estimated 450,000 hip
arthroplasties performed annually in the United States, and these
numbers are projected to rise to 3.5 million and almost 600,000,
respectively, by 2030.
Arthroscopic Procedures
Patients with knee OA often have meniscal lesions evident on
MRI. The contributions of such meniscal lesions to OA symptoms is unclear. In a large study from the Framingham cohort,
meniscal lesions were just as likely to be present in knees with OA
as they were in knees without OA and in those with knee pain
versus without, suggesting that many meniscal lesions are incidental findings and are therefore likely reflective of the underlying
OA disease process affecting numerous articular tissues.
Several
randomized trials have been conducted to date demonstrating that
arthroscopic partial meniscectomies offer no better symptom or
function improvement than usual care or physical therapy, and
in fact, no better than sham surgery.
This was true even
among knees that have mechanical symptoms such as locking or
buckling, symptoms that are traditionally thought to be appropriate for arthroscopic surgery.
Thus, exercise therapy (physical
therapy) and arthroscopic partial meniscectomy appear to offer
similar benefits over the long-term (5-year follow-up in the large
randomized trials),
while there are also the risks of surgery
to consider for arthroscopic procedures. Further, there are some
concerns about the potential for greater OA progression and even
risk of joint replacement from arthroscopic partial meniscectomy
in longer-term follow-up from two trials to date.
Thus,

physical therapy and analgesics as a first-line approach appear to
be a reasonable initial strategy, with the decision to undergo subsequent arthroscopic partial meniscectomy to be undertaken in a
shared decision-making process with full consideration of balancing risks and benefits, as well as patient preferences. There is a lack
of sufficient data regarding hip arthroscopic procedures.
Joint Arthroplasty
Knee and hip arthroplasties are considered definitive treatments
for people with severe OA and difficult-to-manage symptoms
as they are generally efficacious for pain relief and functional
improvement. Clinically, hip arthroplasties have generally better
overall outcomes than knee arthroplasties. At the knee, the joint
replacement can be “total,” meaning that all joint components are
replacement, or “unicompartmental” (e.g., medial compartment
only). Meta-analyses report that 82% of total knee replacements
and 58% of total hip replacements last 25 years.
While these
surgeries have been clinically recognized to improve pain and function, a randomized trial of individuals with moderate-to-severe
knee OA who were eligible for knee arthroplasty demonstrated
that knee arthroplasty followed by nonsurgical care (i.e., exercise,
education, orthotics, and analgesics) confirmed that knee arthroplasty results in meaningful symptom improvement compared
with nonsurgical care alone.
The number needed to treat was
∼
6, supporting the broad benefit of arthroplasty, although with a
higher number of adverse events than conservative management.
There are also well documented racial and ethnic disparities with
patients from minoritized groups demonstrating lower utilization
and worse outcomes with total joint replacement surgeries.
Nonetheless, despite the overall beneficial outcomes of knee
arthroplasty, approximately 20% to 30% of individuals have persistent pain after knee replacement. Risk factors for poor pain
outcomes are not fully understood but include pain due to factors other than knee OA and greater opioid use prior to joint
replacement. There is variability among various providers regarding which patients should be referred for consideration of arthroplasty and at what point in the disease course. The recommended

Other joints affected by OA can also be considered for arthroplasty, including the shoulder and the ankle, and other surgical

such procedures have not been studied to the same degree as they

used for ankle OA, and recently longitudinal data for knee OA
were reported.

## Experimental Osteoarthritis Therapies Under Development

Despite decades of research and drug development, there remains
a paucity of therapies available for managing OA symptoms and
to prevent disease progression. There are several therapies currently in various phases of development that show promise for
OA. Nerve growth factor binds TrkA and p75
receptors. TrkA
and p75 are both involved in neuronal differentiation and survival during development, and TrkA is also involved in peripheral
and central pain mechanisms including pain sensitization through
its activity on various ion channels, receptors, and signaling molecules. Despite promising efficacy data,
due to a rare adverse
event of rapidly progressive OA for which risk mitigation strategies in trials have not been adequately successful, the development
of this agent has been halted by most sponsors at this time.
The ion channel TRPV1 is another OA pain target located in
sensory neurons and activated by capsaicin. A synthetic form of
transcapsaicin is currently being tested as an intra-articular injection. TRPV1 antagonists are also being tested. In addition to the
biologic TNF inhibitors and IL-1 antagonists mentioned above,
other inflammatory mediators are also being tested for OA endpoints, including granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating
factor (GM-CSF), IL-10, interferon (IFN)-
β
, NFκ
B, p38 mitogen-activated protein (MAP) kinase, and vascular adhesion protein-1, among others.
Structural modification has been a major unmet need in OA. A
recombinant form of fibroblast growth factor (FGF)-18 (sprifermin) has shown some promising results of increased cartilage thickness following intra-articular injection with benefit maintained

over 5-year follow-up, although symptom benefit did not occur
concurrently with the structural improvement, and the degree of
cartilage thickness attained is of unclear significance.
A posthoc analysis of a high risk subgroup did suggest symptom as well as
structural benefit.
A Wnt signaling inhibitor, lorecivivint, and a
cathepsin K inhibitor both demonstrated structural but not symptomatic improvement in phase 2, placebo-controlled trials.
However, subgroups with unilateral symptomatic knee OA had
both symptom and structural benefit with one of the lorecivivint
doses, suggesting possible benefit in this subgroup. ADAMTS-4
and -5 inhibition was studied in a phase 2 trial, although there
was no benefit with any of the doses used in terms of structural
changes.
A minimally invasive therapy called geniculate or genicular
artery embolization (GAE), previously used for hemarthrosis, has
received some attention as a potential therapy for OA. GAE is
thought to disrupt angiogenesis and synovial nociceptive nerve
fiber growth to provide improvements in pain and function, particularly in people who are refractory to conservative therapies.
However, further work is needed to determine the long-term efficacy and to compare GAE to other available interventions.
While there is a much activity in these areas of drug development, these development efforts are occurring in the context of
other promising therapies that were not successful in their development for OA, including BMP-7, inducible nitric oxide synthase
(iNOS) inhibition, and calcitonin, among others. Thus ongoing
efforts are urgently needed to provide therapeutic options for the
millions of people worldwide with OA who currently have limited
therapeutic options.
