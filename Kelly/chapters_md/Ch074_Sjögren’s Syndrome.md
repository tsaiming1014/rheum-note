# Sjögren’s Syndrome

E. WILLIAM ST. CLAIR AND DAVID L. LEVERENZ

KEY POINTS

> Sjögren’s syndrome occurs in primary or secondary forms. The primary

> form occurs in 0.06% to 1.5% of the general population.

> The clinical hallmarks of Sjögren’s syndrome are keratoconjunctivitis

> sicca (dry eyes), xerostomia (dry mouth), and parotid gland swelling.

> Extraglandular features of primary Sjögren’s syndrome include fatigue,

> Raynaud’s phenomenon, polyarthralgia/arthritis, interstitial lung

> disease, renal disease, neuropathy, and purpura.

> A chronic mononuclear cell infiltration of the lacrimal and salivary

> glands is a characteristic histopathologic finding.

> The diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome is made by subjective and

> objective assessment of dry eyes and dry mouth, testing for serum

> antibodies, and, in some cases, by obtaining a labial salivary gland

> biopsy.

> Treatment of Sjögren’s syndrome currently aims to provide relief

> from the symptoms of dry eye and dry mouth, as well as control

> of extraglandular manifestations of disease. The development of

> disease-modifying therapy, which is currently lacking, continues to

> be a focus of clinical investigation.

## Historical Perspective

In 1888, Johann von Mikulicz-Radecki described a case of bilateral painless swelling of the lacrimal, parotid, and submandibular
glands,
an entity that later bore his name. Reports soon followed
showing that Mikulicz disease was not a distinct pathologic entity
but rather a clinical potpourri of conditions caused by leukemia,
lymphoma, and tuberculosis. Shortly thereafter, salivary gland disease became linked with dryness of the eyes and oral mucosa when
Henri Gougerot, a well-known and successful French dermatologist, wrote in 1925 about three cases of salivary gland atrophy with
dryness of the eyes, mouth, and vagina. The modern concept of
Sjögren’s syndrome became firmly rooted when Henrik Sjögren,
a Swedish ophthalmologist, reported in 1933 a series of 19 cases
of keratoconjunctivitis sicca, including two cases with swelling of
the major salivary glands.
During the next two decades, Sjögren
and others published extensively on various facets of the disease
that now carries his name (also called Gougerot-Sjögren disease),
with most of these early contributions coming from European
ophthalmologists.
In 1953, Morgan and Castleman published their detailed histopathologic findings from 18 patients with enlarged lacrimal
and salivary glands.
This pathologic treatise consisted of cases
with clinical features resembling those of Mikulicz’s disease and
Sjögren’s syndrome. Notably, 15 of the 18 patients were women
with lacrimal and salivary gland swelling that developed in the
fifth and sixth decades of life. Morgan and Castleman found in
the salivary gland tissue a consistent “lymphoid element” accompanied by a striking proliferation of myoepithelial and epithelial
cells. The salivary gland pathology in these cases was similar to
that of the patients described by Henrik Sjögren with keratoconjunctivitis sicca. Because Sjögren’s cases occurred mostly in middle-aged women, Morgan and Castleman incorrectly reasoned
that Mikulicz’s disease was a subset of Sjögren’s syndrome with
incomplete clinical manifestations. This now classic paper had the
effect of unifying Mikulicz’s disease and Sjögren’s syndrome into
a single disease entity. This belief prevailed for decades until the
discovery of IgG4-related disease, which is now considered to be
the underlying cause of most reported cases of Mikulicz’s disease
and distinct from Sjögren’s syndrome (see
Chapter 126
).
When Joseph J. Bunim delivered the Heberden Oration at
the Wellcome Foundation in London on December 2, 1960, he
described in elegant detail the clinical, pathologic, and laboratory
findings of the 40 patients with Sjögren’s syndrome evaluated by
Kurt Bloch, Martin Wohl, Richard Oglesby, Irwin Ship, and himself at the Clinical Center of the U.S. National Institutes of Health
(NIH).
All patients in their series had at least two of the three
following features: keratoconjunctivitis sicca, xerostomia (with
or without enlargement of the salivary glands), and rheumatoid
arthritis (RA). Bunim also knew from the work of others that keratoconjunctivitis sicca and xerostomia, or sicca complex, occurred
in some patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), scleroderma, polymyositis, and polyarteritis nodosa. In his lecture, he
made the point that the exocrinopathy extended beyond the lacrimal and salivary glands to affect the pharynx, larynx, and trachea,
as well as the vagina. Many of the patients with Sjögren’s syndrome in the NIH cohort also had extraglandular manifestations,
such as Raynaud’s phenomenon, purpura, pulmonary infiltrates,
and peripheral neuropathy. Among the NIH cases, Talal and
Bunim took note of three patients with reticulum cell sarcoma (an
older term that includes non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma [NHL]) and
a fourth patient with Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia, drawing
attention for the first time to the increased risk for lymphoma
in this disease.
They speculated that a “chronic state of immunologic hyperactivity and the proliferation of immunologically
competent cells producing abnormal tissue antibodies predisposes
to the relatively frequent development of malignant lymphoma.”
The NIH group also characterized the serologic markers of
Sjögren’s syndrome. In 1965 they reported that 12 of their 16
patients (75%) with sicca symptoms without another connective tissue disease tested positive for serum anti-nuclear antibody
(ANA) reactivity by indirect immunofluorescence on rat liver
tissue.
Applying the Ouchterlony plate method, they further

showed sera from 13 of 16 (81%) of these patients contained precipitating antibodies to SjD and SjT, self-antigens later named Ro
(SS-A) and La (SS-B). Another group next set the stage for our
modern classification criteria by subdividing their patients into
primary and secondary Sjögren’s syndrome.
Secondary Sjögren’s
syndrome referred to patients with sicca symptoms in the setting
of another connective tissue disease, such as RA, SLE, scleroderma,
or dermatomyositis, whereas primary Sjögren’s syndrome was designated for those patients with a sicca complex in the absence of
another connective tissue disease.
In the late 1960s and early 1970s, labial salivary gland biopsy
was investigated as a diagnostic test for Sjögren’s syndrome, leading to the development of grading systems based on the extent
of chronic inflammation in these tissue specimens. In 1968
Chisholm and Mason proposed a simple grading scale (0 to 4), in
which grades 0, 1, and 2 represented absent, slight, and moderate mononuclear cell infiltrates, respectively, and grades 3 and 4
corresponded to focal inflammatory cell scores of one or more
foci per 4 mm
of tissue, in which a focus equated to an aggregate
of 50 or more mononuclear cells.
In this initial study, 9 of the
10 patients with Sjögren’s syndrome were classified as grade 3 or
grade 4. By comparison, biopsies with lower grades came from
patients with other chronic inflammatory conditions, as well as
controls. Although it was a small study by today’s standards, this
work inspired others to validate and refine these findings.
One group at the NIH subsequently developed a grading
system incorporating scales quantifying the extent of inflammation in the salivary gland tissue and the amount of acinar gland
destruction.
As expected, they found that labial salivary gland
biopsies from patients with the sicca complex alone had higher
grades of cellular infiltrates and acinar destruction than those with
sicca symptoms and RA or RA alone. However, grading acinar
destruction did not add diagnostic value independent of the severity of the infiltrates. One study from the University of California
at San Francisco later modified the Chisholm and Mason grading
scale by analyzing labial salivary gland biopsies from 54 patients
with definite or probable Sjögren’s syndrome together with 21
controls.
Their results reinforced the diagnostic relevance of
the focus score, including its relationship to focus size, and highlighted the presence of germinal centers (GCs). Grade 4 biopsies
(>1 focus per 4 mm
of tissue) were primarily from patients with
Sjögren’s syndrome, with the highest focus scores occurring in
those with the primary form. The focus score was expanded to
range from 1 to 12, in which a score of 12 was arbitrarily assigned
to specimens with confluent foci. It was thereafter adopted as the
“gold standard” for quantifying chronic inflammation in labial
salivary gland biopsies. It is also possible to more precisely and
specifically quantify T and B cell infiltrates in tissue utilizing digital technology, whole slide scanning methods, and software-based
image assessment.

## Definitions and Classification Criteria

The clinical hallmarks of Sjögren’s syndrome are keratoconjunctivitis sicca and xerostomia, or the sicca complex. The term
keratoconjunctivitis sicca
is derived from Latin, and its translation is “dryness
of the cornea and conjunctiva.” Xerostomia is synonymous with
a dry mouth. As already mentioned, Sjögren’s syndrome is subdivided into primary and secondary forms. Secondary Sjögren’s
syndrome refers to patients with keratoconjunctivitis sicca, xerostomia, or both, in the setting of another connective tissue disease such as RA, SLE, inflammatory myopathy, systemic sclerosis,
or a systemic vasculitis such as granulomatosis with polyangiitis.
Patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome, on the other hand,
manifest signs and symptoms of keratoconjunctivitis sicca and
xerostomia in the absence of another connective tissue disease,
provided they also meet serologic and/or histopathologic criteria.
Since primary Sjögren’s syndrome is associated with extraglandular features that overlap with those of other connective tissue diseases, it may be difficult in some cases to distinguish primary from
secondary forms.
Sjögren’s syndrome is often referred to as an
autoimmune exocrinopathy
or
autoimmune epithelitis
.
The term
exocrinopathy
highlights the glandular involvement in this disease, which affects
not only the lacrimal and salivary glands but also the apocrine
sweat glands of the skin and the submucosal glands of the nose,
pharynx, larynx, large airways, and vagina. The term
epithelitis
stems from the understanding that epithelial cells are not simply
bystanders in the pathogenesis of this disease but play important
roles in driving the chronic inflammatory response.

syndrome were proposed by the American-European Consensus

By this method, patients could be classified with primary Sjögren’s

4 (histopathology) or 6 (serology) was positive. Patients could also
meet criteria if they fulfilled any three of the four objective criteria
items (3, 4, 5, and 6). Secondary Sjögren’s syndrome was defined by
the presence of item 1 or 2 plus a positive result for any two of items
3, 4, and 5 in the context of another well-defined connective tissue
disease. Exclusion criteria were the use of anticholinergic drugs, preexisting lymphoma, prior head and neck radiation, AIDS, hepatitis C
infection, sarcoidosis, and graft-versus-host disease.
In 2012, new classification criteria were proposed by the
Sjögren’s International Collaborative Clinical Alliance Cohort
(SICCAC) and provisionally accepted by the American College of
Rheumatology (ACR).
These criteria sought to classify patients

## Epidemiology

Primary Sjögren’s syndrome ranks among the most common of
the autoimmune diseases, with a prevalence rate ranging from
0.06% to 1.5%.
However, the epidemiologic data are confounded by variations in the ages of the study populations and
differences in the classification criteria used for case identification.
The prevalence of primary Sjögren’s syndrome is much higher in
women than in men (e.g., approximately 10:1), with a peak incidence in the fifth and sixth decades of life.
Sex ratio, age distribution, and clinical features can vary depending on the ethnicity
and geographic region of the study population.
Primary Sjögren’s syndrome occurs infrequently in children,
with onset as early as 5 years.
Classification criteria may be less
sensitive for identifying cases of primary Sjögren’s syndrome in
children than in adults because of differences between these two
age groups in the clinical presentation of this disease.
The prevalence of secondary Sjögren’s syndrome ranges between
4% and 31% in RA, 6 and 19% in SLE, and 14% and 21% in
systemic sclerosis.
To be classified with secondary Sjögren’s syndrome, patients must have symptoms of keratoconjunctivitis sicca
or xerostomia and objective evidence of decreased tear or salivary
flow.

## Etiology and Pathogenesis

In primary Sjögren’s syndrome, a common feature of disease
pathogenesis is the presence of serum autoantibodies, indicative
of a loss of immunologic tolerance to self-antigens. The appearance of autoantibodies may precede the onset of clinical disease,
and thus the loss of immune tolerance appears to be permissive and insufficient alone to induce tissue inflammation and
damage. T cells and B cells infiltrate the labial salivary glands of
patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome. However, the evolution of these aberrant cellular responses in the secondary and
tertiary lymphoid tissues and their role in inciting tissue inflammation remain incompletely understood. It is often assumed
that a genetically predisposed innate and adaptive immune system increases disease susceptibility and that environmental and
stochastic factors are involved in triggering the chronic inflammatory response.
Because a disproportionately high number of the cases of primary Sjögren’s syndrome occur in women, the search for environmental triggers has included studies of abnormal regulation
of estrogens and androgens. However, no major differences have
been found between patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome
and healthy controls in serum levels of sex steroid hormones.
Another piece of evidence against the role of sex hormones in

the mechanisms of ongoing disease is that treatment with the
hormone dehydroepiandrosterone, which acts on the androgen
receptor, has shown no clinical efficacy in women with primary
Sjögren’s syndrome.
Among possible viral triggers, human herpesviruses such as
Epstein–Barr virus (EBV) and cytomegalovirus have received the
most attention because of their suppressive effects on T cell immunity, their ability to establish persistent infection, their tropism for
salivary and lacrimal gland tissue, and the knowledge that lytic
replication requires polyclonal B cell stimulation and plasma cell
differentiation.
In patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome,
the EBV genome has been detected in autoantibody-producing
plasma cells located in the periphery of ectopic lymphoid structures; in addition, high levels of EBV DNA are found in salivary
glands from patients with this disease.
Furthermore, patients
with the primary type demonstrate higher positivity rates of serum
antibodies against EBV early antigen that are positive for both
anti-Ro/SSA and anti-La/SSB (48.7%) and anti-Ro/SSA alone
(39.8%) compared with Ro/SSA and La/SSB negative cases
(28.3%), implying a role for EBV in stimulating these autoantibody responses.
Other studies have attempted to link primary
Sjögren’s syndrome to infection with human T-lymphotropic virus
type 1, coxsackievirus, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C. A viral etiology for the primary type is further suggested by the presence of
a type I interferon (IFN) signature, which is a hallmark of host
defense against viral infection. Despite these tantalizing clues, the
available evidence does not yet support a direct role for viral infection as a cause of this disease.

### Genetics and Epigenetics

It has been difficult to estimate the genetic risk for primary
Sjögren’s syndrome because of the absence of large twin studies.
However, many large families have two or more members with
primary Sjögren’s syndrome, attesting to a genetic component.
The primary type is considered a complex genetic disorder, similar
to the heritability of SLE and RA. In fact, the gene variants identified as risk factors in primary Sjögren’s syndrome include many of
the same gene variants identified as risk factors in other systemic
autoimmune diseases. Also, the familial clustering of cases of primary Sjögren’s syndrome with cases of SLE, RA, systemic sclerosis, and autoimmune thyroid disease implies common genetic risk
factors across these autoimmune conditions.
The strongest genetic contributor to disease susceptibility in
primary Sjögren’s syndrome can be found in the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) locus on human chromosome 6, especially
the HLA-class II alleles.
In Caucasian populations, the
most reproducible risk variants are within the DR2 and DR3
alleles in the DRB1 locus. The strongest associations are found
between DRB1*03 and DQB1*02 alleles and the presence of
anti-Ro/SSA and/or anti-La/SSB antibodies. In a meta-analysis
of 1166 cases and 6470 controls of diverse ethnicity, the most
significant risk variants were DRB1*03:01, DQA1*05:01, and
DQB1*02:01.
Efforts to identify disease susceptibility genes outside of the
HLA locus in primary Sjögren’s syndrome have been greatly aided
by genome-wide association studies (GWASs). Results from these
studies have shown the non-HLA risk alleles are much weaker in
effect size than the HLA risk variants and are located in genes
relating to type I IFN signaling, B cell development, and cellular immunity.
Genes in the type I IFN pathway are of particular interest in primary Sjögren’s syndrome, as they are highly
expressed in the peripheral blood and salivary glands of patients
with this disease compared with controls.

Epigenetics can also influence the type of immune response
through the regulation of gene expression by mechanisms other
than a change in DNA sequence of that gene. This type of heritable change in gene expression primarily occurs through the alteration of chromatin structure by DNA methylation and histone
modification in conserved noncoding elements of genes, namely,
promoters, enhancers, and silencers. These regions of DNA bind
transcription factors, which in turn initiate and regulate transcription. Epigenetic modification via methylation and demethylation
and histone modification affects the binding of transcription factors by altering DNA accessibility and chromatin structure. There
is increasing evidence for the contribution of epigenetics to the
development of autoimmune diseases.
Epigenetic modification may be cell type and tissue specific.
Early studies of peripheral blood from patients with primary
Sjögren’s syndrome have shown that naive CD4 T cells from
peripheral blood are hypomethylated in regulatory regions of
several immune response genes, including lymphotoxinα
and
several IFN-related genes, with more conspicuous differences in
those patients with anti-Ro/SSA and/or anti-La/SSB antibodies.
A genome-wide DNA methylation analysis of whole blood
has found that IFN-related genes are also hypomethylated in B
cells compared with controls, resulting in increased IFN gene
expression.
However, not much is known about the chromatin
architecture in the salivary gland tissues of patients with Sjögren’s
syndrome and the limited data thus far lacks cellular granularity. Since many of the non-HLA risk loci associated with primary
Sjögren’s syndrome are located within gene regulatory networks
that may drive the pathology of this disease in the salivary gland
tissue,
a more thorough understanding of the functional implications of these risk loci will require analysis of specific cell types
and tissues at single-cell resolution.
Some of the conserved noncoding elements also encode
microRNAs (miRNAs), epigenetic modulators that also regulate
mRNA expression without altering gene sequences. MiRNAs
influence gene expression in both the innate and adaptive immune
system and play key roles in late B cell differentiation and development and the establishment of B cell tolerance. Their transcription
may too be regulated by DNA methylation and histone modification and RNA modification. Early studies have shown that
miRNA expression patterns in salivary gland tissue can distinguish patients with Sjögren’s syndrome from controls, suggesting a
pathologic role for miRNAs in the regulation of the tissue chronic
inflammatory response.

### Autoantibodies

Anti-Ro/SS-A and anti-La/SS-B antibodies do not appear to have
a pathogenic role in disease mechanisms despite their diagnostic
and prognostic significance. The stimulating antigen(s) for these
antibody responses is unknown. An aberrant response to self may
be provoked by altered expression of autoantigens. Several models have been proposed to explain the altered expression of autoantigens, including differential expression of protein isoforms,
post-translational modification, and abnormal autoantigen presentation via apoptotic blebs, exosomes, or heat shock protein–
mediated cross-priming.
Ro/SS-A and La/SS-B proteins do in
fact show increased expression in the vicinity of immunopathologic lesions where they may induce local immune responses.
Saliva from patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome contains
anti-Ro/SS-A and anti-La/SS-B antibodies.
Although this finding may be the result of local production of autoantibodies, it may
also reflect extravasation of proteins from blood vessels into the
inflamed tissues.
Among the other autoantibody specificities, those directed
against the muscarinic receptor (MR) have attracted the most
interest because of their possible role in causing glandular dysfunction. The MR family of receptors is bound by acetylcholine,
which mediates the effects of the preganglionic and postganglionic
parasympathetic nerve fibers that primarily regulate salivary flow.
The muscarinic type 3 receptor (M3R), the target of autoantibodies in primary Sjögren’s syndrome, is the subtype that predominantly controls salivary flow. Two lines of evidence support the
hypothesis that anti-M3R antibodies reduce exocrine gland function. First, serum immunoglobulins from patients with primary
Sjögren’s syndrome bind M3R receptors on acinar cell membranes
and inhibit acetylcholine-evoked Ca
responses in a salivary
gland cell line.
This Ca
-sensitive response is tightly regulated
through intra-cellular signaling pathways that open the Cl− channels on the apical membrane, resulting in an osmotic gradient and
movement of water into the duct lumen. Second, passive transfer
of anti-M3 antibodies can cause glandular hypofunction in NOD
mice, an animal model of Sjögren’s syndrome.
However, the different assays for detecting serum anti-M3R antibodies in patients
with primary Sjögren’s syndrome have produced inconsistent
results. For this reason, it has been difficult to determine the true
prevalence of serum anti-M3R antibodies in the primary type
and the possible relationship of these autoantibodies to glandular
hypofunction.

### Glandular Epithelium

In primary Sjögren’s syndrome, glandular epithelial cells may in
part drive the abnormal immune response. As discussed previously, mononuclear cells preferentially congregate around the
ductal epithelium in the diseased salivary gland tissue. The epithelial cells in the inflamed glands show upregulated expression
of several cell surface markers, including class I and II HLA molecules, adhesion molecules such as intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (CD54), vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 (CD106),
and E-selectin and costimulatory molecules such as CD40,
CD80, and CD86. They also produce increased levels of proinflammatory cytokines, such as IL-1, IL-6, IL-12, IL-18, TNF,
and BAFF.
Salivary gland epithelial cells express functional
Toll-like receptors (TLRs), including TLR3, TLR7, and TLR9,
endowing them with a capability to sense pathogens and endogenous molecules produced by injured tissues.
Activated epithelial cells are likely important drivers of the aberrant innate and
adaptive immune responses, not only in the lacrimal and salivary
glands but also in the lungs and kidneys and other extraglandular sites.
At least two models have been proposed to explain the glandular hypofunction in Sjögren’s syndrome. In one model, it may
be hypothesized the glandular tissue is destroyed by an immune
onslaught perpetuated by persistent exposure to self-antigens or
other environmental stimulants (e.g., viral infection), leading to
apoptosis of acinar epithelial cells, fibrosis, and irreversible loss of
salivary gland function (
Fig. 74.2A
).
A weakness of this model
is that studies suggest epithelial cells rarely undergo apoptosis
in the salivary gland tissue despite the fact they increase expression of mediators of cell death such as Fas, Fas ligand, and Bax
(B cell lymphoma 2–associated X protein).
Ligand binding to
CD40 expressed on epithelial cells in the salivary gland tissue may
also lead to Fas-mediated cell death by downregulating cellular
FLICE-like inhibitor protein, an inhibitor of Fas-mediated cell
death.
Because no studies have yet shown an imbalance truly
exists between the rates of glandular proliferation and damage,
the relevance of this model to glandular hypofunction is uncertain. It also does not explain why many patients with markedly
diminished salivary flow retain substantial amounts of normalappearing acinar tissue in their salivary glands.

## Clinical Features

### Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca

Chronic inflammation of the lacrimal glands diminishes secretion
of aqueous tears, which, if severe, may destroy the conjunctival
and bulbar epithelium. Aqueous tear deficiency produces dry eye,
causing symptoms of grittiness or foreign body sensation, burning,
photophobia, and eye fatigue. Routine eye examination usually
reveals a reduction in the tear flow, as measured by the Schirmer-I
test. Additional findings may include the absence of tears in the
conjunctival sac and dilated bulbar conjunctival vessels. Thick
mucus may also be seen in the inner canthus of the eye. Slit-lamp
examination allows for more detailed visualization of the corneal
and conjunctival surface. After instillation of lissamine green dye
or fluorescein onto the ocular surface, slit-lamp examination may
expose devitalized cells or epithelial defects, respectively, signs of
corneal and conjunctival damage. Severe dryness may result in
corneal abrasion or ulceration.
The anatomy of the tear film informs the differential diagnosis
of dry eye (
Table 74.4
). The tear film consists of three major layers: the outer lipid layer, the middle aqueous layer, and the inner
mucin layer. The middle aqueous layer is diminished in Sjögren’s
syndrome owing to reduced tear flow. Conditions associated with
infiltration of the lacrimal glands (e.g., sarcoidosis) and the loss of
tear flow from medications, aging, and estrogen deficiency may
also cause dry eye. The lipid layer derives from the meibomian
glands and traps the aqueous tear film on the eyeball and protects it from rapid evaporation. Meibomian gland dysfunction,
or posterior blepharitis, produces dry eyes from rapid evaporation
of tears; it often accompanies aqueous tear deficiency in Sjögren’s
syndrome and can be an aggravating factor in patients with keratoconjunctivitis sicca that requires additional therapy. Meibomian
gland dysfunction is often associated with ocular rosacea and seborrheic dermatitis, two conditions that also lead to dry eye. Lipid
degradation resulting from meibomian gland inflammation leads
to the production of free fatty acids, which irritate the ocular surface and in turn may cause punctate keratopathy. The mucin layer
originates from the goblet cells of the conjunctiva and, if deficient,
leads to an uneven distribution of the tear film over the surface of
the eye. Vitamin A deficiency and Stevens-Johnson syndrome are
examples of conditions associated with an abnormal mucin layer.

### Xerostomia

The changes in the quality and quantity of the saliva are responsible
for the signs and symptoms of xerostomia. Although symptoms of
dry mouth are relatively common in the general population, they
are usually more severe in Sjögren’s syndrome and cause perpetual
difficulties with chewing and swallowing dry food, altered taste
(metallic, salty, or bitter), and struggles with prolonged speaking.
Patients with xerostomia may have problems wearing dentures.
Despite complaints of a dry mouth and reduced salivary flow,
many patients may appear to have a normal oral examination.
Others with more severe hypofunction will manifest a dry, sticky,
or erythematous oral mucosa.
Two complications of xerostomia are important in the care of
patients, especially those with severe deficits in salivary flow. Dental complications include rampant caries, cracked teeth, and loose
fillings. A predisposition to oral candidiasis, another frequent
complication, typically manifests as the atrophic variant, which
is characterized by erythema and atrophy of the oral mucosa and
filiform papillae on the dorsum of the tongue, with angular cheilitis.
Sometimes, a thin, white exudate may appear on the surface
of the tongue. The “thrush-like” variant of oral candidiasis is seen
much less frequently in patients with xerostomia except in the face
of recent antibiotic therapy.
Approximately one-quarter of patients with primary Sjögren’s
syndrome suffer enlargement of the parotid or submandibular
glands during the course of their disease.
Chronic swelling is
usually painless and may be unilateral or bilateral; it is often diffuse and firm by palpation (
Fig. 74.3
). Acute episodes of swelling
are frequently painful and may intermittently punctuate the clinical course of primary Sjögren’s syndrome. These acute episodes,
which likely result from dried mucus transiently obstructing the
major ducts, usually subside within a few days with conservative
therapy. Rarely, bacterial infections may cause acute salivary gland
swelling and should be considered as a possible etiology if the
patient has a fever or other constitutional complaints. Asymmetric
gland enlargement with palpable hard nodules that are increasing
in size may indicate a neoplasm such as a lymphoma.

### Involvement of Other Exocrine Glands

Glandular hypofunction affects the nasal passages (meatal obstruction from dried mucus), larynx (hoarseness), trachea (cough),
vagina (dyspareunia), and skin (pruritus), producing symptoms
of dryness. Virtually any exocrine gland may be involved in this
disease.

manifestation.

Approximately 15% of patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome
develop severe extraglandular disease.
Fatigue
Fatigue, a complex and multifaceted phenomenon, occurs in
approximately 70% of patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
It is also a symptom of depression, chronic anxiety,
fibromyalgia, and sleep deficit, as well as a side effect of certain
medications. The United Kingdom Sjögren’s Interest Group has
developed an instrument called the Profile of Fatigue and Discomfort–Sicca Symptoms Inventory that specifically measures both
somatic (needing rest, poor starting, low stamina, weak muscles)
and mental fatigue (poor concentration, poor memory) in this
disease.
In 547 patients with a confirmed diagnosis of primary
Sjögren’s syndrome, somatic fatigue was the dominant predictor
of physical function and general health.
The relative contribution of behavioral and cognitive variables to fatigue was studied in
94 patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome, finding that fatigue
is common in patients without depression and only partially
explained by psychosocial variables.
The link between fatigue
and “biologic disease activity” is not fully understood in primary
Sjögren’s syndrome. Increased serum cytokines such as IL-6 and
type I IFNs and neuroendocrine and autonomic dysfunction are
postulated to be contributing factors to the physical and mental
aspects of fatigue in this setting.
Raynaud’s Syndrome
Raynaud’s syndrome, reported in 13% to 33% of patients with
primary Sjögren’s syndrome, often precedes the onset of sicca
symptoms by several years.
Digital ulcers occur only rarely.
Skin
Among the dermatologic manifestations of primary Sjögren’s syndrome, the most common are xerosis, or dry skin, eyelid dermatitis, and angular cheilitis. In addition, many patients develop a
variety of other cutaneous manifestations, including annular erythema, purpura, and cutaneous vasculitis.
Annular erythema has been described in three major types:
a donut-ring-like erythema with an elevated border (type I);
a subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus–like lesion with
marginally scaled polycyclic erythema (type II); and a papular insect bite–like erythema (type III).
Histopathologically,
these lesions are characterized by a deep perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate without the epidermal changes associated with
lupus.
In some cases, immunoglobulin and complement
deposition is observed along the basement membrane with liquefaction degeneration in the basal layer of the involved skin.
The type I lesion appears to be specific for primary Sjögren’s
syndrome, occurring predominantly in Asian as opposed to
Western populations.
The most common form of cutaneous vasculitis in primary
Sjögren’s syndrome is palpable purpura, primarily of the lower
extremities.
Purpuric lesions in Sjögren’s syndrome may cause
pain but they rarely ulcerate or lead to severe ischemia. Other
manifestations of cutaneous vasculitis in primary Sjögren’s syndrome include erythematous papules, macules, and urticarial
vasculitis. Patients with cutaneous vasculitis are more likely to
experience other extraglandular manifestations of disease, such as
arthritis, peripheral neuropathy, Raynaud’s syndrome, and livedo
reticularis. Likewise, cutaneous vasculitis is often associated with
abnormal laboratory findings such as anemia, elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate, hypergammaglobulinemia, hypocomplementemia, an elevated serum rheumatoid factor, and positive tests
for serum anti-Ro/SS-A and anti-La/SS-B antibodies.
About
half of cases with palpable purpura are associated with detectable
serum cryoglobulins.
Joints
Polyarthralgia occurs frequently in patients with primary Sjögren’s
syndrome. In a retrospective study, articular symptoms in the
primary type had a prevalence rate of 45%.
A subset of these
patients show objective signs of synovitis, which is nonerosive,
symmetric, and variable in intensity. Joint symptoms may precede
the diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome in as many as onethird of cases.
In two separate studies, anti-citrullinated protein
antibodies (ACPAs) were detected in serum from 7.5% and 9.9%
of patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
However, in
only one of these studies was their presence closely associated with
synovitis,
and in neither study did it appear that serum ACPA
positivity was associated with radiographic erosions or progression
to RA.

Lung
Involvement of the airways and lung parenchyma in primary
Sjögren’s syndrome may take several forms, including xerotrachea
and xerobronchitis, nonspecific interstitial pneumonitis (NSIP),
lymphocytic interstitial pneumonitis (LIP), usual interstitial
pneumonitis (UIP), bronchiolitis, and lymphoma.
The estimated prevalence of pulmonary involvement in primary Sjögren’s
syndrome varies depending on the thoroughness of the evaluation.
In a study of 123 patients with the primary type, 11.4% showed
pulmonary signs or symptoms and/or impaired pulmonary function with abnormal chest computed tomography (CT) findings at
the time of evaluation.
Evidence of small-airway dysfunction is often found in asymptomatic patients with normal radiologic studies. In symptomatic patients, NSIP appears to be the predominant type of lung
involvement. The diagnosis can often be made on the basis of clinical presentation, pulmonary function tests (PFTs), and abnormal
chest CT findings. PFTs in patients with NSIP show a restrictive
pattern with reduced diffusion capacity of lung carbon monoxide (DLCO). Chest CT scans reveal ground-glass opacities and
a reticular nodular pattern. Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL), which
is not usually required for diagnosis, shows evidence of alveolar
inflammation, with elevated neutrophil or lymphocyte counts, or
both. LIP, a subset of NSIP, is also associated with a restrictive pattern on PFTs. Chest CT findings are ground-glass opacities and
thin-walled cysts, with centrilobular nodules, interlobular septal
thickening, and bronchovascular bundle thickening (

).

Kidney
Clinically significant renal disease in primary Sjögren’s syndrome

Sjögren’s syndrome may take several forms, including membranous, membranoproliferative, mesangial proliferative, and focal

Gastrointestinal
Patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome have an increased frequency of gastrointestinal symptoms compared with the general
population. Dysphagia and heartburn are particularly common
complaints that may result from impaired salivary flow or esophageal dysmotility, or both. Approximately one-third of patients
with primary Sjögren’s syndrome have varying degrees of esophageal dysfunction, although many studies have been unable to correlate symptoms of dysphagia with a functional abnormality.
One study found that patients with the primary type do not have
a primary disturbance of esophageal motility but, rather, defective
clearance of esophageal acid that exposes the esophageal lining to
excessive amounts of acid, which in turn produces morphologic
changes and secondary dysmotility.
Other results suggest parasympathetic dysfunction might be at the root of the esophageal
abnormalities.
A case has also been described of a patient with
primary Sjögren’s syndrome in whom esophageal achalasia developed in the setting of a sensory ataxic neuropathy, which damaged the myenteric plexus and was responsible for producing the
esophageal dysfunction.
In addition, chronic atrophic gastritis, which may cause dyspeptic symptoms, has been reported in a
small number of cases of primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
Liver enzyme abnormalities may develop in patients with the
primary type for a variety of reasons, most often from an associated
disorder such as hepatitis C virus infection, autoimmune hepatitis,
primary biliary cirrhosis, celiac disease, or nonspecific hepatitis.
Bowel symptoms such as abdominal pain and constipation occur
more commonly in patients with primary Sjögren’s syndrome
than healthy controls, but their etiology is often obscure.

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Tests

### Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca and Xerostomia

A diagnostic suspicion of Sjögren’s syndrome is initially raised by
complaints of dry eye and dry mouth. However, many patients
may not volunteer this information at first because they do not
feel it is important enough to bring to the attention of the physician. Therefore it is essential for the physician to inquire about
these symptoms if the patient does not mention them during the
initial history taking. In patients with symptoms of dry eye and
dry mouth, the next step is to further investigate these symptoms
by objective testing. An easy method to evaluate aqueous tear flow
is the Schirmer-I test, which is performed by inserting a sterile
piece of filter paper in the middle to lateral third of the patient’s
lower eyelid and measuring the distance tears elute over 5 minutes.
A distance of 5 mm or less is the usual cutoff for abnormally low
tear production.
Although a Schirmer-I test has an approximately 20% falsenegative rate, normal tear flow in the face of ocular foreign body
sensations suggests an alternative diagnosis such as blepharitis.
Patients with moderate to severe symptoms of dry eyes are usually
referred to an ophthalmologist for slit-lamp examination. With a
slit-lamp, the ophthalmologist can examine the ocular surface for
any signs of damage. Instillation of lissamine green or fluorescein
dye onto the surface of the eye displays the integrity of the conjunctival and corneal surface. Lissamine green dye stains epithelial
surfaces lacking mucin (
Fig. 74.7
), whereas the fluorescein dye targets areas of cellular disruption on the ocular surface. Fluorescein
dye can also be used to measure tear break-up time. A rapid tear
break-up time (less than 10 seconds) can result from a deficiency
of the aqueous or lipid layers of the tear film, and thus cannot
distinguish between lacrimal gland destruction and blepharitis.
Lissamine green and fluorescein dye staining are used to determine the SICCA ocular staining score, which is a component of
the 2016 ACR/EULAR classification for primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
Rose Bengal dye, which stains dead or degenerated cells,

the definition for xerostomia according to classification criteria.

Sialography, another technique for evaluating xerostomia,
visualizes radiographic patterns of duct obstruction in the major
salivary glands. It is most useful for differentiating an inflammatory process from a neoplasm. This technique calls for injection of
radiographic contrast dye into the salivary duct, followed by serial
radiographs to image the pattern of dye flow. A water-based contrast is strongly preferred to an oil-based contrast agent because
the latter may damage the adjacent salivary gland tissue. Sialography is not routinely used in clinical practice because it is an
invasive procedure with complications that include duct rupture,
pain, and infection. Scintigraphy is a radionuclide technique for
measuring salivary gland function. After radiolabeled sodium
pertechnetate technetium is injected into the blood, it is absorbed
into the salivary gland and secreted into the mouth, allowing for
determination of the salivary flow rate. The diagnostic sensitivity
and specificity of salivary gland scintigraphy for the diagnosis of
primary Sjögren’s syndrome has been estimated to be 75% and
78%, respectively.
Salivary scintigraphy is not widely available
for routine testing.

### Ultrasound and Other Imaging Tests

The use of ultrasound as a diagnostic and prognostic tool in
primary Sjögren’s syndrome has steadily increased over the last
decade. Ultrasound can detect abnormal parenchymal volume,
echogenicity, inhomogeneity, and vascularity in salivary gland tissue of patients with the primary type (
Fig. 74.8
). Published reports
vary widely in the methods by which these ultrasound parameters
are used in the diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome; however,
most studies suggest that ultrasound has a comparable sensitivity
and specificity to sialography and scintigraphy.
MRI of the major salivary glands has also been explored as
a diagnostic tool in primary Sjögren’s syndrome. In these scans,
the degree of parotid gland inhomogeneity correlates with focus
scores on salivary gland histopathology.
Other studies have
demonstrated potential utility of performing MRI on submandibular and sublingual salivary glands in addition to the parotids.
Dedicated magnetic resonance sialography can also be performed
to visualize ductal pathology noninvasively, thus avoiding the
complications of conventional sialography described earlier.

### Salivary Gland Biopsy

Labial salivary gland biopsy has long been considered the gold
standard for diagnosing primary Sjögren’s syndrome. However,
in clinical practice, its use is often reserved for patients in whom
the diagnosis remains unclear after a thorough clinical and laboratory evaluation. The biopsy is usually performed by an oral surgeon, otolaryngologist, or other appropriately trained individual.
This minor procedure calls for removal of four or more salivary
gland lobules through a small incision in the inner lip. A biopsy
is considered to be positive if histopathologic analysis shows focal
lymphocytic sialadenitis with a focus score of greater than or
equal to 1 per 4 mm
of tissue (a focus is defined as a cluster of
50 or more lymphocytes). With this cutoff, the sensitivity and
specificity of labial salivary gland biopsy for the diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome has been reported at 83.5% and 81.8%,
respectively.
The interpretation of labial salivary gland biopsies is subject to considerable interreader variability, depending
on the experience of the reader.

### Approach to Diagnosis

The clinical diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome is highly
likely if a patient with symptoms of dry eye and dry mouth has
objective evidence of keratoconjunctivitis sicca and/or xerostomia
in conjunction with a positive test for serum anti-Ro/SS-A antibodies and shows no signs of another connective tissue disease.
Patients with anti-La/SS-B antibodies in the absence of anti-Ro/
SS-A antibodies are clinically indistinguishable from patients lacking both antibodies, and thus the presence of anti-La/SS-B antibodies alone is not a diagnostic biomarker of primary Sjögren’s
syndrome.
It may not be necessary to obtain histopathologic evidence
of salivary gland involvement if the other clinical and laboratory

features are consistent with this diagnosis. In some cases, a clinical diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome may be made in the
presence of a moderate or high titer positive test for ANAs, without serum anti-Ro/SS-A antibodies. Because approximately 25 to
50% of patients with the primary type test negative for anti-Ro/
SS-A antibodies, a positive test for ANAs may often be the only
evidence of autoimmunity. Serum ANAs of low titer have relatively little diagnostic value in this setting unless they are accompanied by anti-Ro/SS-A antibodies.
Biopsy of the labial salivary glands may be warranted in cases in
which signs and symptoms of keratoconjunctivitis sicca and xerostomia are extant in the absence of convincing serologic evidence
of autoimmunity. In this case the labial salivary gland biopsy may
be used to further investigate the diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s
syndrome while exploring possible alternative diagnoses, such as
sarcoidosis or amyloidosis.
The diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome in children
and adolescents requires special consideration because younger
patients do not generally present like adults with symptoms of
dry eye and dry mouth. Compared with adults, children and
adolescents more often present with recurrent parotitis, which is
not a criterion in the current classification criteria. However, if
recurrent parotitis is added to the AECG classification criteria,
the sensitivity of this classification scheme for diagnosing primary Sjögren’s syndrome in children and adolescents increases
from 39% to 76%.

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome is broad
because of the variety of conditions that also cause dry eye and
dry mouth, salivary gland enlargement, and similar extraglandular manifestations (see
Table 74.4
). It is important to realize most
patients diagnosed with keratoconjunctivitis sicca by an ophthalmologist do not have primary Sjögren’s syndrome. The etiology of
keratoconjunctivitis sicca in many of these cases is often obscure
and presumably related to aging, hormonal changes, or other
degenerative processes. Evaporative tear loss secondary to meibomian gland dysfunction is another common cause of dry eye. In
addition, medications with anticholinergic effects may contribute
to sicca complaints. Since approximately 25% of the general population has symptoms of dry mouth, it is not surprising that dry
eye and dry mouth often may coexist in a person without having
to invoke a systemic disease such as Sjögren’s syndrome.
Chronic parotitis has several possible etiologies, including sarcoidosis. Sarcoidosis shares many clinical features with Sjögren’s
syndrome, such as dry eye, dry mouth, arthralgia, lymphadenopathy, and lung disease. Bilateral diffuse enlargement of the parotid
glands may also result from sialadenosis, a noninflammatory process associated with diabetes mellitus, severe hypertriglyceridemia,
chronic liver disease, and alcoholism.
Another clinical entity, termed IgG4-related disease, has
emerged as an important consideration in the differential diagnosis of primary Sjögren’s syndrome. IgG4-related disease is characterized in some cases by high serum levels of IgG4 and tissue
biopsies showing a marked lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, a high
number of IgG4
plasma cells, and a storiform pattern of fibrosis.
Compared with primary Sjögren’s syndrome, IgG4-related disease does not show the same female predominance and is associated with a lower frequency of dry eye and mouth, arthralgia,
and serum ANA positivity, as well as a higher rate of autoimmune
pancreatitis.

## Treatment

The treatment of primary Sjögren’s syndrome is primarily aimed
at reducing the signs and symptoms of dry eye and dry mouth and
controlling the systemic manifestations (
Fig. 74.9
). The Sjögren’s
Syndrome Foundation and the British Society for Rheumatology
have proposed guidelines for the management of primary Sjögren’s
syndrome.
These guidelines can serve as a useful reference;
however, they are largely based on expert opinion due to the paucity of high-quality, randomized controlled trials.
In general, sicca complaints may be lessened by withdrawal of
medications with drying effects. For the treatment of dry eye, the
patient is advised to avoid central heating and air conditioning
and windy environments. Tear supplements are available over the
counter in various formulations of different viscosities. Low-viscosity tears are administered more often than high-viscosity tear

Cyclosporine ophthalmic emulsion, 0.05% (Restasis), has
been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
for the treatment of keratoconjunctivitis sicca. In clinical trials,
its use reduced the signs and symptoms of dry eye and achieved
a statistically significant improvement in tear flow as measured
by the Schirmer-I test.
Many patients have trouble tolerating
ophthalmic cyclosporine because its instillation causes burning.
An ophthalmic preparation of lifitegrast (Xiidra) reduces ocular
inflammation through the inhibition of lymphocyte function–
associated antigen-1 and has also been approved by the FDA
for the treatment of dry eye disease. Treatment with ophthalmic
lifitegrast improves symptoms of dry eye compared with placebo;
however, it has not been directly compared with ophthalmic cyclosporine.
Lifitegrast can cause ocular irritation when instilled in
the eye, similar to ophthalmic cyclosporine. A nanomicellular,
clear aqueous formulation of cyclosporine 0.09% (Cequa) has also
been approved for the treatment of dry eye and has lower rates of
instillation site pain and irritation compared with the cyclosporin
ophthalmic emulsion and lifitegrast.
Tears may be conserved by blocking the nasolacrimal drainage

may be performed in patients with persistent symptoms of dry eye
despite use of artificial tears. Plugs are initially placed in the two
inferior punctae because 90% of tears drain through these channels. Plugs may later be used to block the two superior punctae.
Excessive tearing is the most frequent complication of punctual
occlusion, which is the rationale for a trial of temporary blockade
with dissolvable collagen plugs before undertaking permanent closure. Long-term punctual closure may be achieved using silicone
plugs, which in some cases are poorly tolerated, or by cauterization or heat from a laser.

nasal saline spray. The oily content of vitamin E softgel capsules
may be used topically on the skin or the lips for its moisturizing

estrogen cream.

Currently, the therapeutic armamentarium of primary Sjögren’s
syndrome lacks a proven disease-modifying drug. Clinical experience
suggests that symptoms of fatigue, myalgia, and arthralgia/arthritis may respond favorably to treatment with hydroxychloroquine.
However, a double-blind, parallel group, placebo-controlled trial
showed that treatment with hydroxychloroquine was no better than
placebo for reducing dryness, pain, or fatigue.
A post hoc analysis
of patients with arthralgia in this study also found that hydroxychloroquine therapy was not significantly beneficial for reducing
joint pain, although insufficient numbers of patients with synovitis
made it difficult to draw any reliable conclusions on this point. In
patients taking hydroxychloroquine, care should be taken to avoid
doses exceeding 5 mg/kg/day of actual body weight to minimize the
risk of retinal toxicity.
Annual ophthalmologic examinations are
recommended to monitor for possible retinal complications.
Corticosteroids and other immunosuppressive drugs are
employed for the treatment of organ-threatening extraglandular
disease. NSIP and LIP are usually treated with high doses of corticosteroids and other immunosuppressive agents, such as azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, cyclophosphamide, or rituximab.
Because virtually no controlled data are available to support the
use of these agents for the treatment of NSIP or LIP, this approach
is necessarily empiric and demands judicious monitoring and close
follow-up. Bronchiolitis typically improves with high doses of corticosteroids alone, but close follow-up is mandatory for treatment
of relapses. Asymptomatic patients whose PFTs show an isolated
mild reduction in DLCO or evidence of small-airway disease may
be carefully followed up without treatment. Consensus guidelines
have been developed for the management of pulmonary involvement in this disease.
Conservative measures may be the only therapy for a sensory
peripheral neuropathy, utilizing medications like gabapentin, pregabalin, and analgesics to control neuropathic symptoms. High
doses of corticosteroids may produce transient improvement
in the symptoms of a peripheral neuropathy, but it is unclear if
this approach provides any long-term benefits that would justify
the risks of corticosteroid use. Clinically demonstrable weakness
due to a motor neuropathy calls for more aggressive treatment
using corticosteroids and other immunosuppressive agents. Some
patients with severe motor involvement may benefit from treatment with intravenous immunoglobulin.
In patients with lower-extremity purpura, the use of moderate
to high doses of corticosteroids can bring symptomatic relief in
most cases; however, subsequent tapering to low doses and withdrawal usually lead to recurrence of the purpura. Therefore it is
best to avoid corticosteroids in the absence of severe and uncontrollable symptoms or progression to skin ulceration. Support
stockings provide symptomatic relief in many cases and can be
used in combination with analgesics, antihistamines, or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs to manage painful symptoms. These
patients are usually left with postinflammatory changes in the
skin, which may be an acceptable outcome given the long-term
side effects of corticosteroids.
Biologic therapies have been investigated recently for their
clinical efficacy and safety in primary Sjögren’s syndrome. Infliximab and etanercept, which are TNF inhibitors, have failed in
small controlled clinical trials to demonstrate therapeutic benefit.
Rituximab had shown promise in several small trials,
including evidence of improvement in fatigue, sicca symptoms,
and systemic disease activity, although results across trials were
frequently conflicting.
However, two large randomized trials have recently failed to show significant benefit of rituximab
versus placebo.
Currently, rituximab is reserved for treating systemic manifestations of primary Sjögren’s syndrome, an
approach supported by evidence only from case series suggesting
acceptable tolerability and efficacy.
Other biologics have also
not shown efficacy for the treatment of primary Sjögren’s syndrome in randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trials, including baminercept, abatacept, and tocilizumab.
Additional
targeted therapies are in the pipeline.
The EULAR Sjögren’s Syndrome Disease Activity Index
(ESSDAI) and EULAR Sjögren’s Syndrome Patient Reported
Index (ESSPRI) are validated measures to assess disease activity
and patient-reported outcomes in primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
These indices are utilized in clinical trials as outcome measurements to assess systemic disease activity (ESSDAI) and symptoms
of dryness, fatigue, and pain (ESSPRI).

## Outcome

Overall mortality is not increased in patients with primary Sjögren’s
syndrome compared with the general population, although subsets of patients with severe disease are subject to poor outcomes.
In particular, patients with extraglandular involvement, vasculitis,
hypocomplementemia, and cryoglobulinemia are at increased risk
of mortality.
Although much can be done to ease the symptoms of Sjögren’s
syndrome, many patients still suffer significant morbidity as a
result of their disease. An effective disease-modifying therapy for
Sjögren’s syndrome could substantially improve patient outcomes
and represents a critical unmet need at this time. Given recent
advances in our understanding of disease pathogenesis, classification, and diagnosis, this goal seems closer now than ever before.
However, many important questions remain unanswered. What
leads to glandular hypofunction in the early stages of disease
before the accrual of significant glandular damage? Once salivary
flow hypofunction occurs, can it be reversed? Which of the many
cytokines, immune cells, and other mediators of disease are most
integral to disease progression? What outcome measures should be
used in trials to prove therapeutic efficacy? These questions remain
an area of active investigation and will be key to the discovery of
more effective therapies in the future.
The lack of success in identifying disease-modifying therapies
for primary Sjögren’s syndrome partly results from its clinical and
serologic complexity and incomplete understanding of its pathogenesis. To address these challenges, recent research efforts have
attempted to define molecular subsets of this disease that could be
used in the future to guide targeted therapy. To this end, a multiomic analysis of blood from 382 patients with primary Sjögren’s
syndrome has characterized four molecular phenotypes, each with
distinct patterns of immune dysregulation,
which were similar
to those previously described and shared across a diverse array of
seven systemic autoimmune and chronic inflammatory diseases,
including primary Sjögren’s syndrome.
These results will need
to be validated in longitudinal cohorts to determine whether these
molecular patterns remain stable throughout the disease course or
reflect disease stages or disease activity. Nevertheless, such a paradigm could improve the selection of patients for clinical trials and
increase the likelihood of developing effective therapies.

## Conclusion

Primary Sjögren’s syndrome is second in prevalence only to RA
among the autoimmune rheumatologic disorders. Many patients

with mild forms of primary Sjögren’s syndrome probably never
come to the attention of the rheumatologist, but rather are managed exclusively by primary care physicians, ophthalmologists,
otolaryngologists, and dentists. Others may never be referred for a
diagnosis because of the misperception that little can be done for
patients with Sjögren’s syndrome. This tendency toward neglect
could change with the discovery of a disease-modifying therapy.
However, primary Sjögren’s syndrome may result in more serious organ system involvement and lymphoma. While the rheumatologist is trained to accurately diagnose the primary type,
management of serious organ system disease may require a multidisciplinary team of specialists to manage its systemic complications. While much can be done now to care for patients with
primary Sjögren’s syndrome, the discovery of novel therapeutics
in the future holds promise for improving the lives of people with
this disease.
