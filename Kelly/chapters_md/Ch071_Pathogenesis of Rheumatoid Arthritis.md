## Introduction

The pathogenesis of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) has been extensively studied for decades and has revealed an abundance of mechanisms that contribute to the perpetuation of synovitis and joint
damage. These careful observations led to novel agents that target
the inflammatory response that provide significant clinical benefit.
In this chapter, the diversity of pro-inflammatory and destructive
pathways that contribute to RA is described.

Chapter 2

comprises macrophage- and fibroblast-like cells, and the latter is
usually relatively acellular and with fibrous tissue, fat cells, and
a relatively sparse vascular network. In RA, the synovium can

to predict response to therapy has generally been disappointing,
including Janus kinase (JAK) inhibition, B cell depletion, and T
cell co-stimulation.
A variety of classification methods have been used based on histopathology, such as abundance of lymphocytes or myeloid cells,
although clinical correlations with symptoms or response to therapy have had limited success. More recently a new system based
on single-cell transcriptomics could provide more precise ways to
measure cell infiltration.
Six broad “cell type abundance phenotype” (CTAP) patterns were described, including enrichment of T

### Synovial Intimal Lining Cells: Macrophage-like and Fibroblast-like Synoviocytes

Synovial Intimal Lining Phenotype and Function
The synovial intimal lining is a loosely organized collection of
cells that form an interface between the synovium and the synovial fluid space. The intimal lining cells lack tight junctions and
a defined basement membrane. The increase in cell number in
RA can be substantial. In the normal joint, the lining is only one
to two cell layers deep, whereas in RA it is often 4 to 10 cells
deep. Two major cell types are found in the lining: macrophagelike synoviocytes (MLSs, also called
type A synoviocytes
) and FLS
(also called
type B synoviocytes
). The former are derived from the
bone marrow and express macrophage surface markers such as
CD68, Fc receptors, and CD14, as well as abundant HLA-DR,
whereas the latter express little if any class II major histocompatibility complex (MHC) antigens, are devoid of macrophage markers, and have a scant endoplasmic reticulum. FLS express some
proteins that are unusual for mesenchymal cells, including vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 (VCAM-1), podoplanin (PDPN),
CD55 (decay activating factor), cadherin-11, junctional adhesion
molecule C (JAM-C), and the proteoglycan-synthesis enzyme uridine diphosphoglucose dehydrogenase. The relative numbers of
MLS and FLS cells are usually similar in normal synovium. There
is an absolute increase in both cell types in RA, although the percentage of increase in macrophage-like cells is often greater. In
addition, the MLS synoviocytes tend to accumulate in the more
superficial regions of the intimal lining.
Intimal lining synovial macrophages are terminally differentiated cells that do not appear to divide in the joint, and the accumulation of these cells in RA likely results from the ingress of
new bone marrow–derived precursors. In contrast, mesenchymalderived FLS can divide locally in response to the growth factors
generated by the activated immune response. Platelet-derived
growth factor (PDGF), transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
TNF, and IL-1 produced by many different cells combine with
products of arachidonic acid metabolism to induce proliferation
of these cells. In addition, pluripotential mesenchymal stem cells
that arise in the bone marrow and circulate through the blood can
migrate into the synovium and differentiate into FLS.
Retention
of macrophages in the intimal lining is probably due to expression
of adhesion molecules such as VCAM-1 and JAM-C on FLS and
is likely directed by chemotactic factors produced by FLS. For
example, in micromass cultures composed of laminin and FLS,
the FLS migrate to the surface of the micromasses and then can
recruit macrophages in the interior to the “lining” (
Fig. 71.3
).
Formation of this artificial intimal lining is dependent on the
adhesion molecule cadherin-11.
Although local proliferation of cells in the intimal lining can
occur, mitotic figures in rheumatoid synovium are rare, and thymidine uptake indicating DNA synthesis occurs in only a small
percentage of synovial cells. Using a monoclonal antibody that recognizes dividing cells, an even lower rate of cell division (
∼
0.05%)
is apparent.
A somewhat higher percentage of cells that express
the cell cycle–specific antigen proliferating cell nuclear antigen are
present in RA lining compared with OA.
The architecture of the synovial intimal lining is distinct from
other lining layers in the body. In contrast to serosal surfaces, the
intimal lining does not include epithelial cells and lacks a basement membrane. Rather than serving as a discrete barrier, it is a
loose association of cells that is discontinuous in some locations.
As noted above, recent data suggest that CXCR3
macrophages
in the lining can form tight junctions that create a less porous
structure. However, the presence of desmosomes diminishes
in inflamed synovium and allows freer exchange of molecules
between the synovium and the synovial fluid space.
Cadherin-11, from a class of adhesion proteins that are ubiquitous in various tissues, serves as the major mediator of homotypic
aggregation by FLS.
Immunohistochemistry shows abundant
expression of this protein in the intimal lining. Its importance in
the synovial architecture was confirmed in cadherin-11 knockout
mice, in which the intimal lining was virtually nonexistent. Blocking cadherin-11 with antibodies suppresses arthritis in the K/BxN
murine serum transfer model and decreases cartilage damage but
not bone damage.
The origin of the fibroblasts in the rheumatoid synovium is
presumably from circulating mesenchymal stem cells that differentiate after arrival to the joint. A recently described circulating
cell type called
preinflammatory mesenchymal
(PRIME) cells has
been identified in the blood of RA patients.
Normally present
in very low abundance, the number increases preceding a disease
flare. This unusual population represents a possible predictive biomarker, as well as an interesting potential therapeutic target for
flare prevention.
When the enzymatically dispersed synovial adherent cells are
cultured for several passages, mesenchymal cells survive and proliferate, while the macrophages ultimately die, resulting in a population of fibroblast-like cells. Therefore most published reports of
cultured synoviocytes use this population of cells. This cell population is homogeneous; many are likely derived from the lining, but
some might originate from the sublining. The cultured population represents the convergence of multiple fibroblast phenotypes;
expresses varying levels of podoplanin, cadherin-11, VCAM-1,
CD90, and FAP

### Synovial T Lymphocyte Phenotype and Function

T Cell Aggregate Formation in Rheumatoid Arthritis Synovium
Chemokines play a central role in the organization of tissues into
lymphoid structures such as aggregates and germinal centers.
CXCL13 and CCL21 are especially important, and their expression in rheumatoid synovium correlates with the presence of this
microarchitecture.
The former, in particular, is produced by
synovial follicular DCs (FDCs). Similarly, plasma cells expressing
the chemokine receptor CXCR3 are present in the rheumatoid
synovium. The CXCR3 ligand Mig/CXCL9 is highly expressed by
intimal lining synoviocytes and sublining cells and attracts these
cells toward T cell aggregates.
The architecture of lymphoid structures in rheumatoid
synovium is regulated by members of the TNF superfamily.

Synovial T Cell Co-stimulatory Molecules
Many RA synovial T lymphocytes exhibit an activated surface
phenotype, with high expression of HLA-DR, CD69, and CD27.
The co-stimulatory molecule CD28 is highly expressed by synovial T cells in RA. Its ligands, CD80 and CD86, are also displayed
on antigen-presenting cells in the joint, thereby providing an
excellent environment for T cell activation. The importance of the
CD80/86–CD28 interaction is supported by the observation that
abatacept, which blocks CD80/86, is effective in RA. Other costimulatory molecule pairs such as CD40L on T cells and CD40
on antigen-presenting cells are also expressed in RA synovium.

Synovial T Cell Adhesion Molecules
Synovial lymphocytes express adhesion molecules in the very late
activation antigen (VLA) and lymphocyte function–associated
antigen (LFA) integrin superfamily, which facilitates recruitment
to and retention of lymphocytes in the synovium. The cytokine
milieu of the joint induces adhesion molecules such as intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1), VCAM-1, and connecting
segment-1 (CS-1) fibronectin on vascular endothelium. These, in
conjunction with chemokines and other chemoattractants, attract
cells that express the cognate adhesion molecule counter-receptors
on their surface.
Synovial T Cell Chemokine Receptors
Synovial T cells in RA express characteristic receptors to specific
chemokines. The chemokine receptor CCR5 is the ligand for
macrophage inhibitory proteins 1
α
and 1
β
and is highly expressed
in the infiltrating RA T cells. This particular receptor, along with
CXCR3, is preferentially found on Th1 cells, an observation that
might explain accumulation of this phenotype in the rheumatoid
synovium. Expression of nonfunctional CCR5 alleles (CCR5-
d32) that protect from HIV infection has been associated with
diminished disease severity in people with RA who carry the variant (although small molecule CCR5 antagonists have limited clinical efficacy). The chemotactic factor stromal cell–derived factor-1
(SDF-1) is also produced by synovial tissue, and its specific receptor, CXCR4, is displayed by rheumatoid synovial T cells. Other
T cell phenotypes recruited to the rheumatoid synovium include
Th17 cells that express CCR2 or CCR6 and produce IL-17 and
CD4
CD25
regulatory T (Treg) cells that can suppress immune
responses (see the subsequent discussion of T cell subsets). Preferential recruitment of CCR6
Th17 cells into the rheumatoid joint
appears to be due to the local release of the chemokine CCL20.

Early studies of T cell receptors (TCRs)
in RA suggested enrichment for V

β

and are susceptible to activation by superantigens. In addition

Metabolic Mechanisms Contributing to T Cell Accumulation
The dominant mechanism for synovial T cell accumulation most
likely results from antigen-independent processes that include cell
recruitment and retention mediated by chemokines and adhesion
molecules. A high level of telomerase activity is present in rheumatoid synovial lymphocytes, which reflects their proliferative
activity and could indicate repeated cycles of cell division.
In
addition to limited antigen-driven cell growth, hyperproliferation
of T cells due to metabolic derangements can also contribute to
T cell accumulation in RA synovium. For example, RA T cells
have defective glycolytic activity due to increased shunting to the
pentose phosphate pathway and accumulation of NADPH.
This decreases activation of ataxia telangiectasia mutated (ATM)
and enhanced proliferation of pro-inflammatory Th1 and Th17
cells. Restoring redox potential and increasing reactive oxygen
species to normal can potentially suppress synovial inflammation.
Overexpression of the podosome scaffold protein TKS5 is also
regulated by a metabolic state of RA synovial T cells marked by
reduced glycolytic flux.
The pathogenic potential of these T cells
is diminished by restoring pyruvate production or by inhibition of
fatty acid synthesis.
Synovial T Cell Immunoreactivity and Activation
The histopathologic appearance of RA, with exuberant infiltration
of the synovium with T lymphocytes, is characteristic of tissues
with chronic antigen-specific responses. However, the synovium
can only respond to inflammation in a limited number of ways,
and many chronic immune-mediated arthropathies have similar
histologic patterns. In fact, chronic arthritides that clearly are not
mediated by T cells (e.g., chronic tophaceous gout) exhibit many
of the same features.
There are ample opportunities in the rheumatoid synovial
environment for exposure to antigens and T cell activation. For
example, proteins modified through processes such as citrullination provide a rich source of antigenic stimulation by T cell clones
that were not deleted during development. Resultant T cell cytokine production, perhaps in central lymphoid organs, can drive
the subsequent adaptive immune responses. Animal models also
suggest that breakdown of tolerance can be due to events that
occur early in development of the adaptive immune system. For
instance, mutations in ZAP70, which is involved with TCR signaling, can lead to spontaneous T cell–dependent inflammatory
arthritis in mice.
An abnormal
ZAP70
gene leads to defective
positive and negative selection in the thymus and allows autoreactive T cells to escape. As a result, mice produce autoantibodies
(e.g., RF and anti-dsDNA) and develop a severe destructive arthritis. The disease can be transferred by thymocytes or peripheral
CD4
T cells into syngeneic mice with a normal
ZAP70
gene,
which suggests that resetting the immune rheostat in development
or survival of pathogenic clones in the thymus can lead to synovitis and abnormal immune responses later in life.
These studies show that minor changes in the TCR signaling
and lack of subsequent clonal deletion can alter T cell selection
and induce arthritis. The finding is especially relevant in light
of the association of a
PTPN22
SNP with RA (see
Chapter 70
),
because this gene is also involved with TCR signaling. Despite
the clear T cell dependence of the model, the cytokines implicated are remarkably similar to autoantibody-dependent models
of RA. Therefore the cytokine profile in RA may represent a final
common pathway for a variety of immune-mediated mechanisms.
This concept is supported by the diversity of responses to targeted
therapies in RA and suggests that RA is not a single disease but
instead is a group of mechanistically distinct entities with a shared
phenotype.
Although synovial T cells display an activated phenotype,
proliferation and cytokine responses are usually less than normal
in peripheral blood cells or even autologous peripheral blood T
cells from the same patient. Spontaneous and stimulated cytokine
production, including Th1 factors such as IFNγ
and IL-2, are
relatively low. Responses directed toward most recall antigens are
also deficient, although RA synovial T cells can proliferate briskly
to certain heat shock proteins. Th17 cells have been detected in
both the blood and synovium, perhaps because of exposure to a
cytokine milieu that includes IL-1, IL-6, and/or IL-23 that can
promote differentiation toward production of IL-17 family members (especially IL-17A and F).
The mechanisms of decreased responsiveness in the synovial
compartment likely include exposure to suppressive factors (e.g.,
TGFβ
or IL-1Ra), abnormal redox potentials that suppress TCR
signal transduction, the presence of Tregs, or induction of anergy.
Another contributor to local anergy could be the relative lack of
the co-stimulatory molecule CD80 on HLA-DR
FLS, which
could induce anergy. This phenomenon has been observed in cocultures of T cells with synoviocytes.
Cell–Cell Contact-Mediated T Lymphocyte Activation
Even though evidence of local T cell activation is relatively modest
in rheumatoid synovium, direct cell–cell contact permits these cells
to participate in synovial cytokine networks and matrix destruction.
Membranes prepared from activated T cells can directly stimulate
macrophages and FLS to produce cytokines and MMPs.
The
membrane constituents that regulate this process vary, depending
on the culture conditions, but include adhesion molecules such as
LFA-1 and membrane-bound TNF. Hence a T cell displaying these
proteins can potentially contribute to macrophage and fibroblast
activation in an antigen-independent fashion. One of the bestcharacterized consequences of this pathway is the ability of T cells
to enhance synovial macrophage TNF production in a contactdependent manner after exposure to macrophage-derived IL-15.
Because memory T cells accumulate into the joint due to a
chemoattractant gradient, there is no requirement for a specific
antigen through this mechanism. Instead, activation of innate
immunity by nonspecific stimuli permits subsequent ingress of
the correct T cell phenotype to engage resident synovial intimal
lining cells, sublining fibroblasts, or macrophages. The combination of antigen-driven adaptive immune responses and antigenindependent stimulation could contribute to the diversity of
individual responses to targeted therapy and the relative paucity
of complete remissions.
Re-establishing Immune Homeostasis
Restoring Tolerance.
The role of synovial T cell responses and
autoreactivity in the pathogenesis of RA has led to efforts to treat
the disease by restoring tolerance. Early aggressive treatment of

early RA (within the first year of clinical disease) with methotrexate and a TNF inhibitor can, in some circumstances, lead to longlasting remissions.
In some cases, therapy can be withdrawn
after 1 year of treatment, and patients do not experience a flare for
at least an additional year. However, most drug-tapering regimens
result in disease flares, suggesting that even aggressive therapy and
long-lasting remissions do not indicate a return to tolerance.
Additional individualized therapies that enhance Treg function,
alter co-stimulation, or delete pathogenic T cells are other ways to
restore homeostasis and normal immune function.

### Treg Cells and Innate Lymphoid Cells

The role of classically defined inducible Tregs (CD4
CD25
CD127
) in RA remains uncertain. Deficiency of this subset,
which produces IL-10 and TGFβ
and regulates other T cells
through cell contact, could contribute to autoimmunity. The
number of Tregs is generally low in RA synovium, although some
reports suggest that it is not that different from OA synovium.
Interestingly, the number decreases after intra-articular injection
with corticosteroids.
Characteristic regulatory cells that express
Foxp3 and CD39 can be found in RA lymphoid aggregates, especially adjacent to DCs.
As noted previously, Tregs from the joint
can freely circulate and can be detected in peripheral blood.
The transcription factor STAT3 regulates Treg differentiation
in the RA joint. Activated STAT3 is abundant in the rheumatoid
synovium and can enhance Th17 differentiation while suppressing
production of Tregs, whereas STAT5 appears to have the opposite
effect.
Thus modulating the balance between STATs in RA T
cells, such as through JAK inhibition, could change the balance
between pathogenic and immunosuppressive lymphocytes. FMSlike tyrosine kinase 3 ligand (FLT3L) also appears to regulate Tregs
in RA and increase their formation.
Studies of RA peripheral blood generally demonstrate normal
numbers of Tregs, although the CD4
CD25
cells can accumulate
in rheumatoid synovial effusions. However, peripheral blood Treg
responses in vitro after stimulation with anti-CD3 and anti-CD28
antibody displayed an anergic phenotype and were unable to suppress T cell or monocyte cytokine production. This abnormality
might be reversed when patients are treated with TNF inhibitors.
These data suggest that abnormal Treg function could also be
secondary to cytokine imbalance in RA rather than a primary
event. Treatment can have a significant effect on the number and
function of Tregs, which makes evaluation of their role in patient
samples difficult to interpret. Nevertheless, enhancing or restoring Treg activity as a therapeutic intervention has the potential to
downregulate other T cells and cytokine production.
Enhancing the number or activity of Tregs has been evaluated in animal models of arthritis with encouraging results.
Antigen-induced arthritis is exacerbated when CD4
CD25
cells
are depleted and suppressed when they are passively transferred
to affected animals. Treg function is low in some inflammation
models.
Methotrexate also increases Treg function in collageninduced arthritis. Administration of neuropeptides such as vasoactive intestinal peptide appears to suppress collagen-induced
arthritis by enhancing the Treg function in synovium and lymph
nodes. However, it is still difficult to ascribe efficacy of therapeutic
agents in RA to increased numbers or function of Treg.
The role of tissue innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) is increasingly
recognized in immune-mediated diseases. Like Tregs, type 2 ILCs
can suppress inflammation during disease resolution. IL-9 is a particularly important cytokine that regulates type 2 ILC-mediated
Treg induction. In pre-clinical models, IL-9 deficiency leads to
chronic inflammation and increased joint damage.
Interesting
therapeutic strategies focused on enhancing normal processes that
resolve inflammation could take advantage of the ILC–Treg axis.
Type 3 ILCs that express the chemokine receptor CCR6 are also
present in the rheumatoid joint and could increase inflammation
through the production of IL-17 and IL-22.

### Synovial B Cells

Synovial B cell and plasma cell hyper-reactivity are major participants in the initiation and perpetuation phases of RA. This
notion is supported by development of spontaneous arthritis in
mice, such as the K/BxN model, in which loss of tolerance leads
to autoantibody production, activation of innate immunity, and
chronic synovitis. Furthermore, B cell–directed therapies such as
anti-CD20 antibody are often effective in RA. The rheumatoid
synovium contains a relatively small number of B cells (<5% of
the total cell population), but they, along with synovial plasma
cells, are major producers of ACPAs, RFs, and other antibodies
that can form immune complexes and fix complement.

Cytokine Regulation of Synovial B Cells
Although many rheumatoid synovial tissues exhibit a diffuse infiltration with mononuclear cells, a significant percentage also have
discrete lymphoid follicles populated by B cells in the sublining
region. FDCs, B cells, plasma cells, and T lymphocytes collect
in these aggregates. The germinal centers are highly organized
structures in which affinity maturation occurs. B cells are present
in the aggregates and express the maturation marker CD20, as
well as proliferation-associated antigens such as Ki67 (the protein
encoded by the
MKI67
gene). The formation of these structures is
dependent on soluble and membrane-bound cytokines, including
lymphotoxin. B cells accumulate in lymphoid aggregates in RA
synovium under the influence of a variety of chemotactic factors,
including CCL21 and CXCL13.
A member of the TNF superfamily of cytokines known as B
lymphocyte stimulator (BLyS, also called BAFF) is a key molecule
that regulates B cell differentiation. BLyS binds to transmembrane
activator and CAML interactor (TACI), which is present on both
B and T cells. If this system is blocked using recombinant TACIIg to absorb the cytokine and a related B cell differentiation factor
called a proliferation-inducing ligand (APRIL), then the number
of B cells is dramatically reduced, and antibody production is
decreased. The same construct is effective as a therapeutic agent in
collagen-induced arthritis, a model that depends on autoantibody
production.
BLyS is produced in RA synovium, especially by macrophages
and to a lesser extent by TNF-stimulated B cells. APRIL is localized mainly to DCs in synovial germinal centers. TACI-Ig disrupts the germinal centers and decreases Ig receptors in SCID
mice implanted with rheumatoid synovium.
However, results
of clinical trials neutralizing BLyS were disappointing, indicating
that targeting of this cytokine is not as effective as B cell depletion
with anti-CD20 antibody. Although improved benefit might be
possible with a biologic agent that blocks both APRIL and BLyS,
TACI-Ig markedly decreases serum immunoglobulin concentrations but has limited efficacy in RA.
Synovial B Cell Maturation and Clonality
B cells isolated directly from germinal centers of RA synovium
demonstrate a heterogeneous pattern of V-gene usage and

rearrangement. The majority of VH genes are not mutated, suggesting that they are recent immigrants from the peripheral blood
and are activated locally. For RF-producing cells, shared mutations
containing an identical sequence throughout the variable domain of
immunoglobulins have been identified in synovial tissue.
Preferential utilization of a limited number of VH and DH gene segments
and marked preference for a DH reading frame encoding particular
hydrophilic residues have also been observed, consistent with antigen-related selection and maturation. Analysis of expressed heavychain variable domains supports the notion that the B cell response
in RA synovium is oligoclonal. Recently a highly enriched synovial B
cell population characterized by co-expression of a family of orphan
nuclear receptors (NR4A) has been described. NR4A
B cells coexpress lymphotoxins
α
and
β
and IL-6, supporting functions in
ectopic lymphoid‐like structures (ELS) promotion. Expanded and
shared clones between synovial plasma cells and NR4A
B cells suggest an in situ differentiation of this B cell population.
Analyses of gene usage on synovial B cells and plasma cells demonstrate oligoclonality. Most of the cells are class-switched and
enriched for immunoglobulin heavy gene V4-34 and have longer
CDR3 lengths compared with B cells analyzed from peripheral
blood. Interestingly, these heavy chain genes are also associated
with chronic lymphocytic leukemia and autoimmunity.
B
cells in synovium that produce ACPAs also have abundant somatic
mutations consistent with affinity maturation and antigen-driven
selection.
Of interest, studies of serial blood samples from individuals with ACPA
RA found increasing numbers of IgG and
especially IgA expressing plasmablasts that bind to citrullinated
peptides over time with shared complementary-determining
region 3 sequences. As the disease progressed, divergent somatic
mutations provide evidence of expanding polyreactivity of citrullinated proteins.
The resultant antibodies increasingly display
the ability to form immune complexes and activate macrophages
to produce cytokines like TNF. The data suggest that B cells go
through repeated cycles of trafficking from mucosal sites through
the blood to synovium, with antigen spread and increasing avidity
for modified peptides.
Plasma cells in the synovium also have distinct rearrangements
compared with the B lymphocytes. Although plasma cell rearrangements are not always similar to the B cells, groups of plasma
cells use similar genes, albeit with distinct mutations. Therefore
the plasma cells are likely either derived from antigen-driven synovial B cell clones in the joint or migrate to the synovium after
affinity maturation.
B cell differentiation and survival depend on stromal cells and
cells with nurse-like properties that support lymphocyte maturation in the thymus. The synovium of patients with RA also contains nurse-like cells, which can increase expression of CD40 and
class II MHC proteins on B cells. B cell survival is supported by
this population of cells, whereas autoreactive clones evade deletion and produce autoantibodies. A variety of cytokines including
granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF),
IL-6, and IL-8 are produced by RA nurse-like cells, and direct
contact with B cells is critical for maximal proliferation and antibody production.
Clinical Improvement After B Cell Depletion
Anti-CD20 antibody (rituximab) is effective in RA with benefit
similar to other biologics. Response rates are higher in patients
with antibody profiles that include RF and ACPA, and high levels of chemokines like CXCL13 that attract B cells to synovium
might also serve as a biomarker associated with efficacy. Increasing
serum levels of CXCL13 also predict the return of B cells in the
peripheral circulation and could be a useful marker for stratifying
patients or planning additional courses of rituximab.
Metabolomics studies in RA patients treated with rituximab suggest that
some polar metabolites like choline and phenylalanine or lipid species at baseline were associated with subsequent clinical improvement.
A variety of metabolic changes occurred after treatment,
including alterations in polar and lipid compounds.
On the other hand, higher numbers of plasmablasts, identified
by IgJ expression, in the blood are associated with poor response
to B cell depletion by ocrelizumab and rituximab. Because antiCD20–depleting antibody does not affect plasma cells, which lack
CD20, persistent disease could be related to the presence of plasmablasts in the blood or joint.
In RA, serial synovial biopsies demonstrate only partial B cell
depletion after rituximab therapy despite the virtual absence of
peripheral B cells. The clinical responses do not always correlate
closely with the extent of synovial depletion, although some patients
with the most impressive responses appear to have marked declines
in the number of B cells in the post-treatment synovial specimens.
Surprisingly, B cell depletion does not consistently decrease autoantibody production or cytokine production in the synovium, nor
does a change in ACPA or RF levels predict a clinical response.
Longer-term biopsy studies suggest that plasma cells are decreased
in synovium 24 weeks after treatment with rituximab and that this
outcome correlates with decreased symptoms. A large prospective
clinical trial recently also failed to show a correlation between B cell
infiltration in RA synovium and response to rituximab.
The synovial biopsy data on synovial autoantibody production
suggest that B cells play a more complex role in the disease than
simply producers of ACPAs or RF. B cells are potent antigen-presenting cells that could also contribute to disease in the inflamed
synovium or in central lymphoid organs. In a SCID mouse model
using rheumatoid synovial explants, for example, T cell activation,
lymphangiogenic, and cytokine production depend on B cells.
In the same model, treatment with TACI-Ig to inhibit APRIL and
BLyS decreases synovial inflammation and IFNγ
production in
tissues with germinal centers.
B cells can also contribute to rheumatoid synovitis by producing cytokines, including comparatively small amounts of LT
α
,
TNF, and IL-6. Although macrophages and synoviocytes produce
quantitatively greater amounts, B cells in strategic locations like
lymphoid aggregates might help drive synovitis. Certain B cell
subsets could play a role, such as the FcRL4
B cell subset that
produces TNF and RANKL in RA and synovial CD79a
B cells
that correlate with erosive disease in patients with long-standing
RA.
Alternatively, regulatory B cells, like Tregs, could decrease
inflammatory responses in RA through the elaboration of suppressive cytokines like IL-10 and TGFβ
.

### Dendritic Cells

DCs are potent antigen-presenting cells that populate synovial
tissue and synovial effusions of patients with RA. In established
RA, synovial DCs could present antigens in the joint to elicit a
local immune response. DCs also produce type I IFNs, which
have been implicated in numerous autoimmune diseases such as
lupus and RA. Moreover, coordinated expression of genes that are
regulated by IFNs, sometimes called the
IFN signature
, has been
observed in the joint and blood of patients with RA.
Rheumatoid synovium in many ways functions like extralymphatic lymphoid tissues, with mature DCs expressing CD86,

CD83, and DC-LAMP localized in perivascular lymphocytic
infiltrates and aggregates. Ultrastructural analysis of the synovium
shows the DCs in contact with lymphocytes, where they can pre
sent antigens. The presence of DCs is not unique to RA and has
been identified in other inflammatory arthritides, including gout
and spondyloarthropathies.
DCs can express the chemokine receptor CCR7, which drives
their migration to lymphoid tissue and orchestrates organization
into the appropriate germinal centers. Synoviocytes in RA express
two CCR7 ligands (CCL19 and CCL21), which provide a signal
for the DCs to remain in the peripheral tissue. Immature DCs
and plasmacytoid DCs are also scattered throughout the synovial sublining region. B cell–enriched lymphoid follicles, which
are usually organized around FDCs, are also found in RA joints.
Cytokines in the rheumatoid synovium such as GM-CSF influence the proliferation and maturation of DCs. Increased numbers
of DCs are found in the tissue and synovial effusions, where they
can constitute up to 5% of mononuclear cells.
FDC-containing ectopic lymphoid structures in rheumatoid
synovium also express high levels of activation-induced cytosine
deaminase, a gene that participates in Ig class switching. ACPA
plasma cells surround these aggregates, suggesting that the aggregates play a role in autoantibody production. The architecture and
ACPA
cells persist when tissue is transplanted into SCID mice.
Therefore synovial DCs not only help organize the microarchitecture of synovial tissue but also participate in the development of
high-affinity IgG autoantibodies.
In addition to FDCs, several other subsets are present in the
rheumatoid joint. CD1c
DCs in the synovium accumulate in
inflamed synovium, are highly responsive to Toll-like receptor (TLR) 7/8 stimulation and have a distinctive transcriptome
compared with their peripheral blood counterparts.
Recently
described CD141
DCs, distinct from CD1c
DCs, are associated with viral and tumor immune responses and are also enriched
in inflammatory synovium.
These DCs can activate CD4
and
CD8
T cells and increase GM-CSF, TNF, and IFNγ
production. They can also increase the invasive potential of synoviocytes.
Another population of DCs, namely CD209/CD14
, are derived
from rheumatoid monocytes produce greater amounts of cytokines than the cells derived from health controls.

### Mast Cells

Mast cells are present in the synovial membranes of patients with
RA and, in some patients, are located at sites of cartilage erosion.
Rheumatoid synovial membranes contain more than 10 times
as many mast cells than control synovial samples from patients
undergoing surgery for meniscectomy. Mast cells and histamine
are also found in a majority of synovial fluid specimens from
inflammatory synovitis. A strong correlation exists between the
number of mast cells in synovial tissue and the degree of lymphocyte infiltration, autoantibody positivity, and high disease activity.
Mast cells from RA synovium express significantly higher
amounts of the C5a receptor and express higher amounts of prostaglandin D2 (PGD2) compared with OA synovium.
Resident mast cells in synovium respond to cytokines that
stimulate mast cell growth and chemotaxis. Extracts of mast cells
can induce adherent rheumatoid synovial cells to increase production of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and collagenase, and immunostaining of RA synovial mast cells demonstrates tryptase and
TNF. Mast cell–derived heparin has significant effects on connective tissue. In particular, it can modulate the effects of hormones
on osseous cells and thereby alter the balance of bone synthesis
toward degradation. Synovial mast cells also express Fc
ε
receptors,
as well as Fc
γ
receptor types I and II, but not type III. Immune
complexes that ligate type I receptors release PGD2 and TNF,
whereas type II receptor ligation releases histamine.
Mast cells have been implicated in the initiation of disease in
the K/BxN serum transfer model,
but some studies cast doubt
on that conclusion.
The role of mast cells in established disease is
also uncertain, yet mast cell depletion in the pre-clinical phase of
murine collagen-induced arthritis is protective.
In RA, treatment of synovial tissue explant cultures with a c-Kit tyrosine kinase
inhibitor blocks stem cell factor signaling and decreases production of TNF.
These data suggest that mast cells might contribute to synovial cytokines. Mast cells might also contribute to the
cytokine milieu of RA synovium because they contain pre-formed
IL-17A granules.
Furthermore, cultured mast cells release IL-17
when they are stimulated with TNF or immune complexes.
A clinical trial using the c-Kit (a receptor tyrosine kinase that
facilitates mast cell growth and survival) inhibitor masitinib suggested clinical efficacy in RA, but a phase III study did not confirm benefit. In addition, a controlled trial of a histamine H4
receptor antagonist to block immunomodulatory effects of mastcell derived histamine did not show significant clinical benefit.
Thus, there is no direct evidence that blocking mast cell function
will be useful in RA.

### Polymorphonuclear Leukocytes

Neutrophils are common in RA synovial effusions, but very few
infiltrate the synovium. Even though circulating neutrophils from
patients with arthritis resemble those from healthy controls, neutrophils in joint fluid cells exhibit IFNγ
, as well as TNF, IL-6,
and hypoxia gene signatures.
Neutrophils are immunologically
active and can also produce IL-17B and IL-1Ra. Synovial neutrophils also release extra-cellular traps (NETs), which are composed
of extruded DNA. Synovial, synovial fluid, peripheral blood neutrophil, and even pulmonary NETosis are increased in RA. It can
be further enhanced by RA serum (especially ACPA-containing
serum), IL-17A, and TNF.
The resultant extra-cellular DNA
includes PADI4 and a variety of citrullinated peptides.
The
decorated NETs can then induce other cytokines, IL-6, IL-8, and
chemokines. Citrullinated proteins bound to NETs or released
from neutrophils due to bacterial products like leukotoxin A can
also serve as antigens to drive ACPA production, cytokine release,
or immune complex formation.

### Natural Killer Cells

NK cells are also present in RA synovium.
A potential role in RA
is suggested by an RA-associated gene variant of
NKG2D,
which
is a C-lectin receptor present on NK cells (as well as some additional immune cell subsets).
Cytotoxic NK cells contain large
amounts of granzymes, which are serine proteases. One potentially important immunoregulatory role of NK cells is that they
can stimulate B cells to produce RFs. A subset of NK cells that
expresses large amounts of CD56 is abundant in RA synovial tissue and fluid.
These cells produce cytokines like GM-CSF
and enhance pro-inflammatory cytokine production by T lymphocytes and macrophages. IL-32 exacerbates arthritis and joint
damage in collagen-induced arthritis, possibly by increasing the
number of synovial NK cells.
Less information is available on
invariant NK T cells (iNKTs), which respond to lipid antigens.

However, blocking CD1d and iNKT function decreases the severity of collagen-induced arthritis in mice.

### Bone Marrow Cells

Subcortical bone and bone marrow likely contribute to synovial
inflammatory responses in RA. Primitive bone marrow mesenchymal cells can traverse cortical bone through pores in murine collagen-induced arthritis before the onset of clinical disease, take up
residence in the synovium, and produce mediators that enhance
synovitis.
The cell migration is TNF-dependent and is abrogated in mice that lack TNF receptors. CD34
mesenchymal cells
in rheumatoid bone marrow are more highly activated as judged
by their NFκ
B status and their ability to differentiate into fibroblast-like cells that produce MMPs and proangiogenic factors.
Bone marrow also can contribute other relevant cells, including
macrophage lineage cells that migrate to the synovium and nurselike cells that support the survival of B cells. Just as the bone marrow can influence the synovium, the reverse is also true. Invasive
pannus can rupture through cortical bone and invade the marrow
space in some patients. When this occurs, B cell aggregates are
especially prominent in the marrow and occur in an environment
rich in B cell chemoattractants such as CCL21, and B cell survival factors such as BLyS B cells enriched in the subchondral and
endosteal bone marrow areas were found adjacent to osteocalcin
osteoblasts in two murine RA models and expressed high levels of
osteoblast inhibitors CCL3 and TNF, inhibiting osteoblast differentiation by activating extra-cellular signal-regulated kinase
(ERK) and NFκ
B signaling pathways.
The bone marrow changes in RA could be either a result of
synovitis or a primary event.
The most common explanation
for RA is that synovitis occurs first and expands into the marrow through soluble factors and direct extension (the “outsidein” hypothesis). The notion that bone marrow reactivity might
be a primary event and that the activated cells migrate directly to
the synovium through cortical bone or via the bloodstream has
received increasing attention (the “inside-out” hypothesis). The
bidirectional interactions between the synovium and the bone
marrow suggest that both inside-out and outside-in mechanisms
participate in RA.

### Synovial Biology at Various Stages of Rheumatoid Arthritis

The observation that autoantibody levels are elevated in patients
with RA many years before the clinical arthritis manifests also supports the notion that a pre-clinical phase can precede symptomatic
synovitis. In addition, profiling of cytokine and chemokine levels
in stored serum samples of people in whom RA ultimately develops shows increased levels of chemokines and cytokines, especially
in the ACPA
patient population.
This finding reinforces the
concept that RA is a continuum that can progress to clinical synovitis. However, the environmental stimuli and stochastic events
that cause progression are not known.
In murine models, synovial inflammation and transcription
factor activation are present before clinical synovitis is apparent.
In contrast, synovial biopsies from high-risk individuals
with arthralgia but no evidence of arthritis are largely normal,
even if they are ACPA
and/or RF
and progressed to bona fide
RA within 2 years. There was a trend toward higher numbers of
CD8
T cells in the synovium but no other apparent increase in
cellular infiltrate or cytokine production.
During the first few weeks of symptomatic RA, the synovium
occasionally demonstrates a paucity of lymphocyte infiltration,
endothelial cell injury, tissue edema, and neutrophil accumulation; in general, it has a histologic appearance similar to longstanding disease. The extent of lymphoid aggregation, T cell
infiltration, and synovial-lining hyperplasia can resemble chronic
disease even when symptoms have been present for a short period.
The cytokine patterns of these biopsies as determined by immunohistochemical analysis indicated similar levels of T cell factors
(such as IFNγ
) and non–T cell factors (such as IL-1 and TNF).
The tumor suppressor gene
p53
is also expressed in early-stage RA,
likely as a result of intense oxidative stress.
Biopsy specimens of asymptomatic joints from patients with
early- or late-stage RA also show lymphocyte infiltration, cytokine production, and
p53
expression. Although IFNγ
, IL-1, and
TNF levels are increased compared with normal synovium, they
are modestly lower than in clinically active joints. The levels of
some cytokines such as IL-8 and the number of macrophages are
higher in the painful joints. Macrophage and plasma cell infiltration in early RA might predict more erosive or severe disease.
Pre-RA treatment with rituximab was evaluated in the PRAIRI
study and modestly delayed transition to RA.
However, it did
not ultimately prevent disease. Recent data suggest that abatacept
treatment in pre-RA might delay or even prevent the transition to
classifiable RA.
Behavior modification to eliminate risk factors
like smoking in high-risk populations is another approach that
might have an impact on evolution from pre-RA to early RA.
Understanding the distinct mucosal, systemic, and synovial
mechanisms that contribute to risk for RA and are responsible for
transitions will be a critical step for disease interception. Distinct
mechanisms could be responsible for each stage and location. For
example, epigenetic data suggest that cell imprinting, at least in
FLS, is plastic and can change over time. This creates opportunities for “stage-specific” interventions that address pathogenic
mechanisms characteristic for early versus long-standing disease.
Ultimately biomarkers that serve as prognostic markers and stratify individuals based on patient-specific pathogenic pathways will
be critical.
The amount of rheumatoid synovial transcriptome data that
has accumulated in recent years is daunting and has contributed
to enormous progress to understanding pathogenesis. Even now,
however, the precise mechanisms of disease are not fully defined,
and it is more likely that there are multiple pathogenic pathways
leading to a common clinical phenotype. The role of immune
responses to modified peptides at mucosal surfaces and the migration of this response to the synovium is a likely critical step in
the transition from pre-RA to clinical disease. These adaptive
immune responses by T cells and B cells are antigen specific, albeit
to altered proteins in the initial stages, and set in motion a multistep process that requires additional “hit(s)” to initiate synovitis.
Preventing protein modification or inducing tolerance is an attractive approach for disease prevention or treatment. Importantly,
adaptive immunity does not work in isolation, and a robust innate
response involving macrophage and fibroblast (and perhaps DC,
mast cell, and NK cell) activation plays an integral role in disease
perpetuation. Mechanisms are diverse and can depend on when in
the pre-RA to RA continuum a patient happens to reside, which
cells are most important at that time, and what antigens are the
most important.
As tempting as it may be, reducing the RA biology and
response to therapy to synovial histologic patterns and cell lineage infiltration has had limited capacity to predict outcomes

or clinical benefit to targeted agents to date. This might not be
surprising, as mechanisms can vary between patients, between
locations (mucosa versus blood versus synovium), over time, and
even between joints. Stratification algorithms to determine for
individualized treatments will likely require integrated approaches
genomic, epigenomic, behavioral, and environmental influences.

## Synovial Fluid and the Synovial Fluid Cartilage Interface

KEY POINTS

> RA synovial effusions contain neutrophils and mononuclear cells,

> including T lymphocytes, B cells, and macrophages.

> Immune complexes that contain autoantibodies such as RFs or ACPAs

> can fix complement, leading to the generation of chemoattractants

> that recruit inflammatory cells into the joint.

> Small molecule mediators of inflammation such as prostaglandins and

> leukotrienes are present in RA synovial fluid.

Synovial effusions accumulate in the joints of most patients with
active RA. An inverse relationship exists between the molecular
weight of proteins and their concentrations in minimally inflamed
synovial fluid. High-molecular-weight serum proteins gain access
more easily to synovial fluid in inflamed joints, and the relatively
high concentration of IgG in RA synovial fluid is good evidence for
local synthesis of immunoglobulins. Markedly increased permeance
of proteins in patients with rheumatoid disease is due to microvascular damage in rheumatoid synovitis. Assays for autoantibodies or
assessments of exudate versus transudate in synovial fluid generally
have little value; the concentration of serum proteins in effusions is
usually about half to two-thirds the level in the blood.
Evaluation of the synovial fluid proteome in RA using mass
spectroscopy provides some insights into the most important
mediators. More than 400 proteins in RA effusions have been
identified as potential biomarkers of disease activity, including
C-reactive protein and six members of the S-100 family of calcium granule–binding proteins.
Additional studies show higher
levels of proteins related to complement activation, as well as
MMPs and neutrophil-derived proteins. None of these is a particular surprise given the genes expressed by the synovial intimal lining and the abundance of neutrophils in RA fluid. Perhaps more
interesting, the “citrullinome” of RA fluids—that is, the pattern of
protein citrullination—showed about 3000 different citrullinated
peptides, including multiple peptides from vimentin, fibrinogen,
and
α
-enolase.
In another study, immune-mediated membranolytic pathways mediated by perforin and the membrane attack
complex of complement, both of which are hallmarks of RA effusions, markedly increased citrullination of neutrophils.

### Synovial Fluid Neutrophils

Large numbers of polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMNs) accumulate in rheumatoid synovial effusions and enter via postcapillary venules in the synovium. Neutrophils adhere to activated
synovial microvasculature as a result of their abundant surface
expression of selectins and the
β
2 integrins. After adherence, chemotactic agents produced by endothelium and resident synovial
cells facilitate exit from the intravascular space into the tissue.
Thus considering the survival time of PMNs in synovial fluid, an
average rheumatoid effusion containing 25,000 PMNs per mm
could exceed 1 billion cells each day. The ultimate fate of these
cells is usually apoptosis or NETosis with release of DNA and
citrullinated peptides. Neutrophil survival requires expression of
the forkhead transcription factor gene
FOXO3.
Mice that lack
this gene are resistant to inflammatory arthritis because of a shortened PMN life span.
High concentrations of chemotactic agents in the synovial fluid
in RA recruit a large number of cells to the intra-articular cavity.
Few PMNs are seen in the synovium itself; once in the tissue, they
move rapidly to the synovial fluid, drawn by agents such as C5a
that are generated as a result of complement activation, LTB4,
platelet-activating factor, and chemokines. CXC chemokines
including ENA-78 and IL-8 are especially abundant in synovial
fluid and can attract neutrophils into the intra-articular space. RA
PMNs can also release chemokines such as macrophage-inflammatory protein (MIP)-3
α
into the milieu that promote migration
of additional cells into the joint space.
Once in the joint, neutrophils engage immune complexes through
Fc receptors, especially FcR
γ
I and FcR
γ
III, which activate spleen
tyrosine kinase (Syk). Receptor ligation initiates a signaling cascade that includes the mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs)
and NFκ
B, cytoskeletal reorganization, release of granule content,
generation of reactive oxygen and nitrogen species, and enhanced
phagocytosis. Many of the cells contain immune complexes within
phagosomes that include IgG and IgM along with RF and complement proteins such as C1q, C3, and C4. PMNs from synovial fluid
in RA release de novo synthesized proteins including matrix proteins
such as fibronectin, neutral proteinases, and IL-1. Neutrophils also
secrete IL-1Ra and oncostatin M, which is a member of the IL-6
family. Although the amount of IL-1Ra each neutrophil produces is
low compared with macrophages, the sheer number of PMNs allows
them to produce large amounts in synovial effusions.
Neutrophils also release numerous proteases that can adversely
affect the lubricating properties of synovial fluid and the integrity of
the cartilage, including elastase and trypsin. Neutrophil collagenase
(MMP-8) can digest native collagen in cartilage, which then makes
it susceptible to degradation by other MMPs, such as the stromelysins. Synoviocytes are generally considered major producers of
the MMPs, but animal models of arthritis suggest that neutrophilderived proteases also play a significant role in cartilage damage.

### Synovial Fluid Mast Cells

Mast cells and mast cells mediators such as histamine and tryptase
are present in RA synovial fluid (SF). The activation of mast cells
can also induce NETs formation. Human synovial mast cells from
RA patients produced greater amounts of PGD2 via the PTGS2-
miR-199a-3p axis compared with mast cells from OA patients.

### Synovial Fluid Lymphocytes

The lymphocyte subsets in synovial fluid differ from those of
peripheral blood and synovial tissue. Even though synovial effusions contain an abundance of T cells, the CD4 to CD8 ratio is
actually reversed compared with that of blood or synovial tissue,
with an excess of CD8
cells relative to CD4
lymphocytes. In
addition, synovial tissue is nearly devoid of neutrophils, which
often constitute 50% to 75% of synovial fluid cells. The percentage of Tregs (CD4
CD25
) is also higher in synovial fluid than
in peripheral blood.

Synovial fluid T cells generally express high levels of surface
HLA-DR antigens. Surface activation antigens are increased on
synovial fluid lymphocytes, including VLA-1 and CD69. Of
CD4
cells in rheumatoid synovial fluid, most are memory cells
and express CD45RO on their surface. Despite the activated phenotype, synovial fluid T cell function is usually less than peripheral
blood cells. For instance, synovial fluid lymphocyte proliferation
in response to mitogens or most recall antigens such as tetanus
toxoid is significantly less than that of paired blood T lymphocytes. Mycobacterial antigens and the 60 kDa heat shock protein
(HSP) are exceptions because proliferation is actually greater in
synovial fluid cells, perhaps because they can also activate TLRs.
Cytokine production by synovial fluid T cells in vitro is also low,
including mitogen-induced expression of IFNγ
and IL-1.
Anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1Ra and TGFβ
contribute to the decreased responsiveness of synovial fluid T cells.
Nonspecific components of joint effusions such as hyaluronic
acid can indirectly suppress T cell activation. Diminished T cell
activation also could be due to defective TCR signaling. Articular T cells have diminished tyrosine phosphorylation of proteins
after stimulation, especially the key signal transduction pathway
p38 MAPK. Furthermore, tyrosine phosphorylation of the TCR
ζ
chain, an early event in TCR signaling, is low compared with
peripheral blood T cells. The hyporesponsiveness of synovial fluid
T cells correlates with a significant decrease in the levels of the
intra-cellular redox-regulating agent glutathione.
Restoration
of intra-cellular glutathione increases proliferation of RA synovial
fluid T cells. Therefore oxidative stress in the inflamed environment can suppress antigen-specific T cell responses.

### Synovial Fluid Fibroblasts

Fibroblasts are present in RA SF and can be expanded in tissue
culture. They have presumably been shed from the synovial intimal lining and might serve as surrogates for tissue disaggregation as they are easier to obtain. The cells that emerge are, like
traditional cultured FLS, CD34-CD45-PDPN
CD90
and can
produce pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-1
β
and IL-6.
Like
traditional synovial tissue–derived FLS, they also can invade the
matrix of cartilage explants in the SCID mouse model.

### Synovial Fluid Platelets and Platelet Microparticles

Platelets are a rich source of growth factors and cytokines, including PDGF. They are present in rheumatoid synovial effusions and
can form microparticles that contain immune complexes and are
present in high concentrations compared with OA effusions.
Some of these particles can display citrullinated peptides, which
can increase innate and adaptive responses and require collagen
receptor glycoprotein VI.
The microparticles activate cultured
synoviocytes and, through an IL-1–dependent mechanism, stimulate cytokine secretion. Platelet depletion also suppresses arthritis
severity in animal models.

### Intra-articular Immune Complexes and Complement Fixation

Synovial Fluid Immune Complexes
Complexes containing Igs are abundant in the blood and synovial
fluid of patients with RA, especially those containing IgM and
citrullinated proteins. The most prevalent antigens in these aggregates contain IgG bound to RF as a result of their ability to bind
the Fc portion of Ig, as well as ACPAs and citrullinated peptide
complexes. Use of more sensitive techniques has revealed that circulating immune complexes in RA contain up to 20 polypeptides,
including albumin, Ig, complement, type II collagen, fibrinogen,
and acute-phase reactants, as well as DNA. These complexes in
synovium and also in the circulation or mucosal sites can potentially fix complement, releasing chemotactic peptides and factors
that activate neutrophils and other inflammatory cells.
The aggregates of RF, immunoglobulin, ACPAs, and various
peptides can embed into cartilage and other tissues that are in
contact with synovial fluids. Electron microscopic studies demonstrate immunoglobulin complexes with damage to the cartilage
matrix in the microenvironment. Immune complexes are absent
under areas of cartilage invaded actively by synovial pannus, suggesting that phagocytic cells in the invasive synovium bind to and
ingest the immune complexes. Rheumatoid cartilage contains
more than 40-fold more IgM and more than 10-fold more IgG
than healthy cartilage extracts. IgM RF is found in the majority
of RA cartilage extracts but not in OA or healthy control subject
extracts. In addition, more than 60% of the RA cartilage extracts
are positive for native and denatured type II collagen antibody.

Synovial Fluid Complement
The liver is the major source of complement synthesis in humans,
and passive transfer of serum proteins into effusions accounts for
many of the complement proteins found there. However, synovial
tissue also actively produces many complement proteins, including C2 and C3. Macrophages and fibroblasts produce complement proteins under the influence of cytokines such as IFNγ
,
IL-1, and TNF. Synovial tissue analysis shows that complement
genes from the classic pathway are expressed in RA, as well as in
healthy synovium.
Despite the local production of complement components,
the levels of C4, C2, and C3 in rheumatoid synovial effusions
are lower compared with synovial fluids from other arthritides.
Although the most prominent evidence of activation implicates
the classic pathway, cleavage products of the alternate pathway,
including factor B and properdin, also occur in RA. IgM RF
appears to be a more important determinant of complement activation than IgG RF in serum and synovial fluid, and accelerated
catabolism of C4 in RA and the presence of C4 fragments in the
plasma correlate with titers of IgM RF.
Neutrophil lysosomal lysates contain enzymes that cleave complement proteins and generate chemotactic activity, such as C5a
from serum. C5a, in addition to being a principal chemotactic
factor in inflammatory effusions, mediates lysosomal release from
human PMNs and induces cytoskeletal rearrangement. The chemotactic anaphylatoxins C3a and C5a are often present in rheumatoid effusions, as are the terminal complement components
that comprise the C5b–C9 membrane attack complex.
Targeting Complement
Inhibiting complement proteins has therapeutic potential in RA.
Several pre-clinical models support this, such as intra-articular
treatment in the rat antigen-induced arthritis model where soluble
complement receptor (sCR1) inhibits joint swelling. C5-deficient
mice have markedly decreased joint inflammation in collageninduced arthritis and the K/BxN serum transfer model. Absence
of C3 or factor B also inhibits collagen-induced arthritis. Unlike
the C5 knockout mice, which have normal antibody responses,

C3- and factor B–null animals have lower levels of anti–type II
collagen antibodies in that model.
These data suggest that classic and alternative complement
pathways participate in inflammatory arthritis. For instance,
effective therapy of RA with either methotrexate or a TNF inhibitor decreases circulating levels of the terminal complement complex.
However, an anti-C5 antibody demonstrated only modest
evidence of clinical efficacy in a controlled RA clinical trial.
Similarly, a C5a receptor antagonist did not decrease inflammation in a short-term placebo-controlled synovial biopsy study,
even though the compound was effective in pre-clinical models.
Recent approval of the C5a inhibitor avacopan in ANCA-associated arthritis might lead to further studies in other diseases or
stimulate other approaches to complement inhibition for RA.

### Polyunsaturated Fatty Acid (PUFA) Metabolites

Prostaglandins
Arachidonic acid belongs to omega-6-PUFA, and its metabolites
are produced in the inflamed joint via oxidation by cyclooxygenases (COXs) to prostaglandins and thromboxanes or by lipoxygenases (LOXs) to leukotrienes (LTs). COX inhibitors are clearly
effective in RA, although the effect is modest and is due to combination of local synovial effects and spinal analgesic action. Animal
models of arthritis are variably sensitive to prostaglandin; indomethacin almost completely prevents adjuvant arthritis in rats but
has minimal benefit in collagen-induced arthritis in mice.
Stable prostaglandins, especially PGE2, produce vasodilation,
increase vascular permeability, and are involved centrally in fever.
Synovial fibroblast production of prostaglandins together with
inflammatory factors drive HBEGF
inflammatory macrophages
that promote fibroblast invasiveness. They also display some antiinflammatory activities that could account for limited efficacy. For
example, misoprostol, which is a prostaglandin analogue, has modest but significant anti-inflammatory or immunomodulatory effects.
Physiologic concentrations of PGE2 inhibit IFNγ
production by T
cells, HLA-DR expression by macrophages, and T cell proliferation.
Production of prostaglandins in RA depends on both COX-1
and COX-2 (see
Chapter 27
). The former is constitutively
expressed and is responsible for the normal endogenous production of prostaglandins in the joint, as well as in other tissues.
Cytokines such as IL-1 and TNF induce COX-2 gene expression by cultured synoviocytes and macrophages. COX-2 mRNA
and protein are increased in RA synovium.
Much of the antiinflammatory activity and analgesia in RA results from COX-2
inhibition. Targeting prostaglandin receptors such as E2 or E4 or
prostaglandin E synthases is an alternative approach that might
have less deleterious effects on the gastric mucosa and cardiovascular disease. The PGE receptor 4 (EP4) can also act on DCs and
T cells to enhance production of Th1 and Th17 cells.
PGI2, like PGE2, can also contribute to synovial inflammation. For instance, mice lacking the PGI2 receptor have significantly decreased arthritis severity in the collagen-induced arthritis
model compared with wild-type mice, even though they make
similar amounts of anticollagen antibodies.
Leukotrienes
LTBs are potent pro-inflammatories produced by neutrophils and
chemotactic for neutrophils, eosinophils, and macrophages. They
also promote neutrophil aggregation and adherence to endothelium.
Peripheral blood PMNs from patients with rheumatoid disease have
an enhanced capacity for LTB4 production compared with healthy
people.
In murine collagen-induced arthritis, LTB4 antagonists
decreased paw swelling and joint destruction, suggesting a pivotal
role for this potent chemoattractant. Unfortunately, LTB4 blockade
has limited minimal efficacy in RA, and this suggests that they might
contribute but do not drive inflammation.
Specialized Proresolving Mediators
Certain arachidonic acid metabolites such as 15-Deoxy-delta12,14-prostaglandin J2 (15d-PGJ2) activate peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs) and inhibit cytokine production
and inflammation in animal models of arthritis. Cyclopentenone
prostaglandins can also inhibit NFκ
B by blocking one of the
enzymes that activates this pathway, namely I
κ
B kinaseβ
(IKK
β
).
LXs and resolvins also represent a unique class of lipid mediators
that help resolve inflammatory diseases. LXs have a trihydroxytetraene structure and are produced from arachidonic acid via the
LOX pathways. LXA4 binds with high affinity to a G protein–
coupled receptor denoted LXA4 receptor (ALXR). Activation of
ALXR inhibits recruitment of neutrophils by attenuating chemotaxis, adhesion, and transmigration into tissues and by diminishing
chemokine and cytokine production. LXA4 significantly decreased
cytokine and MMP expression in FLS through an NFκ
B–dependent mechanism. Resolvins, omega-3-PUFA–derived metabolites,
are increased in murine arthritic tissues and modulate T cell phenotype. Of interest, blood proresolving mediators are linked with
synovial pathology and are predictive of disease-modifying antirheumatic drug (DMARD) responsiveness in RA.

## Peripheral Blood Lymphocytes

The number of CD4
Th cells is mildly increased in the circulation of patients with RA, with a concomitant decrease in CD8
lymphocytes. The surface phenotype of circulating T cells in RA is
highly variable. For instance, an increased percentage of
αβ
and
γδ
TCR–bearing cells expressing HLA-DR and the adhesion protein
VLA-4 (
α
4
β
1 integrin) has been observed. The latter is especially
important because VLA-4 helps cells enter the synovium through
interactions with VCAM-1 on endothelial cells. Other markers
of activation are not always elevated on RA T cells in the circulation. Therefore peripheral-blood T cells express some, but not
all, phenotypic characteristics of prior activation. It is not clear
whether this occurs in the peripheral or central lymphoid organs
or whether cells are activated in the synovium and re-enter the
circulation via the synovial lymphatics
As noted for articular T cells, RA peripheral lymphocytes also
have impaired responses to some antigens. For example, IFNγ
and IL-2 production is significantly suppressed in RA lymphocyte
cultures. Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)–infected B lymphocyte growth
is greater due to a defect in T cell function in RA. The abnormal T
cell response correlates with disease activity, although the abnormality is present in T cells of some patients with inflammatory
arthropathies other than RA.
T cell diversity and maturation are abnormal in RA. Whereas
thymic output normally decreases with age, senescence appears to
be accelerated in RA.
The presence of TCR rearrangement excision circles (TRECs) is a measure of thymic release of mature T
cells. Using this parameter, the thymic output in RA may decline
prematurely. Some evidence points to RA T cell senescence, perhaps due to continued immune stimulation. Telomeres are shorter
in RA T cells, perhaps due to repeated cell divisions. Premature
aging has also been observed in antigen-unprimed naïve T cells,
suggesting alternative mechanisms might contribute.

In addition, DNA surveillance is defective in RA T cells and
contributes to cellular aging.
For example, deficiency of recombination 11 homolog A (MRE11A), which senses double-stranded
DNA breaks and helps amplify DNA repair, occurs in RA T cells
and allows DNA damage to accumulate.
The observation is
analogous to impaired DNA mechanisms previously described for
RA FLS that, along with abnormalities in genes like
ATM
(see earlier), increase invasiveness and arthritogenic potential of T cells.
Abnormal DNA methylation of peripheral blood T cells has also
been observed, especially in the naïve T cell population.
Of interest, DNA methylation patterns in peripheral blood T
cells are quite different from T cells isolated from synovium of
the same patient.
The genes and pathways that are differentially marked primarily in synovium involve a variety of immune
processes. This enrichment could result from preferential recruitment/retention of T cells with abnormal DNA methylation or
local expansion of immunologically active cells in the joint. Treatment of RA with TNF inhibitors also alters the DNA methylation
patterns, especially in pathways associated with T cell activation,
and correlates with clinical response.
Activated B lymphocytes in peripheral blood appear to traffic
between mucosa and synovium, with evidence of affinity maturation
of RFs and ACPAs. The number of circulating B cells that spontaneously produce RF and other autoantibodies is significantly higher in
people with RA compared with those without disease. B cells that are
enriched in autoantibody production are characterized by a surface
determinant CD5. This antigen is normally expressed by T cells but
is also displayed by fetal B cells and a small number of immature
B cells in adults. In addition, regulatory B cells that produce IL-10
are reduced in patients with RA. Treatment with TNF inhibitors
increases the number of these cells in the peripheral circulation and
decreases expression of the activation marker CD69 on B cells.
Elevated IgA- and IgG-producing plasmablasts are also increased in
the blood of people at high risk of developing RA.
Patients with RA who have normal circulating numbers of
lymphocytes show an abnormal
κ
-toλ

with control subjects. Alterations in gene usage are consistent with
oligoclonal B cell proliferation, which was confirmed by single-cell
sequencing studies. As noted above, barcode-enabled sequencing
of peripheral blood plasmablasts demonstrates that the antibodies
produced by these cells bind to citrullinated proteins. Increased
plasmablasts in blood can precede B cell repopulation and disease
flare after treatment with anti-CD20 antibody.

KEY POINTS

> Multiple T cell subsets have been implicated in the pathogenesis of RA,

> including novel cell subsets that have been defined by synovial cell

> transcriptome studies.

> Relatively low levels of many T cell cytokines are present in RA

> synovium compared with macrophage or fibroblast products.

> The T cell cytokines that are present, such as IFN-

> γ

> and IL-17, can

> contribute to the pathogenesis of RA and can be produced by Th1

> cells or Th17 cells, respectively.

> Treg cell function, which suppresses activation of other T cells, might be

> low in RA synovium.

> T cells can also contribute to synovial inflammation through antigen-

> independent mechanisms such as direct cell–cell contact with

> macrophages and synoviocytes.

The cytokine milieu in RA is rich in factors produced by many cell
lineages. Many factors produced by T lymphocytes are surprisingly low in RA, whereas those generated by macrophages and by
synovial fibroblasts are markedly increased (
Table 71.2
). Although
low, the T cell cytokines can have profound effects, especially in
the synovial microenvironment. The production and contribution
of T cell–derived cytokines can be examined on the basis of functional lymphocyte subsets.

### Type 1 T Helper Cell Cytokines

Early investigation into the cytokine profile of RA suggested a
bias toward the Th1 phenotype, cells characterized by the production of cytokines such as IL-2 and IFNγ
, and expression of the
chemokine receptors CXCR3 and CCR5. Th cell differentiation
might be driven, in part, by local production of IL-7 by stromal
cells and macrophages, which supports the production of Th1 and
Th17 cells. Unexpectedly, lower serum IL-7 levels in patients with
early-stage inflammatory arthritis are associated with progression to
RA.
Many T cell clones derived from RA synovial tissue produce
a Th1 cytokine pattern. Considerable data have accumulated on the
relative abundance and function of the prototypic Th1 cytokine,
IFNγ
, which is the most potent inducer of HLA-DR and enhances
antigen presentation. IFNγ
also induces adhesion molecules on the
surface of endothelial cells and can help recruit inflammatory cell
accumulation at sites of injury. One important function of IFNγ
is
its capacity to alter the balance of extra-cellular matrix synthesis and
degradation by decreasing collagen synthesis and inhibiting MMP
production by cytokine-stimulated cultured FLS.
Relatively low concentrations of IFNγ
are found in RA joints.
Immunohistochemical analysis clearly demonstrates IFNγ
protein
in RA synovial T cells, although the percentage of cells containing
IFNγ
is less than in tonsils. However, the role of IFNγ
is quite
complex because IFNγ
knockout mice or IFNγ
receptor deficiency exacerbates collagen-induced arthritis in mice. This serves as
a reminder that the effects of cytokines are often context dependent.
Another major Th1 cytokine, IL-2, is a T cell–derived cytokine that serves as an autocrine or paracrine T cell growth factor. IL-2 is detected in only a small percentage of RA synovial
effusions and synovial tissues and, when present, is only found
in low concentrations.
The relative absence of IL-2 could be
pro-inflammatory because of decreased numbers of CD25
Tregs.
TNF, GM-CSF, and IL-6 can be expressed by Th1 and Th2 cells
and are abundant in synovial fluid and produced by RA synovial
tissue. Some data suggest that T cells derived from disaggregated
RA synovial tissue express TNF using single cell RNAseq. However, the primary sources of these cytokines in the rheumatoid
joint are macrophages and fibroblasts.

### Type 17 T Helper Cytokines

Th17 cells have been implicated in autoimmunity, especially
psoriasis and spondyloarthritis, and are present in RA synovial
effusions. Although IL-17 protein is present in some sublining
T cells, mast cells appear to be an important alternative source.
The pro-inflammatory cytokine produced by Th17 cells, IL-17, is
encoded by six genes (IL-17A through F). IL-17A and, to a certain
extent, IL-17F mimic and synergize with IL-1 and TNF effects on
FLS, including induction of collagenase and cytokine production.
IL-17A is present in modest but functionally relevant concentrations in RA synovial effusions.
IL-17 receptors IL-17RA and
IL-17RC are expressed by synoviocytes, activate the transcription
factor NFκ
B, and initiate an inflammatory cascade.
IL-21 is also produced by RA T cells, which can serve to further
enhance Th17 cell numbers and IL-17 production and induce MMP
production by FLS and activate osteoclasts.
Similarly, IL-22 produced by Th17 cells is present in RA synovial effusions. Like IL17A, IL-22 activates FLS to produce osteoclast-activating factors
such as RANKL. Animal models of arthritis demonstrate that IL-17
inhibition is anti-inflammatory and protects animals from bone and
cartilage destruction.
IL-1, IL-23, and/or TGFβ
in the joint can
potentially enhance Th17 cell differentiation in humans.
The contribution of the IL-17 family to inflammation is
impressive in animal models of arthritis, in which blocking IL17A in particular has dramatic effects on inflammation and matrix
destruction. The results in RA are less robust, and clinical trials
with IL-17 blockers show only modest improvement compared
with TNF inhibitors.
This contrasts with psoriasis or psoriatic
arthritis, in which anti–IL-17A antibody is more impressive.
Alternative approaches that inhibit both IL-17A and F, such as
soluble receptors, do not increase efficacy.

### Type 9 Helper T Cell and Innate Lymphoid Cell Cytokines

T cells differentiate to the Th9 phenotype under the influence of
cytokines like IL-4 and TGBβ
. The transcription factors STAT6,
IFN regulatory factor 4 (IRF4), and GATA3 are required for this
transition. The prototypical Th9 cytokine is IL-9, which is found
in RA synovial fluid and synovial tissue, as well as in inflammatory bowel disease.
This glycosylated polypeptide is part of the
IL-7/IL-9 family and binds to receptors on a wide variety of cells,
including T and B lymphocytes.
RA T cells express IL-9 receptors, and exposure to the cytokine promotes T cell survival and
proliferation.
In addition to Th9 cells, type 2 ILCs can produce IL-9 and can
regulate the proliferation and activation of Tregs. In the absence
of IL-9 in mice, chronic arthritis develops, and treatment with
IL-9 facilitates ILC-dependent Treg proliferation. RA synovial tissue contains IL-9–expressing ILCs, especially in individuals whose
disease is in remission.

### T Helper Cell Cytokine Imbalance

The presence of Th1, Th9, and Th17 cells, as well as the recently
described Tph cell described above, in the rheumatoid synovium
suggests that they play a role in the initiation and perpetuation of
RA or perhaps in response to tissue damage caused by the disease.
Th2 cytokines and cellular responses that normally suppress Th1
activation are nearly absent, so this counter-regulatory mechanism
cannot mediate disease resolution. This concept is supported by the
observations that exogenous IL-10 or IL-4 suppresses synthesis of
pro-inflammatory cytokines and MMPs by cultured RA synovial
tissue explants and cells.
In addition, IL-10 and IL-4 increase
release of other anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1Ra by
synovial cells. Although IL-10 protein is present in RA synovial fluid
and the gene is expressed by synovial tissue cells, in vitro studies of
cultured synovial cells suggest that not enough IL-10 is produced to
suppress the limited amounts of IFNγ
produced even further.
The notion that Th1 and Th17 cytokines initiate and perpetuate arthritis, in part as a result of the influence of IL-7, IL-9, IL-21,
and IL-22, whereas Th2 cytokines are suppressive, is supported by
studies in animal models. For instance, IL-4 and IL-10 administered individually have a modest affect in collagen-induced
arthritis, but combination treatment was more impressive.
The complexity of cytokine networks in inflammatory arthritis is
underscored by studies on the role of IL-12 in collagen-induced
arthritis. In early-stage arthritis, IL-12 administration increases
the incidence of collagen-induced arthritis, whereas anti–IL-12 is
beneficial.
However, in late-stage disease, IL-12 administration
suppresses arthritis, and anti–IL-12 causes an exacerbation.
Therapeutic approaches aimed at increasing Th2 cells or cytokines have thus far been unsuccessful. For example, a clinical
trial using IL-10 in RA did not demonstrate significant clinical
benefit or improvement in histologic evidence of synovial inflammation.
It is possible that combinations of Th2 cytokines will
be required to coordinate a maximum effect. Inhibitors of T cell
cytokine signaling through JAK3 demonstrate efficacy in RA.
However, many of these compounds also block JAK1, and synovial biopsy studies suggest that this is the most important JAK for
treatment of RA. Notably, selective JAK1 inhibitors like upadacitinib are as effective as drugs that block multiple JAK isoforms.
The precise role of individual T cell subsets is not clear and
could also vary with the stage of disease and vary between patients.
Newer single-cell technology can potentially identify rare subsets
and might provide clues to enable targeting of individual T cell
populations.

## Macrophage and Fibroblast Cytokines

KEY POINTS

> Macrophage and fibroblast cytokines are abundant in RA synovium.

> Cytokine networks involve pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1, TNF,

> IL-6, IL-15, IL-18, GM-CSF, and IL-33. These and many other factors

> help perpetuate synovial inflammation.

> Chemokines that recruit inflammatory cells into the joint are commonly

> produced by macrophages and fibroblasts.

> Cytokines with anti-inflammatory activity such as IL-1Ra, IFN-

> β

> , and

> IL-10 are produced in rheumatoid synovium, although in amounts

> insufficient to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine function or

> production.

Macrophage and fibroblast pro-inflammatory mediators are abundant in the RA synovium, and many have been successfully targeted with biologic therapeutics. Macrophages, in particular, are
vigorous producers of cytokines, and fibroblasts produce many of

the proteases. Studies that profile blood cytokines and chemokines
indicate that these cytokines and chemokines increase many years
before the onset of clinical disease.

### Pro-inflammatory Macrophage and Fibroblast Cytokines

IL-1 Family
The IL-1 family is a ubiquitous group of polypeptides with a wide
range of biologic activity; they include IL-1
α
, IL-1
β
, IL-1Ra (see
below), IL-18, and IL-33. Abundant pre-clinical data indicate that
IL-1 can serve as a key regulatory factor in inflammatory arthritis. For instance, recombinant IL-1
β
induces the accumulation of
PMNs and mononuclear leukocytes in the joint space and the
loss of proteoglycan from articular cartilage when injected directly
into rabbit knee joints. Transgenic mice that overexpress IL-1 also
develop inflammatory arthritis, whereas mice that lack the natural
IL-1 antagonist IL-1Ra have increased susceptibility to collageninduced arthritis. In most cases, IL-1 blockade in animal models
modestly decreases synovial inflammation and markedly diminishes bone and cartilage destruction.
Interleukin-1.
Synovial macrophages are a prolific source of
IL-1 in the rheumatoid joint, and nearly half of all macrophages
in the RA synovium express IL-1
β
.
IL-1 protein is highly
expressed by synovial lining macrophages adjacent to FLS and in
sublining macrophages near blood vessels. IL-1 in the lining can
subsequently activate FLS to proliferate and secrete a variety of
mediators. A broad range of stimuli induce IL-1 production by
macrophages; for example, immunoglobulin Fc fragments and, to
a lesser extent, immune complexes can generate IL-1 production
by rheumatoid synovial macrophages. Collagen fragments can
induce IL-1 production, and type IX collagen in articular cartilage
is a potent inducer of IL-1 by monocytes.
Within the rheumatoid joint, IL-1 induces fibroblast proliferation; stimulates the synthesis of IL-6, IL-8, and GM-CSF by synovial cells; and enhances collagenase and prostaglandin production.
It increases glycosaminoglycan release in human synovial fibroblast cultures, although the effect of IL-1 on the production of
intact proteoglycan molecules by intact articular cartilage explants
can be the opposite. IL-1 induces a number of adhesion molecules
on FLS and endothelial cells, including VCAM-1 and ICAM-1,
and enhances bone resorption.
IL-1 has been implicated in RA, but inhibition of this mediator using various IL-1–targeted biologic agents has only modest
clinical benefit. Even combinations of IL-1 and TNF-directed
therapy do not provide significant benefit beyond TNF blockade
alone despite biologic effects on acute-phase reactants and rates of
infection.
In contrast, diseases with a well-defined role for IL-1,
such as the autoinflammatory diseases, have remarkable efficacy.
One explanation for the relatively modest benefit of IL-1 in RA
relates to its signaling mechanisms. IL-1 activates NFκ
B through
the kinase MyD88, which is the same pathway as TLRs. When
IL-1 signaling is blocked, TLR ligands in the synovium, including
exogenous ones such as peptidoglycan or endogenous ones such as
HSPs, can potentially overcome IL-1 blockade. This concept was
confirmed in the K/BxN serum transfer model, in which small
amounts of the TLR4 ligand LPS can induce aggressive disease
in mice that lack IL-1 receptors. Thus IL-1 blockade might not
control RA synovitis as long as TLR signaling remains intact.
Interleukin-18.
IL-18, which is another member of the IL-1
family, has also been implicated in RA. In collagen-induced arthritis, IL-18 inhibition significantly attenuates disease.
Subsequent
studies showed that IL-18 induces GM-CSF, nitric oxide (NO)
production, and TNF expression by synovial macrophages. IL-18
is expressed by RA synovial tissue, especially by synovial fibroblasts and macrophages, and its production is markedly increased
by TNF and IL-1
β
. A natural inhibitor, IL-18 binding protein,
could potentially be used as a therapeutic agent. However, other
IL-18–directed approaches, like anti-IL-1 therapeutics, have been
disappointing in RA. For example, IL-1 convertase inhibitors have
only a modest benefit, even though they block IL-18 processing
required to produce the active cytokine.
Interleukin-33 and Other Alarmins.
IL-33 signals through
the ST2 surface receptor and is an “alarmin” that provides a danger signal as a result of tissue damage and necrosis with release
of intra-cellular contents. Extra-cellular, but not intra-cellular,
IL-33 signals primarily through the transcription factor NFκ
B
to increase transcription of the pro-inflammatory program.
Blocking IL-33 decreases inflammation in several animal models
of arthritis, including collagen-induced arthritis, antigen-induced
arthritis, and ST2-deficient mice in the K/BxN model.
IL-33
can also bias T cells toward the Th2 phenotype, but the relative
lack of Th2 cells suggests that this is not important in the context
of RA. TLR3 ligands like poly I:C increase IL-33 production by
FLS, and exogenous IL-33 increases FLS RANKL and chemokine
production.
High mobility box 1 (HMGB1) is also highly expressed in
RA joints and can activate pro-inflammatory pathways similar
to other alarmins. Blocking HMGB1 decreases damage and
immune responses in a variety of autoimmune models. It might
contribute to the aggressive RA FLS phenotype, and incubating OA FLS with HMGB1 can recapitulate this behavior.
HMGB1 polymorphisms have been associated with RA in a
Chinese Han population, suggesting that it can contribute to
disease susceptibility.
Tumor Necrosis Factor and the Tumor Necrosis Factor
Superfamily
The TNF superfamily is a broad group of related genes that play a
major role in inflammation, immune responses, cell survival, and
apoptosis. At least 19 ligands in the family have been identified,
with TNF identified as the eponymous member. Each cytokine
has its own preference for cell surface receptor, although there is
some overlap of receptor binding and functional overlap. The TNF
superfamily members exhibit conserved amino acid sequences,
suggesting a single ancestral gene. Many TNF superfamily members include type II membrane protein characteristics and can be
released from cell surfaces after proteolytic cleavage. A C-terminal
conserved domain called the
TNF-homology domain
is also shared
by several superfamily members. The active forms of the proteins
are homotrimers, except for LT
α
and LT
β
, which can form either
heterotrimers or homotrimers.
TNF is a pleiotropic cytokine that has been implicated as a key
pro-inflammatory cytokine that is detected in rheumatoid synovial fluid and serum. It is produced as a membrane-bound protein, primarily by synovial macrophages in RA, which is released
from the cell surface after proteolytic cleavage by TNF convertase,
a membrane MMP. IL-1 and TNF have many similar activities,
including the ability to enhance cytokine production, adhesionmolecule expression, proliferation, and MMP production by
cultured synoviocytes. In some systems, the effects of these two
agents are synergistic. Although they share many functions and
signal transduction pathways, IL-1 and TNF use distinct surface
receptors and intra-cellular signaling pathways.

TNF, like IL-1, stimulates collagenase and PGE2 production
by synoviocytes, induces bone resorption, inhibits bone formation
in vitro, and stimulates resorption of proteoglycan and inhibits its
biosynthesis in explants of cartilage. Animal models have also supported the general role played by TNF in inflammatory arthritis.
For instance, overexpression of TNF in transgenic mice leads to
severe destructive synovitis. In fact, arthritis also spontaneously
occurs in transgenic mice that express only a membrane-bound
form of TNF on T cells.
TNF blockade is an effective antiinflammatory agent in many animal models of arthritis, although
the effects on bone and cartilage destruction are less prominent
than with IL-1 inhibitors.
Similarly, TNF inhibition in RA significantly decreases or prevents joint damage.
The efficacy of TNF inhibitors in RA confirms its role in many
patients with the disease; however, diversity of responses to TNF
inhibitors and other targeted agents also highlights the heterogeneity of disease mechanisms despite similar clinical features. Patients
with an inadequate response to a TNF inhibitor can still respond
to other targeted agents like rituximab or abatacept, which supports the notion that multiple independent pathways can contribute to the pathogenesis of RA and the heterogeneous nature of the
disease. Early therapy with TNF inhibitors can induce long-term
remissions in a limited number of patients even after therapy is
withdrawn. Therefore interventions in the earliest stages of disease
could potentially prevent the establishment of chronic synovitis
and prevent the evolution of epigenetic imprinting or other factors that promote perpetuation.
Patterns of synovial gene expression are associated with a higher
likelihood of a good response to a TNF inhibitor, but the predictive power is relatively minor and not useful for clinical decisions.
Thus far, various algorithms that include autoantibodies,
blood cytokine profiles, synovial tissue TNF levels and histologic
patterns, and TNF promoter polymorphisms have limited capacity for predicting a clinical response to a TNF inhibitor. When a
difference is noted, the magnitude has not enabled clinical decision-making yet. Surprisingly, patients with little or no clinical
improvement still have significant delay or arrest of joint damage
when treated with a TNF inhibitor, most likely due to inhibition
of osteoclast differentiation and activation.
Interleukin-6 Family
IL-6 is a complex cytokine produced by many cell types including T cells, monocytes, and FLS. A striking correlation between
serum IL-6 activity and serum levels of acute-phase reactants such
as C-reactive protein,
α
1-anti-trypsin, fibrinogen, and haptoglobin occurs in patients with RA. Very high levels of IL-6 are present
in RA synovial fluid, and synovial cells in culture from diverse
inflammatory arthropathies produce IL-6. In situ hybridization
of synovial tissue also shows IL-6 mRNA in the intimal lining,
and immunohistochemistry studies show IL-6 protein in the lining and sublining regions. Although many synovial macrophages
express the IL-6 gene, much of the IL-6 in the joint is produced
by synoviocytes in the intimal lining.
The pivotal role of IL-6 in RA was confirmed by efficacy of
monoclonal antibodies that bind to IL-6R or to IL-6 itself. The
clinical responses are similar to TNF inhibitors, including protective effects on bone and cartilage damage.
IL-6R antibody is
also effective in some patients who have not responded to TNF
blockade. Therefore the cytokine network in RA is complex, without a clear hierarchy implicating a single cytokine like TNF at the
apex for all, or even most, patients. Neutropenia, increased liver
enzymes, and altered blood lipids have been observed with IL-6
inhibition, although the relative importance of these observations
is still uncertain. JAK inhibitors have also demonstrated efficacy in
RA, which decreases activation of STAT1 and STAT3 in FLS and
mimics the effect of IL-6 blockade by decreasing IL-6 signaling.
Cytokines with structural similarity to IL-6 and that share
receptor subunits have also been implicated in RA. Several of
these cytokines—IL-11, leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF), and
oncostatin M—are expressed by rheumatoid synovium and can
be detected in synovial effusions. LIF is especially interesting
because it could account for sustained STAT4 activation by RA
FLS.
The biologic effects of these factors are complex and can
be either protective (e.g., by increasing expression of protease
inhibitors such as tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinase [TIMP])
or pro-inflammatory (e.g., by increasing expression of chemokines
or MMPs), depending on the culture conditions or the specific
model evaluated. This dichotomy among the family members is
demonstrated by the fact that IL-11 administration ameliorates
collagen-induced arthritis, whereas antibodies to oncostatin M are
protective.

Interleukin-12 Family
The IL-12 family includes cytokines that play a role in the differentiation of T cells and inflammation. IL-12 is a heterodimeric
cytokine with two subunits (e.g., p35 and p40) encoded on separate genes and produced by antigen-presenting cells. IL-23 and
IL-27 have similar heterodimeric structures to IL-12, with p29/
p40 and p28/EB13 components, respectively. In the context of
antigen presentation, IL-27 can bias T cell responses toward the
Th1 phenotype. IL-23 plays a pivotal role in the production of
Th17 cells in combination with IL-1 and TGFβ
. The IL-12 family members are generally produced by macrophages in the rheumatoid joint in addition to other antigen-presenting cells such
as DCs. Blocking IL-12 or IL-12/23 has limited benefit in RA,
which is consistent with the modest effect of IL-17 blockers and
contrasts with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis.
Interleukin-15
IL-15 is an IL-2–like cytokine that regulates numerous immunologic functions relevant to RA, including T cell chemotaxis and
proliferation, production of immunoglobulins by B cells, and the
generation of NK cells. Although IL-15 can serve as an IL-2–independent mechanism for activating T cells, its role in RA may be
related to its role in TNF regulation. Macrophages are the primary
source of IL-15 in RA, and the cytokine is macrophage TNF production that requires contact with T cells. This network provides
a potential mechanism whereby local IL-15 production in the
synovium can lead to autocrine production of TNF in a T cell–
dependent but antigen-independent fashion. IL-15 is expressed
by RA synovial macrophage cells. The soluble IL-15 receptors can
function as an inhibitor and decreases joint inflammation in collagen-induced arthritis when administered systemically. Clinical trials in RA using an anti–IL-15 antibody showed benefit, although
it was not enough to justify continued development.
Interleukin-32
IL-32 activates NFκ
B and induces the production of several proinflammatory cytokines and chemokines, including TNF, IL-1,
IL-6, and IL-8. It is expressed by RA synovial tissue, especially
in synovial-lining macrophage-like cells.
The level of IL-32
expression correlates with the presence of other cytokines implicated in RA, including TNF, IL-1, and IL-18. Injection of IL-32
into the joints of naïve mice causes a robust transient synovitis.

The synovial response could be partially abrogated by anti-TNF
antibodies, suggesting that IL-32 induces this cytokine in vivo.
These data suggest that IL-32 might be upstream from several proinflammatory mediators in RA and could represent a therapeutic
target.
Colony-Stimulating Factors
GM-CSF supports the differentiation of bone marrow precursor
cells to mature granulocytes and macrophages and participates
in immune responses. It is a potent neutrophil and macrophage
activator. Its actions include the induction of HLA-DR expression, IL-1 secretion, intra-cellular parasite killing, and priming for
enhanced release of TNF and PGE2.
RA synovial fluid and synovium contain GM-CSF.
The
major source is synovial macrophages, although IL-1– or TNFstimulated FLS also express the
CSF2
gene (which encodes
GM-CSF). Its ability to induce HLA-DR gene expression on
macrophages might be of particular importance in RA. GM-CSF,
not IFNγ
, is the major DR-inducing cytokine in RA synovial
effusions. Collagen-induced arthritis in mice is less severe in animals that lack a functional
CSF2
gene or are treated with anti–
GM-CSF antibody, which supports the hypothesis that
CSF2
is
an important pro-inflammatory mediator. Clinical trials demonstrate clinical benefit with anti–GM-CSF antibodies in RA.
Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis, which occurs in
GM-CSF
–deficient mice and in autoimmune diseases marked by anti–GM-CSF
antibodies, has not been a significant issue in RA patients treated
with these agents.
Macrophage CSF (M-CSF, also called
CSF-1
) is also expressed
by RA synovium and is present in synovial effusions. Its primary
pathogenic role in RA probably relates to its osteoclast-differentiating capacity. This factor cooperates with RANKL to facilitate
bone erosions. The M-CSF receptor also binds to a novel cytokine
that is produced by FLS, namely IL-34. The cytokine is expressed
in RA synovium and represents an alternative therapeutic target
for M-CSF receptor-driven events in synovitis.
Interferons
Type I IFNs, especially IFNβ
, are expressed by RA synovium.
The IFNs possess a range of biologic activities and can be immunostimulatory or inhibitory depending on the context. In murine
models of arthritis, exogenous IFNβ
has prominent anti-inflammatory activity. However, a similar benefit has not been observed
in RA. One possible explanation relates to the mechanism of
action in arthritis: In murine models, IFNβ
acts by increasing
IL-1Ra production.
Although IL-1 is a therapeutic target in
pre-clinical models, it is less important in RA. Exogenous IFNβ
does increase synovial IL-1Ra production in patients with RA, but
blocking IL-1 is not sufficient for robust clinical effects. Another
IFN, IL-29, has been identified in RA synovium and can increase
IL-6, IL-8, and MMP production by cultured FLS.
Chemokines
Chemokines are a family of related chemoattractant peptides that,
with the assistance of adhesion molecules, summon cells into
inflammatory sites. Many chemokines are produced in the rheumatoid joint. IL-8, a CXC chemokine that was originally characterized as a neutrophil chemoattractant, along with immune
complexes and other chemotactic peptides such as C5a, contribute
to the large influx of PMNs into the joint. Immunohistochemical
analysis of synovial tissue demonstrates IL-8 protein in sublining
perivascular macrophages, as well as in scattered lining cells.
Cultured synovial tissue macrophages constitutively produce IL-8,
and FLS express the same gene whether they are stimulated with
IL-1 or TNF. IL-8 accounts for about 40% of the neutrophil chemoattractant activity in synovial fluid. In addition, IL-8 activates
neutrophils through G protein–coupled receptors and is a potent
angiogenesis factor. Macrophage-inhibitory protein-1
α
, macrophage-inhibitory protein-1
β
, MCP-1, and regulated on activation,
normally T cell expressed and secreted (RANTES)—members of
the CC subfamily—are also produced by RA synovium, as are
many other CC chemokines.
CXCL16 and epithelial neutrophil-activating peptide-78
(ENA-78) are CXC chemokines and are particularly abundant.
CXCL16 binds to CXCR6 on T cells and contributes to the
recruitment of lymphocytes into the synovium. ENA-78 accounts
for about 40% of the chemotactic activity for neutrophils in RA
synovial fluid. Concentrations of these chemokines are higher in
RA synovial effusions compared with non-inflammatory arthritides such as OA. Although the chemokines also can be detected
in the blood, the levels are considerably lower than in the joint,
thereby providing a gradient that signals cells to migrate into the
synovium.
Lymphocyte-specific chemotactic factors contribute to the germinal center architecture of RA. The CXC factor B cell–activating
chemokine-1 (BCA-1; CXCL13) binds to specific CXCR5 receptors on B cells. CXCL13 is expressed in the RA synovial tissues,
especially by FDCs in germinal centers, and likely accounts for B
cell migration to these regions.
CCL21 and several other factors participate in the anatomic organization of germinal centers,
marginal zones, and other regions of lymphoid follicles. Another
chemokine, SDF-1, is expressed by synoviocytes and endothelial
cells and serves as a chemoattractant for T cells in synovium via
its receptor, CXCR4. Unlike other chemokine receptors that can
bind multiple members of the family, CXCR4 is highly specific
for SDF-1 and is expressed by memory CD4
lymphocytes.
Chemokines have attracted considerable attention as therapeutic targets to prevent recruitment of immune cells into the
synovium. Numerous pre-clinical models support the use of
chemokine blockers. For instance, antibodies to fractalkine
(CXC3L1) suppress murine collagen-induced arthritis, even
though anti–type II collagen antibody production was not
affected.
Anti-CXCL16 antibody also decreases clinical arthritis in the same model. However, the system is highly redundant,
and several different chemotactic proteins can bind to the same
receptor. No clinical improvement in RA was observed in a study
using anti–MCP-1 antibody, perhaps because the antibody also
altered the kinetics of MCP-1 metabolism. Anti–IL-8 antibodies
met with limited success in psoriasis. More promising results were
observed with antibody to IP-10 (CXCL10), which demonstrated
some efficacy in a phase II study in RA, but clinical development
was discontinued.
IP-10 also serves as a biomarker for JAK activation in RA, and blood levels decrease within hours after patients
receive the pan-JAK inhibitor tofacitinib.
Chemokines bind to G protein–coupled receptors and could
be targeted to block multiple factors. For instance, CCR1 antagonists, which block RANTES and MIP-1
α
, have been evaluated in
clinical trials.
Although one compound significantly decreased
synovial infiltration by CCR1-expressing cells in a synovial biopsy
study, no significant clinical improvement was observed. Chemokine receptor blockade has other levels of complexity such as
CCR2-deficient mice, which develop more severe arthritis in some
models. Several CCR2 antagonists, which block MCP-1, have not
demonstrated significant efficacy in RA. These data suggest that

the chemokine system, including the receptors, is both redundant
and complex.
Chemokines and other chemotactic factors (such as C5a and
LTB4) can signal through a variety of mechanisms, although many
pathways converge on PI3K. Of the several PI3K isoforms, PI3K
is relatively specific for chemokine signal transduction, which
provides an opportunity to block multiple chemokine receptors
simultaneously. PI3K
γ
knockout mice have less synovial inflammation in passive and active collagen-induced arthritis than wildtype mice.
Therefore, targeting shared intra-cellular pathways
could potentially overcome some of the limitations presented by
the complex chemoattractant system. A combined PI3K
γ
/
δ
inhibitor did not show efficacy in an RA clinical trial.
Platelet-Derived Growth Factor and Fibroblast Growth
Factor
PDGF is a potent growth factor that is a chemoattractant and a
mitogen for fibroblasts and induces collagenase expression. It is
the most potent stimulator of long-term growth of synovial cells
in culture. PDGF is expressed in vascular endothelial cells, platelets, and other synovial sublining cells in rheumatoid synovium,
compared with healthy tissue. Multiple isoforms of this molecule
have been identified (PDGFs A through D), all of which have
been detected in RA synovial membranes. PDGF D has been
identified as an especially potent stimulator of MMP-1 expression in cultured synoviocytes. PDGF activates the PI3K pathway,
which leads to Akt phosphorylation and supports synoviocyte survival through activation of the
α
and
δ
isoforms.
FGFs are a family of peptide growth factors with pleiotropic
activities. In patients with rheumatoid disease, it is likely that
heparin-binding growth factor, the precursor of acidic FGF, is
a major mitogen for many cell types and stimulates angiogenesis. Interactions between FGF and proteoglycans are required
for biologic activity. It is a potent angiogenic factor, inducing
capillary endothelial cells to invade a three-dimensional collagen
matrix and form capillaries. RANKL is also produced by FGFstimulated synoviocytes, thereby enhancing osteoclast activation
and bone resorption. FGF is present in RA synovial fluid, and
the genes are expressed by synovial cells. Synovial fibroblasts
express FGF receptors and proliferate after exposure to the
growth factor.

### Immunosuppressive Cytokines and Cytokine Antagonists

The pro-inflammatory cytokine network in RA is offset by a variety of suppressive and anti-inflammatory factors that attempt to
re-establish homeostasis. Low production of suppressive cytokines
could contribute to the perpetuation of the synovitis.
Interleukin-1 Receptor Antagonist
IL-1Ra is a naturally occurring IL-1 inhibitor that binds directly
to types I and II IL-1 receptors and competes with IL-1 for the
ligand-binding site. Interaction of IL-1Ra with the IL-1 receptors
does not result in signal transduction, and, in contrast to IL-1
α
or
-
β
, the receptor-ligand complex is not internalized after it binds
to the IL-1 receptor. Even though IL-1Ra has high affinity for the
IL-1 receptor, it is a relatively weak inhibitor because IL-1 can
activate cells, even if only a small percentage of IL-1 receptors
are occupied. As a result, a substantial excess of the inhibitor is
required to saturate the receptor and thereby block IL-1–mediated
stimulation (usually 10- to 100-fold excess of IL-1Ra).
IL-1Ra is present in rheumatoid synovial effusions; much of it
is produced by neutrophils and macrophages.
In RA synovium,
IL-1Ra protein is found in perivascular mononuclear cells and in
the synovial intimal lining. The IL-1Ra protein and mRNA can be
γ
detected in synovial macrophages and, to a lesser extent, in type B
synoviocytes. The presence of IL-1Ra in synovium is not specific
to RA because OA synovial tissue also contains IL-1Ra, albeit in
lesser amounts. Normal synovium contains little, if any, IL-1Ra
protein. Synovial cell culture experiments show that the amount
of IL-1Ra in the rheumatoid joint is not sufficient to antagonize
synovial IL-1.
Interleukin-10
IL-10 protein is present in RA synovial fluid, and the gene is
expressed by synovial tissue macrophages. Serial synovial biopsies
in patients with RA who were treated with recombinant IL-10 did
not show any significant histologic improvement, and significant
clinical improvement was not observed.
Transforming Growth Factorβ
TGFβ
is a member of the TGF superfamily, which includes the
bone morphogenic proteins (BMPs) that signal through intracellular signaling molecules known as
SMAD.
TGFβ
is widely
distributed in different tissues and is produced by many cells,
including T cells, monocytes, and platelets. It suppresses the production of collagenase and induces the expression of TIMP. Substantial amounts of TGFβ
are present in synovial fluid, although
it is mainly present in an inactive, latent form, and the mRNA can
be detected in RA synovial tissue.
In RA, TGFβ
is one of the factors responsible for blunted
responses of synovial fluid T cells. TGFβ
also downregulates
IL-1 receptor expression on some cell types, including chondrocytes. When it is injected directly into the knees of animals,
fibrosis and synovial lining hyperplasia develop. In streptococcal cell wall arthritis, parenteral administration or systemic gene
therapy with the TGFβ
gene ameliorates the disease. However, intra-articular administration of anti–TGFβ
antibody
decreases arthritis in the injected joint but not in the contralateral joint in the same model. Although mainly considered antiinflammatory, TGFβ
also can play a role in the development
of autoimmunity through the differentiation of T cells into the
Th17 phenotype.
Soluble Cytokine Receptors and Binding Proteins
Soluble cytokine receptors and binding proteins can absorb free
cytokines and prevent them from engaging functional receptors
on cells. For example, TNF receptors can be released from the
cell surface after proteolytic cleavage. Soluble p55 and p75 TNF
receptors are present in RA synovial fluid, sometimes in high concentrations. Based on studies with TNF inhibitors and soluble
TNF receptor–Fc fusion proteins like etanercept, it is clear that
the levels of soluble TNF receptors are not high enough to block
TNF biologic activity.
Many other soluble receptors and binding proteins are produced in RA, albeit in concentrations too low to effectively suppress the pro-inflammatory cytokine milieu of the joint. For
instance, the IL-1 type II receptor is present in RA synovial fluid,
along with lesser amounts of the type I receptor. These soluble
receptors can bind to IL-1 or IL-1Ra in synovial effusions. Soluble receptors to IL-15 and IL-17 that neutralize their respective
ligands have been characterized, and an IL-18 binding protein
also can inhibit cytokine activity.

### Perpetuating Synovitis Through Cytokine Networks

Mapping the cytokine profile in RA led to targeted biologic therapy to inhibit cytokines and supports the concept that cytokine
networks play a key role. The network pathway is not autonomous, and except for certain patients with very early disease,
discontinuation of anti-cytokine therapy often results in disease
flares. Nevertheless, paracrine and autocrine cytokine networks
in the synovial intimal lining contribute largely to inflammatory
arthritis in RA (
Fig. 71.6
).
Several cytokines that have been identified in the synovium or
synovial fluid can participate in this system and might explain lining cell hyperplasia, HLA-DR and adhesion-molecule induction,
and synovial angiogenesis. The list of potential candidates in this
highly redundant system is extensive. Several of these candidates,
including IL-1, TNF, GM-CSF, and IL-6, now have well-defined
roles. The first two, along with numerous other factors (e.g.,
IL-15, IL-18, and IL-32), are produced by synovial macrophages
and stimulate synovial fibroblast proliferation and secretion of
IL-6, GM-CSF, and chemokines, as well as effector molecules
such as MMPs and prostaglandins. GM-CSF, which is produced
by both synovial macrophages and IL-1

synovial fibroblasts, can, in turn, induce IL-1 secretion, creating a
positive feedback loop. GM-CSF, especially in combination with
TNF, also increases HLA-DR expression on macrophages. Macrophage and fibroblast cytokines also could indirectly contribute
to local T cell and B cell activation, including ACPA production.
Alarmins can also contribute to the macrophage-fibroblast cytokine network such as IL-33 and HMGB1, by activating mast cells
and other cell lineages.
The cytokines in the joint can recruit other immune and
inflammatory cells into the synovium through the production of
chemokines. Many of these chemokines are produced by macrophages and fibroblasts and attract neutrophils, macrophages,
and subpopulations of T and B cells. The sublining chemokine

Intra-cellular signal transduction systems transmit information from the environment to the interior of a cell, which can

targeted therapies directed at signal transduction have met with
limited success, recent approvals of oral JAK inhibitors continue
to expand the therapeutic armamentarium for RA.

JAKs have become a focus of interest because of the efficacy
demonstrated by several JAK inhibitors in RA, including tofacitinib.

### Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinases

MAPKs are signal transduction enzymes activated in response
to cellular stress and are composed of parallel protein-kinase cascades that regulate cytokine and MMP gene expression. There
are three main families of MAPKs known as c-Jun N-terminal
kinase (JNK), p38, and ERK. MAPKs phosphorylate selected
intra-cellular proteins, including transcription factors, which subsequently regulate gene expression by transcriptional and posttranscriptional mechanisms.
The MAPKs are widely expressed in synovial tissue and are
activated in rheumatoid synovium. Phosphorylated ERK, p38,
and JNK can be detected by immunohistochemistry or Western
blot analysis. All three kinases and their upstream regulators are
constitutively expressed by cultured FLS and can be activated
within minutes after exposure to cytokines. They regulate production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and MMPs.
p38 inhibitors are effective anti-inflammatory agents in murine
collagen-induced arthritis and rat adjuvant arthritis, possibly by
decreasing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines. In addition, p38 inhibitors block TNF and IL-6 production by cultured
macrophages and synoviocytes, as well as cultured synovial tissue
cells. Spinal p38 also plays a major role in pain processing, and
inhibitors have potential for both analgesic and anti-inflammatory
action. Recent studies suggest that p38 in the CNS can regulate
peripheral inflammation because intrathecal administration of a
p38 inhibitor suppresses inflammation and joint destruction in
rat adjuvant arthritis.
Given the putative role of p38 in RA, it is surprising that clinical efficacy of the selective p38 inhibitors is modest, at best.
The likely cause of these disappointing results was delineated in
the passive K/BxN serum transfer model, in which lack of p38
α
in macrophages paradoxically increased arthritis severity.
This
could be due to lower production of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 by p38-deficient macrophages. Expression of phosphatases that deactivate other MAPKs is also suppressed, resulting in
markedly higher levels of phosphorylated JNK and ERK. Thus a
compensatory increase in pro-inflammatory pathways abrogates
the beneficial effect of p38 inhibition.
Instead of blocking p38 directly, upstream kinase or downstream might be targeted. MKK3 and MKK6, both of which regulate p38, are expressed and activated in the rheumatoid synovium.
MKK3 knockout mice and MKK6 knockout mice have markedly
decreased joint inflammation in the passive K/BxN model and
collagen-induced arthritis.
MK2 is downstream of p38 and also
regulates TNF gene expression. Several compounds are currently
being developed that target MK2 in order to avoid tachyphylaxis
observed with direct p38 antagonists.
Two of the three JNK isoforms (JNK1 and JNK2) regulate
a variety of genes in inflammation, including TNF and metalloproteinases. Using a JNK inhibitor, marked protection of bone
destruction was observed in the adjuvant arthritis model, along
with decreased synovial AP-1 activation and collagenase gene
expression.
No benefit was observed in the JNK1
mice, and
only modest cartilage protection was seen in the JNK2
animals,
which has decreased the enthusiasm for targeting this pathway.
The ERK pathway regulates cell growth and some inflammatory mediators and can be targeted by inhibiting its upstream
kinases MEK1 and MEK2. A variety of MEK inhibitors, including trametinib, binimetinib, selumetinib, and cobimetinib, are
approved in the United States for use in cancer and could potentially be explored in inflammatory arthritis. However, a clinical
trial with a small molecule inhibitor (ARRY-438162) found minimal benefit in RA.

### Nuclear Factor- κ B

NFκ
B is a ubiquitous transcription factor that plays a critical
role in the expression of many genes central to RA, including

IL-1, TNF, IL-6, and IL-8. The signaling components of the
NFκ
B pathway, including the I
κ
B kinases, are described in more
detail elsewhere (see
Chapter 18
). It is abundant in rheumatoid
synovium, and immunohistochemical analysis demonstrates p50
and p65 NFκ
B proteins in the nuclei of cells in the synovial intimal lining indicate that the transcription factor is activated.
NFκ
B activation is much greater in RA than OA because phosphorylation and degradation of its inhibitor, I
κ
B, is prominent in
RA intimal lining cells.
Nuclear translocation of NFκ
B in cultured FLS occurs rapidly
after stimulation with many pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g.,
IL-1, TNF, and IL-17) or TLR ligands (e.g., peptidoglycan and
LPS). NFκ
B is also a survival factor for cells and is responsible for
resistance to apoptosis in some cell lineages after activation. This
characteristic is especially important in intestinal inflammation, in
which IKK
β
deficiency actually increases mucosal damage because
the lack of NFκ
B can increase cell death in epithelium.
Treatment of FLS with TNF for at least 3 days remodels the chromatin,
leading to acetylation of histones and increased NFκ
B recruitment to the CXCL10 promoter.
These data suggest that persistent NFκ
B activation in RA might be due, in part, to TNF
sensitization by prolonged exposure to synovial cytokines.
NFκ
B activation is required in numerous animal models of
arthritis. In the pre-clinical phases of collagen-induced arthritis,
synovial NFκ
B is rapidly activated, often long before clinical
arthritis is evident. Adjuvant arthritis in rats is ameliorated using
intra-articular gene therapy with a dominant-negative IKK
β
construct that blocks the IKK pathway, and streptococcal cell-wall
arthritis is blocked with decoy oligonucleotides or a dominantnegative I
κ
B adenovirus.
NFκ
B inhibition is associated with
decreased synovial cellular infiltration, as well as increased apoptosis. Small molecule IKK
β
inhibitors also suppress arthritis and
joint destruction in murine collagen-induced arthritis. The translation of this science to humans has been difficult because of the
pivotal role that NFκ
B plays in host defense.

### Activator Protein-1

AP-1 regulates many genes implicated in RA, especially TNF and
the MMPs, which have AP-1 binding motifs in their promoters.
AP-1 activity can be induced by many signals including cytokines, growth factors, tumor promoters, and the Ras oncoprotein.
AP-1 comprises hetero- and homodimers that include various Jun
and Fos family members. AP-1–driven gene expression is greatly
enhanced when one of its components, such as c-Jun, is phosphorylated by the MAP kinase JNK.
AP-1 expression and nuclear binding are elevated in RA
synovium, especially in the intimal lining layer.
c-Jun and c-Fos
proteins are also expressed in the sublining inflammatory infiltrate, albeit to a lesser degree. AP-1 activation in lining correlates
with high expression of MMPs and various cytokines. AP-1 proteins are usually not detected in normal synovium, although modest amounts have been detected in OA.
Cytokines such as IL-1 and TNF and TLR ligands contribute
to the activation of AP-1 in RA synovium. These factors are potent
inducers of AP-1 nuclear binding in cultured FLS. IL-1 is particularly effective and can dramatically increase AP-1 binding at very
low concentrations. The specific Jun family members that constitute AP-1 in synoviocytes have unique functions. For instance,
c-Jun increases the production of pro-inflammatory mediators,
whereas JunD suppresses cytokine and MMP production.
AP-1
decoy oligonucleotides suppress collagen-induced arthritis and
inhibit cytokine production by synovial tissue. Therapeutic interventions that specifically block AP-1 have not been developed for
RA. However, blocking the downstream sequelae, such as with
MMP inhibitors, was evaluated, with several compounds showing
limited clinical improvement.

### Interferon Regulation: IKK-Related Kinases and Interferon Regulatory Factors

IFN signatures have been noted in several autoimmune diseases,
such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and RA. Type I
IFNs are expressed in RA synovium, especially by synoviocytes
in the intimal lining. Regulation of IFN production and the IFN
response genes such as
CCL5, CXCL10,
and
CCL2
involves a
pathway that runs parallel to the canonical NFκ
B pathway and
includes two IKK-related kinases, known as IKK
ε
and TANK
binding kinase 1 (TBK1). Triggered when TLR3 is ligated by viral
double-stranded RNA (dsRNA), IKK
ε
and TBK1 phosphorylate
the transcription factor IRF3 and induce production of an array of
genes that orchestrate this response. IKK
ε
and its substrate IRF3
are expressed and highly activated in RA synovium. Using a combination of IKK
ε
mice and genetic constructs that block endogenous IKK
ε
and TBK1 activity, the role of IKK-related kinases as
regulators of IFNβ
, RANTES, and MMP expression in cultured
FLS was observed.
In contrast to DCs, in which IRF7 is the
primary IRF that regulates IFN responses, IRF3 is a pivotal factor
that is responsible for the IFN signature in FLS.
Some IRF3-driven genes, most notably IFNβ
, could be beneficial in inflammatory arthritis. Mice with collagen-induced
arthritis that are injected with IFNβ
or transduced fibroblasts
expressing IFNβ
have less severe disease compared with control subjects, including decreased bone and cartilage destruction.
However, a clinical trial of IFNβ
in RA showed no benefit, possibly because IFN acts by increasing IL-1Ra, which is only modestly effective itself as a therapeutic agent. An alternative approach
might be to inhibit IKK-related kinases to block the chemokines
associated with the IFN response and concomitantly provide
treatment with low levels of exogenous IFNβ
.

### Spleen Tyrosine Kinase

Several other signaling molecules have been implicated in RA and
are potential therapeutic targets. For example, Syk is involved in
immunoreceptor signaling in a variety of cell types and plays a
critical role in Fc receptor signaling in macrophages and mast
cells. It also participates in B cell activation after ligation of the
B cell receptor. A small molecule Syk inhibitor, fostamatinib,
demonstrated efficacy in patients with RA in early clinical trials,
although off-target effects were dose limiting, and the larger phase
III showed less efficacy than early studies.
Another more selective compound, MK-8457, also had evidence of efficacy but was
complicated by increased infections.

### PI3 Kinases and Other Signaling Pathways

The PI3Ks, especially the
δ
and
γ
isoforms, are attractive therapeutic targets because of their role in innate immunity and cell
recruitment, respectively. PI3K
γ
is the convergence point for
chemokine signaling and could permit targeting of several chemoattractants with a single compound. The
δ
isoform is notable
because it not only regulates FLS invasion but is also required
for B cell differentiation and activation. PI3K
δ
or BTK inhibitors

represent B cell–targeted therapy and block activation of myeloid
cells. A combined PI3K
δ
/
γ
was not effective in RA, and one BTK
inhibitor, fenebrutinib, decreased disease severity in a phase II
study.
However, another BTK blocker did not show efficacy in
RA.
There is no shortage of targets, and many others, including upstream MAPK regulators, Ras, IL-1–associated kinases
(IRAKs), and sphingosine kinase-1 (SK-1), could be evaluated for
a variety of immune-mediated diseases. Combinations of signal
inhibition at suboptimal dosage are also potential strategies to
improve the risk–benefit ratio.

## Cell Survival and Death in Rheumatoid Synovium

KEY POINTS

> Reactive oxygen and nitrogen in RA joints contribute to a toxic

> environment that can damage cells and increase inflammation and

> alter T cell reactivity.

> Inadequate apoptosis, or cell death, can contribute to the accumulation

> of cells in rheumatoid synovium.

> Abnormalities of key regulatory genes either through dysregulation or

> somatic mutations such as the p53 tumor suppressor can enhance

> accumulation of cells in the joint.

> Inducing apoptosis can potentially suppress synovial inflammation and

> joint destruction.

This section will discuss aberrant regulation of cell division, oxidative damage, programmed cell death, and permanent changes in
the genome and how they contribute to RA.

### Reactive Oxygen and Nitrogen

Oxidative stress in the joints of patients with RA results from
increased pressure in the synovial cavity, reduced capillary density,
vascular changes, an increased metabolic rate of synovial tissue,
and locally activated leukocytes. The generation of reactive oxygen
species can also be facilitated by repetitive ischemia–reperfusion
injury in the joint. Tissue injury releases iron and copper ions and
heme proteins that are catalytic for free-radical reactions.
Increased reactive oxygen species occurs in patients with RA,
which elevates lipid peroxidation products, degrades hyaluronic
acid by free radicals, decreases ascorbic acid levels in serum and
synovial fluid, and increases exhaled pentane. The levels of thioredoxin, a marker of oxidative stress, are also significantly higher in
synovial fluid from patients with RA compared with other forms
of arthritis. Peripheral blood lymphocyte DNA from patients with
RA contains significantly increased levels of the mutagenic 8-oxohydrodeoxyguanosine, which is a product of oxidative damage to
DNA and is a marker of the genotoxic effects of oxidative stress.
NO production is also high in rheumatoid synovial tissue. Low
levels of NO are constitutively produced by endothelial or neuronal NO synthases, and enzyme levels are substantially increased by
inducible NO synthase after stimulation by cytokines or bacterial
products. Numerous proteins are nitrosylated in the rheumatoid
joint, and nitrite levels in synovial fluid are elevated in patients,
indicating local NO production. In addition, the urinary nitrateto-creatinine ratio is increased, and inducible NO synthase is present in the synovium.

### Apoptosis and Autophagy

Programmed cell death, or apoptosis, removes cells safely from
living tissue and permits remodeling, or cell deletion, without
causing an inflammatory response. Apoptosis is a normal process
that is tightly regulated and can be initiated by withdrawal of hormones and growth factors. Autophagy represents an alternative
adaptive mechanism for cell survival that breaks down some cellular components through lysosomes and maintains critical functions to ensure that the cell does not die.
Genes Regulating Apoptosis
The accumulation of cells in RA results from a balance of cell
recruitment, cell egress, local proliferation, and local death. Ultrastructural studies in RA synovial tissue show very few apoptotic
nuclei.
Macrophage apoptosis is probably low because macrophages express high levels of the caspase 8 inhibitor FLICE-like
inhibitory protein (FLIP), which can inhibit Fas-mediated apoptosis. T cell apoptosis in RA synovial effusions, for instance, is
significantly less than lymphocytes from crystal-induced arthropathy. High expression of the anti-apoptotic molecule Bcl-2 is found
in lymphoid aggregates and protects synovial T cells from programmed cell death. Resistance to apoptosis in vitro increases if
RA T cells are co-cultured with FLS. The specific adhesion molecules involved are not defined, although the integrin-binding
RGD motif (arginine–glycine–asparagine) blocks the protective
effects of synoviocytes.
Fas and its TNF superfamily counter-receptor Fas ligand regulate cell death for many cell types, including synovial T cells and
synoviocytes. Fas is expressed by rheumatoid synovial fluid T cells,
and the number of Fas
cells in the peripheral blood of patients
with RA is greater than in healthy control subjects.
Anti-Fas
antibody, which cross-links Fas on cell surfaces, rapidly causes
apoptosis in synovial fluid B and T lymphocytes in RA, although
peripheral-blood T cells are more resistant. Another member of
the TNF superfamily, TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand
(TRAIL), binds to two receptors (DR4 or DR5) to induce caspase-dependent apoptosis. DR5 is expressed in RA FLS but not
OA cells, and apoptosis can be induced by either TRAIL or agonistic anti-DR5 antibody.
It is comparatively difficult to induce apoptosis in synoviocytes
by Fas ligation. p53, which typically induces cell-cycle arrest and
either DNA repair or apoptosis, is also expressed in the synovial
lining and sublining. However, expression of a key effector of
p53-mediated apoptosis, p53 upregulated modulator of apoptosis
(PUMA), is low in the synovium and cultured synoviocytes. Thus
synoviocytes are resistant to p53-mediated apoptosis even when
cells overexpress the protein by transfection with a p53 expression
vector.
Synoviocyte apoptosis can, however, occur after oxidative stresses such as hydrogen peroxide or nitrogen oxide.
The relative paucity of apoptotic cells in the affected tissues
of RA patients may also be partially explained by patterns of
gene expression that favor cell survival. The protease ADAM15
was implicated in FLS resistance induced by Fas ligand through
activation of the focal adhesion kinase pathway.
Many other
genes in the same pathway are differentially methylated and could
also increase resistance to cell death.
Sentrin-1, a ubiquitin-like
protein, regulates cell survival by modifying proteins involved in
apoptosis. Sentrin-1 is expressed in RA synovium, especially at
sites of cartilage invasion, and it protects cells from Fas-mediated
death. A second protein, phosphatase and tensin homolog on
chromosome 10 (PTEN), protects against tumorigenesis through

antagonism of PI3K, Akt, and many other proliferative pathways.
Low PTEN in RA is a feature of the rheumatoid synovial intimal
lining, as well as cultured FLS.
Genes Regulating Autophagy
Genes associated with increased autophagy, such as those encoding LC3 and Beclin-1 and the miRNA miR-30a, are increased in
the RA synovial intimal lining compared with OA synovium.
These genes program cells for improved survival through autophagy rather than directing cell death through apoptosis. In RA FLS,
induction of autophagy by inhibiting proteasome-mediated protein degradation protected against cell death, whereas endoplasmic reticulum stress increased apoptosis. Therefore the degree to
which autophagy regulates cell survival is context specific.
Ferroptosis and Pyroptosis
Ferroptosis is a nonapoptotic cell death process that requires cellular iron and the accumulation of lipid peroxides. This process
is dysregulated in RA, which can have profound effects on the
cell survival and response to therapeutics (
Fig. 71.8
). For instance,
TNF regulates ferroptosis resistance by upregulating solute carrier family 7 member 11 (SLC7A11), glutamate–cysteine ligase
catalytic subunit (GCLM), and glutamate–cysteine ligase regulatory subunit (GCLC). This process promotes cystine uptake and
the biosynthesis of cellular glutathione (GSH) that protects from
oxidative stress. As a result, low concentrations of a ferroptosis
inducer together with etanercept, a TNF inhibitor, induce ferroptosis in fibroblasts and attenuates arthritis progression in murine
collagen-induced arthritis.
The impact of this novel pathway on
clinical outcomes is not yet fully understood but could potentially
explain some of the variability in response to targeted therapies
Pyroptosis is a pathway to cell death mediated by gasdermins and the activation of caspases. Compared with cells from
healthy controls, monocytes and synovial macrophages from
RA patients showed increased expression of activated caspase 3
and gasdermin E, and gasdermin E deficiency effectively alleviated arthritis in a mouse model of collagen-induced arthritis.
Another work described that myeloid cell–specific deletion of the
RA susceptibility gene A20/Tnfaip3 in mice triggered a spontaneous erosive polyarthritis by enhancing inflammasome-mediated
caspase-1 activation, pyroptosis, and IL-1
β
secretion.
Although
this mechanism was described in myeloid cells, it has also been
involved in FLS aggressive phenotype.
Interventions to Increase Apoptosis in Arthritis Models
Fas-induced death has been used successfully in murine collagen-induced arthritis using anti-Fas antibodies and adenovirus
encoding for Fas ligand. Anti-Fas antibody also induces synovial
cell death in RA synovial tissue explanted in SCID mice. In the
SCID mouse model using RA synovial explants, anti-DR5 antibody decreased cartilage erosion. Similarly, adenoviral transfer of
TRAIL in a rabbit model of arthritis decreases synovial inflammation.
The importance of apoptosis as a regulator of inflammation was confirmed in murine collagen-induced arthritis, in
which genetic DR5 deficiency exacerbated the disease. Blocking
Foxo3a in PMNs is also an effective method of deleting these
inflammatory cells and suppressing inflammatory arthritis in
mice.
The Bcl-2 homology 3 (BH3) domain–only proteins are
potent inducers of apoptosis. The challenge is how to get these
proteins expressed in the target cell.

## Synovial Vasculature

Blood vessels play an active role in inflammatory processes, not
only as a means of selecting which cells should enter the tissue,
but also as a determinant of tissue growth and nutrition through
the proliferation of new capillaries. Understanding the structure
and function of the microvasculature provides insights into how
a highly catabolic tissue such as the rheumatoid synovium can
flourish.

### Angiogenesis: Feeding the Starved Synovium

The importance of luxurious new capillary growth early in the
development of synovitis has been recognized for many years
(
Chapter 28
). The absolute number of blood vessels is increased
in RA synovium (
Fig. 71.9
), with a rich network of sublining
capillaries and postcapillary venules in histologic sections stained
with endothelium-specific antibodies. These blood vessels are not
normal, however, with a predominance of straight, branching
morphologic features compared with tortuous vessels in psoriatic
arthritis synovium. The vasculature in the inflamed synovium is
less mature, probably as a result of increased DNA damage and
decreased recruitment of pericytes. These immature blood vessels
are selectively depleted by TNF inhibitors in RA.

### Lymphatic Vessels

The lymphatic system is strongly implicated in the onset of
synovitis and disease progression in animal models of arthritis presumably by the removal of catabolic factors, cytokines,
and inflammatory cells from the inflamed synovium. Persistent
inflammation results in chronic damage to the lymphatic vessel,
reduced lymphatic drainage, and ultimately severe synovitis and
joint erosion. A recent study assessed the differences in lymphatic
function of hands in patients with RA and active synovitis via
near infrared-indocyanine green (NIR-ICG) imaging, and the
lymphatic drainage in the hands of RA patients with active disease was reduced compared with controls, suggesting that targeting lymphatics represents an innovative strategy for therapeutic
intervention for RA.

### Proteases: Mediators of Joint Destruction

Matrix Metalloproteinases
The MMPs are a family of enzymes that participate in extra-cellular matrix degradation and remodeling (see
Chapter 8
). They
are usually secreted as inactive proenzymes, and their proteolytic
activity requires limited cleavage or denaturation to reveal a zinc
cation at the core. Their activation can be mediated by other proteases, including trypsin, plasmin, or tryptase. The substrates for
MMPs are varied but quite specific for individual members of the
family. Many different families of proteinases are found in the
joint (
Table 71.3
), but the MMPs are thought to play a pivotal
role in joint destruction.
Regulation of Matrix Metalloproteinase Production.
The
cytokine milieu can induce the biosynthesis of MMPs by synovial
cells and alter the balance between extra-cellular matrix production and degradation. IL-1 and TNF, in particular, induce MMP
gene expression by many cells, especially FLS and chondrocytes.
The two cytokines are additive or synergistic when used in combination. Many other cytokines and TLR ligands implicated in
rheumatoid synovitis can also induce MMP expression, including
IL-17, LIF, LPS, and peptidoglycans. Counter-regulatory mechanisms also inhibit MMP expression, such as miRNA-4423-3p,
which targets MMP-13 in cultured FLS.
Culture medium from rheumatoid synovium stimulates cartilage degradation in vitro, mainly because IL-1 stimulates MMP
production by synovial intimal lining cells. IL-6 does not generally
induce MMP production by synovial cells but instead increases the
production of TIMP-1, a naturally occurring inhibitor of MMPs.
TGFβ
inhibits collagenase synthesis and enhances the production
of TIMP and collagen by fibroblasts and chondrocytes, thereby
shifting the balance from destruction to matrix repair.

Collagenases and stromelysins have the capacity to degrade
virtually all the important structural proteins in the extra-cellular

triple-helical collagen molecule at a single glycine–isoleucine bond

collagenase-3 (MMP-13) has more favorable kinetics. Neutrophil

Matrix Metalloproteinase Expression in Synovium.
Collagenase-1 and collagenase-3 (MMP-13) genes are produced by RA
synovial tissue, and the latter is highly expressed by chondrocytes
in cartilage. In situ hybridization studies show that the primary
location of collagenase-1 gene expression in the synovium, like
many other MMPs, is the intimal lining, especially in FLS.
Subchondral bone is another region in which proteinase expression
occurs in RA and could participate in bone resorption. Increased
MMP gene expression is an early feature of RA and occurs during
the first few weeks of clinically active disease. High expression of
collagenase-1, as well as gelatinases such as MMP-2, early in the
disease process correlates with rapidly progressive erosions. Similarly, increased blood levels of the proenzymes are also associated
with more severe disease.
Stromelysin-1 (MMP-3) and the other members of the stromelysin family degrade type IV collagen, fibronectin, laminin, proteoglycan core protein, and type IX collagen. Stromelysin removes
the NH
-terminal propeptides from type I procollagen and can
activate procollagenase, creating a positive feedback loop that
amplifies damage. Like collagenase, stromelysin gene expression
occurs mainly in the synovial intimal lining (
Fig. 71.10
). Despite
the presumed importance of this enzyme in matrix destruction,
stromelysin knockout mice are susceptible to collagen-induced
arthritis and develop as much joint destruction as mice with functional stromelysin.
MMP inhibitors suppress bone destruction and inflammatory
synovitis in pre-clinical models of RA. In OA models, deletion
of MMP genes such as stromelysin does not improve outcomes.
Several clinical trials in RA using nonselective inhibitors have had
minimal success and significant adverse effects, possibly related
to decreased matrix turnover. TNF converting enzyme (TACE)
inhibitors can unexpectedly increase disease severity in RA, perhaps as a result of higher levels of membrane-bound TNF. One of
the most consistent adverse effects experienced by patients treated
with MMP inhibitors is increased joint stiffness, which is thought
to result from deposition of fibrous tissue without sufficient protease activity to permit removal of matrix proteins.
Cysteine Proteases: Cathepsins
Cathepsins are a family of cysteine proteases that have broad
proteolytic activity, including activity on types II, IX, and XI
collagen and proteoglycans. Like MMPs, the cathepsins are regulated by cytokines and by proto-oncogenes such as Ras. IL-1
and TNF induce cathepsin L expression in cultured FLS.
In
situ hybridization studies demonstrate cathepsin B and L RNA
transcripts in RA synovium, especially at sites of erosion. A
ribozyme that cleaves cathepsin L decreases FLS invasion and
cartilage destruction in the SCID mouse model with implanted
cultured synoviocytes, which suggests that this protease has clinical relevance.
Cathepsin K has been implicated in bone resorption by
osteoclasts. This protease is unique among the cathepsins
because it can degrade native type I collagen. It is expressed in
RA synovial tissue by both macrophages and fibroblasts and is
present in significantly higher concentrations than in OA.
Serum levels of cathepsin K correlate with the extent of radiographic damage. A potential role of cathepsins as mediators of
bone destruction in arthritis was confirmed in studies in which
cysteine protease inhibitors significantly decrease joint damage
in the rodent arthritis models. In the TNF-transgenic mouse
model, cathepsin K deficiency decreased, but did not eliminate,
bone erosions.
Cathepsin K inhibition could potentially block bone erosions due to synovitis, as well as generalized bone loss that
occurs in RA. One agent, MIV-711, was evaluated in OA and
preserved femoral bone volume and reduced blood biomarkers
of cartilage and bone degradation.
Of note, the inhibitor
did not improve pain, which would limit its utility in a disease like RA because pain is an important symptom. Another
inhibitor, odanacatib, was tested in post-menopausal women
with osteoporosis but was discontinued due to an increased
incidence of stroke.
Aggrecanases
Aggrecan is a major proteoglycan component of articular cartilage.
Because of its large size and negative charge, it contains a considerable amount of water, which increases compressibility. Two proteolytic sites are available on aggrecan in its globular domain. One
site is susceptible to MMP cleavage, whereas the other, located
32 amino acids toward the C terminus, is the site for cleavage
by a family of enzymes known as
aggrecanases.
The two sites can
be identified in tissues using monoclonal antibodies after cleavage
when specific neoepitopes are revealed.
Normal cartilage contains a surprising amount of aggrecanase
neoepitope, suggesting continuous matrix turnover. The level of
aggrecanase cleavage product increases with age. Two aggrecanase genes, aggrecanase-1 and aggrecanase-2, have been cloned
and are members of the “a disintegrin and metalloproteinase
with thrombospondin motif” (ADAMTS) family of proteins
(ADAMTS-4 and ADAMTS-5, respectively). They are expressed
in OA and RA cartilage, and their proteolytic activity can be
detected in synovial fluids. Especially high levels of the neoepitope are present in arthritic cartilage.
IL-1 increases aggrecanase
expression in cartilage explants, as well as cultured chondrocytes.
Aggrecanase-1 and aggrecanase-2 are constitutively expressed
by RA and OA FLS and synovial tissues.
Aggrecanase-1, but
not aggrecanse-2, is induced in synoviocytes by cytokines, especially TGFβ
. Genetic deletion of aggrecanse-1 has no effect on a
murine OA model, but loss of aggrecanase-2 prevents degenerative changes.

### Regulation of Cartilage Destruction

Macrophage-like and fibroblast-like cells migrate onto the surface of cartilage, and the FLS in particular penetrate into cartilage
matrix. This invasive pannus is more commonly found in metatarsophalangeal joints, compared with hip and knee joints, perhaps
explaining why erosions often occur around small joints.
FLS from the intimal lining are major effectors of cartilage
destruction in RA. They produce prodigious amounts of proteases, bind to cartilage, and invade the extra-cellular matrix (see
Fig. 71.11
). The pivotal role of synoviocytes in cartilage destruction was demonstrated in arthritis models using cadherin-11
blockade to disrupt the intimal lining. Cartilage destruction is
markedly attenuated, even though bone erosion progress.
Other cells in the joint, including macrophages and synovial fluid
neutrophils, could also be responsible for cartilage damage, whereas
osteoclasts are responsible for bone erosions. More primitive mesenchymal cells isolated directly from the cartilage–pannus junction
express phenotypic and functional features of both synoviocytes and
chondrocytes and have also been described in the synovium.
Cartilage is destroyed in RA by enzymatic and mechanical processes. The enzymes induced by factors such as IL-1, IL-17, TNF,
phagocytosis of debris by synovial cells, and mechanical trauma
degrade the matrix proteins. Early in synovitis, proteoglycans are
depleted from the tissue, most likely as a result of the catabolic
effect of cytokines such as IL-1 on chondrocytes with the production of MMPs and aggrecanases, and this depletion leads to
mechanical weakening of cartilage.
As proteoglycans are depleted, cartilage loses elasticity and
becomes susceptible to mechanical fragmentation and fibrillation.
Eventually the tissue loses functional integrity concurrent with its
dissolution by collagenases and stromelysins. Some of the MMPs
responsible for this process are also derived from the chondrocytes
themselves. Multiple MMPs, especially stromelysin (MMP-3) and
collagenases (MMP-1 and MMP-13), are expressed in RA cartilage,
and in situ hybridization studies confirm the presence of their RNA
transcripts in chondrocytes.
Hence the cartilage is under attack
from a multitude of sources: It is bathed in protease-rich synovial fluid, under extrinsic attack from the invasive pannus, damaged from within by chondrocytes, and fragmented by mechanical
forces.
Enzymes released by PMNs in synovial fluid, including neutrophil collagenase and multiple serine proteases, also contribute to
cartilage loss. Immune complexes containing RFs are embedded
in the superficial layers of cartilage and can attract and activate
neutrophils. Electron microscopic examinations of articular cartilage in RA reveal evidence of breakdown of collagen and proteoglycan as a result of superficial activity of joint fluid enzymes.
Most animal studies suggest that IL-1 is a key regulator of
matrix degradation in arthritis. For example, when IL-1 is injected
directly into rabbit joints, proteoglycan depletion in cartilage
occurs rapidly and correlates with MMP levels in synovial fluid.

### Regulation of Bone Destruction

Marginal erosions are the most characteristic location for bone
damage in RA. These often occur in metacarpal phalangeal joints
and proximal interphalangeal joints traditionally ascribed to a
“bare area” where cartilage does not protect the underlying bone
from invasive pannus.
However, anatomic pathology and CT
studies suggest that erosions actually occur near collateral ligaments in these small joints where microdamage was observed in
cadaveric studies. It is possible that local factors related to stress are
more important than the anatomic location of overlying cartilage.
Osteoclasts are the primary cells responsible for bone degradation in RA. It was recently described that osteoclasts in pannus
originate from circulating bone marrow–derived cells and not from
locally resident macrophages. Murine CX
CR1
Ly6C
F4/80
IA
/I-E
macrophages are the osteoclast precursor-containing population in the inflamed synovium and are regulated by FOXM1
(forkhead box protein M1).
RANKL is perhaps the single
most important factor that modulates bone resorption. Osteoclast development is complex and involves the differentiation of
monocytes under the influence of cytokines such as M-CSF in
combination with RANKL. RANKL is produced by many cell
types, including activated T cells and FLS. The LTB4 receptor
(BLT1) regulates osteoclast differentiation, with BLT1 deficiency

increasing the number of osteoclasts and sensitizing cells to the
effects of RANKL.
Similar processes contribute to generalized bone loss in RA,
beginning with periarticular osteopenia and progressing to diffuse osteoporosis. Osteoclast activation and osteopenia occurs in
ACPA
healthy individuals, with decreased cortical thickness and
increased cortical porosity compared with controls.
Polyclonal
and some monoclonal ACPAs induce bone loss when injected into
mice and confirm the importance of protein citrullination and
pathogenic antibodies on osteoclast activation.
The pathogenic
effect of ACPA binding to osteoclasts or osteoclast precursors,
especially in the presence of IL-8, is responsible for this effect.
Extra-cellular miRNA-574-5p contained in microvesicles can also
enhance osteoclast differentiation via TLR7/8.
Abundant evidence implicates this powerful mechanism in
bone destruction caused by inflammatory arthritis. For instance,
administration of osteoprotegerin (OPG), a RANKL decoy receptor, to rats with adjuvant arthritis inhibits bone destruction but
has almost no effect on inflammation or clinical signs of arthritis.
RANKL
mice are also protected from bone erosions in
the passive K/BxN model of arthritis, although cartilage destruction still occurs. Animal models of arthritis point to IL-17 as a
mediator of osteoclast generation. Genetic deficiency of IL-17 or
anti–IL-17 antibodies has remarkable bone-sparing effects in these
experiments.
RANK, RANKL, and OPG (as well as M-CSF and IL-17) have
been detected in the synovium and synovial fluid of patients with
RA. The ratio of RANKL to OPG is significantly higher in RA
synovial effusions than in either OA or gout, which is consistent
with the more destructive nature of RA. Osteoclasts expressing

present, bone destruction is favored; when absent, bone formation
occurs. Other factors can also stimulate osteoclastogenesis, such as

### Tissue Repair

Extra-cellular matrix turnover in RA has been likened to wound
healing as a result of the critical role of collagen production, proteases, and protease inhibitors. Remodeling the matrix by removing
damaged proteins is a key element in early repair. Subsequently,
the balance shifts to protease inhibition, production of cytokine
inhibitors, removal of inflammatory cells through apoptosis, and
release of anti-inflammatory eicosanoids such as LXs to suppress
inflammation. Neutralization of oxidants via glutathione reductase or superoxide dismutase further limits tissue damage. Normalization of oxidant signaling can also reprogram the adaptive
immune system and suppress arthritogenic T cells.
Tissue repair mechanisms then permit either a return to normal architecture or scar formation. TGFβ
, in particular, plays a
key role in that it increases collagen deposition, suppresses MMP
expression, and enhances production of the TIMPs. Although
TGFβ
levels in the rheumatoid joint are substantial, they are
mainly in the inactive latent form and are not sufficient to overcome the impressive array of pro-inflammatory cytokines that
induce protease expression. Persistent antigen-driven T cell activation, continued antibody-driven complement fixation, or activation of other cell lineages like macrophages or FLS overwhelms
the negative feedback loops that attempt to restore homeostasis.
Strategies to shift from tissue damage by enhancing endogenous
mechanisms could suppress symptoms and enhance appropriate
remodeling of the matrix to restore homeostasis.
Because of invasive behavior by rheumatoid synovium, the possibility that the tissue contains immature cells or embryonic genes
that regulate repair has been explored. The embryonic growth
factors from the Wnt and Fz gene families are expressed by RA
synovium. Normally, these proteins participate in bone marrow
progenitor differentiation and limb bud mesenchyme. Wnt5a and
Fz5, in particular, are markedly elevated in RA tissues and cultured synoviocytes. When normal fibroblasts are transfected with
the
WNT5A
gene, cytokine expression, such as IL-6, increases
significantly. Antisense
WNT5A
and dominant-negative
WNT5A
vectors diminish cytokine expression by synoviocytes.
These data raise the possibility that immature mesenchymal
cells populate the synovium in RA, either as a primary event or
as a repair or inflammatory mechanism. Primitive mesenchymal

cells circulate in the peripheral blood of both people with RA
and healthy people. The former might decrease inflammation,
while the latter might presage disease flares (e.g., PRIME cells
[see above]). In pre-clinical models like collagen-induced arthritis, they infiltrate the synovium before synovial inflammation
becomes clinically apparent. Treatment with either adipose- or
bone marrow–derived mesenchymal stem cells has been proposed
as a treatment modality.
However, cell therapy remains primarily an experimental approach that is currently focused on degenerative diseases like OA and would need carefully controlled studies
to avoid widespread and inappropriate use.
Tissue repair in RA is a complex process that also involves the
ingress or dedifferentiation of mesenchymal cells that can remodel
the matrix. These cells are modulated by TGFβ
and the BMPs.
The BMPs are members of the TGFβ
superfamily, and like TGFβ
,
they signal through the SMAD pathway. Several members, including BMP-2 and BMP-7, are expressed in the joint and facilitate
repair, although inappropriate release can also enhance joint damage
or lead to ankylosis or enthesophyte formation.
BMP function is
also regulated by a family of inhibitors such as Noggin, which can
limit cartilage damage when it is overexpressed in murine antigeninduced arthritis. Modulating the relative balance and timing of
BMP expression ultimately could be used to modify the destructive
influence of synovitis or to regenerate damaged tissues.

## Conclusion

The causes and pathogenesis of RA are complex and involve the
interplay between environment and genes. Many years before
clinical disease is apparent, evidence of immune dysregulation is
observed, including antibody responses to modified peptides and
increased production of cytokines and chemokines. A tipping
point is ultimately reached, perhaps after a “second hit,” when
antibodies activate complement in the joint and attract other
immune cells from the blood. The local synovial milieu is rich with
cytokines, inflammatory cells, and proteases that can drive further
adaptive immune responses and joint damage. Understanding the
diverse pathogenic events involved in RA helps us understand the
diversity of responses to targeted agents that block TNF and IL-6,
interfere with T cell co-stimulation, deplete B cells, or inhibit
cytokine signal transduction. As we gain additional information
about the critical events prior to clinical disease, namely “pre-RA,”
approaches that prevent rather than treat RA might be feasible.
