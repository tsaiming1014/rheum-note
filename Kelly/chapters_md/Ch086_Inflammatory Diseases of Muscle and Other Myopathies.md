## Introduction

Idiopathic inflammatory myopathies (IIM), or myositis, are a
heterogeneous group of systemic autoimmune rheumatic disorders characterized by chronic muscle weakness, muscle fatigue,
and varying involvement of other organs. Most cases have varying
degrees of mononuclear cell infiltration into skeletal muscle. These
disorders were described in the literature more than a century ago
as generalized muscle disorders affecting principally the trunk and
proximal limb muscles, with or without skin involvement.
It
was also recognized that these diseases can range from acute and
even fatal to slow, progressive, chronic, insidious conditions, with
patterns of relapse and remission. Steiner’s summary of myositis
cases in 1905 made a clear distinction between idiopathic polymyositis (PM) and other forms of myositis caused by bacteria and
parasites,
and Stertz in 1916 first reported an association between
dermatomyositis (DM) and internal malignancy.
At about the
same time, Batten
described the first case of DM with classic histologic features in a child.
Since the 1940s, it has been recognized that IIM may occur
in the absence of cutaneous lesions, muscle pain, or constitutional symptoms. It may present in an acute, subacute, or chronic
insidious form, with some fraction of cases showing systemic features or involvement of organs and tissues.
The differential diagnosis has been described independently by several investigators,
and the most chronic form was differentiated from an adult variety of muscular dystrophy.
The first classification scheme and
set of diagnostic criteria for myositis were proposed by Bohan and
Peter in 1975.
They include PM and DM but not inclusion
body myositis (IBM). IBM was later defined by the presence of
distinct histopathologic changes, including vacuoles and nuclear
and cytoplasmic inclusions, as well as by distinct clinical features,
including distal and asymmetric muscle involvement in contrast
to proximal symmetric muscle involvement seen in PM and DM
as well as nonresponsiveness to glucocorticoids.
More recently we have recognized an evolving spectrum of the
IIM including more systemic autoimmune disorders with frequent involvement of muscle, lungs, joints, skin, the gastrointestinal (GI) system, and the heart. The myositis disease spectrum has
broadened from primary inflammatory muscle disorders including PM, DM, and IBM to encompass newer clinical subgroups
such as immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy (IMNM), antisynthetase syndrome, amyopathic DM, and cancer-associated
myositis.
This breakthrough is very much based on the identification
of new autoantibodies that are highly specific for myositis spectrum disorder and are strongly associated with distinct clinical
phenotypes.

## Epidemiology

The annual incidence of IIM may vary in different populations.
A recent population-based registry study suggests an incidence
rate for IIM, overall, of 11 (13 for women and 9.7 for men) per
1 million person-years in Sweden.
Several retrospective studies
have reported an annual incidence of less than 10 per million individuals
(

). This number may be an underestimate
if a positive muscle biopsy was required for diagnosis. Data on

to 14 per 100,000 in two recent population-based Scandinavian

The age-adjusted prevalence of IBM for those older than 50 years

In some geographic areas,
IBM appears to be the most common acquired progressive myopathy, representing 16% to 28% of all inflammatory myopathies.
However, there may be referral biases in these studies. The incidence-prevalence studies need to be interpreted cautiously, given
that most have not reported confidence intervals for their rates.
The incidence of the various IIM subgroups varies according to
race, age, and sex. Some studies have reported that the incidence
of PM is higher in Black patients than in White patients.
IIMs
can occur in any age group, from early childhood to late in adult
life. The onset of PM is usually in the late teens or older; the mean
patient age at onset is 50 to 60 years. DM shows two peaks: 5 to
15 years and 45 to 65 years. IBM is commonly seen in individuals
older than 50 years and is rare in younger adults. Some studies
have reported sex-specific incidence rates. For example, in the case
of PM and DM, females are more commonly affected than males
(ratio >2:1), whereas in IBM, the converse is true (again, >2:1
ratio). Epidemiology data on the new IIM subgroups are lacking.
Inflammatory myopathies can occur in association with other
autoimmune connective tissue diseases such as systemic sclerosis, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), Sjögren’s
syndrome, mixed connective tissue disease, polyarteritis nodosa, and
sarcoidosis. Significant proportions of all patients with myositis (11%
to 40%) have an associated connective tissue disease.
Several
studies have also confirmed an association between malignancies and
inflammatory myopathies. A retrospective study of myositis patients
over a 20-year period revealed that myositis in approximately 12%
(37/309) of the patients was associated with malignancy. A majority
(81%, 30/37) of the patients studied had DM, and the remainder
(19%) had PM. Overall, the frequency of malignancies has varied
but, in general, the inciwidely (4% to 42%) in different studies
dence of malignancy is higher in DM patients than in IBM or PM
patients.
It is difficult to determine the relative risks for a particular malignancy because a variety of malignancies are associated with
myositis, and only small numbers of individual malignancies have
been reported in any one study.

## Etiology of Myositis

### Genetic Risk Factors

An association with immune response genes and occasional
reports of familial clustering of myositis support the role of
Polymorphisms in class I
genetic factors in these diseases.
and II human leukocyte antigen (HLA) genes are known genetic
risk factors for several autoimmune diseases that include myositis, but the mechanisms for these associations remain unclear.
One possibility is that because the gene products influence T
cell repertoire development, tolerance, and immune responses
to foreign agents, certain polymorphisms are selected on
the basis of environmental triggers. It appears that the HLADRB1*0301 and HLA-DQA1*0501 alleles are the strongest
known genetic risk factors for all forms of myositis in Whites.
However, different phenotypes have additional HLA risk and
protective factors.
In Black/African American patients, neither DRB1*0301 nor DQA1*0501 is strongly associated with
myositis. Instead, the HLA-DRB1*08 allele shows the highest
general risk for myositis, whereas the HLA-DRB1*14 allele is
strongly protective in Black/African American patients. The
HLA-B8/DR3/DR52/DQ2 haplotype is found in a significant
proportion of IBM patients.
The risk and protection conferred
by HLA associations differ significantly among different racialethnic and serologic groups. For example, in some populations
(e.g., Koreans, Mesoamericans), there is no known association
with HLA genes.
Furthermore, HLA-DRB1*0301, which is
a risk factor in Whites, is a protective factor in the Japanese
population.
The HLA-DRB1*0301, HLA-DQA1*0501, and
HLA-DQB1*0201 alleles are strongly associated with myositisspecific antibodies in PM patients.
Recent studies identified
the HLA-DRB1*0101 and HLA-DRB1*1301 alleles as risk factors for IBM.
Mechanistic data supporting the role of HLA
molecules in disease pathogenesis are, unfortunately, lacking at
present. Some studies reported that maternally derived chimeric cells are present in the peripheral blood and muscle tissues
of juvenile dermatomyositis (JDM) patients, suggesting that
HLA alleles control the occurrence of chimerism and explain the
HLA association found in these disorders.
Like other autoimmune disease conditions, myositis is a complex multigenic
disorder involving other non-HLA immune response genes
(e.g., cytokines and receptors, including tumor necrosis factor
[TNF], interleukin [IL]-1, and TNF receptor-1); complement
components (e.g., C4, C2); immunoglobulin (Ig) heavy-chain
allotypes; and T cell receptors.
Polymorphisms in other nonHLA genes, such as signal transducer and activator of transcription 4 and C8orf13–BLK, appear to contribute prominently in
Asian populations.
Candidate and whole exome sequencing
studies have identified that rare variants in VCP, SQSTM1, and
FYCO1 genes are associated with IBM, suggesting impaired
autophagy as a mechanism of IBM pathogenesis.
A recent study investigating gene copy number (GCN) variations for complement C4 in myositis patients showed that low
GCNs for C4T, C4A, and C4L played significant roles in increasing the risk of IIM.
The exact contribution of the genetic component in these disorders is currently unknown, in part because of their rarity, the small
number of subjects in any single cohort, and the heterogeneity
seen in the disease phenotype. Recent studies have confirmed the
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) as the major genetic
region associated with DM, JDM, PM, and IBM and some of
the autoantibody-defined subgroups in large Caucasian populations.
Furthermore, DM also shares non-MHC genetic features with other autoimmune diseases, suggesting the presence of
additional novel risk loci.
International collaborative efforts are
currently under way to identify novel genetic and environmental
risk factors in myositis.

### Environmental Risk Factors

The temporal association of myositis onset and environmental
agents in certain individuals suggests that specific exposures in
the context of certain genetic backgrounds can initiate muscle
inflammation. A number of different environmental agents have
been implicated as risk factors in myositis and include infectious
organisms, such as viruses and bacteria, and noninfectious agents,
including ultraviolet light irradiation, cigarette smoking, drugs,
and others (
Table 86.2
).
Infections
Recent epidemiology data indicate that infections, in particular
infections of the respiratory and GI tracts, are associated with an
increased risk of IIM.
For example, enteroviruses (influenza,
coxsackievirus, echoviruses) and retroviruses (human T-lymphotropic virus-I) are known to induce muscle inflammation. The
myositis associated with enteroviruses usually occurs in children
and is generally self-limited. A viral cause is strengthened by the
presence of high-titer anti-viral antibodies and viral particles in
as well as the induction of
patients’ serum and tissue samples,
muscle inflammation by enteroviruses in animal models. A more
recent association was observed between a previous cytomegalovirus infection and a subphenotype of PM and DM patients with
a high frequency of so-called
CD28-null T cells
, possibly reflecting chronic stimulation (see later), indicating that some viral
infections may influence the immune system and lead to chronic
inflammation.
Similarly, some microorganisms, such as staphylococci, clostridia, and mycobacteria, are known to affect skeletal
muscle and cause acute muscle inflammation, but there is no evidence that these organisms actually cause chronic, self-sustaining
muscle inflammation.
The recent emergence of myositis as a rare manifestation of
COVID-19 has led to increased interest in the etiology, clinical
presentation, and treatment challenges associated with this condition. COVID-19-induced myositis can vary widely in its presentation, from typical DM to rhabdomyolysis and back pain.
The
mechanism underlying virus-mediated muscle inflammation is
attributed to angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 receptor–mediated
viral entry and affliction of muscle fibers, which leads to innate and
adaptive immune activation. Recent research has focused on the
similarities between anti–melanoma differentiation-associated protein 5 (MDA5)–positive myositis and COVID-19, with the aim of
finding common etiopathogenic bases and therapeutic strategies. In
addition, during the COVID-19 pandemic, there have been reports
of patients developing myositis after receiving vaccination against
COVID-19 including rare forms such as anti-MDA5-positive myositis.
However, this is an association and the possibility of causation is unclear, especially in the setting of a global vaccination drive.
Parasites such as
Toxoplasma gondii
,
Trypanosoma cruzi
, and
Borrelia burgdorferi
have been implicated in the triggering of IIMs.
The evidence in support of a parasitic cause includes the recovery of parasites from some myositis patients and their serologic
response to the parasites; improvement in myositis symptoms
after treatment with anti-parasitic drugs; a histologic picture of
inflammation, including infiltration of macrophages and CD4+ T
cells; and induction of myositis after parasitic infection in animal
models.
Ultraviolet Light Irradiation
Ultraviolet (UV) light irradiation is likely to be a risk factor for
DM because epidemiologic data have demonstrated a latitude gradient of PM and DM, with the latter more frequent closer to the
equator and the former more frequent in northern countries. The
ratio between PM and DM is associated with a latitude gradient
and is directly correlated with UV light irradiation. This observed
correlation is particularly strong in a subset of DM patients with
anti-Mi-2 autoantibodies, indicating that UV light may be an
environmental risk factor for its development. The association
between UV light exposure and the subtype of myositis suggests
that UV light is an exogenous modifier that can influence the
clinical and serologic phenotype in myositis.
Cigarette Smoking and Other Inhalant Biohazards
Smoking is a risk factor for a subtype of myositis, the anti-Jo-1
positive (one of the anti-synthetase syndromes—see the section on autoantibodies), and has a possible interaction with the

HLA-DRB1*03 genotype.
Environmental and occupational
agents such as dust, gases, or fumes are also reported risk factors
for patients’ systemic autoimmune diseases, including PM or
DM, as reported in rescue and recovery workers after 9/11 as well
as in patients with anti-synthetase syndrome.
These inhaled
risk factors, together with the high frequency of interstitial lung
disease (ILD) as a manifestation of the anti-synthetase syndromes,
indicate a role for the lung as a target of the immune reaction in
this subset of myositis.
Drugs
Commonly used lipid-lowering drugs such as statins (e.g., atorvastatin, lovastatin) can cause an autoimmune myopathy. Statins
inhibit 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A (HMG-CoA)
reductase, a rate-limiting enzyme involved in the conversion
of HMG-CoA to mevalonic acid, thereby preventing the synthesis of bioactive sterol and nonsterol metabolic intermediates
in the cholesterol synthetic pathway. The mechanism by which
these drugs cause an autoimmune myopathy is not yet clear.
Patients with anti–3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase
(HMGCR) antibodies often have prominent myofiber necrosis with little or no muscle inflammation, also referred to as
IMNM. It has been suggested that statins upregulate HMGCoA autoantigen in regenerating muscle cells that, in turn,
sustain an autoimmune response even after statin withdrawal,
providing a mechanism for statin-induced IMNM.
However, recent studies have indicated that some IMNM patients
with anti-HMGCR antibody are statin-naive, indicating other
potential triggers for this disorder
(see details in “ImmuneMediated Necrotizing Myopathy”). The autoimmune form of
statin myopathy requires immunosuppressive therapy as with
other forms of IIM.
A more recent family of drugs that has been demonstrated
to induce inflammatory myopathies is the new cancer therapies, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs).
ICIs enhance the
immune system’s ability to attack cancer cells by blocking inhibitory immune checkpoints. Although ICIs have shown remarkable
efficacy in the treatment of several malignancies, they are also associated with immune-related adverse events that can affect various
organs and tissues, including skeletal muscles (see
Chapter 130
).
Cases with symptoms and signs indicating an inflammatory
myopathy have been reported, ranging from myalgia to a severe
course with pronounced muscle weakness with inflammation and
muscle fiber necrosis evident in muscle biopsies. These patients
rarely present with autoantibodies associated with myositis.
The
diagnosis of ICI-induced myositis requires a thorough evaluation
of clinical, laboratory, and imaging findings and exclusion of other
causes of myositis. A recent study using transcriptomic profiling
identified three distinct types of ICI-induced myositis that the
authors termed ICI-DM, ICI-MYO1, and ICI-MYO2: (1) the
IL-6 pathway was overexpressed in all groups of myositis, (2) the
type I interferon (IFN) pathway activation was specific for ICIDM, and (3) the type 2 IFN pathway was overexpressed in both
ICI-DM and ICI-MYO1.
Treatment involves prompt cessation
of the offending ICI and initiation of immunosuppressive therapy
with glucocorticoids and other immunosuppressive agents. Overall, ICI-induced myositis is a rare but serious complication of cancer immunotherapy that requires a high index of suspicion and
prompt management to minimize morbidity and mortality.
TNF inhibitors have been associated with the onset of autoimmune diseases such as vasculitis and a lupus-like syndrome. Recent
reports have suggested that TNF inhibitors in inflammatory
arthritis patients may either induce or exacerbate DM or anti-Jo1-positive PM.
Malignancy
Malignancy is an additional risk factor for myositis, and there is a
strong association between DM and malignancies. This early clinical observation has been confirmed in epidemiologic studies.
With regard to PM and IBM, the association with malignancy
is less convincing. The increased risk of malignancy associated
with DM has been established both at the time of DM diagnosis and more than 10 years after diagnosis. A subgroup of DM
has been named cancer-associated DM. Cancer-associated DM is
often used to classify patients with DM with a detected cancer
within 3 years before or after DM diagnosis, although the time
span between the malignancy and DM varies between studies. No
specific malignancy is associated with DM; rather, the most frequent malignancies are also the most common malignancies in
the population.
The pathophysiologic mechanism for the association
between malignancy and DM has not been clarified, but there
could be several explanations. The strong association between
malignancy and the onset of DM, the observation that removal
of a tumor sometimes results in amelioration of muscle weakness, and the fact that tumor reappearance sometimes coincides
with muscle weakness suggests that DM could be a paraneoplastic phenomenon. That is, the development of myositis is
a consequence of the malignancy (related to autoantigens), or
the malignancy and DM share disease mechanisms. The molecular mechanisms underlying this unique association are currently unclear, however. A recent report has shed some light on
the connection between DM and malignancy by showing that
myositis-specific antigens are highly expressed in cancer tissues
and in regenerating muscle cells of myositis patients.
It has
been proposed that in cancer-associated myositis, an autoimmune response directed against cancer cross-reacts with regenerating muscle cells, enabling a feed-forward loop of tissue
damage and antigen selection. This is further supported by the
association between malignancy-associated DM and two newly
identified myositis-specific autoantibodies (TIF1-
γ
and NXP2)
in adult patients (see the paragraph on myositis autoantibodies). Moreover, a wider immune response with more than one
autoantibody seemed to protect patients with anti-TIF1-
γ
from development of overt cancer.
For malignancies that
develop during established disease, the potential explanations
include the presence of chronic inflammation or prolonged
immunosuppressive treatment, which could contribute to the
development of malignancy.
Comorbidities
Associations of myositis with hypertension, diabetes, and ischemic
heart disease have been reported. The prevalence of hypertension
and diabetes in this population was 62% and 29%, respectively,
considerably higher than the background prevalence of 9.4% and
4%. The authors of this report suggest that hypertension and
ischemic heart disease are more likely to be present before the
diagnosis of myositis, whereas hypertension and diabetes occur
more frequently following the diagnosis of myositis in patients
with DM than in patients with PM or IBM, suggesting that it is
essential for the physician to perform a comprehensive assessment
of possible comorbidity as these conditions may predispose to IIM
risk.
The same group also reported that patients with IIM are
at a 75% increased risk for mortality, and cardiovascular diseases

followed by infection and malignancy account for the most common causes of death.
Cataloging genetic and environmental risk and protective factors in well-defined disease phenotypes is an important first step
for discovering gene–environment interactions that could lead to
preventive strategies.

## Pathogenesis

Significant advances have been made in our understanding of the
pathogenesis of the human inflammatory myopathies.
It is
generally thought that IIMs are autoimmune in origin because
they are frequently associated with other autoimmune diseases
(e.g., Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, scleroderma) and many patients
exhibit an autoantibody response, including the presence of myositis-specific autoantibodies associated with specific clinical phenotypes. The development of extramuscular organ manifestations
such as ILD, arthritis, or skin rash as the first clinical manifestations in many patients has also brought up the possibility that the
immune reaction may actually start at sites other than the skeletal
muscle. Although we can now identify clinical subgroups of myositis based on autoantibody profiles, most of the studies reflecting molecular pathogenesis have so far been performed in patients
classified as having PM, DM, or IBM and these “older” subgroups
are referred to in the paragraphs on pathogenesis.

### Humoral Immune Response

More than 50% of all IIM patients have uniquely defined autoantibodies, some of which are specific to myositis and some of which
are merely associated with myositis. These are generally referred
to as
myositis-specific autoantibodies
(MSAs) and
myositis-associated
autoantibodies
(MAAs), respectively. MAAs include autoantibodies to various nuclear and cytoplasmic antigens. Anti-nuclear antibodies present in myositis are not particularly associated with any
disease subgroup, whereas MSAs that are directed against antigens of the protein synthesis pathway (e.g., aminoacyl–transfer
RNA [tRNA] synthetases and signal recognition particles) and
nuclear components (e.g., nuclear helicase [Mi-2]) are often associated with distinct clinical disease groups and subgroups (e.g.,
tRNA synthetases with ILD, Mi-2 with DM) (
Table 86.3
). These
autoantibodies are sometimes present before the onset of clinical
disease.
Anti-histidyl-tRNA synthetase (Jo-1) antibodies are the
most frequent MSA seen and are present in approximately 16%
to 20% of patients with IIM and in about 90% in patients
with IIM and ILD.
Antibodies against other aminoacyltRNA synthetases—such as threonyl-tRNA synthetase (PL-7),
alanyl-tRNA synthetase (PL-12), isoleucyl-tRNA synthetase
(OJ), glycyl-tRNA synthetase (EJ), asparaginyl-tRNA synthetase (KS), tyrosyl-tRNA synthetase (Ha), and phenylalanyltRNA synthetase (Zo)—are found less frequently (1% to 5%).
Anti-Mi-2 antibodies are strongly associated with DM,
with prominent features such as Gottron’s papules, heliotrope
rash, the V sign, and the shawl sign. Anti-TIF1-
γ
and antiNXP2 are associated with cancer-related DM in adults and
with JDM.
Anti-MDA5 antibodies are associated with
clinically amyopathic DM and rapidly progressive ILD in the
Asian population, whereas the clinical phenotype is less specific
in Caucasians.

### Cell-Mediated Immune Response

The cellular immune response has mainly been investigated in
muscle tissue and in peripheral blood of patients with IIM. T
cells and macrophages generally predominate muscle infiltrates
in myositis.
Cytokines produced by these T cell subsets induce
macrophages to polarize into pro-inflammatory M1 (classic) or
proresolution M2 (alternative) phenotypes. M1 macrophages
infiltrate the muscle in the early stages to help clear necrotic debris,
whereas M2 macrophages arrive later to sustain tissue healing.
The relative proportion of lymphocytes and macrophages in skeletal muscle seems to vary in different clinical entities; for example,
in anti-HMGCR myopathy, M2 macrophages predominate over
CD4+ and CD8+ T cells and CD123+ plasmacytoid dendritic
cells (DCs). The presence of M2 macrophages is consistent with

### Summary of Proposed Mechanisms of Muscle Damage

Currently available data suggest that both immune (cell-mediated
and humoral) and nonimmune (ER stress, hypoxia) mechanisms
play a role in muscle fiber damage and dysfunction in myositis.
Innate immune cell activation occurs as a result of injury to skeletal muscle, resulting in the release of damage-associated molecular
patterns (DAMPs). TLR signaling activates various antigen-presenting cells (APCs) in the muscle. These APCs then activate
CD4 T cells and initiate autoantigen-specific T cell responses and
autoantibody responses. Class I MHC overexpression on muscle
also leads to the activation of CD8
T cell–mediated cytotoxicity. DAMPs bind to surface or endogenous TLRs expressed in
muscle-infiltrating immune cells, capillaries, and fibroblasts. TLR
signaling activates NFκ
B and inflammasomes, resulting in the
production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and
adhesion molecules. Cytokines such as TNF can directly induce
the death of muscle cells and also effectively inhibit myogenesis
through microRNAs. TLR signaling also leads to inflammasome
activation (leading to IL-1 secretion) and, potentially, to induction of pyroapoptosis in the affected muscle, whereas activation
of TRAIL induces autophagy in the muscle. Recent studies using
patient muscle samples and experimental models of myositis
describe necroptosis, a caspase-independent form of cell death that
can create pores in the plasma membrane that enable the influx of
calcium or sodium ions, leading to the bursting of the cell, leakage of cellular contents and, eventually, necroptosis.

## Clinical Features

IIMs can be subgrouped based on differences in clinical, serologic and histopathological features into DM, clinically amyopathic DM, anti-synthetase syndrome, IMNM, IBM, and PM.
The inflammatory myopathies most commonly occur as distinct disease entities as discussed below; however, myositis can
sometimes coexist with other rheumatic conditions, and are
then called overlap myositis. The rheumatic diseases most often
associated with inflammatory myopathies are systemic sclerosis,
Sjögren’s syndrome, mixed connective tissue disease, and SLE;
however, RA may also be associated with inflammatory myopathies. IBM may be associated with Sjögren’s syndrome, SLE, and
other autoimmune diseases.
The clinical features of IIM are
discussed in detail in the following section, and subsequently
distinctive clinical features of the subsets of IIM are discussed,
including DM, anti-synthetase syndrome, clinically amyopathic
DM, IBM, and IMNM. Notably, with the identification of
IMNM and anti-synthetase syndrome, the new PM subgroup
has become less frequent, is often seronegative, and requires further clinical and histopathological characterization.

### Muscle Symptoms

The predominant symptoms in most patients with IIM are muscle weakness and low muscle endurance. The weakness is most
pronounced in proximal muscle groups—typically in the neck,
pelvic area, thigh, and shoulder muscles—with a symmetric distribution. Patients generally experience more problems with performing repetitive movements than with single-strength exercises,
and they report difficulty walking uphill or upstairs, working with
their arms above their shoulders, or rising from chairs. Also, in
severe cases, distal muscles may be involved, affecting grip strength
and more severely impacting health-related quality of life.

### Lungs

Lung involvement is frequent (30% to 40%) in PM and DM and
is evident in up to 80% of patients with anti-synthetase syndrome,
serving as a major cause of morbidity and mortality in these disorders. Clinical symptoms such as dyspnea and cough are common.
Lung involvement can be caused by weakness of the respiratory
muscles or inflammation of the lung tissue (ILD). Weakness of
respiratory muscles may lead to restrictive lung disease, and
involvement of the pharyngeal muscles is a risk factor for aspiration
pneumonia. ILD, caused by inflammation at the alveolar level, is
common in PM and DM and is more strongly associated with
anti-synthetase (70% to 80% ILD) and anti-MDA5 (50% to 80%
ILD) autoantibodies. ILD is diagnosed using sensitive techniques
such as high-resolution CT of chest and measurement of pulmonary function and diffusion capacity.
In most cases, the changes
are present at the time of diagnosis of myositis, but ILD can precede development of clinical myositis and sometimes develop after
immunosuppressive treatment has been started for myositis. In
most cases, the ILD is mild to moderate and has a slowly progressive course. Anti-synthetase and anti-MDA5 autoantibodies are
strongly associated with ILD, and thus serve as biomarkers for this
extramuscular manifestation. ILD, especially in patients with antiMDA5 and sometimes in anti-synthetase syndrome, can be rapidly
progressive, leading to death, hospitalization, or supplemental oxygen requirement within a few months of disease onset.
In general, the clinical course and histopathology of ILD in
myositis is similar to those in idiopathic ILD. However, response
to immunosuppression and prognosis is generally more favorable compared with idiopathic forms of ILD such as idiopathic
pulmonary fibrosis. The most common histopathologic findings
are nonspecific interstitial pneumonia (NSIP), usual interstitial
pneumonia (UIP), cryptogenic organizing pneumonia (COP),
and diffuse alveolar damage (DAD). Some studies suggest that
NSIP and COP respond favorably to corticosteroids and immunosuppression, whereas histopathologic changes compatible
with DAD or UIP show less robust responses and have a poor
prognosis.

### Arthritis

Joint pain and arthritis are common in patients with PM, DM,
and anti-synthetase syndrome, and can sometimes be a presenting feature. The most common form of arthritis is a symmetric
arthritis of the small joints of the hands and feet, and therefore it
can mimic RA. This arthritis is typically nonerosive but can sometimes be erosive and destructive. Most frequently, arthritis is seen
in patients with anti-Jo-1 antibodies and other anti-synthetase
autoantibodies, but it is also seen in patients with anti-MDA5
autoantibodies and in overlapping syndromes of other rheumatic
diseases.

### Heart

Cardiovascular disease is a risk factor for death among patients
with IIM. However, clinically evident heart involvement is rare,
perhaps indicating that cardiac involvement may be overlooked
in these conditions. Subclinical manifestations are frequently
discovered when patients with IIM are formally evaluated for an
underlying cardiac disorder. The most frequently reported subclinical manifestations are conduction abnormalities and arrhythmias detected by electrocardiogram (ECG), and subclinical
cardiomyopathies are common when cardiac MRI is performed,
even in patients thought to be in remission.
The underlying
pathophysiologic mechanisms that can lead to cardiac manifestations in patients with IIM are myocarditis and coronary artery
disease and involvement of the small vessels of the myocardium.
Pulmonary hypertension may occasionally be seen, particularly in
patients with anti-synthetase syndrome.
Examination with ECG is recommended in patients newly
diagnosed with IIM. Serum tests such as creatine kinase–myocardial band (CK-MB) to detect cardiac involvement are unreliable
in patients with inflammatory myopathies because CK-MB can
be released from regenerating skeletal muscle fibers, a common
feature in biopsies from patients with IIM. The CK-MB/total CK
ratio may be greater than 3%, a threshold value that is used to
define myocardial damage. A more specific marker for myocardial
damage in myositis patients is increased serum levels of cardiac
isoform troponin I. The other cardiac troponin isoforms, troponin
C and troponin T, are less specific and are also expressed in adult
skeletal muscle; increased serum levels have been reported in various muscle disorders.

### Gastrointestinal Tract

Difficulty swallowing is frequent in patients with inflammatory
myopathies, particularly those with IBM and severe cases of DM,
IMNM, and PM. Muscle weakness occasionally becomes severe and
causes problems with nutrition and aspiration pneumonia. Pathophysiology is related to weakness in the tongue, pharyngeal muscles,
and sometimes the lower esophagus. Reflux that requires special care
is common, occurring in 15% to 50% of patients. Constipation,
diarrhea, and stomach pain are less frequent symptoms and may
result from disturbed motility of the gut or GI tract inflammation.
Vasculitis in the blood vessels of the GI tract is rare but may be complicated by intestinal bleeding and is mostly seen in DM.

## Classification and Diagnostic Criteria

At present, there are no prospectively validated diagnostic or classification criteria for myositis. Dividing diseases into homogeneous
subsets serves several important functions, including allowing
us to estimate disease incidence and prevalence, understand disease pathogenesis and natural history, and evaluate the patient’s
response to therapy and prognosis. More than four decades ago,
Bohan and Peter
proposed a set of five criteria to facilitate
the diagnosis of patients with IIM. They classified IIMs into five
groups: primary idiopathic PM, primary idiopathic DM, IIMs
associated with malignancy, childhood IIMs associated with vasculitis, and IIMs associated with collagen vascular diseases. The
exclusion criteria were several and included signs of central or
peripheral neurologic disease, a family history of muscle disease,
and symptoms and signs suggestive of muscular dystrophy, granulomatous myositis, infections, drug-induced myopathy, toxic
myopathy, rhabdomyolysis, metabolic disorders, endocrinopathies, and myasthenia gravis.
IBM was later recognized as a separate disease entity.
Since Bohan and Peter proposed their classification criteria,
advances in clinical research have led to the identification of certain autoantibodies that are strikingly associated with some clinical phenotypes of myositis (
Table 86.3
).
A multidisciplinary collaboration has developed data-driven
classification criteria for IIM, the 2017 European Alliance of
Associations for Rheumatology/American College of Rheumatology (EULAR/ACR) classification criteria for juvenile and
adult IIM and their major subgroups (
Table 86.4
).
Developed using cross-sectional data, two models were developed:
one for patients with available muscle biopsy data and one for
patients with typical DM skin rash without muscle biopsies.
These models resulted in a high sensitivity and specificity, and
they also performed well in classifying the subsets DM, JDM,
PM, IBM, and CADM. The new criteria include anti-Jo-1
autoantibodies, emphasizing the need for standardized autoantibody tests to minimize the risk of false-positive or falsenegative results.
As a complement to the EULAR/ACR classification criteria,
separate criteria based on expert consensus have been proposed
for the classification of IMNM. These are based on presence of
anti-SRP or anti-HMGCR autoantibodies in combination with
proximal muscle weakness and elevated serum CK levels.
There
are also updated criteria for IBM that include both clinical and
pathologic definitions.

### Physical Examination Findings

Although most patients are first seen with muscle weakness or
fatigue, the IIMs are systemic connective tissue diseases, and other
organs are frequently involved; therefore the physician should
conduct a full clinical examination when patients are seen with
muscle symptoms. This examination could also be helpful in distinguishing IIMs from non-inflammatory myopathies.
The muscle problems reported by many patients consist of not
only muscle weakness but also muscle fatigue or reduced muscle
function and pain. Thus the evaluation must differentiate between
strength and fatigue by evaluating muscle strength and testing
repetitive movements for muscle fatigue. Symmetric proximal
more than distal muscle weakness is seen in most patients with
IIM, whereas both proximal and distal muscle involvement is seen
in IBM and can be asymmetric as well. In the early phases, atrophy is usually not a pronounced phenomenon in IIM. In later
phases, a moderate symmetric atrophy of proximal muscles may
be present. More severe atrophy of the quadriceps muscles and the
flexor muscles of the forearms, which may be asymmetric, often
develop in patients with IBM, and deformities in the finger joints
and difficulty making fists may also develop.
Muscle strength can be tested in various ways. The physician
can quickly screen for weakness in proximal lower leg muscles by
asking the patient to stand up from a sitting or squatting position
without support. A more standardized test that is easy to perform
in the clinic is the manual muscle test, with grading according
to the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale. There are many
variants of this test, but a short form, addressing eight muscles on
the dominant side, is recommended as part of the disease activity score by the International Myositis Assessment and Clinical
Studies.
In many patients with IIM, muscle strength as assessed
by the manual muscle test is good in most of the tested muscle
groups, despite patient report of muscle weakness and fatigue.
In these patients, testing that involves a number of repetitions is
often a more sensitive method of detecting muscle impairment.
The Functional Index in Myositis-2 (FI-2)—or the shorter version, FI-3—is a myositis-specific outcome measure that assesses
a number of repetitions.
With this test, proximal muscle
groups are more involved than are distal muscles. This index is
often used by physical therapists. In patients with IBM, knee
extensors and finger flexors are often weak.
The physician should also examine the skin to detect changes,
including those in nailfolds and the scalp. Joints can be affected
by arthritis, and heart and lung changes should be carefully looked
for.

### Imaging

Muscles
Ultrasonography, CT, and MRI are the three general imaging
techniques used to evaluate skeletal muscle. MRI has emerged
as the method of choice for the examination of soft tissue muscle abnormalities because it efficiently visualizes and quantifies
inflammation, fat infiltration, calcification, and alterations in
muscle size and localizes pathologic changes to specific muscle
groups (
Fig. 86.13
). MRI examinations can be helpful guides for
muscle biopsy sampling, assessing active muscle disease versus
damage as well as an aid in diagnosis given some distinctive MRI
finding of DM, IBM, and IMNM.
MRI is a potential outcome
tool to be used in the longitudinal analysis of responses to therapy
and in clinical trials, although its sensitivity to changes has not
been determined.
Ultrasonography can be used to detect abnormal vascularization, and rates of blood flow can be monitored effectively with
color Doppler imaging. The usefulness of ultrasound in diagnosis
or as a tool to monitor treatment in patients with IIM still needs
to be determined. CT is the modality of choice for identifying
calcifications in soft tissues (e.g., JDM), but it is not useful for
detecting inflammatory changes in muscle tissue. Cross-sectional
CT images allow quantification of muscle atrophy and fat replacement in deep muscles that may not be generally accessible to ultrasonography. A combination of MRI and P-31 MR spectroscopy
examinations produces the most comprehensive and accurate
evaluation of patients but has not been evaluated for use in clinical practice.
Lungs
High-resolution CT of the lungs is important for detecting ILD
and should be considered at the time of myositis diagnosis because
the prevalence of ILD is high. In contrast, conventional radiography may not always be sensitive enough to detect ILD. These are
also important tests for assessing the effects of immunosuppressive
treatment. Baseline high-resolution CT chest is recommended in
patients with myositis even without any pulmonary symptoms,
with the possible exception of IBM where ILD is rare.

### Electromyography

Changes on electromyography (EMG) are a useful indicator of
muscle pathology, which are highly sensitive but usually nonspecific for myopathies. The major abnormalities include abnormal
electrical irritability, a decrease in the mean duration and amplitude of motor unit potentials (short duration and low amplitude)
or increase in the percentage of polyphasic motor unit potentials
(increase polyphasia), and rapid firing of the motor unit potentials
in relation to the level of activity (increased recruitment). Later in
the course of the disease, fibers are lost from some motor units,
and recruitment is reduced. Abnormal electrical irritability in
DM and PM involves increased insertional activity, trains of positive sharp waves, and fibrillation potentials. Spontaneous electrical activity is a reasonable measure of disease activity in DM and
PM. Earlier studies indicated that EMG abnormalities correlate
with alterations in muscle strength and serum muscle enzymes;
however, there is an agreement that EMG is not very sensitive
to change in muscle strength. The inflammation in IIMs is often
patchy, and EMG is useful in determining which muscle should
be sampled for biopsy. Because EMG can cause histopathologic
changes that complicate the interpretation of the biopsy, it is best
to perform EMG on one side and obtain the muscle biopsy from
the same muscle on the contralateral side. Even for IBM, in which
the disease is often asymmetric, this technique is useful as long as
the contralateral muscle is also weak.

obtaining an objective assessment of respiratory involvement.
Typically, patients demonstrate a restrictive ventilatory impairment, with decreased total lung capacity, functional residual
capacity, residual volume, forced expiratory volume in 1 second
(FEV

elevated FEV

Acid Maltase Deficiency.
This autosomal recessive glycogen

the 20s as a progressive myopathy that resembles PM or limbgirdle muscular dystrophy, with additional respiratory symptoms.
Serum muscle enzymes (e.g., CK, AST, and LDH) are increased
in all three forms of the disease, and EMG indicates myopathy in
all three cases.

McArdle’s disease is the most common
of the nonlysosomal muscle glycogenoses. Exercise intolerance
is the characteristic feature of this disease, and it often manifests
as early fatigue, myalgia, and stiffness of exercising muscle that
is relieved by resting. The EMG is normal in some patients and
shows nonspecific myopathic changes in others. Forearm ischemic
exercise testing shows virtually no increase in venous lactate in
most patients. Serum CK levels, however, are variably elevated in
these patients. Muscle biopsies show subsarcolemmal deposits of
glycogen at the periphery of the fibers.
Mitochondrial Myopathies.
Mitochondrial diseases are heterogeneous and often present a diagnostic challenge. It has been
suggested that myopathy is the result of mutations in mitochondrial DNA in the skeletal muscle.
The clinical course of the
pure myopathy varies from rapidly progressive to almost reversible disease, with disease onset occurring from infancy through
adulthood. The weakness is facioscapulohumeral and more
proximal than distal, with involvement of the orbicularis and
extraocular muscles. Patients often complain of exercise intolerance and fatigue and have recurrent episodes of myoglobinuria.
Muscle biopsy plays a critical role in the diagnosis of these conditions, especially the use of special mitochondrial stains that detect
succinate dehydrogenase, Cox staining, and Gomori trichrome
staining.
Endocrine Myopathies
Cushing’s Syndrome.
Cushing’s syndrome is an endogenous
glucocorticoid excess disease that manifests as muscle weakness
and wasting. Chronic corticosteroid treatment results in similar
manifestations and significant loss of strength within a few weeks
of treatment. Muscle biopsy shows increased vacuolations and glycogen accumulation in type II muscle. The onset of weakness is
usually insidious. The weakness is primarily proximal, with more
severe involvement in the legs than in the arms. These patients
generally show normal serum muscle enzyme levels. Muscle wasting can often be reversed if the glucocorticoid levels are returned
to the normal range.
Hyperthyroid and Hypothyroid Myopathy.
Myopathy due
to thyroid disease is characterized primarily by proximal muscle
weakness and muscle wasting. When distal weakness occurs, it
often follows proximal myopathy. Exercise intolerance, fatigue,
and breathlessness are common complaints, and weakness of the
respiratory muscles may result in respiratory insufficiency and the
need for ventilatory support. Patients often have difficulty rising
from a sitting position or lifting their arms above their heads.
Serum muscle enzymes are often normal or low in hyperthyroidism and elevated in hypothyroidism. EMG findings are variable,
with short-duration motor unit potentials and increased polyphasic potentials in proximal muscles; fibrillations and fasciculations
are uncommon.
Infectious Myopathies
Human Immunodeficiency Virus Myopathy.
Neuromuscular manifestations are common in HIV-induced myopathy (see

as proximal symmetric muscle weakness with or without muscle
wasting, similar to that in IIMs. EMG shows spontaneous activity,

## Management and Outcome

The recommended treatment for patients with IIM is based on a
combination of pharmacologic therapy and physical exercise. The
optimal pharmacologic treatment in patients with IIM is unclear.
Few controlled trials have been undertaken, mostly involving the
older subgroups, so recommendations are largely based on clinical
observations from case series. With these limitations in mind, a suggested outline of treatment for patients with IIM excluding IBM
is depicted in

important to give patients adequate information about their disease
and its optimal management. This educational component is best
provided by a rheumatology team and patient support groups.

### Pharmacologic Treatment

The initial pharmacologic treatment in patients with IIM is highdose glucocorticoids: 0.75 to 1 mg/kg body weight per day of
prednisone (or equivalent) for 4 to 8 weeks, followed by slow taper
to low-dose prednisone (5 to 10 mg) over 6 months, and subsequent attempt to taper and discontinue prednisone by 12 months.
Most experts recommend that glucocorticoid treatment be combined with another immunosuppressive drug to reduce the side
effects of the glucocorticoid and to boost the immunosuppressive effect. The most frequently used immunosuppressive agents
are methotrexate and azathioprine. In the extension phase of one
of the few double-blind, placebo-controlled trials that have been
reported, the combination of azathioprine and glucocorticoids,
compared with prednisone alone, was associated with better functional ability and a lower requirement for prednisone after 1 and
3 years.
The recommended azathioprine dosage is 2 mg/kg
per day. The dosing regimen for methotrexate is similar to that for
RA—as much as 25 mg weekly, although there have been reports
of higher doses used. Pulmonary involvement related to myositis
is not necessarily a contraindication for methotrexate.
The combination of methotrexate and azathioprine has proven
to be successful in a few patients with refractory myositis in a prospective, randomized, open-label crossover study comparing two
aggressive approaches. There are several newer case reports and
series that suggest that mycophenolate mofetil can be an effective second-line therapy in patients refractory to glucocorticoids,
methotrexate, and/or azathioprine. Mycophenolate is increasingly being used for refractory DM rashes and ILD in myositis.
Similarly, several beneficial reports of cyclosporine A or tacrolimus
have been reported and is another potential alternative therapy,
especially in patients with ILD.
In patients with ILD, cyclophosphamide could be of value; however, rituximab is being used
more commonly in patients refractory to traditional oral immunosuppressive drugs. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) is
a therapy that has been proven to be effective in several studies
including a placebo-controlled randomized trial in DM. It can be
used as an adjuvant to other treatments for myositis but is most
often used for treatment-resistant DM, cancer-associated myositis, dysphagia, ILD, and in the setting of infection or pregnancy.
In a placebo-controlled trial, IVIG was effective in treatmentresistant DM and is now approved by the U.S. Food and Drug
Administration for this subset of IIM.
IVIG can also be effective in anti-HMGCR-positive necrotizing myopathy. However,
IVIG use is limited as a result of its high cost, requirement of long
and repetitive infusions, tolerance issues, and a therapeutic effect
that may be transient.
In patients with severe, rapidly progressive disease that might be life threatening and moderate to severe
ILD, high-dose pulses of IV methylprednisolone are beneficial.
Pharmacologic treatment, including glucocorticoid tapering,
should be guided by clinical outcome measures. As discussed earlier, the most appropriate outcome measures are muscle endurance
and muscle strength and monitoring of organ-specific manifestations such as skin rashes or ILD. Side effects of high-dose glucocorticoids are frequently observed. Prophylaxis against osteoporosis is
recommended with vitamin D and calcium and, when clinically
indicated, bisphosphonates. Steroid myopathy is another possible consequence of glucocorticoid treatment that is particularly
problematic in patients with inflammatory myopathies. There is
no specific test to verify steroid myopathy but, in the absence of
active clinical disease, steroid myopathy could contribute to muscle weakness. If steroid myopathy is suspected, tapering of the glucocorticoid dose with careful evaluation of the clinical response is
recommended. Glucocorticoids may also cause hypokalemia and,
if this is not corrected, it may be associated with muscle weakness
and incorrectly interpreted as active myositis.
The depletion of B cells has emerged as a treatment strategy in
autoimmune diseases. One approach is to use rituximab (monoclonal antibody against CD20). Although a large multicenter randomized clinical trial (RIM trial) in patients with refractory PM,
DM, and JDM did not meet its endpoint, a post-hoc analysis
indicated that patients positive for anti-Jo-1 or anti-Mi-2 autoantibodies may have a favorable response to rituximab.
There are
several favorable case series of rituximab use in patients with ILD,
especially in anti-synthetase syndrome. Use of TNF inhibitors has
produced mixed results but are not currently recommended for
myositis, especially given cases of exacerbation of disease post antiTNF. One trial examining the use of infliximab in patients with
PM, DM, or IBM found that only 30% of patients experienced
an improvement in disease symptoms, whereas the remaining participants suffered from adverse side effects or worsening of the disease.
In addition, a pilot study of etanercept showed worsening
of the rash associated with DM.
IBM is usually nonresponsive to glucocorticoids. There are
occasional case reports of “stabilization” for a period of months,
but this condition probably reflects the natural history of the
disease. Prolonged administration of glucocorticoids to patients
with IBM may actually lead to worsening of clinical aspects of
the disease, despite the improvement in CK levels and reduced T
cell numbers in biopsies. Prednisone treatment also increases the
number of amyloid-containing fibers. A few small studies have
shown some beneficial effect of IVIG, methotrexate, anti–T lymphocyte globulin, or mycophenolate. However, an open-label trial
comparing methotrexate with methotrexate in combination with
anti–T lymphocyte globulin showed deterioration in those receiving methotrexate alone.
Anabolic steroids and oxandrolone may have some beneficial
effects on muscle strength, although this benefit needs to be confirmed in larger studies. Most experts consider their use justified in
combination with glucocorticoids for a limited period in patients
who have inflammatory infiltrates on muscle biopsy, or in combination with a more aggressive immunosuppressive treatment (e.g.,
methotrexate, azathioprine) in patients with another connective
tissue disease.

### Nonpharmacologic Treatment

With immunosuppressive treatment, approximately 75% of all
patients improve, but few recover normal muscle function, even in
the absence of muscle inflammation. In previous decades, patients
with myositis were advised against exercise because of concerns
over possible muscle damage and inflammation, but recent publications have demonstrated that combining exercise and immunosuppressive therapy is a safe approach and has clear beneficial
effects on muscle strength and function.
Chronic inflammation
leads to loss of skeletal muscle because of low levels of physical
activity, negative effects of glucocorticoids, and circulating cytokines. In addition, in late phases of disease, patients with PM and
DM may still experience muscle weakness, despite the absence
of muscle inflammation.
Exercise has proven safe and able to
improve muscle performance and health-related quality of life in
both early and late phases of disease. Emerging evidence suggests
that exercise can even decrease muscle and systemic inflammation.
Similar to healthy individuals, exercise has a short-term
benefit and needs to be implemented on a regular basis.
In a pair of small studies of immunosuppressed myositis
patients, personalized submaximal exercise programs improved
muscle function without causing any increase in inflammation.
This improvement was true for patients who had individually tailored exercise regimens
and self-administered home exercise
routines.
Examinations of biopsies both before and after exercise indicated that the patients showed improved muscle strength
as the result of an increase in the proportion of slow-twitch (type I)
muscle fibers and a reduction in inflammation and fibrosis.
More evidence suggests that combining exercise and immunosuppressive therapy is a safe approach and can improve performance
without disease exacerbation, and exercise can decrease muscle
and systemic inflammation.
The exercise regimen should be
individualized and supervised by a physiotherapist to avoid the
overuse of muscles, and it should combine aerobic and resistance
exercise, starting as soon as the condition of the patient permits.
Physical exercise is now recommended as a combination therapy
with immunosuppressive treatment.

### Assessing Disease Activity and Outcome

The most important variable to measure in myositis patients is
muscle strength or physical function. It is equally important to
evaluate whether impaired muscle strength or function reflects
active disease or irreversible muscle damage. In patients with dominating extramuscular manifestations, these should be evaluated
and followed with relevant tests.
Assessments of Muscle
Manual Muscle Test.
There are several tools to measure muscle
performance, but the method used most often in clinical practice and clinical trials is the manual muscle test with the MRC
scale (see earlier). The drawback is that these tools measure muscle
strength but not muscle endurance, which is often a major problem in PM or DM. Previously, the number of muscle groups tested
using the manual muscle test varied, and different scales were used
(5 or 10 grade). Recently a consensus was reached to assess eight
muscle groups on the dominant side using a 0- to 10-point scale,
in which 0 indicates no muscle contraction; 5, the ability to hold
the test position without any added pressure; and 10, the ability to hold the test position against strong pressure. The points
between these scores are based on gradual increased resistance
against the examiner’s pressure. The eight muscle groups tested are
neck flexors, shoulder abduction (deltoid middle), biceps brachii,
wrist extensors, knee extensors (quadriceps), dorsiflexion of ankle,
gluteus maximus, and gluteus medius. The score achieved varies
between 0 and 80.
Functional Index in Myositis.
The Functional Index in Myositis and its revised form, the Functional Index in Myositis-2, were
developed as outcome measures for patients with PM or DM. This
test measures the number of repetitions that can be performed
in defined muscle groups.
It is a more sensitive method
of measuring impaired muscle function in patients with PM or
DM.
The drawback is that it takes long to perform, requires
significant training, and therefore it is difficult to use in everyday
clinical practice. Preferably, the Functional Index in Myositis-2 is
administered by a physiotherapist and can be combined with the
manual muscle test by a physician in the office. A shorter version,
Functional Index in Myositis-3, has recently been validated and,
with only three muscle groups tested, it will be more feasible to
use regularly in the clinic.
Other Timed Tests.
In addition, various timed tests are utilized to evaluate muscle function, endurance, and fatigability for therapeutic trials or observational studies. These tests
comprise the 2-minute or 6-minute walking distance test, the
timed 10-minute walk or run test, the timed up-and-go test, the
30-second chair stand test, and the Short Physical Performance
Battery. However, these tests have not been fully validated in all
subsets of adult patients with IIM. Validated functional measures can be important secondary endpoints in clinical trials or
studies of patients with IIM, particularly in patients who have
baseline muscle weakness. Standardization and normative data
are of critical importance in the interpretation of these tests. On
the other hand, questionnaires and scales that assess a patient’s
self-report of daily activities can be self-administered or administered by an interviewer. While functional questionnaires are
feasible and easily administered, they do not directly evaluate
physical activity.
Muscle Enzymes.
There are five muscle enzymes that should be
evaluated in any patient with myositis: CK, LDH, AST, ALT, and
aldolase. While CK is most commonly used for initial diagnosis
and assessments of subsequent treatment responses, in some individuals, AST, ALT, and LDH might be more commonly deranged.
Therefore the most abnormal muscle enzyme at baseline (relative to
its upper limit of normal) should be used for treatment responses
or relapse assessment. Although muscle enzyme testing provides an
objective assessment of active muscle disease, it is beneficial mainly
to patients with PM and necrotizing autoimmune myopathy. In
patients with DM or IBM, levels of muscle enzymes may not correlate well with disease activity. AST and ALT often cause confusion between active muscle disease versus hepatotoxicity associated
with myositis therapies, which can be mitigated by the use of other
liver-specific biomarkers such as gamma-glutamyl transferase,
alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin, and, most importantly, glutamate
dehydrogenase.
Imaging.
MRI and muscle ultrasound could be valuable tools
for muscle disease assessment and in quantifying longitudinal
treatment responses in the future. However, standardization and
validation are currently lacking, with only a few specialized centers
able to perform longitudinal assessments of myositis with MRIs.
Assessments of Extramuscular Involvement
In some patients, extramuscular symptoms predominate among
the clinical features. These symptoms may require other assessment tools, such as those used to evaluate ILD. For monitoring
the effects of treatment of ILD, PFTs and high-resolution CT

are recommended when there is worsening of pulmonary function of unclear explanation. Similarly, when rash or joint pain
is the dominating clinical feature, frequent clinical examination
of rash or arthritis is carried out to assess treatment responses,
respectively. A clinical tool called the Cutaneous Dermatomyositis
Disease Area and Severity Index, which is a physician-based scoring of DM rash, can be used for semiquantitative evaluation for
treatment responses to rash. Similarly, disease activity tools for RA
such as tender or swollen joint counts can be used clinically for
articular assessments and treatment responses; however, these have
not been validated for use in myositis.
Patient-Reported Outcomes
The use of patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) is vital
to standardize clinical trial outcomes and provide valuable data on
both treatment efficacy and quality of life, which are immediately
relevant to the management of disease activity. In patients with
IIM, disease-specific PROMs are particularly relevant due to the
heterogeneity in disease presentation and the variable effects of
disease on patients. Currently there are many general PROMs that
are being increasingly validated in myositis such as Short Form36, Health Assessment Questionnaire, visual analogue scale or
numerical rating scale scores of pain, fatigue, Facit-fatigue, itch,
skin quality-of-life indices, and Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System scores of pain, fatigue, and physical function. To assess the impact of IIM on the daily lives of
patients, the Outcome Measures in Rheumatology Clinical Trials
consortium is developing IIM-specific PROMs and focus groups
suggested the top five rated domains included muscle symptoms,
fatigue, interaction with health-care providers and authorities,
medication adverse effects, and pain.

Measures of Physical Activity
Physical activity is one of the domains important to myositis
patients as a treatment outcome. Fortunately, it can be monitored
at home using wearable devices such as accelerometers, which
have been pilot-tested in patients with IIM.
Major advantages of this approach include practicality, objectivity, continuous
longitudinal monitoring, and a lack of cognitive input from the
patient or examiner. However, the interpretation of results must
consider that completing physical activities depends on behavioral
factors, not just motor capacity.

Outcome Measures in Myositis Clinical Trials
The International Myositis Assessment & Clinical Studies Group
(IMACS), a consortium of health professionals interested in myositis, made a consensus recommendation that outcome measures for
patients with myositis include tools that measure disease activity,
damage, and health-related quality of life. The IMACS network has
developed one outcome measure to assess myositis disease activity
and one to measure organ damage: the myositis disease activity
assessment tool and the myositis damage index, respectively.
Given that myositis is a complex and heterogeneous disorder, six
different myositis core measures covering different aspects of the
disease have been defined by expert consensus to be used in all

also be useful in clinical practice, although they require training.
Furthermore, these six myositis core set measures were combined
