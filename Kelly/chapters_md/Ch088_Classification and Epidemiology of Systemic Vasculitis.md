## Introduction

The vasculitides are a group of complex disorders characterized
by inflammation of blood vessel walls that can lead to ischemia
and organ damage. A vasculitis is considered primary when no
underlying etiology such as infection, another rheumatic disease,
drugs, or malignancy has been identified and secondary when the
vasculitis is considered a consequence of one of these comorbidities or exposures. The heterogeneous and protean nature of the
vasculitides’ clinical courses and outcomes, as well as the lack of
a single “gold standard” test for either diagnosis or classification,
have resulted in great efforts to develop criteria useful in both
clinical care and research. The first part of this chapter will review
current classification criteria in vasculitis, and the second part will
discuss current epidemiologic data on the vasculitides.

## Classification of Vasculitis

### Introduction to the Concept of Classification

The vasculitides are a diverse group of disorders, each with a distinct pathogenesis, natural history, and associated therapeutic
approach, which underscores the value of accurate categorizations.
Due to the limited understanding of the pathophysiology of these
complex diseases and their overlapping features, several categorization systems have been developed and revised by international
collaborations to ensure consistency in disease definitions (i.e.,
nomenclature) and classification criteria.
There are important differences in the purposes of a nomenclature system, classification criteria, and diagnostic criteria, and
therefore, they should not be used interchangeably. A
nomenclature system
is the name that should be used to define a specific disease process. A nomenclature system provides a framework upon
which classification and diagnostic criteria can be developed. In
1994 the first International Chapel Hill Consensus Conference
(CHCC) on the Nomenclature of Systemic Vasculitides convened
to address the need for standardized terms and definitions; a
revised version was developed in 2012.
Prior to the CHCC, different names were being used for the same disease, and conversely,
the same name was being used to refer to different diseases. For
example, the term
polyarteritis nodosa (PAN)
was initially used to
describe nearly any form of necrotizing arteritis; thereafter, antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)–associated vasculitides
were noted to have a separate clinical presentation and course
and were given distinct names to differentiate them from PAN.
Over the past decade, academic medicine has shifted away from
eponyms to descriptive or etiology-based nomenclature, and this
movement has led to several changes in disease names within vasculitis (e.g.,
Wegener’s granulomatosis
was replaced by
granulomatosis with polyangiitis,
and
Henoch–Schönlein purpura
was replaced
by
IgA vasculitis
).
Classification criteria
are a set of standardized observations
that are intended to create a relatively homogeneous cohort of
patients for clinical research.
Classification systems build upon
the nomenclature system and include features of a disease that
distinguish it from other similar diseases. The goal of classification criteria is to standardize patient populations by identifying
patients who share key features of a condition and is less focused
on capturing all possible patients with the condition. Classification criteria are less useful for individual patients and more important in the study of groups of patients, allowing comparison of

results across studies and ensuring appropriate enrollment into
clinical trials. Classification criteria aim to have high specificity,
while a lower priority is placed on sensitivity; therefore the falsenegative rates of most classification criteria make them less useful
in routine clinical care (i.e., a given patient may have the correct
diagnosis but may not meet classification criteria).
Unlike classification criteria,
diagnostic criteria
are a set of symptoms, signs, and tests that guide the care of individual patients and
must be high in both sensitivity and specificity. Fewer attempts
have been made in developing diagnostic criteria in vasculitis,
owing to the challenges of forming criteria that can apply to the
heterogeneous manifestations seen in vasculitis.
The first set of classification criteria for several forms of vasculitis was published in 1990 and endorsed by the American College of Rheumatology (ACR). As knowledge of vasculitis evolved,
there was a need to revise the 1990 criteria and develop new criteria for microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), a distinct disease that
was not included in the 1990 classification criteria. An international effort, the
Diagnostic and Classification Criteria for Vasculitis
(DCVAS)
study was conducted to develop validated, data-driven
sets of revised classification criteria for several vasculitides.
Given
the increased understanding of vasculitis, newer diagnostic tests,
such as testing for ANCA, and advancements in vascular imaging, reappraising existing criteria was in order and signifies the
substantial gains made in this field. It is important to note that
the classification criteria were intended to be applied to patients

Classification by Anti-neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody
Specificity
Within GPA and MPA, subclassification by antigenic specificity
of ANCA (PR3 vs. MPO) is a common alternative to clinicopathologic classification. The ANCA-based approach is based on
data from genomic studies and clinical trial data linking ANCA
specificity to disease outcomes. Several studies have demonstrated
that ANCA has a pathogenic role in AAV, although clinical observations suggest that ANCA alone is not sufficient to induce disease activity.
Two genome-wide association studies found that
genetic distinctions are more closely aligned with ANCA specificity than clinical presentations, further supporting that ANCA
may indeed contribute to disease susceptibility and pathogenesis.
Furthermore, several studies have found that, compared
with classification by disease type (GPA vs. MPA), ANCA specificity is more strongly associated with treatment response, relapse,
and other long-term outcomes.
These clinical and genetic
associations with ANCA specificity suggest that ANCA type may
classify subsets of AAV better than the traditional GPA/MPA
classification.
Immune Complex–Mediated Small-Vessel Vasculitis
The remaining primary vasculitides that predominantly affect
small vessels have a common immunopathologic feature of
immune complex–mediated inflammation that leads to tissue
injury. This is in contrast to AAV, which is pauci-immune (i.e.,
few or no immune deposits). Among the immune complex–mediated small-vessel vasculitides, IgA vasculitis is the only disease to
have a validated classification criteria available.
IgA Vasculitis (Henoch–Schönlein).
IgA vasculitis is characterized by IgA immune complexes and IgA deposition on tissue
biopsy. Common features include rash (often purpura), arthritis/
arthralgias, abdominal pain, and renal disease (hematuria, proteinuria). The 1990 ACR criteria were developed to distinguish IgA
vasculitis from other types of vasculitis seen in adults
(see
Table
88.1
). In 2005 the EULAR/PReS developed criteria for children.
Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis.
Deposition of circulating cryoglobulins leading to organ damage is the hallmark feature of cryoglobulinemic vasculitis (CV). Importantly, it is possible to have
asymptomatic cryoglobulinemia without a clinically apparent
disorder of CV, and therefore, the detection of circulating cryoglobulins alone is insufficient to diagnose CV. CV is routinely
classified further by the immunoglobulin composition of the
cryoglobulins
:
• Type I—Cryoglobulins are monoclonal immunoglobulins,
typically IgG or IgM. Type I cryoglobulinemia develops in
the setting of monoclonal gammopathies or B cell lineage
malignancy (e.g., multiple myeloma, Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia, or chronic lymphocytic leukemia).
• Type II—Cryoglobulins are composed of monoclonal IgM
(or IgG or IgA) with rheumatoid factor activity and polyclonal immunoglobulins. Type II cryoglobulinemia is most
often associated with hepatitis C virus infection but can also
be seen in infections with hepatitis B virus or HIV, malignancy, or autoimmune diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus and Sjögren’s syndrome.
• Type III—Cryoglobulins are a mixture of polyclonal IgG
and polyclonal IgM, and type III is often secondary to autoimmune disorders but also infections such as with hepatitis
C virus.
Both type II and type III are referred to as mixed cryoglobulinemia and cause cutaneous vasculitis, peripheral neuropathy, and
membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis, as well as nonspecific
symptoms such as fatigue, arthralgias, and myalgias.
Hypocomplementemic Urticarial Vasculitis.
Hypocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis (HUV)
refers to an autoimmune
disorder that involves urticarial vasculitis, which typically affects
the skin and is associated with hypocomplementemia.
When
additional systemic features are present, such as arthritis, glomerulonephritis, uveitis, episcleritis, or recurrent abdominal pain,
the disease is referred to as
hypocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis
syndrome (HUVS).
HUV is commonly associated with low serum
C1q levels and anti-C1q autoantibodies.

### Medium-Vessel Vasculitis

Polyarteritis Nodosa
The definition of PAN has evolved considerably since it was initially described in the 1800s. Early classifications of PAN included
what was later referred to as microscopic polyangiitis and hypersensitivity vasculitis, but new insights led to recognition that
these were separate diseases. PAN predominantly affects medium
arteries such as main visceral arteries and their branches and commonly involves inflammatory aneurysms and arterial stenoses. To

distinguish PAN from MPA, the definition of PAN specifically
excludes the presence of glomerulonephritis and ANCA. The
1990 ACR criteria for classification of PAN includes many of the
common manifestations such as nonspecific constitutional symptoms, neuropathy, renal involvement, and testicular pain. PAN is
strongly linked to hepatitis B virus infection and, to a lesser degree,
is associated with hepatitis C virus and HIV. EULAR/PReS proposed separate criteria for childhood PAN, which removed the
criteria for hepatitis B infection and modified the other criteria.
It is also important to distinguish idiopathic PAN from deficiency
of adenosine deaminase 2
(see section on Monogenic Vasculitides below).
Kawasaki’s Disease
Kawasaki’s disease (KD) typically occurs in children under the age
of 5 and predominantly affects medium and small arteries. Coronary arteritis is a common complication. The American Heart
Association published diagnostic criteria and treatment guidelines for KD that were developed by a committee of experts and
updated in 2017.
Diagnosis of classic KD requires 5 or more
days of fever plus at least four of the five principal clinical features.
Patients with suspected KD who did not meet these diagnostic criteria are considered to have incomplete KD but may still
benefit from therapy. Other groups from Japan and Europe have
produced similar diagnostic criteria.

### Large-Vessel Vasculitis

Giant Cell Arteritis
Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is considered a disease exclusively seen
in older adults and, by definition, onset occurs only in those aged
50 years or older. Early classification criteria, such as the 1990
ACR classification criteria of GCA, were focused on cranial symptoms of headache, temporal artery abnormalities, and an abnormal
artery biopsy, typically a temporal artery biopsy; hence, in the past
GCA was commonly referred to as
temporal arteritis.
However,
replacement of the term
temporal arteritis
with
GCA
was motivated by an increased recognition that GCA can affect many other
extracranial large arteries, including the aorta and its branches.
Advancements in imaging of the vasculature now demonstrate
that up to 30% of patients with GCA have extracranial large vessel involvement such as thoracic aortic aneurysms and subclavian
artery involvement.
The 2022 ACR/EULAR GCA Classification Criteria includes a wider spectrum of disease manifestations,
including thoracoabdominal vessel involvement, polymyalgia
rheumatica (present in about 50% of patients with GCA), and
vision loss
(
Table 88.1
). In addition, newer vascular imaging
modalities, including ultrasound of the temporal artery and positron emission tomography (PET) of the aorta, were incorporated
into the criteria, reflecting advances in imaging techniques for vasculitis. It is notable that GCA is a good example of a vasculitis that
has the potential to have a range of arterial sizes affected, including
large and medium arteries (e.g., aorta) and smaller arteries (e.g.,
central retinal artery).
Takayasu’s Arteritis
As opposed to GCA, Takayasu’s arteritis (TAK) typically occurs in
patients under the age of 50 to 60 years, and the classic presentation of TAK involves signs and symptoms related to its effects on the
aorta and its major branches. In addition to expanding the inclusion
of signs and symptoms of ischemia, imaging findings were a major
addition to the 2022 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria for TAK
(
Table 88.1
). Similarly to GCA, the inclusion of vascular imaging to
the TAK criteria reflects the central role of imaging in clinical practice
and enhances sensitivity to detect earlier-stage disease.

Similarities between GCA

### Other Classifications

Variable-Vessel Vasculitis: Behçet’s Disease and Cogan’s
Syndrome
Since Behçet’s disease was first described in the 1930s, there have
been over 15 classification criteria proposed for the disease. In an
effort to develop a standardized criteria, the international study
group (ISG) for Behçet’s disease created a new set of criteria in
1990 that required at least three recurrences of oral ulcers over a
12-month period and at least two of the following: recurrent genital ulceration, eye lesions, skin lesions, or positive pathergy test.
In several subsequent validation cohorts, specificity of the ISG criteria was high, but sensitivity was low. In 2014 an international
team of 27 countries developed the new International Criteria for
Behçet’s Disease (ICBD) to improve sensitivity compared with the
ISG criteria. The ICBD criteria no longer required the presence of
oral aphthosis and included vascular lesions and neurologic manifestations. Despite the reported higher sensitivity of the ICBD
criteria, ISG remains the preferred classification criteria for use in
clinical trials of Behçet’s disease.
There are currently no validated classification or diagnostic
criteria for Cogan’s syndrome, a disease characterized by ocular
inflammatory lesions (notably interstitial keratitis), sensorineural
hearing loss, vestibular dysfunction, aortitis, aortic aneurysms,
and valvulitis.
Single-Organ Vasculitis
Vasculitis can also occur in a single organ, such as the skin or
the central nervous system (CNS). In these cases, comprehensive
evaluation is needed to ensure the presentation is not an early or
limited expression of a systemic vasculitis. Often, patients originally classified as having single-organ vasculitis will later develop
other organ manifestations that require reclassification of disease.
Primary angiitis of the CNS is the most clearly recognized singleorgan vasculitis and can involve both medium- and small-sized
arteries.
Secondary Causes of Vasculitis
Many other systemic autoimmune diseases are associated with
vasculitis, including systemic lupus erythematosus, Sjögren’s

syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, and relapsing polychondritis.
Vasculitis can also be attributed to a wide variety of medications,
toxins, or infectious agents. The most common causes of druginduced severe systemic small vessel vasculitis include hydralazine
and propylthiouracil, which are associated with MPO-ANCA
and can potentially lead to alveolar hemorrhage and glomerulonephritis. Almost every class of medication has been associated
with causing vasculitis, although true causality can be difficult to
prove. With the advent of immunotherapy to treat cancer, there
is recognition of vasculitis associated with immune checkpoint
inhibitors (ICIs).
Vasculitis has been observed in several ICIs
and typically resolves with administration of glucocorticoids or
withdrawing the ICI.
Monogenic Vasculitides
Although vasculitis is generally considered a polygenic disease, the growing availability of whole-genome and exome
sequencing has led to the discovery of single gene mutations
in subsets of patients with vasculitis. Deficiency of adenosine deaminase 2 (DADA2) was the first monogenic vasculitis
syndrome described and can present with features of PAN,
especially ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke, often in younger
patients
Because of the need for genetic counseling and
the recognition that tumor necrosis factor inhibitors are effective in treating DADA2 but not used regularly in treatment
of PAN, it is important to consider screening patients with
suspected PAN for DADA2.

### Geography

The incidences of various systemic vasculitides vary tremendously
by geography. This variation may reflect differences in genetics,
environmental exposures dictated by continent and latitude,
and the prevalence of other risk factors for the disease. Although
Behçet’s disease is rare in North Americans (affecting only approximately 1 in 300,000), the condition is substantially more common among inhabitants of countries that border the ancient Silk
Route.
Similarly, although TAK is rare in the United States,
the disease is reportedly the most common cause of renal artery stenosis in India. Several studies indicate that the prevalence of GCA
in Olmsted County, Minnesota, is similar to that of Scandinavian
countries, with an annual incidence rate of approximately 240
cases for every 1 million people older than 50 years.
The similar prevalence calculations across these countries probably reflect
shared genetic risk factors for this condition, because many of the
current inhabitants of Olmsted County are descended from Scandinavian and Northern European immigrants. With the steady
increase in worldwide migration and increasingly mobile societies,
the prevalences of the vasculitides will continue to change in many
countries, and clinicians need to avoid over-reliance on assumptions of the rarity of some diseases to avoid missing the diagnosis.

### Age, Sex, and Ethnicity

Age is an important consideration in the epidemiology of vasculitis. Among patients with KD, 80% are younger than 5 years.
In contrast, GCA almost exclusively occurs in patients older
than 50 years, and the mean age is approximately 70 years. Age
also may affect disease severity and outcome. Most cases of IgA
vasculitis occur in children and have self-limited courses that
resolve within several weeks. In adults, IgA vasculitis has a higher
likelihood of chronicity and a greater likelihood of a poor renal
outcome.
There are notable sex differences in the epidemiology of several
types of vasculitis. TAK has an overwhelming tendency to occur
in women (9:1 female-to-male ratio).
Pauci-immune forms of
vasculitis such as GPA occur in men and women with approximately equal frequencies, but some evidence exists for a female
predominance in patients with the nonsevere form of the disease
and a male predominance among patients with severe GPA.
Behçet’s disease tends to be more severe in younger male patients
with a greater frequency of advanced ocular disease.
Some forms of vasculitis have striking variations in tendencies to affect specific ethnic groups. For example, GCA and GPA
occur with a pronounced predominance in White people, whereas
TAK and KD have higher incidences in people of Asian ancestry.
Additionally, the relative proportion of patients
with AAV who have antibodies to anti–proteinase 3 (PR3-ANCA)
versus antibodies to myeloperoxidase (MPO-ANCA) is quite different between Northern Europe (majority anti-PR3 ANCA) and
Japan (vast majority anti-MPO ANCA).

### Genetics

Although genetic risk factors are undoubtedly important in the
susceptibility to some forms of vasculitis, familial cases are rare
(with the exception of GCA, as subsequently discussed). The
rarity of familial cases in vasculitis indicates that the genetics
of these disorders, aside from monogenic causes (see section on
Monogenic Vasculitides above), are polygenic and complex. For
example, Behçet’s disease is associated with human leukocyte
antigen (HLA)-B51, which is found in about 80% of patients of
Asian ancestry with the disease.
The prevalence of
HLA-B51
is
significantly higher among patients with Behçet’s disease in Japan
than among control subjects without the disease (55% vs. <15%).
However, among the sporadic cases of Behçet’s disease involving
White patients in the United States,
HLA-B51
occurs in fewer
than 15% of cases. In addition to increasing the risk of disease
susceptibility in some patients,
HLA-B51
increases disease severity. Patients with this gene are more likely to have posterior uveitis,
CNS involvement, or other severe manifestations.
Reports of familial aggregation in GCA are common. Genetic
studies have indicated roles for class II HLA alleles such as HLADRB1*0401 and HLA-DRB1*0101, although the specific associations have varied from study to study.
Other work indicates
that certain TNF microsatellite polymorphisms may contribute to
disease susceptibility.
Two genome-wide association studies confirmed a modest
genetic component to the pathogenesis of AAV.
These studies both demonstrated that the strongest genetic associations are
with the type of ANCA (anti-PR3 vs. anti-MPO) rather than
with the clinical diagnosis of GPA versus MPA. In particular,
PRTN3, SERPINA1,
and
PTPN22
genes confer a higher risk of
PR3-ANCA AAV, confirming the central role of anti–proteinase 3
autoreactivity in the disease process.

### Environment

Several environmental and occupational exposures have been
linked to the development of vasculitis, although confirmatory
data are frequently lacking. Medications and certain infections
have well-known associations with vasculitides. As examples, antibiotics such as the penicillins and cephalosporins are common
causes of drug-induced vasculitis, and virtually any medication can
trigger vasculitis. Hepatitis B and C viruses have well-recognized
links to PAN and mixed CV, respectively. Moreover, both medications and infections appear to possibly trigger IgA vasculitis.
Associations have been reported, but not confirmed, between
exposure to inhaled silica dust and some types of pauci-immune
vasculitis.
Precise understanding of the relationships between
exposures and vasculitis is complicated by difficulties in obtaining
reliable measurements of the levels of such exposures, the likelihood of recall bias among patients who are diagnosed with vasculitis, and the choice of appropriate control groups.
The epidemiologic differences among individual types of vasculitis raise compelling questions about the etiologies of these
diseases. Ultimately, better insights into the pathogenesis of these
conditions should explain these differences and facilitate the
development of new treatments and more refined classification
schemes. In particular, elucidation of the cellular and molecular
pathways, as well as genetic risk factors of vasculitis and other
rheumatic diseases, will likely enhance current classification criteria. Most likely, criteria will move toward a hybrid of clinical,
imaging, genetic, and molecular markers of disease, and the relative weights of these individual components will be determined by
the degree of understanding of the pathogenesis of the disease and
importance of the variables to clinical outcomes. Therefore disease
classification is continually evolving and should be responsive to
changes in understanding of the disease processes.
