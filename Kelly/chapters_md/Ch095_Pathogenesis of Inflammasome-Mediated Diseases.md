## Introduction

The immune system uses a limited number of germline-encoded
pattern recognition receptors to detect pathogen- and damageassociated molecular patterns (PAMPs or DAMPs, respectively)
(see
Chapter 19
). Many of these danger signals are detected by
intracellular multiprotein complexes, called inflammasomes
that, when activated, lead to proteolytic cleavage of caspases and
procytokines, mature pro-inflammatory cytokine release, and cell
death. These immune sensors came to the attention of rheumatologists when mutations in the genes that code for inflammasome
proteins were identified in patients with familial autoinflammatory syndromes, a group of rare inherited inflammatory disorders
characterized by hyperactive innate immune responses (see
Chapter 99
). Since that discovery, inflammasomes have been implicated in numerous diseases that are seen by rheumatologists. This
chapter focuses on inflammasome structure and function in the
context of inflammatory diseases.

## Inflammasome Biology

### Inflammasome Structure and Assembly

Inflammasomes consist of up to five types of proteins: one or more
multidomain sensor molecules for which the complex is named, a
multidomain adapter molecule known as apoptosis-related specklike protein containing caspase activation and recruitment domain
(ASC), enzymatic effector molecules such as caspases, interacting
regulatory or scaffolding proteins, and chaperone proteins. The
sensor molecules are either Nod-like receptors (NLRs) containing
either a Pyrin domain (NLRPs) or a caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD, NLR family CARD-containing protein
[NLRCs]), Pyrin, Pyrin and HIN (PYHINs), or CARD8. There
are 22 known NLRs in humans, each identified by a conserved
arrangement of domains including a PYRIN domain (PYD) or
CARD.
However, only a subset (NLRP1, NLRP3, NLRP6,
NLRP7, NLRP12, and NLRC4) appear to form functional
inflammasomes capable of executing an inflammatory response
and mature cytokine release. CARD8
and Pyrin also form
inflammasomes
and in the PYHIN family, absent in melanoma
2 (AIM2) and interferon (IFN)-inducible protein 16 (IFI16) can
form functional inflammasomes.
The oligomeric NLR inflammasomes form in the cytoplasm with component proteins coming together through specific intradomain interactions (
Fig. 95.1

drive self-assembly involving seven or more of the same protein
molecules.

conformational changes from cage-like inactive structures to
disk-like active complexes. These changes allow for binding to
microtubules and interactions through scaffold proteins with
one or more intracellular organelles including mitochondria
or Golgi apparatus. Activation allows for binding to one of
the PYDs of NLRPs to ASC, an adaptor protein also known

### Inflammasome Regulation

Inflammatory reactions must be tightly controlled in order to prevent chronic inflammation and tissue damage (
Fig. 95.1
). Inflammasome pathways autopropagate by ASC speck release described
previously and also by a positive feedback loop of cytokine-driven
transcriptional upregulation of inflammasome and procytokine
expression. Therefore regulation of inflammasome driven pathways is required at multiple steps. Expression of sensor proteins
and many other inflammasome-associated proteins are regulated
both at the RNA level and by post-translational modifications and
proteolysis at the protein level.
Intraprotein domain interactions and interprotein domain oligomerization can be mediated
by regulatory/scaffold and chaperone proteins.
Activation
of caspase-1 and release of mature cytokines are dependent on
proteolytic cleavage to form biologically active forms. Cytokinedriven inflammation is dependent on binding to their respective
receptors. Competitive inhibition by IL-1 receptor antagonist and
IL-18 binding protein regulates cytokine binding to the respective
receptors. Dysregulation at any of these steps could drive chronic

### Expression Patterns of Inflammasome Proteins

Inflammasome function has mostly been studied in myeloid cells
based on the primary expression of NLRP3 in monocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils. While many other inflammasome sensor
proteins are also expressed in the myeloid compartment, increasing attention has been focused on the role of inflammasomes in
other cells including epithelial cells and lymphoid cells, in which
downstream effector functions may be different. This is relevant
since NLRC4 is expressed in gut epithelial cells and NLRP1 is
expressed in skin and lung epithelial cells, which correlate with the
symptoms observed in autoinflammatory syndromes.

### Inflammasomes and Autoinflammation

The investigation of autoinflammatory disease and inflammasomes
has involved a strong connection between carefully phenotyped
patients and scientific studies at the bench. Over the last 20 years,
this translational loop has been instrumental to the development of
novel, targeted therapies for such patients
(see
Chapter 99
). While
NRLP1 was the first inflammasome described in 2002,
it was the
earlier discovery of mutations in
NLRP3
in select patients with
autoinflammatory disease that solidified the importance of inflammasomes in human disease.
Likewise, while Pyrin was discovered
in 1997,
it took almost 10 years to confirm that it formed an
inflammasome. The elucidation of inflammasome molecular pathways increased our understanding of basic innate immune mechanisms, solved several questions regarding the mechanisms of IL-1
β
release, and led to successful therapy of many of the monogenic
autoinflammatory diseases with IL-1–targeted therapy. Each of
the known functional inflammasomes are described in the sections
below, including their unique structural and functional features and
the pathogenesis of associated monogenic diseases.

## Functional Inflammasomes

### NLRP1

NLRP1 (CARD7, NALP1) was the first NLR shown to form an
inflammasome
and is unique in that it contains a function to find
domain (FIIND) in addition to a CARD, both of which appear
to be critical to NLRP1 inflammasome formation and function.
Since NLRP1 has its own CARD domain, ASC is not always necessary for NLRP1 activation. Further studies have demonstrated
that NLRP1 activity requires post-translational autolytic cleavage
within the FIIND.
Recently, it was identified that this proteolytic cleavage generates a novel N terminus that is recognized by the
N-end rule pathway that ubiquitinates and degrades proteins.
This mechanism, named functional degradation, uniquely generates a break within the FIIND domain that prevents simultaneous
degradation of the NLRP1 C-terminal fragment with the NLRP1
N-terminal fragment. The C-terminal fragment is therefore freed
to recruit and activate pro-CASP1 and induce pyroptosis.
This is important as there is a naturally occurring splice variant
that results in excision of exon 14 that might increase the distance
between the catalytic and the cleavage sites and result in inhibition of IL-1
β
release. It is also the domain that interacts with the
protease DPP9, an inhibitor of NLRP1 inflammasome activation,
and the location of a mutation identified in an NLRP1-
associated autoinflammatory phenotype.
This new functional
degradation model is key for several reasons; first, it explains why
proteasome activity and FIIND autoproteolysis are necessary for
inflammasome activation; second, it indicates that other currently
unknown mechanisms must exist to prevent sterile inflammasome
activation during normal protein turnover; and third, suggests
that NLRP1 might serve as a “decoy” for other mammalian NLR
proteins.
Early in vitro studies suggested muramyl dipeptide (MDP) as a
synthetic inducer of NLRP1 inflammasome formation.
In  vitro
data, driven by cohorts of patients with susceptibility to toxoplasmosis, has shown that human NLRP1 is important for host response
to
Toxoplasma gondii.
One group showed recently that N-terminal
proteolysis is sufficient to activate NLRP1 and proposed that NLRP1
variants evolved to recognize diverse pathogen-encoded proteases.
Additional potential inducers have been identified including rhinovirus
and SARS CoV2,
which is consistent with respiratory epithelium expression. Two other recently identified regulatory proteins
include TRX1, which interacts with the NLRP1 NACHT (NAIP,
CIITA, HET-E, and TP-1)–leucine-rich repeat (LRR) domain to
respond to ribotoxic stress,
and ZAK
α
, a kinase that responds to
ultraviolet B and phosphorylates NLRP1 in the PYD resulting in
inflammasome activation. These proteins are clinically relevant as they
could be mutated in yet-to-be-defined NLRP1-associated diseases.
NLRP1 in Autoinflammatory Disease
NLRP1 is widely expressed in multiple immune cells, including T
cells and Langerhans cells, which might explain the recently described
NLRP1-associated diseases.
One group
described a new syndrome
called
NLRP1
-associated autoinflammation with arthritis and dyskeratosis (NAIAD). Patients displayed elevated serum C-reactive protein
(CRP), positive anti-nuclear antibody (ANA) levels, and abnormal B
cell subsets in addition to elevated serum levels of caspase-1, IL-1
β
,
and IL-18. Additionally, dominant mutations in
NLRP1
were linked
to three distinct conditions: palmoplantar carcinoma, familial keratosis lichenoides chronica,
and inherited corneal intraepithelial
dyskeratosis.
Homozygous variants have been described in a family with generalized keratotic inflammatory nodules.
Variants were
identified in the PYD and LRR domains of NLRP1 and resulted in
increased spontaneous ASC speck formation and increased IL-1
β
in
in vitro studies. It was proposed that disease-associated mutations disrupt the PYD and LRR domains, which are essential for maintaining
an inactive state, and therefore lead to increased self-oligomerization
and inflammasome activation.
A more common variant in NLRP1, M1184V, has been associated with asthma, inflammatory bowel disease, and diabetes.
Molecular studies demonstrated that the new valine stabilized
the complex, leading to enhanced DPP9 binding to the FIIND
domain.
These findings emphasized the role for DPP9 in autoinhibition of NLRP1. Therefore it is not surprising that patients
with autosomal recessive mutations, loss of function of DPP9, and
therefore enhanced NLRP1 activity have a severe phenotype of
failure to thrive, pancytopenia, atopy, recurrent fevers, and infections, especially with herpes viruses.

### NLRP3

NLRP3 (CIAS1, PYPAP7, NALP3) is the most well studied inflammasome.
NLRP3
was discovered in 2001 as the gene
responsible for familial cold autoinflammatory syndrome (FCAS)

and Muckle–Wells syndrome, two originally defined distinct rare
autosomal dominant disorders (see
Chapter 99
).
NLRP3
codes for a protein often referred to as cryopyrin to highlight the
association with cold-induced symptoms in patients with FCAS
and illustrate the presence of an N-terminal PYRIN domain.
Similar to other NLRs, NLRP3 contains a central NACHT
domain
and a C-terminal LRR domain. The PYRIN domain
of cryopyrin interacts specifically with ASC to form the core of
the canonical NLRP3 inflammasome protein complex with caspase-1.
The NACHT domain includes an ATPase-specific P
loop and is involved in protein oligomerization.
Most diseaseassociated mutations are located in the NACHT domain, which
suggests an important role for this domain in NLRP3 function.
NLRP3 regulates IL-1
β
, IL-18 secretion,
nuclear factor (NF)-
κ
B activation,
and cell death.
NLRP3 is primarily
expressed in leukocytes and chondrocytes, with detectable expression in several other tissues and cells.
Under normal conditions in many cells, the NLRP3 inflammasome is inactive and regulated by a 2-step activation process:
initial priming (signal 1), followed by inflammasome formation
(signal 2). During signal 1, PAMPs or DAMPs are recognized by
TLRs, leading to activation of NFκ
B with transcriptional upregulation of inflammasome component proteins and procytokines.
Additional transcriptional regulators include TTP and miR223
by binding upstream on NLRP3 and affecting its expression.
Post-translational modifications both positively and negatively
regulate NLRP3 activation (reviewed in refs. 22, 66). Ubiquitination of lysine residues by the E3 ligases
and deubiquitination of the LRR domain are suggested to be required for full
inflammasome activation.
S-Nitrosylation by nitric oxide
and small ubiquitin-like modifier (SUMO)-mediated regulation
has also been described.
Finally, several groups have identified serine
and tyrosine
phosphorylation of NLRP3 as an
important dynamic regulator allowing the cell to rapidly respond
to threats. It is likely that some degree of cross-talk between these
pathways is required to keep inflammasome priming and assembly
carefully in check.
There are several proposed regulatory mechanisms for signal 2,
including extracellular nucleotides such as adenosine triphosphate
(ATP), that activate the P2X7 receptor driving ion efflux,
mitochondrial damage–derived signals including reactive oxygen species (ROS) and calcium signaling, and lysosomal rupture
induced by crystals. This complexity suggests that NLRP3 does
not directly bind to its activators but rather senses a common
downstream intracellular signal. It is possible that these pathways
work together to drive NLRP3 assembly.
NLRP3 can be activated by a variety of PAMPs and DAMPs.
NLRP3 detects intracellular bacterial products, such as peptidoglycan and lipopolysaccharide (LPS),
and the synthetic PAMP
muramyl-dipeptide (MDP).
Cold, potassium efflux, generation
of ROS in mitochondria, and membrane disruption by crystalline (calcium pyrophosphate dehydrate, cholesterol, monosodium
urate [MSU] crystals) and peptide aggregates (amyloid
β
) all activate NLRP3, possibly by driving lysosomal rupture leading to
the cytosolic release of lysosomal proteases such as cathepsins B
and L.
Several hypotheses have been proposed to explain how
such a diverse group of stimuli can activate NLRP3. More recent
data have suggested a role for activation and regulation by cellular
organelles beyond lysosomal rupture. Mitochondria may provide a
subcellular scaffold for inflammasome assembly, and some groups
suggest that stimuli-induced synthesis of mitochondrial DNA and
mitochondrial ROS is a key mechanism for NLRP3 activation,
although this remains controversial. The endoplasmic reticulum, ribosomal proteins, the endosomal network, and the Golgi
apparatus have been proposed based on subcellular localization
of NLRP3 inflammasomes to affect protein translation, cholesterol synthesis, and vesicular trafficking which may contribute to
NLRP3 inflammasome activation, suggesting that organelle dysfunction may be the common stimulus so long sought after.
Several NLRP3 inflammasome binding proteins that regulate
function have been identified. CARD8 and the Pyrin-only proteins (POPs), so-called
decoy proteins
, inhibit oligomerization and
activation.
Nima-related kinase 7 (NEK7) and microtubule
affinity regulating kinase 4 (MARK4), two kinases with a role
in microtubule dynamics, and Bruton’s tyrosine kinase (BTK), a
protein known mostly for its role in B cell development, all play
important roles in inflammasome activation.
Caspase-8 and
its adapter Fas-associated death domain (FADD) are recruited to
the NLRP3 inflammasome with variable regulatory functions.
Caspase-8 could induce activation of NFκ
B, thereby maintaining
levels of pro–IL-1
β
mRNA and protein after microbial stimulation or directly and specifically cleave caspase-1, suggesting a role
in both priming and post-transcriptional activation of the inflammasome components.
These interacting proteins and others
that regulate the post-translational modifications described above
could be clinically relevant as future therapeutic targets.
NLRP3 in Autoinflammatory Disease
NLRP3
mutations were first described in families with FCAS and
Muckle–Wells syndrome
and then subsequently identified in
more severely affected patients with neonatal-onset multisystem
inflammatory disease (NOMID).
This disease continuum resulting from heterozygous gain-of-function mutations in
NLRP3
is
commonly referred to as cryopyrin-associated periodic syndromes
(CAPSs) or the cryopyrinopathies. CAPS patients share symptoms of recurrent fever, urticarial-like rash, headaches, joint pain,
and conjunctivitis; however, there are unique clinical features and
fairly consistent genotype–phenotype correlations that can be
used to define where patients fall on the CAPS disease spectrum.
Somatic variants in
NLRP3
have also been described in some
patients with another rare autoinflammatory disease, Schnitzler
syndrome.
Ex  vivo studies using CAPS mononuclear cells suggest that
mutations in
NLRP3
result in an overactive cryopyrin (NLRP3)
inflammasome with increased caspase-1 cleavage, as well as IL-1
β
and IL-18 release in response to innate immune stimulation or
reduced temperature (in FCAS).
CAPS cells also have elevated
levels of ROS due to elevated redox stress, resulting in inflammasome activation and ineffective anti-inflammatory mechanisms.
Recent data suggest that CAPS mutations affect NLRP3 phosphorylation, leading to overactivation.
Mutant CAPS mouse
bone marrow–derived and peritoneal macrophages are also hyperresponsive to LPS and cold. Disease in CAPS mutant mice is
dependent on ASC and caspase-1 and partially dependent on
IL-1
β
, IL-18, and pyroptotic cell death.
The success of IL-1–
targeted therapies in CAPS suggests that IL-1
β
is the primary
mediator of human disease.
Role of NLRP3 in the Pathogenesis of TNF-Associated
Periodic Syndrome
TNF-associated periodic syndrome (TRAPS) is another monogenic autoinflammatory disorder characterized by prolonged
episodes of fever, rash, and musculoskeletal symptoms. While initial studies pointed to TNF receptor shedding as a key player in

disease pathogenesis, more recent data support a role for NLRP3
inflammasome activation through a number of potential mechanisms, including elevated mitochondrial ROS generation, which
can activate inflammasomes.
This mechanism is further supported by the successful treatment of TRAPS patients with IL-1–
targeted therapy. See
Chapter 99
for further information on these
familial autoinflammatory disorders.
NLRP3 in More Common Diseases
A role for NLRP3 has been shown in more common rheumatologic diseases including gout (see
Chapters 96 and 97
) and pseudogout (see
Chapter 98
) and in other common diseases including
cardiovascular disease, Alzheimer’s disease (reviewed in ref. 100),
and Crohn’s disease,
which might be predicted based on the
diverse metabolic stimuli that trigger the NLRP3 inflammasome.
However, the pathophysiologic pathways involved are complicated since numerous key activators are often present at the same
time. For example, oxidized low-density lipoprotein (LDL), cholesterol crystals, and ROS, each of which trigger NLRP3 activation
independently, have all been described in atherosclerotic plaquederived macrophages. Comorbidities including obesity may contribute to the metabolic danger signals driving NLRP3 activation,
by adding to the abundance of free fatty acids and oxidative stress.
The identification of roles for NLRP3 and common downstream
inflammation driven by IL-1 in each of these disorders, however,
provides new opportunities for therapeutic intervention, which
is supported by effective studies of colchicine and IL-1–targeted
therapy in patients with cardiovascular disease.

### CARD8

CARD8 is the only other protein to have a FIIND domain similar
to NLRP1. The CARD domain of CARD8 directly interacts with
the CARD of procaspase-1 and forms an inflammasome independent of ASC.
CARD8 has six different isoforms, with the T48
isoform often considered the canonical form,
suggesting that
distinct isoforms of CARD8 may have different roles in mediating signal transduction and inflammation. CARD8 was initially
described as a regulator of NFκ
B and caspase-1 activation
and was more recently suggested to be an inhibitor of NLRP3
activation by preventing binding to ASC and therefore inflammasome formation.
The similarities between CARD8 and NLRP1
have led some to speculate that they may have related functions
in responding to reductive stress and DAMPs including anthrax
lethal toxin,
Toxoplasma gondii, Shigella flexneri,
and the small
molecule DPP8/9 inhibitor, Val-boroPro.
CARD8 in Human Disease
Study of CARD8 in patients with CAPS suggested that mutations in NLRP3 resulted in an inability to bind to CARD8.
Similarly, a missense mutation restricted to the T60 isoform of
CARD8 was identified in patients with Crohn’s disease, reducing
its ability to downregulate NLRP3-driven IL-1 production.
A
frameshift variant in
CARD8
was found to be significantly associated with risk for the pediatric syndrome periodic fever with
aphthous stomatitis, pharyngitis, and cervical adenitis (PFAPA)
with an odds ratio of 4.96 in a European cohort.
The authors
attributed this risk to formation of truncated CARD8 protein,
which lacked the FIIND and CARD domains and could no longer interact with NLRP3.
Genetic variations in
CARD8
with
or without associated variants in
NLRP3
have been implicated in
risk of rheumatoid arthritis (RA),
ankylosing spondylitis,
cardiovascular disease,
and diabetic nephropathy.
However,
the absence of a CARD8 homolog in rodents has made study of
these associations challenging, and further research is necessary to
confirm the true role of CARD8 in human inflammatory diseases.

### Pyrin

Pyrin was first implicated in the pathogenesis of familial Mediterranean fever (FMF) in 1997, but only recently have we begun to
understand the regulation of this inflammasome, with much of
our knowledge stemming from murine models of
MEFV.
Cellular expression of pyrin is coincidental, with clinical symptoms of disease with increased expression noted in granulocytes,
monocytes, and synovial and peritoneal fibroblasts.
The
interaction between pyrin and ASC regulates leukocyte apoptosis and NFκ
B activation,
although these processes are
less well understood.
Ex  vivo studies from pyrin hypomorph and knockout mice
demonstrated increased caspase-1 activation, increased IL-1
β
processing and secretion, and a defect in apoptosis. While knock-in
murine models solidified the role of the inflammasome in vivo,
until recently the stimulus for activation of pyrin itself remained
difficult to discern. Early mechanistic studies demonstrated interactions of pyrin with actin, and recent studies have implicated
actin depolymerization as a basic mechanism for pyrin inflammasome activation.
The specific activation of the pyrin inflammasome was first described in response to bacterial toxins, notably
Clostridium difficile
toxin b (TcdB), which inhibits RhoA signaling by mediating the glucosylation, adenylation, ADP-ribosylation, or deamidation of various residues in RhoA with resulting
inhibition of guanine nucleotide binding and GTPase activity.
This inhibition of RhoA signaling pathways leads to activation
of the pyrin inflammasome,
with pyrin interacting specifically with ASC, similar to NLRP3.
The pyrin–ASC
interaction results in colocalization to actin polymers,
leading to caspase-1 activation and cleavage and release of mature
IL-1
β
.
Pyrin is further regulated by phosphorylation and constitutive inhibition. Recent work has shown that
phosphorylation at two serine sites maintain the inactive state
through binding by 14-3-3 proteins. Pyrin dephosphorylation
is triggered by toxin stimulation and bacterial infection, with
14-3-3 dissociation resulting in pyrin inflammasome activation
in a process dependent on microtubules.
Downstream, the
small Rho GTPase cell division cycle 42 (CDC42) regulates pyrin
activation by increasing its phosphorylation levels and is essential
for oligomerization.
Furthermore, cytokine signaling by TNF
has been linked to induction of pyrin expression, which suggests a
further autoamplification loop through IL-1.
Pyrin in Autoinflammatory Disease
Mutations in
MEFV,
which encodes pyrin, underlie two distinct
autoinflammatory syndromes. FMF, likely the most well known
of the autoinflammatory syndromes, is characterized by discrete
episodes of fever with serositis, synovitis, and rash.
To date, more
than 300 variants in
MEFV
have been described, primarily base
pair substitutions occurring in exons 2 and 10. While the disease phenotype is classically described as inherited in an autosomal recessive fashion, there are increasing reports of patients with
an FMF phenotype but only one identifiable
MEFV
mutation,
with clear autosomal dominant inheritance.
Some of those
MEFV
mutations (specifically, H478Y, T577S, T577A, T577N,
M694del, M694I, E148Q, and L110P) cause dominant FMF in

patients from different populations.
The threonine at position 577 specifically seems to result in unique features including urticarial-like rash
or recurrent episodes of chest pain in
the absence of fever, with greater risk of amyloidosis,
suggesting a critical role for this amino acid in pyrin function,
but also suggesting that phenotypes with autosomal dominant
inheritance be defined as pyrin-associated dominant disease or
PADD, to highlight their unique features. Ex vivo studies in
blood-derived macrophages from patients with both autosomal recessive and autosomal dominant FMF show increased
IL-1
β
release in the absence of pyrin inflammasome triggers.
Similarly, IL-18 and S100A12 are spontaneously released from
neutrophils collected from patients with FMF, in a
MEFV
gene-dosage fashion.
Pyrin-associated autoinflammation with neutrophilic dermatosis (PAAND) has been described as a distinct, autosomal dominant syndrome caused by mutations in
MEFV.
Affected patients
experience recurrent episodes with fever, neutrophilic dermatosis, arthralgia, and myalgia/myositis beginning in early childhood. Elevation in serum acute-phase reactants is observed during
inflammatory episodes. To date, two families with PAAND have
been identified with mutations, which result in decreased 14-3-3
binding, subsequent inflammasome activation, IL-1
β
and IL-18
secretion, as well as pyroptotic cell death.
The pyrin inflammasome may sense changes in the cytoskeleton since Rho GTPases control features of actin dynamics. Thus,
mutations in pyrin regulators and related proteins may also
lead to autoinflammatory disease. Patients with neonatal-onset
cytopenias with dyshematopoiesis, autoinflammation, rash, and
hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis were found to have mutations in the pyrin regulator CDC42 that have since been classified
as NOCARH syndrome.
Patients all showed significantly
elevated levels of IL-18.
In actin regulatory genes, biallelic
missense mutations in
WDR1
have been reported in patients
with periodic fevers with immunodeficiency and thrombocytopenia (PFIT)
or an infantile-onset inflammatory bowel
disease phenotype.
Serum studies demonstrated high serum
levels of IL-18 and increased caspase-1 cleavage consistent with
inflammasome activation in some patients.
These findings
parallel those from knockout murine studies suggesting that
the disease phenotype was driven by pyrin-dependent IL-18
secretion and could be blocked by treatment with colchicine.
Relatedly, patients with early-onset immunodeficiency, thrombocytopenia, eosinophilia, small vessel vasculitis, and inflammatory bowel disease have loss of function mutations in
ARPC1B
encoding the ARPC1B subunit of actin-related protein complex 2/3 (ARP2/3).
Since murine studies showed that
cells deficient in
Wdr1
secreted reduced amounts of IL-18 in
the presence of Arp2/3 inhibitors, this pathway may be similarly
related to the pyrin inflammasome. The pathogenic mechanisms
involving the pyrin inflammasome are supported by the successful use of IL-1–targeted therapy in PAAND and colchicineresistant FMF.
Role of Pyrin in the Pathogenesis of Mevalonate Kinase
Deficiency
Hyperimmunoglobulinemia D and periodic fever syndrome
(HIDS) and the more severe mevalonic aciduria (MA) result
from autosomal recessive mutations in
MVK
gene encoding
mevalonate kinase, the first enzyme in the 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl (HMG)-CoA reductase (HMGR) pathway required
for cholesterol and isoprenoid synthesis.
Mutations lead to
impaired protein stability and absent/reduced enzymatic activity,
and a shortage of isoprenoid lipid precursors, which is evident in
MKD patient cells, even in the absence of clinical symptoms.
Isoprenoids, specifically geranylgeranyl diphosphate, via protein prenylation, are required for diminishing the inflammatory
response, even to mild stimuli such as physical stress and elevated core body temperatures. Murine models suggest that just
a mild increase in core temperature reduced any residual enzymatic activity and further exacerbated the prenylation defect.
Consistently, in vitro studies of cells from HIDS patients have
demonstrated a reduced ability to clear antioxidant stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and deficiency in autophagy, all of which
are implicated in inflammasome activation.
New evidence
suggests that GTPases, including the Rho/Rac/Rap families, are
significantly affected by reduced MK activity associated with
disease mutations. Therefore the GTPases remain unprenylated,
leading to increased IL-1
β
secretion from macrophages, likely in
a pyrin inflammasome-dependent fashion.
However, others have shown that the lack of prenylation leads to enhanced
formation of an NLRP3-dependent and pyrin-independent
inflammasome upon stimulation with excess IL-1 and IL-18
release, suggesting differential metabolic effects on different cell
types.
Consequently, more severe patients may experience
only a partial response to IL-1 blockade.

### NLRC4

NLR family CARD domain-containing protein (NLRC)4, is
unique among the inflammasomes in that it complexes with
another NLR family member, NLR family apoptosis inhibitory
protein (NAIP).
NAIP acts as a cytosolic receptor for bacterial flagellin and T3SS rod/needle proteins, with phosphorylation acting as a preactivation stimulus and specific binding of the
bacterial ligand initiating the assembly of the NLRC4 inflammasome.
The NAIP-NLRC4 inflammasome has been shown
by electron microscopy to result from the assembly of 11 to 12
polymers in to the typical disk shape, with the LRR domains at
the perimeter and the NLRC4 CARD domain remaining exposed
for activation of caspase-1 alone or in the presence of ASC.
NLRC4 also has the ability to recruit the proapoptotic procaspase-8 in an ASC-dependent fashion.
NLRC4 in Autoinflammatory Disease
Specific autoinflammatory syndromes have been linked to mutations in NLRC4, although the phenotypes are clinically heterogeneous. To date, four phenotypes have been described with milder
symptoms associated with winged helix domain (WHD) variants
and more severe symptoms linked to mutations in the NBD and
HD1 domains; however, the identification of additional patients
suggests that genotype–phenotype correlation may be more complex than previously appreciated.
The symptoms described are
consistent with the CAPS disease spectrum, but patients with
unique clinical features including enterocolitis, splenomegaly, and
recurrent episodes of macrophage-activation syndrome (MAS), as
well as serum elevations primarily in IL-18 but also in IL-1
β
, have
also been reported.
The significant gastrointestinal manifestations suggest that pathogenesis likely involves intestinal epithelial cells. Mutations are predicted to affect the autoinhibitory
domain, leading to spontaneous formation, and activation of the
NLRC4 inflammasome, leading to cytokine release. Early success
with IL-18–targeted therapy suggests that IL-18 is the primary
pathologic mediator as opposed to other inflammasome-mediated

familial autoinflammatory diseases that respond to targeting
IL-1.

## Other Inflammasomes

Several additional proteins form inflammasomes, at least under
certain conditions, such as the atypical inflammasome-associated NLR proteins NLRP6, NLRP7, NLRP12, and the PYHIN
proteins AIM2 and IFI16. Experimental models have shown
that these inflammasomes, specifically NLRP6 and NLRP7,
may have roles in regulation of homeostasis with host microbiota and embryologic development. However, due to differences in murine and human genes, proteins and experimental
models, as well as the lack of monogenic disease associations, far
less is known about these proteins. Additional work is needed to
understand the role of these inflammasomes in the pathogenesis
of human disease.

### NLRP6

NLRP6 is primarily expressed in the gut epithelial cells, where it
plays an important role in maintaining intestinal homeostasis via
mucosal self-renewal and proliferation.
NLRP6 forms an
ASC–caspase-1–dependent inflammasome, leading to the cleavage of
pro–IL-1
β
and pro–IL-18. However, most of the functional information to date has been derived from studies of NLRP6 knockout
mice, demonstrating that its role is primarily as a negative regulator
of gut microbiota,
although its presence may negatively affect
the development of graft vs host disease independent of the microbiome.
A monogenic disease in humans has not yet been described,
but a role for NLRP6 in oral–gastrointestinal diseases, such as periodontitis, metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH),
and portal fibrosis, is suggested by increased expression of NLRP6
compared with patients without disease.
Beyond the gut,
genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) analysis have linked
NLRP6
to platelet counts
and susceptibility to essential hypertension,
suggesting a role for
NLRP6 in cells outside the gastrointestinal tract. Consistently, ex vivo
studies of synovial tissues from patients with RA showed reduced
NLRP6
expression compared with patients with osteoarthritis.

### NLRP7

In vitro studies of human macrophages have demonstrated that
NLRP7 forms an active inflammasome, triggered by acylated
bacterial lipopeptides,
leading to the release of IL-1
β
and
IL-18 in a caspase-1–dependent fashion, downstream of TLR2.
It has been suggested that NLRP7 and TLR2 contribute to
the host defense against intracellular gram-positive bacteria,
inhibiting replication of
Listeria monocytogenes
and
Staphylococcus aureus
infection. In THP-1 cells,
Mycobacterium bovis
also activates the NLRP7 inflammasome, but this has not been
demonstrated in primary human macrophages.
NLRP7
colocalizes intracellularly with the Golgi complex and microtubule-organizing center, suggesting potential role in the negative regulation of inflammation.
NLRP7-related diseases
have been limited to the reproductive system
and specifically in patients with recurrent hydatidiform moles.
NLRP7
is highly expressed in the placenta and throughout the first
trimester of human pregnancy, with increased expression and
activation in cases of fetal growth restriction.
Studies suggest
a role for NLRP7 in the generation and/or maintenance of the
maternal imprint, with cells expressing NLRP7 variants, resulting in autosomal dominant aberrant imprinting marks in the
offspring. While further evaluation of this role for NLRP7 in
epigenetics is needed, these findings suggest new sensor functions that have not previously been associated with NLRP family members and may have important implications for disease
pathogenesis.

### NLRP12

NLRP12 (Monarch-1, Pypaf7) was among the first NLRs identified, but much remains unknown about this sensor, and questions
remain on whether it forms a complete inflammasome.
Studies demonstrate that it forms a six-helical bundle death domain
fold, similar to the other NLRs
; however, gene-silencing studies
suggest that NLRP12 acts as a negative regulator of TNF driven
NFκ
B signaling.
Gain of function variants in NLRP12 have
been linked to enhanced inflammation in cold-associated periodic
fevers, similar to NLRP3.
Other variants have been proposed as causes of noninfectious fevers in children with elevated
serum acute phase reactants and buccal aphthous stomatitis.
However, in many cases, healthy first-degree relatives have been
described to carry
NLRP12
mutations, suggesting that NLRP12-
related phenotypes may depend on additional genetic and environmental factors.
Thus much of our knowledge about NLRP12 function stems
from murine knockout lines and investigations of colitis and colon
tumorigenesis, similar to NLRP6, in that NLRP12 has a protective role in intestinal inflammation by suppression of NFκ
B
activation resulting in reduced colitis susceptibility.
Since
microbial dysbiosis is associated with inflammatory bowel disease,
it is proposed that NLRP12 plays an anti-inflammatory role by
regulating gut microbial communities.
In a meta-analysis of
human 16S RNA data from twins affected by ulcerative colitis,
NLRP12
expression was found to be significantly down-regulated
in cohorts with active ulcerative colitis, compared with healthy
controls and patients with inactive disease.
Murine models have
also suggested a role for NLRP12 in rapid myelopoiesis,
as well
as in reducing neuroinflammation in experimental autoimmune
encephalomyelitis, an experimental model of multiple sclerosis.
It remains to be seen whether similar tissue-specific functions can
be attributed to human NLRP12.

### PYHIN Proteins

AIM2 is perhaps the most well known non-NLR protein capable of forming an inflammasome. AIM2 forms an intracytoplasmic sensor that directly binds cytoplasmic bacterial or viral
dsDNA or self-DNA.
AIM2 is activated in two steps,
with step 1 involving type 1 IFNs. Similar to NLRP3, AIM2
is important as a guardian of cellular integrity, as well as bacterial, viral, fungal, and parasitic pathogens, and has been associated with disease processes including cancer and autoimmunity.
IFI16 is another DNA sensor that forms an inflammasome in
the nucleus in response to specific DNA viruses.
IFI16 has
been proposed to play a role in cancer through regulating cellular senescence, as well as in aging-associated inflammation.
More recently, autoantibodies to both AIM2 and anti-IFI16
were identified in patients with systemic lupus erythematosus
(SLE), with the hypothesis that AIM-like receptors result in sustained IFN signaling, leading to promotion of autoantigens and
worsening disease activity.

## Inflammasomes in Autoimmunity

A pathogenic role for inflammasomes in monogenic autoinflammatory disorders is clear based on Mendelian genetics and robust
evidence from human and mouse immunologic studies. In contrast,
the evidence supporting inflammasomes mediating autoimmune
disorders is less direct and is limited to genetic association studies,
altered mRNA gene expression, and human cell or mouse disease
models that have generated conflicting data. The variety of inflammasome triggers (temperature, ion flux, crystals, metabolites, microbial products) suggests that the stimuli may determine the character
of downstream inflammatory responses. However, the wide array
of phenotypes associated with polymorphisms even within a single
gene may indicate that the effects of these genetic variants ultimately
lay at the transcriptional level, as different inflammasome proteins
have unique expression patterns, or at the translational level, either
through disruption of domain interactions or reducing the threshold for inflammasome activation. Nevertheless, a growing set of
in vitro and ex vivo data implicates a role for inflammasome activation in a multitude of rheumatic diseases including SLE, celiac
disease, crystal-induced arthropathies, and RA
(
Table 95.2
).

### Ex Vivo and Murine Studies of Inflammasomes and Autoimmunity

Baseline expression of inflammasome sensors has guided initial studies on the pathogenesis of these multifactorial diseases.
For example, NLRP1 and AIM2 have long been known to be
expressed in the skin, and recent studies have linked polymorphisms in these genes to susceptibility to several autoimmune
skin disorders. Unsurprisingly, AIM2 expression is increased in
keratinocytes in active psoriatic skin lesions.
Studies of human
keratinocytes have demonstrated, at least in vitro, that stimulation
with dsDNA activates AIM2 in these cells, leading to IL-1
β
secretion.
Similarly, the level of AIM2 expression in macrophages
has been further associated with severity of disease in patients with
SLE,
suggesting a role for this DNA sensor in autoimmunity.
Adding further complexity, inflammasome molecule expression
studies indicate the variability of gene expression based on tissue
of origin and cell type and may be influenced by disease activity.
In mononuclear cells from RA patients, the expression of common
inflammasome components ASC and caspase-1 are increased,
while the expression of NLRP3 and inflammasome activity are variable in mononuclear cells from RA patients depending on treatment.
At the tissue level, NLRP3 expression is increased in
studies of RA synovium compared with other forms of arthritis.
NLRP3 expression is similarly increased in several other autoimmune disease-associated tissues including skin in systemic sclerosis
and kidneys in SLE,
with increased IL-1
β
production from
patients with SLE nephritis.
However, despite the evidence for
increased NLRP3 expression in RA synovium, NLRP3 knockout
mice have generated variable results, suggesting a contributory role
of other inflammatory pathways in disease pathogenesis.
Finally, recent work has suggested that effector and memory
CD4+ T cells may instruct antigen-presenting myeloid cells to
produce mature IL-1
β
independent of caspase-1 and inflammasome activation. This activation was thought to parallel
inflammasome-driven IL-1
β
release but acts through a TNFR–
Fas–caspase-8 pathway. Mice deficient in these pathways were
protected from developing T cell–mediated autoimmunity in an
experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis model.
Despite
these challenges, active targeting of the inflammasome or downstream cytokines may be a valuable therapeutic approach for these
complex patients.

### Clinical Assays of Inflammasome Activity

For patients without a molecular genetic diagnosis, including
those with autoimmunity or likely multifactorial disease processes, the contributory role of inflammasomes can be difficult
to discern clinically. Most studies of the inflammasomes have
been driven by primary cells and human and murine cell lines,
investigating IL-1
β
/IL-18 release, ASC speck formation, and/or

cell death, limiting their use currently to research laboratories.
More recently, PCR- and bead-based immunoassays for the detection of cytokines have become more readily available to clinicians,
but age- and sex-appropriate reference ranges accounting for diurnal cytokine variations and linking to disease specificity have yet
to be established.

## Inflammasome-Targeted Therapies

The role of inflammasomes in multiple disease processes has
prompted a concerted effort to identify therapies directly targeting the inflammasome
rather than downstream cytokines
as are currently available.
Recently, the mechanism of several
older anti-inflammatory drugs such as colchicine has been linked
to inflammasomes. While a number of different approaches are
underway which take advantage of the numerous regulatory
steps of inflammasome biology, the most promising are drugs
that appear to inhibit NLRP3 activation directly by either modifying the active conformation or preventing oligomerization in
response to stimuli.
Some of the most promising NLRP3-
targeted therapeutics are based on the drug known as CRID3 or
MCC950, which has been used in numerous models and which
binds NLRP3.
Given what is currently known about structural
changes and conformation in NLRP3, some drugs may benefit
patients with certain genetic variants over others.
Understanding the link between disease phenotype and pathogenesis remains
the cornerstone of autoinflammatory syndromes and will inform
future development of agents targeting inflammasome pathways.

## Conclusion

Over the last few decades, inflammasomes have been implicated
in numerous diseases that are diagnosed and treated by rheumatologists. Inappropriate activation of these protein multimers in
response to varying stimuli generates an inflammatory cascade
with acute on chronic inflammation characteristic of many rheumatologic disorders. Given the known morbidity and mortality
secondary to undiagnosed disease, evidence-based recommendations for diagnosis, treatment, and monitoring of patients with
autoinflammatory diseases have been proposed to standardize
their management worldwide.
While significant progress has
been made in understanding the molecular relationship between
inflammasome regulation and activation, much remains to be
unveiled. Questions related to downregulation of the inflammasome reaction, influences on the adaptive immune system, and
maintenance of homeostasis remain. For autoimmunity disorders,
increased clinical focus on next-generation sequencing and the
modulation of symptoms with targeted biologics may reveal how
the combination of genetic polymorphisms, time, inflammation,
and epigenetic changes affect the inflammasomes and their interaction within the cellular microenvironment.
