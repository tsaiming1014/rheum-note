# Clinical Features and Treatment of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

IMELDA BALBONI, DANA GERSTBACHER, TZIELAN C. LEE,
AND CHRISTY I. SANDBORG

KEY POINTS

> Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is the umbrella term for a family of

> heterogeneous childhood arthritides of unknown cause.

> JIA affects at least 1 in 1000 children. The goals of treatment in JIA

> are complete suppression of inflammation on medications and

> remission when possible.

> Oligoarticular JIA, characterized by early age of onset, female

> predominance, anti-nuclear antibody positivity, and chronic anterior

> uveitis, occurs only in childhood.

> Systemic JIA is distinct from other subgroups of JIA in its unique

> systemic manifestations, activation of innate immune system

> features, and preferential responses to interleukin (IL)-1 and IL-6

> inhibition compared with the other types of JIA.

> Juvenile spondyloarthritis typically occurs in children older than 6 years

> with majority having enthesitis, but sacroiliitis may not develop until

> adolescence.

> Because bone erosions on conventional radiographs are late findings

> in growing children, detection of early joint damage requires other

> imaging modalities such as ultrasound and MRI.

> Considerable progress in understanding the genetics, biology, and

> pathogenesis of JIA has revealed subtype-specific associations, as

> well as some common mechanisms of disease that will translate to

> more effective and targeted therapies.

## Introduction

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is the most common rheumatic
disease in childhood, but actual estimates of prevalence and incidence vary remarkably in different geographic regions, reflecting
variations in disease reporting, classification, and racial/ethnic and
environmental differences in disease expression.
The reported
incidence ranges from 0.83 per 100,000 children in Japan to 23
per 100,000 in Norway.
Worldwide reports of JIA prevalence vary
between 3.8 and 400 per 100,000 children, with a pooled estimate of 20.5 per 100,000, making JIA one of the most common
chronic diseases of childhood.
There are significant differences
in the disease manifestations in children compared with adults.
This chapter discusses the current understanding of the key clinical and diagnostic features of the various forms of JIA, treatment
approaches, and outcomes. The rapid growth in understanding
the biologic basis for JIA coupled with the ongoing development
of targeted therapies for rheumatic diseases are likely to lead to
enhancements in these current recommendations.
Data concerning long-term disease course and outcome in children with JIA
in this modern treatment era remain limited, but there are international prospective inception cohorts of children with arthritis
designed to address these gaps in knowledge.
Research shows
JIA treatment continues toward the era of personalized therapy.

The symptoms in children with rheumatic diseases can overlap
with other concerning diagnoses, such as malignancies, infection,
and trauma including nonaccidental trauma (abuse). Therefore it
is crucial to evaluate these diagnostic possibilities before accepting the diagnosis of JIA, as JIA is a diagnosis of exclusion. The

if a patient does not easily fit into the ILAR classification system,

## Oligoarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

Oligoarticular JIA accounts for as many as 20% of all new rheumatic diagnoses in the general pediatric rheumatology clinic
and
has an annual incidence of 10 in 100,000 children.
It is the
most prevalent of all the JIA subcategories, comprising 50% of all
patients with JIA in North America and Europe.
The peak age of
onset occurs in children ages 2 to 4 years, and the female to male
ratio is 3:1.
This subgroup is rare in African Americans,
Native
Americans,
and southern Asians.
There is a strong association
with HLA-DRB1*11 and HLA-DRB1*08.
There are two ILAR
subgroups within oligoarticular JIA: (1) persistent oligoarticular
JIA, in which the number of joints affected by arthritis remains
fewer than five throughout the disease course, and (2) extended
oligoarticular JIA, in which arthritis develops in additional joints
6 months after disease onset. Extension occurs in 30% to 50% of
patients at 5 to 7 years after onset of disease.
Symmetric disease,
ankle and/or wrist involvement, and an elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) in the first 6 months of disease have been
associated with extension.
Oligoarticular JIA usually manifests as asymmetric arthritis of
the lower extremities affecting one or two large joints, with the
knee the most commonly affected, followed by the ankle, wrist,
and elbow. Small joints of the hands can be involved. Hip and
back complaints, especially in young children, are so unusual that
extensive evaluation is warranted to rule out other conditions such
as infection or malignancy. Significant constitutional and systemic
symptoms are atypical in oligoarticular JIA and, if present, should
raise concern regarding the accuracy of the initial diagnosis. Pain
in an obviously inflamed joint is surprisingly minimal compared
with septic arthritis, and in as many as 25% of cases, the symptoms may be subtle, with parents only noticing a limp and joint
swelling. In a young child, there is reluctance to walk and bear
weight, with a return to crawling after a period of immobility,
such as in the morning or after a nap. There is a high risk for
a relatively asymptomatic chronic uveitis, especially in ANA
individuals, requiring regular ophthalmology examinations to
detect early changes (see Uveitis).
Among children with oligoarticular JIA, a positive ANA in
low to moderate titers (1:40 to 1:320) is seen in 70% to 80% of
children with persistent oligoarticular JIA and 80% to 95% with
extended oligoarticular JIA.
The rate of ANA positivity is even
higher in girls with early-onset disease.
The typical child with
oligoarticular JIA will have normal white blood cell counts, normal or mildly elevated acute phase reactants, and, in some cases,
mild anemia.
The differential diagnosis of oligoarticular JIA includes other
JIA subtypes (juvenile spondyloarthritis [JSpA] and juvenile psoriatic arthritis [JPsA]); other rheumatic diseases of childhood,
or other inflammatory conditions such as inflammatory bowel
disease (IBD). Nonrheumatic causes of joint pain and swelling
should also be ruled out, such as septic arthritis, benign or malignant tumors, reactive arthritis, foreign body synovitis, pigmented
villonodular synovitis, arterial–venous malformation, bleeding
disorders (such as hemophilia), or bony fractures, including nonaccidental trauma. Mild trauma is rarely a cause of joint swelling
in young children because serious sprains or orthopedic problems
such as meniscal tears or ligamentous injury are exceedingly rare.
In general, ligaments and tendons are stronger than growing bone
in children, and the bone–ligament or bone–tendon junction is
the weakest link. Therefore fractures are more common in children than adults. Transient joint effusions can develop in children with hypermobility after exercise.
Lyme disease can cause
recurrent monoarticular arthritis (typically involving the knee and
often with popliteal cysts), usually for <6 weeks, and appropriate
serology should be obtained if there is any exposure history.

## Rheumatoid Factor–Negative Polyarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

RF
polyarticular JIA is a heterogeneous subgroup that makes
up 15% to 20% of all JIA cases, with a bimodal distribution of
age of onset, with the first peak at 1 to 3 years of age and the
second peak at 9 to 14 years.
It has an approximate incidence
of 0.3 to 6.5 per 100,000 children and a prevalence of 1.64 to
33.4 per 100,000 children, although there is significant variability
in incidence and prevalence among different populations worldwide
Girls are more commonly affected than boys, with a ratio
of 2 to 4:1, even more so with onset in adolescence.
Chronic
anterior uveitis occurs in 15% to 20% of cases.
Patients with
RF
polyarticular JIA frequently have involvement of the small
joints of the hands and feet, as well as the cervical spine and temporomandibular joints (TMJ). They can also have involvement of
larger joints including hips, shoulders, knees, wrists, and ankles.
RF
polyarticular JIA is quite heterogeneous, with at least three
clinical phenotypes identified. One phenotype resembles oligoarticular JIA and is characterized by ANA
girls aged 6 years or less,
with an asymmetric arthritis in more than four joints, increased
association with HLA DRB1*0801 (DRw8), and high risk of
uveitis.
The similarities between ANA
, RF
polyarticular JIA,
and oligoarticular JIA have led to the hypothesis that these two
entities are actually on the same disease spectrum.
A second
phenotype resembles adult-onset RF
rheumatoid arthritis and is
characterized by ANA
older patients, symmetric synovitis of large
and small joints, a greater cumulative number of affected joints,
elevated ESR, increased association with DPB1*0301, and less
uveitis.
A third phenotype with “dry synovitis” is characterized

by ANA
school-age patients with minimal palpable synovial fluid
or thickening, notable joint stiffness and limitation, and normal
to moderately elevated ESR.
This phenotype is often refractory
to treatment and can result in destructive joint disease.
RF
polyarticular JIA may be associated with low-grade fevers,
fatigue, poor growth, weight loss, elevated acute phase reactants,
mild anemia, and ANA positivity in as many as 50% of patients.
ACPAs have been reported in
∼
6% to 7% of RF
polyarticular
JIA patients
and are correlated with more severe disease.
In
the future, these ACPA
patients may be reclassified into the RF
polyarticular JIA subgroup. The heterogeneity of patients with
RF
polyarticular JIA has also been supported by gene expression
studies showing multiple distinct gene expression signatures.
The differential diagnosis of RF
polyarticular JIA includes
the other JIA subtypes, such as enthesitis-related arthritis (ERA),
which should be considered particularly in boys >6 years because
sacroiliac (SI) involvement may not occur until adolescence. Other
major considerations include other rheumatic conditions; malignancies such as lymphoma and leukemia; and infections such as
Neisseria gonorrhoeae,
rubella, parvovirus, and Lyme disease.

## Rheumatoid Factor–Positive Polyarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

RF
polyarticular JIA is a well-characterized JIA subcategory and is
part of the same disease spectrum as adult RF
RA, sharing immunogenetic, serologic, and clinical factors.
RF
polyarticular JIA has
similar HLA associations as adult RF
RA.
RF
polyarticular JIA
makes up
≤
5% of cases of JIA, with mean age of onset of 9 to 11
years and reported female-to-male ratios between 4 and 13:1.0.
It
has an incidence of approximately 0.1 to 0.72 per 100,000 children and a prevalence of 0.28 to 10.3 per 100,000 children.
RF
polyarticular JIA more frequently affects children of color.
RF
polyarticular JIA typically is a progressive, symmetric polyarthritis affecting the small and large joints of the upper and lower
extremities, as well as the cervical spine and temporomandibular
joints, while the sacroiliac joints and thoracolumbar spine are typically spared.
Hip involvement is common and may be debilitating.
The arthritis can be quite severe, often resulting in bony erosions
and joint destruction. Radiologic changes tend to take place early,
especially in hands and feet. Rheumatoid nodules occur in as many
as 30% of cases, most frequently around the elbow.
Other extraarticular manifestations are reported less often than in adults. Uveitis is unusual, seen in <5% of patients.
Active polyarticular JIA may be associated with mild systemic
symptoms, including fatigue and weight loss, as well as mild to
moderate inflammation, such as elevated acute phase reactants and
a normocytic, normochromic anemia. By definition, all patients
have immunoglobulin (Ig)M–anti-IgG RF. The ANA test is positive in up to 55% of patients,
and ACPAs have occurred in 57%
to 73%.
The prevalence of ACPAs varies in polyarticular JIA
but usually coincides with RF status.
Positive ACPAs are highly
specific for this disease subgroup, which led to its inclusion in the
new proposed JIA criteria.

## Juvenile Spondyloarthritis and Enthesitis- Related Arthritis

JSpA has been divided into two forms: undifferentiated and differentiated. The undifferentiated form is made up of seronegative
enthesopathy and arthropathy syndrome (SEA) and ERA (JIA
subtype in the ILAR classification), whereas the differentiated
form includes juvenile ankylosing spondylitis (AS), psoriatic
arthritis, reactive arthritis, and arthritis associated with IBD.
JSpA prevalence accounts for approximately 20% of all JIA cases
but varies by geographic region and can comprise one-third of all
JIA in parts of eastern and southern Asia.
It has been recently
reported that JSpA is the most common JIA subtype in some
Asian countries such as Taiwan, where it makes up 40% of the
JIA patients.
The clinical features of JSpA differ from adult spondyloarthritis, making adult classification criteria inaccurate for this pediatric group. Because of the significant heterogeneity of this disease
phenotype, it has been difficult to propose a unified pediatric
classification criterion. In fact, ILAR classification does not recognize JSpA as a distinct entity.
Onset of JSpA is usually after
age 6 years, and there is a higher prevalence in boys (male:female
ratio 7:1), although girls may be under-recognized because of
milder disease and less axial involvement.
In many cases, JSpA
is diagnosed initially as oligoarticular JIA in the younger ages.
Patients rarely develop sacroiliitis until adolescence and instead
usually are seen with lower-limb arthritis and enthesitis. Multiple
studies have shown that disease onset before 16 years is associated with more peripheral arthritis and less axial symptoms.
The articular manifestations of JSpA include hip and peripheral
arthritis with an asymmetric oligoarthritis most commonly in
the lower limb.
As many as one-third of JSpA children will have
the classic finding of tarsitis, which is inflammation of the subtalar joint and the surrounding tendon sheaths.
Approximately
25% of JSpA patients have polyarthritis presentation at onset.
Sacroiliitis is the most common presentation of axial involvement in JSpA despite children being less likely to have back pain
and stiffness at presentation.
Children instead may only report
vague buttock pain, groin pain, heel pain, or excessive pain from
enthesitis or tenosynovitis.
A subgroup of children with ERA
will progress to features more typical of adult AS, with SI joint
and spinal inflammation during adolescence. This progression is
more likely in boys who are human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-
B27
and have spinal or SI pain within 1 year of diagnosis.
In addition, higher active joint and tender entheses count at
diagnosis; hip arthritis; elevated C-reactive protein (CRP); and
HLA-B27 positivity have associations with sacroiliitis.
Compared with adults, in whom radiologic evidence of sacroiliitis is
the diagnostic hallmark of AS, radiologic evaluation in children
with JSpA is less often diagnostic because sacroiliitis is rare as a
presenting symptom. Interestingly, the presence of hip disease
in a patient with JSpA increases the likelihood of future sacroiliitis 11-fold, whereas the presence of dactylitis decreases it.
Modified Schober’s test can be used to evaluate lower lumbar
flexibility, with a change <6 cm considered abnormal.
Because
thoracic excursion varies greatly in a growing child, only sequential measurements are helpful. In the adolescent, any thoracic
excursion <5 cm should be regarded as abnormal.
Enthesitis
(inflammation of the tendon insertion site) is seen in 60% to
80% of JSpA patients. It is an early manifestation and occurs
more frequently in children than in adult-onset AS.
Children
with enthesitis are more often older, male, and with polyarthritis
and sacroiliac involvement.
In a 2018 cohort study, the most
frequent locations were the calcaneal plantar fascial insertion
(39%), Achilles insertion (31%), tibial tuberosity (30%), and the
inferior pole of the patella (27%).
In addition, other enthesitis
locations include the 2 o’clock and 10 o’clock positions on the

patella, greater trochanter, the iliac crest, metatarsal heads, and
the base of the fifth metatarsal.
Common features of enthesitis
in JSpA include being symmetric, persistent, and multifocal with
25% exhibiting more than three sites.
Enthesitis can also be
seen in overuse or injury states; therefore caution must be taken
to rule out those etiologies. Enthesitis can be visualized using
ultrasound (US) or whole body MRI.
JSpA is associated with an acute anterior uveitis in 7% to 10%
of children, which typically presents as an acutely red, painful, and
photophobic eye that needs immediate medical attention because,
if left untreated, it may lead to blindness.
The HLA-B27 status
is not statistically associated with development of uveitis or uveitis
symptoms.
Cardiovascular disease, although uncommon, can be severe in
patients with juvenile AS, with inflammatory aortic regurgitation
reported in as many as 10% of patients.
Subclinical gastrointestinal inflammation has been known to be associated with JSpA,
with findings of as many as two-thirds of patients observed to
have changes similar to patients with Crohn’s disease.
Fecal stool
calprotectin (fCAL) can be used to screen for gastrointestinal (GI)
inflammation and is more elevated in children with ERA subtype
compared with other JIA subtypes. In addition, fCAL was significantly higher in ERA patients with SI joint inflammation on
MRI.
Arthritis associated with IBD is also seen in children with
two distinct patterns of joint involvement: (1) peripheral arthritis, which is more common than (2) sacroiliitis and spondylitis.
Additional clinical manifestations unique to arthritis associated
with IBD include erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum,
chronic nonbacterial osteomyelitis (CNO), and rarely, hypertrophic osteoarthropathy.
The peripheral arthritis activity correlates
with gut disease activity, unlike the SI joint activity, which does
not necessarily correlate.
Reactive arthritis, categorized under JSpA, occurs after infection with organisms from the gastrointestinal tract (i.e.,
Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, Campylobacter, Clostridium difficile
) or the
genitourinary tract (i.e.,
Chlamydia
or
Ureaplasma
) without actual
infection in the joint tissues. Usually self-limited, the arthritis can
at times persist and transform into a more chronic arthropathy.
In addition to the arthritis, urethritis and uveitis have been documented in children to complete the classic triad for reactive arthritis. The clinical infection usually precedes the arthritis, enthesitis,
or extra-articular disease by 1 to 4 weeks.
HLA-B27 is present in 38% to 68% of patients with JSpA,
depending on the ethnicity and cohorts evaluated.
The ESR may
be mildly or markedly raised, and there may be a mild anemia,
but these should also raise the suspicion that the child may have
subclinical IBD. RF is negative, and ANA may be positive.
The differential diagnosis of JSpA includes true infectious or
postinfectious arthritides (viral, rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal, Lyme). More benign entities such as toxic synovitis and
benign nocturnal limb pains of childhood (growing pains), as
well as more concerning entities such as malignancies and solid
tumors, should also be considered. Orthopedic diagnoses, such
as Legg–Calvé–Perthes disease, slipped capital femoral epiphyses
disease, and Osgood–Schlatter disease, and less common rheumatologic diseases, such as chronic nonbacterial/recurrent multifocal osteomyelitis (CNO/CRMO); synovitis, acne, pustulosis,
hyperostosis, and osteomyelitis (SAPHO); Kawasaki’s disease, and
vasculitis, can share similar articular and extra-articular manifestations with JSpA. Both Behçet’s disease and familial Mediterranean
fever (FMF) can have sacroiliitis features and therefore could be
mistaken for JSpA.

## Juvenile Psoriatic Arthritis

Juvenile psoriatic arthritis (JPsA) was initially defined as juvenile-onset arthritis associated with psoriasis occurring at some
point during the disease course. This definition has been further
expanded to arthritis in the presence of psoriasis or at least two
minor criteria—nail pits/onycholysis, dactylitis, or a first-degree
family history of psoriasis—in the absence of specific exclusions.
JPsA represents 2% to 5% of all JIA, with a slight
female preponderance and a bimodal pattern of onset (2 to 4 years
and 10 to 12 years).
There are noted differences between the
two age-onset groups, with the younger-onset cohort more likely
to be ANA
, female, HLA-DR5–positive, with chronic uveitis
and more autoimmune features, whereas the older-onset cohort
more is likely to have HLA-B27 positivity, enthesitis, and axial
disease.
The psoriasis typically occurs within 2 years of the onset of
arthritis, and for the majority of children, the skin symptoms follow the arthritis symptoms.
In children, classic psoriatic vulgaris
or plaque psoriasis characterized by well-demarcated, erythematous, scaly lesions occurring over extensor surfaces (elbows and
knees), scalp, and trunk are the most common, followed by guttate or “drop-like” psoriasis.
In children younger than 2 years,
the most common finding is psoriatic diaper rash.
Additional
areas that should be evaluated include the hairline behind the ears,
the navel, intertriginous, and anogenital regions (e.g., superior to
the gluteal clef).
Nail involvement (onycholysis) can be seen and
is thought to reflect enthesitis of the distal insertion of the extensor tendons at the nailbed.
Because JPsA shares similar manifestations with multiple subtypes of juvenile arthritis, there is not one specific articular presentation. Patterns of joint involvement can be oligoarthritis or
polyarthritis, with 55% to 70% of patients with JPsA having an
oligoarthritis onset (fewer than five joints), although evolution
to polyarthritis can occur in as many as 52%.
Involvement
of distal interphalangeal joints is rare at onset but highly suggestive of JPsA, and axial involvement is seen in 10% to 30% of
patients.
Dactylitis, swelling in the finger including tenosynovitis of the flexors, synovitis in the nearby joints, subperiosteal bone
formation, and enthesitis at multiple insertions of tendons and
ligaments resulting in the fusiform “sausage finger,” is frequently
seen in JPsA.
In one study, as many as one-third of patients were
diagnosed with another JIA subtype before the diagnosis of JPsA
after a mean of 4.2 years.
There were no differences in ANA
status, HLA-B27 positivity, or uveitis frequency between oligoarticular JIA versus JPsA patients with fewer than five joints
involved at onset. However, JPsA patients with fewer than five
joints involved at onset did have more dactylitis, more small joint
involvement, and less large joint involvement than oligoarticular
JIA patients.
Children with psoriatic arthritis may have
mildly elevated acute phase reactants and mild anemia of chronic
disease. However, as many as one-third have no laboratory evidence of inflammation.

## Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

Systemic JIA (sJIA), which makes up 10% to 20% of all JIA, has
unique characteristics that include fever, specific rash, and significantly elevated inflammatory markers in addition to arthritis.
Recent research has identified biologic differences between sJIA
and the other JIA subcategories, including prominent involvement of components of the innate immune system especially at

the onset of disease. However, aspects of the adaptive immune
system such as autoimmune T cells can occur later in the chronic
phase, suggesting that sJIA is on the spectrum between autoinflammatory and autoimmune disease.
sJIA occurs at any age, with a tendency toward patients
between 1 to 5 years, peaking around 2 years of age with no sex
difference.
Rarely, it occurs in adults and is called
adult-onset
Still’s disease.
The systemic features of sJIA are striking and are
always present at onset, often predominating the clinical presentation. The arthritis may not be present for weeks up to months after
onset, although arthralgias and myalgias are almost universally
present. The fevers are typically spiking in character with a peak of
39° C daily, occurring once or twice a day, and can be subnormal
between fevers. The child appears unwell and irritable during the
fever but often recovers when afebrile. In 80% of patients, the
fevers are accompanied by an evanescent, migratory, salmon pink,
and sometimes urticarial macular rash (
Fig. 109.1
). The rash typically spares the face, can occur without specific distribution, can
be subtle and scattered or diffuse, and can be difficult to identify
in dark-skinned individuals. A helpful diagnostic feature is that
the rash can be elicited by the Koebner phenomenon.
Other systemic features include lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, serositis (pleural/pericardial involvement/abdominal pain), and poor growth. Clinical and subclinical pericarditis
and pericardial effusions are common, occurring in up to 36% of
patients.
Serious life-threatening complications of sJIA including
macrophage activation syndrome
(MAS) and the newly recognized
sJIA-related lung disease (sJIA-LD) are discussed in the Special
Considerations section. The arthritis in sJIA can vary from minimal to oligoarthritis to polyarthritis presentation
and typically
does not correlate with the severity of the systemic manifestations.
Tenosynovitis and synovial cyst formation can also be seen.
Although the systemic manifestations can be serious initially, the
long-term morbidity is the result of severe articular damage with
up to one-third having joint destruction, especially in the prebiologic era, and adverse effects of medications, especially chronic
corticosteroids. There are no diagnostic tests for sJIA, but there are
characteristic patterns of laboratory abnormalities, including high
inflammatory markers, significant leukocytosis with neutrophilia
and bandemia, thrombocytosis, and anemia. Liver transaminases,
aldolase, ferritin, fibrin split products, and coagulation screen may
be abnormal in severe cases and can be signs of early or impending
MAS. ANA or other autoantibodies are rarely present.
Because of the nonspecific nature of sJIA, diagnosis should be
approached as a diagnosis of exclusion. It is necessary to consider

### Special Considerations: Macrophage Activation Syndrome and sJIA-Related Lung Disease

Overt life-threatening MAS will develop in approximately 10%
of patients with sJIA, and a milder form will develop in as many
as 30%.
If inadequately treated, the milder form could lead to
full-blown MAS.
MAS is a form of secondary or acquired
hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis seen with rheumatic disease. The mortality rate can be 6% to 30% and accounted for the
majority of the mortality in JIA prior to 2001.
The main manifestations include unrelenting high fevers, hepatosplenomegaly,
lymphadenopathy, severe cytopenias, liver dysfunction, CNS
involvement (seizures/coma), and coagulopathy and bleeding.
Ferritin often exceeds 10,000 ng/mL. Full-blown MAS is heralded by decreased leukocyte and platelet counts, falling ESR,
liver dysfunction, hyperferritinemia, and elevated D-dimers.
Histiophagocytosis by histopathology may not be diagnostically
sensitive.
New criteria for MAS in sJIA have been developed
with good sensitivity and specificity to aid in diagnosis.
There
is no sex, age, or race predilection for MAS in sJIA. Half of
MAS cases studied were associated with active disease; one-third
of cases were associated with infectious triggers, most commonly Epstein–Barr virus; and a minority of cases were associated with medication exposures or unknown causes.
Current
understanding of pathophysiology includes excessive inflammatory cytokines, especially interferon
γ
, activation of T cells
and macrophages and poor natural killer and CD8 cell cytolytic
activity.
Soluble IL-2RA, CXCL9 and CXCL10 levels, and
soluble CD163 are increased in MAS and may be helpful in
monitoring response to therapy.
A serious chronic form of lung disease has emerged in the
past 20 years and is recognized as a significant potentially lifethreatening complication of sJIA first described in 2013.
A
total of 89 children with sJIA-LD have been studied from two
multicenter retrospective studies reported in 2019.
Exposure
to IL-1 and/or IL-6 inhibitors has increased the prevalence of
this condition by up to fivefold since 2002, increasing significantly the recognition of this previously rare finding.
Both
cohorts identified onset of younger age (<2 years), mild pulmonary symptoms (no or mild hypoxia and chronic cough),
and high incidence of erythematous clubbing at onset. Marked
hyperferritinemia, MAS, elevated IL-18 levels, eosinophilia, and
a prominent interferonγ
gene signature are frequent associations. Radiologic findings include patchy ground glass opacities
and septal thickening without prominent fibrosis, and pathology suggests a unique inflammatory form of pulmonary alveolar proteinosis-like disease. There are no characteristic genetic
associations identified including the lack of genetic associations
with PAP, although patients with sJIA and Trisomy 21 were
over-represented in the sJIA-LD cohort.
There was over 30%
mortality in patients with sJIA-LD.
An additional observation is that some patients have a drug-related eosinophilia and
systemic symptoms (DRESS)–like reaction with association
with the DRESS-associated allele HLA DRB1*15, suggesting a delayed hypersensitivity reaction.
The relationship of

drug-related delayed hypersensitivity reactions and sJIA LD is
not clear, and there are two hypotheses that are currently being
evaluated internationally: whether IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors act
as antigens with direct effects on hypersensitization or whether
the biologic effects of IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors sets the stage for
delayed hypersensitivity reactions through a cytokine plasticity
hypothesis.
Understanding a potential iatrogenic complication
associated with cytokine inhibitors is critical for best determining how to manage children with sJIA. Patients need to be monitored closely for development of hypersensitivity and pulmonary
symptoms.

## Treatment—Medical Therapy

With the development of increasingly more effective biologic
and targeted synthetic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs
(DMARDs) for arthritis, pediatric rheumatologists now aim
to achieve clinically inactive disease (on or off therapy). Several
studies support the paradigm of treating aggressively to reach
inactive disease as early as possible in the disease course or at least
to minimize disease activity, which may ultimately lead to better outcomes, including quality of life, shorter periods of active
disease, and less long-term joint damage.
The evaluation
of step-up therapy, combined early therapy, and treat-to-target
are facilitated by rigorous disease activity measures including
the JADAS, American College of Rheumatology (ACR) Pediatric 30/50/70, as well as well-validated definitions of clinical
inactive disease and minimal disease activity.
The treatment
approaches discussed in this section are based on integration
of the current understanding of best treatments including the
ACR-published guidelines for treatment of JIA
; comparative
effectiveness studies based on consensus treatment plans developed by rigorous consensus methodology
; and randomized
control trials (RCTs). In addition to treating active arthritis, several studies evaluate optimal strategies for de-escalation of therapy when clinical inactive disease has been achieved. In general,
most studies in polyarticular-course JIA suggest that about half
of the patients who have discontinued medications after reaching clinically inactive disease may flare by 6 to 12 months after
discontinuation, with variations depending on multiple factors,
such as time after onset of initiation of biologic or time in clinical inactive disease. Studies showed that approximately 50% of
patients who flared after medication discontinuation could be
recaptured within 6 months.
The pharmacology of individual drugs is discussed elsewhere. Special considerations for children are provided below.

### Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drug Use in Children

Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and/or intraarticular steroid (IAS) injections are the first-line treatment in oligoarticular JIA to control symptoms of pain and stiffness. NSAIDs
have both analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties by blocking
prostaglandin formation via inhibition of cyclooxygenase-1 and
cyclooxygenase-2. NSAIDs do not alter the natural history of JIA
but are frequently used as an adjunctive therapy to more definitive therapies. In general, NSAIDS should only be considered as
monotherapy in initial therapy in patients with low disease activity. If control is not achieved in 1 to 2 months, additional therapy
should be initiated.

### Intra-articular Steroid Injections

Early treatment with IAS is associated with better outcome,
and
IAS can bring about rapid sustained control of monoarthritis,
especially of the knee.
It remains to be seen whether IAS can be
used as the sole therapy in persistent oligoarticular JIA, as arthritis
in uninjected joints can develop, illustrating that IAS therapy does
not prevent arthritis extension to previously uninvolved joints. An
RCT of IAS treatment alone versus IAS with oral methotrexate
(MTX) in oligoarticular JIA patients did not show significant differences in outcome between the two groups, but post-hoc analysis suggests that MTX use may help lengthen the effect of IAS
therapy.
Intra-articular triamcinolone hexacetonide (TH) has been
found to be superior to other preparations of intra-articular
corticosteroids, such as triamcinolone acetonide (TA). Unfortunately, triamcinolone hexacetonide is no longer available in
the United States. IAS injections with TA are expected to result
in clinical improvement of arthritis for 1 to 2 months. If arthritis recurs, joint injections can be repeated as many as three
times in a 12-month period. Difficult-to-reach joints, such as
the hip, SI joint, TMJ, and subtalar joint, may be injected by
using US or fluoroscopy. The effect of IAS injections on joint
cartilage and subchondral bone remains unclear. Some research
indicates that IASs can be a risk factor for development of subchondral bone lesions.
IAS should be administered under
sedation or general anesthesia for the young. The use of IAS
for active arthritis has been recommended by the ACR guidelines for oligoarthritis but is considered adjunct therapy for
polyarthritis.

### Corticosteroid Use in Children

In general, systemic corticosteroids should be used sparingly in the
treatment of any subgroup of JIA because of the severe morbidity
associated with chronic use, even at low doses. Newer therapeutic
modalities such as biologics may eliminate the need for any corticosteroids or limit the doses needed. This is especially true in sJIA,
in which high-dose corticosteroids have been a mainstay of therapy
for severe systemic manifestations. There is also a role for systemic
corticosteroids in the treatment of systemic JIA with macrophage
activation syndrome.
Otherwise, the evidence for use of corticosteroids for the synovitis of JIA is controversial and conflicting.
Systemic glucocorticoids are used only as a bridge to provide symptomatic relief as more definitive treatments are started; they are not
routinely recommended for the treatment of polyarthritis per ACR
recommendations. The Childhood Arthritis and Rheumatology
Research Alliance (CARRA) consensus treatment protocols (CTPs)
also include optional prednisone use at diagnosis in each treatment
arm as an adjunctive treatment, with recommendations to taper by
3 months if possible. Both topical and systemic corticosteroids have
a role in the treatment of JIA-associated uveitis.
There are many
adverse events associated with glucocorticoids, so they should be
used with caution in the treatment of JIA.

### Potential New Therapies

The therapeutic armamentarium for JIA and all autoimmune diseases is expanding at a remarkable rate. Biologic DMARDS and
most recently targeted synthetic DMARDS (JAK inhibitors) are
effective in JIA, and new clinical trials are ongoing in children
with JIA as described above for each subgroup. These approaches
will continue to be refined for targeting different aspects of JIA
and related disease complications.
Entirely new approaches may be on the horizon. Recent studies of T cell therapies including chimeric antigen receptor T cells
in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and approaches to developing engineered regulatory T (Treg) cells are two examples of
potential future strategies
The mucosal origins hypothesis,
proposing that mucosal sites of chronic inflammation resulting
from environmental interactions may generalize over time to
involve other tissues such as synovium, is increasingly of interest,
with a recent study demonstrating autoantibodies from individuals at risk for RA against an arthritogenic gut bacteria.
This
mucosal origins hypothesis may yield an array of potential therapies that that could be preventative, therapeutic, or even curative
when linked with the emerging sophisticated molecular and cellular approaches that are currently available or under development.

## Treatment of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis Subtypes

### Oligoarticular Arthritis

Treatment of patients with arthritis in four or fewer joints is usually approached in a stepwise fashion starting with NSAIDs and/
or IAS for patients with low disease activity and quickly escalating up to MTX if not effective in 1 to 2 months.
Poor
prognostic features, including involvement of the hip or cervical spine or ankle or wrist involvement with high inflammatory
markers, should prompt the use of MTX as first-line therapy.
Radiographic changes, such as erosions and joint space narrowing
warrant initiation of a biologic such as a TNFi.
Children with
ongoing arthritis and who fail anti-TNF agents may receive other
biologics such as tocilizumab or abatacept.

### Polyarticular Arthritis

The CARRA developed CTPs for new-onset polyarticular JIA.
The CTP include three treatment arms: (1) a “step-up” arm initiating csDMARD therapy, (2) an “early combination” arm starting both csDMARD and bDMARD together, and (3) a “biologic
only” arm using any bDMARD as initial treatment. Oral prednisone and/or IAS are optional in all three arms.
The CTP provide guidance for escalation of therapy if patients demonstrate
inadequate response after 3 months of therapy. The Start Time
Optimization of Biologics in Polyarticular JIA (STOP-JIA) study
compared the effectiveness of the three CTPs in a prospective,
observational study.
While this study did not demonstrate a
difference in the primary outcome measure of meeting the ACR
criteria for clinically inactive disease without glucocorticoids at
12 months after starting treatment, more patients enrolled in the
early combination CTP achieved inactive disease based on the
clinical JADAS-10 and achieved ACR Pedi 70.
A 3-year extension of this study demonstrates that patients in the early combination had significantly more time in clinically inactive disease
compared with the other arms.
The ACR and the Arthritis Foundation (AF) published updated
treatment guidelines for patients with nonsystemic polyarthritis
in 2019.
Initial treatment of children and adolescents with JIA
and polyarthritis should include csDMARD therapy, with MTX

preferred over leflunomide or sulfasalazine and with NSAIDs and
IAS as adjunct therapy.
For patients with risk factors including
positive RF or ACPAs or joint damage and high-risk joints such
as cervical spine, wrist, or hips, bDMARDs may be considered as
initial therapy.
In patients with ongoing disease activity, escalation of therapy is recommended. For patients treated with TNFi
who have moderate/high disease activity, switching to a non-TNFi
bDMARD is recommended. However, with sequential use of biologics, the response rate is attenuated.
Rituximab, tocilizumab,
abatacept, or different combinations of various csDMARDs and
bDMARDs have all have been used after failure of response to
TNFis, with varying degrees of success.
Because children with RF
polyarticular JIA are at higher risk
of prolonged erosive arthritis compared with other subtypes of
JIA, these children should be considered to be in the more severe
disease category, requiring rapid escalation of treatment if even
mild disease activity persists.
Three RCTs highlight the use of early aggressive treatment for
polyarticular-course arthritis. In 2011 an open-label, multicenter
RCT comparing combination csDMARDs (MTX, SSZ, and
hydroxychloroquine) versus MTX and infliximab versus MTX
alone in JIA patients with early, active polyarthritis, demonstrated
that MTX and infliximab combination was the most effective
arm.
In 2012 an RCT called Trial of Early Aggressive Therapy
(TREAT) compared two aggressive treatment arms (combination of corticosteroids, etanercept and MTX versus MTX alone)
for patients with active polyarticular-course JIA within 1 year of
diagnosis. There was a trend toward improvement in the MTX,
etanercept, and prednisone arm, and it showed that clinical inactive disease (CID)
at 6 months was attainable by using either of
the two early aggressive treatment arms. This study also suggested
a treatment window of opportunity: the shorter the disease duration before treatment, the higher the likelihood of reaching CID
at 6 months (odds ratio [OR] per month, 1.3;
P
< .011).
However, 65% of children were not able to sustain CID beyond
6 to 12 months, especially with medication taper,
suggesting
that longer therapy may be helpful. In 2021 a multicenter doubleblind placebo-controlled randomized trial compared etanercept
plus MTX with placebo plus MTX in active polyarticular JIA
patients.
This study demonstrated that patients treated with
etanercept plus methotrexate achieved inactive disease and remission more quickly than those treated with placebo plus methotrexate and had a more rapid decrease in JADAS-71.

### Juvenile Spondyloarthritis

To date, there have been few studies or trials on JSpA.
Primarily, treatment is based on arthritis activity and in particular
the presence or absence of sacroiliitis. For severe symptoms or
evidence of potential joint damage (such as erosive sacroiliitis),
the use of TNFis has been effective based on small pediatric studies and adult studies.
Adalimumab and etanercept have
been evaluated in RCTs that show reduction in flare risk with
the biologic and decreased disease activity and patient-reported
pain.
The 2019 ACR treatment recommendations for
JIA recommend TNFi over csDMARDs in the setting of active
sacroiliitis or enthesitis despite NSAIDS.
For patients with predominance of enthesitis symptoms, TNFis or bridging therapy
with a short course of steroids is recommended over conventional DMARDS (csDMARDS), while patients presenting with
predominantly peripheral arthritis could reasonably start with a
trial of csDMARDs. Intra-articular steroid joint injections of the
sacroiliac joints improve symptoms 3 months after injection; however, within 2 years 47% of patients needed therapy escalation.
Case reports of IL-12 and IL-23 targets (ustekinumab) showed
improvement in children with ERA.
IL-17 inhibitors are also
now being used to treat adult spondyloarthritis with early promising results and studies with ixekizumab (
ClinicalTrials.gov
NCT04527380) are ongoing in children.
In addition to treatment with medications, physical activity has been part of the therapeutic program for patients with JPsA. The LEAP study showed
improvement in fatigue with a 6-month home exercise program;
however, adherence was low.

### Juvenile Psoriatic Arthritis

There have been only two open-label prospective studies examining the efficacy of biologics in JPsA, both of which showed
improved response within 3 months.
A study comparing
treatment regimens of patients with JPsA versus oligoarticular JIA
patients and polyarticular JIA patients found no difference in use
of NSAIDs, MTX, and TNFis.
In general, the treatment of JPsA
should follow the treatment approaches for patients with oligoarticular- or polyarticular-course arthritis. MTX is of benefit for
both skin psoriasis and arthritis in children.
In children with
more aggressive or axial disease, TNFis (which have also been successful in treating psoriasis) are indicated and may significantly
limit bony destruction.
Newer treatments include the
targeted synthetic DMARDs (i.e., Janus kinase inhibitors) and
IL-17 and IL-12/IL-23 blocking molecules, which show promising results in adults but have not been published in JPsA to date.

### Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

Treatment for sJIA is an active area of research with evolving understanding of the autoinflammatory and autoimmune biology and
the potential therapeutic roles for targeted biologics. Approaches
to therapy of sJIA discussed in this chapter are informed by a variety of studies and publications including the 2021 ACR guidelines for sJIA,
CTPs for sJIA,
and published RCTs.
Therapies such as MTX and TNFis are less helpful in controlling
systemic symptoms of sJIA but may be helpful if the disease progresses to chronic arthritis with few systemic features.
Since
the onset and course of sJIA can vary from mild to severe in both
the systemic manifestations and the synovitis, with few predictors
of the course or severity, therapy should be tailored to the patient’s
status and response to treatment, escalating as necessary.
Mild sJIA, defined as mild systemic symptoms and synovitis
without organ system involvement, can sometimes be successfully treated with anti-inflammatory doses of NSAIDs. Certain
NSAIDs are helpful for different aspects of the disease (e.g., indomethacin can be used for fever control and serositis symptoms). In
addition, if there are only a few large joints involved, IASs are an
additional option. If symptoms do not respond or worsen, IL-1 or
IL-6 inhibitors are increasingly being successfully used as the first
line of disease-modifying therapy,
avoiding the use
of high-dose corticosteroids in many cases.
With more severe intensive systemic symptoms and in the
absence of MAS, corticosteroids may be a useful addition, either
as daily high dose prednisone (60 mg/day) or IV methylprednisolone (30 mg/kg/dose, maximum 1 g/day) for up to 3 days,
followed by tapering doses of oral prednisone.
IL-1 and IL-6
inhibitors should be added if not already started to facilitate corticosteroid taper. Cyclosporine and monthly IV immunoglobulin

have shown some benefit for systemic symptoms in pre-biologic
era studies for steroid-sparing effects.
Tofacitinib, a member
of the JAK/STAT inhibitors family, is the first targeted synthetic
DMARD approved for polyarticular course JIA by the FDA in
2020.
Tofacitinib and other JAK/STAT inhibitors appear to be
effective in active systemic JIA and adult onset Still’s disease, as
well as interferonγ
(IFN
γ
)–mediated inflammatory monogenic
or nongenetic disorders in children in small studies.
Baricitinib
is currently in clinical trials in sJIA for patients between 1 to 18 y
(
ClinicalTrials.gov
NCT04088396). The presence of high levels of
IL-18 in sJIA have prompted studies of IL-18 inhibitors including
the IL-18–binding protein inhibitor tadekinig
α
, which is currently in a clinical trial (
ClinicalTrials.gov
NCT02398435). For
very severe refractory cases, stem cell transplantation (SCT) has
been performed, and some cases have shown benefit.
The optimal treatment for MAS continues to evolve with
understanding the underlying immunopathophysiology of the
roles of IL-18 and IFN
γ
in sJIA-related MAS and cytokine storm
syndromes including SARS-CoV-2 infection and related multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children and adults.
Current therapy includes initiation with high-dose pulse corticosteroids (sometimes with cyclosporin A) and IL-1 inhibitor anakinra.
Results of a current RCT (
ClinicalTrials.gov
NCT02780583) studying high-dose anakinra to the treatment of
MAS associated with sJIA are expected in 2023. The IL-6 inhibitor
tocilizumab may be helpful, but both canakinumab (IL-1 inhibitor) and tocilizumab IL-1 and/or IL-6 inhibitors did not prevent
MAS in RCTs.
At the time of this writing, emapalumab, an
anti-IFN
γ
monoclonal antibody, has just been demonstrated to be
effective and safe in a phase II trial of sJIA with MAS unresponsive
to corticosteroids and suggests that targeting the significant IFN
γ
-
mediated inflammatory response in MAS may be the most effective therapy.
Emapalumab has been approved by the FDA for
use in familial hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH),
and it is possible that its use will increase in sJIA-related MAS
with the evolving research in this area.
Approaching management of the sJIA-LD is more challenging
due to its complex immunopathophysiology with several potential
pathways. At this time, the IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors are first-line
therapy for disease-modifying agents in sJIA and have been transformational in management of sJIA and prevention of long-term
disability and corticosteroid dependence. Decisions about how to
manage the rare occurrence of lung disease need to be evaluated
on a case-by-case basis. Active multicenter international research
efforts are focusing on potential drivers of lung disease including
(1) immune dysregulation due to IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitor treatment
leading to major shifts in levels of IL-18 and type I and II interferons, (2) the contribution of direct drug-related hypersensitivity
reaction DRESS due to IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors, or 3) a complex
immunologic milieu of multiple interacting factors.
Management strategies are not yet developed regarding when
and if it is necessary to discontinue IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors,
whether to type patients for the DRESS susceptibility allele HLA
DRB1*15, and most importantly, what other medications would
be effective in sJIA that may help treat the lung disease and/or
may not be associated with lung disease. The effectiveness of Jak/
Stat inhibitors in JIA
may be helpful in managing these patients
with severe lung disease if IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors are discontinued.
As the research progresses in refining immune targets for these small molecule inhibitors and understanding the
underlying drivers of lung disease, more definitive approaches will
be developed.

## Clinical Features and Management of Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis–Associated Uveitis

Chronic anterior uveitis (CAU) is the most frequent extra-articular manifestation of JIA and can cause significant vision complications, including blindness. Diagnosis is often delayed since it
is asymptomatic without conjunctival inflammation.
In a large,
prospective inception cohort of new onset JIA patients, the incidence of uveitis development was 2.8% per year in the first 5 years
with the majority.
Uveitis is most common in patients with oligoarticular JIA who have a positive ANA (17% to 26%), patients
with RF
polyarticular JIA (4% to 25%), and patients with
JPsA (10%). It is rare in patients with RF
polyarticular JIA and
sJIA.
Uveitis incidence demonstrates geographic and racial/
ethnic variability, with higher rates in those of northern European
descent and lower rates in those of African and Asian descent.
Overall uveitis prevalence is considered to be approximately 20%,
with some subgroups having much higher risk and prevalence.
Risk factors for uveitis include ANA positivity, age at JIA onset
6 years or younger, disease duration 4 years or less, oligoarticular JIA subtype, JPsA subtype, and elevated ESRs at baseline or
Use of MTX alone or combined with etanercept
follow-up.

development especially if started early in disease.
Chronic anterior uveitis is typically nongranulomatous, recurrent, usually bilateral, and asymptomatic. If unrecognized, uveitis
can lead to serious visual deficits, which may worsen with time.
Uncontrolled, long-standing inflammation can lead to posterior
synechiae, band keratopathy, cataracts, secondary glaucoma,
macular edema, hypotony, retinal detachment, and optic nerve
edema, all of which may lead to irreversible vision loss. Because
of its insidious onset, regularly scheduled screening by an experienced ophthalmologist with slit lamp examination is required
for early diagnosis with specific screening guidelines as shown in

Treatment of uveitis requires close collaboration
between the child’s rheumatologist and ophthalmologist. Newly
diagnosed patients are ideally screened within 6 weeks of diagnosis
because approximately 30% of uveitis cases have been found at
the onset of JIA,
and 5% to 10% have occurred before diagnosis.
In addition, 90% of uveitis cases are diagnosed in the
first 4 years after JIA diagnosis, but uveitis has occurred even into
adulthood.
In contrast, children with JSpA have acute anterior uveitis, which is usually highly symptomatic (sudden onset
of erythema, pain, and photophobia) and therefore requires only
annual screening. The clinical course of the uveitis and arthritis
may not parallel one another.
The differential diagnoses include infectious causes, such as
tuberculosis, toxoplasmosis, cytomegalovirus, herpes simplex
virus, syphilis, HIV, Lyme disease, cat scratch disease, and fungus.
Uveitis can occur secondary to a systemic illness, such as in Behçet’s
disease, sarcoidosis, autoinflammatory syndromes, multiple sclerosis, tubulointerstitial nephritis and uveitis syndrome (TINU),
and Vogt–Koyanagi–Harada syndrome. It is also important not to
miss a malignancy such as lymphoma or retinoblastoma.
The goal of uveitis treatment is to diagnose early, control the
acute inflammation and complications, treat the underlying systemic disease, minimize medication side effects, prevent recurrences, and preserve vision. Inflammation should be controlled to

## Imaging

Imaging is becoming increasingly important in JIA to confirm the
diagnosis; to exclude other non-inflammatory diseases; to monitor therapy and disease activity; and to track evidence of joint
damage. Imaging in children with inflammatory arthritis is challenging given the unique features of the anatomically changing
joints during the normal growth process. Because children have
a large amount of cartilage in their joints, a significant amount
of destruction can occur before erosions are identified. Furthermore, physiologic features of normal bone growth can be misinterpreted as abnormal. Local growth disturbances can also be
seen in children with JIA, including bony overgrowth, particularly

because of chronic hyperemia. In contrast, premature fusion of
the epiphyses can lead to shortening of certain joints, most notably TMJ resulting in micrognathia.
The articular cartilage of
growing children has unique regenerative qualities, and studies
have shown that children with JIA can have improvement in their
radiographic joint damage.
Joint protection is important in
pediatric patients, and damage can be as frequent and as severe
as in adult patients with RA, with a higher risk for bilateral hip
damage in patients with JIA.
The trend toward early aggressive treatment to prevent erosive disease shifts the imaging need
away from plain radiographs and toward other imaging modalities
that are more sensitive in detecting early disease activity,
such
as MRI and US. Different radiographic modalities are now recognized to improve evaluation of certain aspects of disease.
Conventional radiography is good at ruling out trauma and
orthopedic conditions, as well as evaluating late findings such
as ankyloses, joint misalignment, enlarged epiphysis, premature
physeal fusion, and spinal deformities.
In early disease, radiographs only show soft tissue swelling, effusions, and periarticular
ostoepenia.
For lower extremities, weight bearing views are recommended to evaluate for joint space loss. In addition, standing
scanograms can evaluate for leg-length discrepancies. In addition,
pelvic radiographs can demonstrate axial disease involvement with
joint space narrowing, sclerosis, erosions, and ankylosis. The iliac
side of the sacroiliac (SI) joint tends to shows damage earlier than
the thicker cartilage lined sacral side of the joint. In children, the
contour of the maturing SI joint can be very irregular making
evaluation more difficult and can lead to overinterpretation of
erosions.
US is being utilized more in children because it is
noninvasive, safe, fast, and relatively inexpensive. Many pediatric
rheumatology programs now are utilizing point-of-care US during the clinic visit for both diagnosis and to assist in therapeutic
injections. The normal standards of joint cartilage thickness by
age and sex have now been established, and it is noted that JIA
patients have reduced cartilage thickness compared with healthy
controls.
Patients with polyarticular JIA or sJIA appear to
have thinner cartilage than patients with oligoarticular JIA.
Recently, normal US standards in children for the hip, shoulder,
and wrist joints have also been published.
Ultrasound is more
sensitive than plain radiography in the detection of effusions,
synovial thickening, and synovial cysts especially in the smaller
joints.
It is an excellent tool to detect early inflammatory
changes in synovitis, tenosynovitis, tendonitis, and enthesitis. In
experienced hands, even cartilage thinning and bone erosions can
also be seen. Furthermore, US evaluations of patients that were in
clinical remission that demonstrated the presence of subclinical
synovitis had a higher risk of disease flare, thus possibly predicting disease activity earlier.
Another study showed that the risk
of flare in US-positive patients versus US-negative patients was
almost four times higher.
Inflammatory involvement of the
hip, shoulder, and elbow is more frequently accurately detected by
US compared with a clinical examination.
Color and power
Doppler US can facilitate the evaluation of hyperemia and vascular abnormalities in affected joints and tendon involvement.
It
is still difficult to monitor disease activity with ultrasound due to
lack of standard and validated scoring systems.
MRI is the most complete and sensitive imaging modality currently available for detecting enthesitis, synovitis, bone marrow
edema, and bone erosions; however, its use is limited by expense
and the need for anesthesia in younger children. The use of contrast can accurately differentiate between active and inactive states;
however, new findings and implications of gadolinium deposition
in the brain need to be further studied in children.
One study found subclinical synovitis present in >35% of presumed clinically inactive JIA patients.
Certain joints are best
evaluated using MRI in children, including the TMJ joint, SI
joint, and cervical spine. The Adult Assessment of Spondyloarthritis International Society (ASAS) definition of radiologic sacroiliitis was found to have low sensitivity in children.
MRI is
the imaging of choice for suspected SI joint involvement and is
best visualized on fluid-sensitive sequences (STIR or T2-weighted
fat saturation images).
Interestingly, MRI is not the best modality to evaluate entheses due to cost, availability, and potential need
for sedation.
Recent work with diffusion-weighted MRI (DWI)
that does not need contrast has shown promise by detecting arthritis with a sensitivity of 93% and a specificity of 81%.
Because
work to standardize normals in children is ongoing, caution must
be taken when analyzing MRI imaging in children. A pilot study
showed that contrast-enhanced MRI of the knee was useful for predicting flares in children with clinically inactive JIA.
Regarding
long-term outcomes, asymptomatic patients with a positive MRI
and signs of inflammation on US are more likely to flare or show
disease progression at 6-month follow-up.
Furthermore, a longterm outcome study on radiographic damage showed restricted
mobility in a joint at 15 years is the most important predictor of
joint damage at 29 years of disease duration.
Emerging imaging
techniques for JIA evaluation include infrared thermal imaging
of knee and ankle joints, three-dimensional imaging particularly
of the TMJ, and motion detection with accelerometer and gyroscopes to quantify joint movement and gait analysis.

## Outcomes

With earlier, more aggressive, targeted therapies, the overall outcome of JIA is clearly improving. In a prospective inception cohort

(ReACCh-Out), within 2 years after diagnosis on contemporary
treatments, the probability of zero active joints was >78% for all
JIA patients, and the probability of inactive disease (no active
joints, no extra-articular manifestations, and a physician global
assessment of disease activity <10 mm) was >70% in all categories
except for RF
polyarticular JIA. Within 5 years, the probability
of disease remission (inactive disease
≥
12 months of no treatment)
was 46% to 57% in all JIA subgroups with the exception of 0% in
RF
and 14% in RF
polyarticular JIA subtypes.
The STOP-JIA trial showed there were no significant differences in CID score between polyarticular JIA patients with three
different treatment protocols (CTPs) (step up versus early combination versus biologic first) at the 3-year timepoint. However, the
more important statistic is that the proportion of patients who
achieved clinical remission at any time in the study was only 47%
to 67%.
Therefore even with new diagnostic and treatment approaches,
JIA may persist through adulthood; flares are common off medicine and cause significant morbidity, functional disability, joint
damage, growth impairment, decreased health-related qualityof-life (HRQoL),
chronic pain,
and economic hardship.
Chronic pain in JIA patients persists despite absent clinical activity,
and a patient with low disease activity can have decreased
HRQoL.
While the majority of JIA patients can achieve
inactive disease with treatment at some point, it is uncertain how
long to treat and how to withdraw medication. More studies are
needed to approach medication withdrawal in JIA. There is also
substantial variation in disease remission frequencies depending
on JIA subgroup, but only some patients are able to maintain
remission of medications for long term.

### Oligoarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

The long-term outcome of oligoarticular JIA has improved
steadily in the last 15 years.
In the ReACCh-Out cohort, 96%
of patients with oligoarticular JIA had inactive disease, and 57%
were in remission off medication at 5 years.
In general, patients
with persistent oligoarticular JIA have the best prognosis of all JIA
subtypes, with 66% to 80% of patients achieving clinical remission off medication.
Extended oligoarticular JIA patients
usually follow a similar chronic disease course as RF
polyarticular JIA and have higher cumulative duration of active arthritis,
more erosive disease, and a higher risk of chronic disability.
Of
children with extended oligoarticular JIA, 21% to 50% were in
remission off medication.
Relapses occur, with disease flares
occurring 2 years on or off medication in 41% to 45% of patients
with persistent oligoarticular JIA and 47% to 56% with extended
oligoarticular JIA.
A large prospective inception cohort
study showed a 42.5% risk of flares in the year after reaching
clinically inactive disease while on stable treatment.
Ongoing
disease activity and extension of joint involvement in oligoarticular JIA patients are related to a poor outcome and radiographic
damage,
and therefore emphasis on control of disease activity
is critical.

### Rheumatoid Factor–Negative Polyarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

RF
polyarticular JIA patients have variable outcomes, but the
overall prognosis appears to be improving with new treatment
strategies.
Early response to treatment is an important predictor
of long-term outcome, and patients who have at least one episode
of inactive disease within the first 5 years of disease may have less
joint damage and a better functional outcome.
Symmetric
arthritis and early hand involvement predicts future disability
and poorer overall well-being.
Reported remission rates vary,
depending on cohorts evaluated and remission definitions used.
Earlier studies show remission rates of 14% at 5 years
and up
to 52% at 30 years.
In a more recent study reporting outcomes
of JIA patients treated during the biologic era with follow-up of
18 years, less than 20% of patients were in remission off medication,
∼
25% to 30% were in remission on medication, and
∼
50%
continued to have active disease.

### Rheumatoid Factor–Positive Polyarticular Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

Children with RF
polyarticular JIA have the most severe articular
outcome with the worst joint prognosis of all the JIA subtypes,
especially in the era before biologic therapies but even now with
contemporary treatments.
This type of arthritis persists
into adulthood, should be treated more aggressively, and can cause
significant functional disability. In a recent cohort of patients on
biologic treatments, there was a 93% probability of inactive disease, but there was 0% probability of disease remission off medications at 5 years.
In a long-term outcome study of JIA patients
from the pre-biologic era with 30 years follow-up, only 17% of
patients with RF
polyarticular JIA reached complete remission
off medication.
Children with RF
polyarticular JIA in the
ReACCh-Out cohort had a 33% probability of significant flare at
1 year and 52% probability at 2 years following inactive disease.
A radiographic study published in 2003 reflecting the pre-biologic
treatment era showed that 9 years after disease presentation, RF
JIA polyarticular patients had more joint space narrowing and
erosions (75%) compared with RF
polyarticular JIA (39%), sJIA
(63%), and oligoarticular JIA patients (25%).

### Juvenile Spondyloarthritis

The long-term outcome of JSpA is not well studied, but as many
as 40% of these children appear to progress to the adult form of
AS. Compared with adult AS, juvenile AS had less severe axial
involvement but worse hip involvement and worse functional
impairment.
Patients with earlier biologic treatment (<2
years) were twice as likely to be in remission off medication and
had better functional outcomes than those starting biologic treatment >5 years out from diagnosis.
Children with ERA who were treated with TNFi in the first
year after diagnosis had statistically significant improvement in
joint count, patient-reported pain, and disease activity compared
with patients only treated with csDMARDS.
Children with
ERA who were treated with TNFi in the first year after diagnosis
had statistically significant improvement in joint count, patientreported pain, and disease activity compared with patients only
treated with csDMARDS.
Predictors of failure to achieve remission include family history of AS in a first degree relative, HLA-DRB1*08 genotype,
and ankle arthritis within 6 months of onset.
Factors that are
associated with poorer prognosis overall include: increased body
mass index, ankle arthritis, hip arthritis, sacroiliitis, enthesitis,
and HLA-B27.
If the arthritis had persisted for >5 years,
the chance of remission was only 17% in the pre-biologic era.
Despite TNF inhibitors helping control inflammation, patients
were not able to discontinue therapy without recurrence and may

not prevent ankylosis or the eventual fusion of the SI joints.
Recent studies have also shown that patients with JSpA have
poorer patient-reported outcomes of pain intensity, physical
function, and health status.
In addition, children with
enthesitis regardless of juvenile arthritis subtype reported worse
patient-reported outcomes (quality of life, function, and pain)
than those without enthesitis.

### Juvenile Psoriatic Arthritis

Because of the heterogeneity in the patterns of arthritis in JPsA,
the outcomes are variable and tend to track with the pattern of
joint involvement. In one study, erosions were less common in
JPsA compared with polyarticular JIA (23% versus 46%, respectively).
Older reports note that JPsA patients have more persistent disease and worse functional outcomes than other subtypes
of JIA.
However, in more recent cohort studies, the outcomes
appeared somewhat improved, with up to 60% of JPsA patients
achieving inactive disease on therapy; however, much fewer
achieve inactive disease off therapy.

### Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

The advent of effective IL-1 and IL-6 blockade in sJIA has been
transformational in effectively treating both systemic and articular disease and preventing long-term damage due to arthritis or
corticosteroid dependence. The overall response rates for IL-1 or
IL-6 inhibitor therapy in the RCTs demonstrated that 50% to
90% achieved pediatric ACR70 improvement and 30% to 50%
achieved CID. In addition, 30% to 50% of patients discontinued corticosteroids.
Participants in these clinical trials often
had chronic active arthritis and corticosteroid dependence and
were nonresponsive to other therapies. These results are especially
remarkable in light of the natural history of sJIA before biologic
therapies, where the course could be (1) continuous with persistent often destructive arthritis and disability, often more prominent after the regression of systemic symptoms in approximately
40% the patients; (2) relapsing, characterized by flares of systemic
features with mild to moderate arthritis in 30%; or (3) monocyclic with remission within 2 years.
Over 50% of patients
received corticosteroids and multiple DMARDs.
Patients with
severe disease can have flares of extra-articular features at any time
and may have active arthritis into adult life despite therapy.
The
severe growth delay seen before the use of IL-1 and IL-6 therapies is significantly decreased with biologic therapies in part due
to decreased corticosteroid use, as well as growth delay due to
systemic inflammatory features.
Long-term articular damage
has greatly decreased since the use of IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors,
although in many cases, long-term discontinuation is possible in
only a minority of patients, and some level of cytokine blockade is
needed for most.
Currently, there are several ongoing studies
addressing whether there are predictors of which patients respond
better to IL-1 versus IL-6 blockade.
Systemic JIA accounts for the majority of the mortality seen
in all JIA. The life-threatening complication of MAS occurs in
approximately 10% of patients and does not appear to be prevented
by ongoing treatment with IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors. However, the
initially high mortality rates of 22% have decreased significantly
since 2001
to around 5% to 8% with improved understanding
and new therapeutic and management approaches to MAS.
The new anti-INF
γ
monoclonal antibody, emapalumab, holds
significant promise for patients with MAS associated with sJIA.
The emergence of severe lung disease (sJIA-LD) corresponds
with the use of IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors in the majority of
patients
with a mortality that may be as high as 30%. Multiple complex immune dysregulation interactions appear to contribute including cytokine imbalances and drug hypersensitivity
reactions,
and dissecting the various potential factors and
management strategies are under active study. Amyloidosis was
previously a major cause of death in sJIA but is now much less
common, most likely because of the use of more aggressive therapy to better control inflammation.

## Special Considerations in Children

Because most bone formation and growth occur in the first 20
years, arthritis in children can cause significant musculoskeletal
abnormalities, both generalized and localized. Some joints, like
the knee, may hypertrophy and cause leg-length discrepancy;
other joints may shorten or have delayed growth, especially the
TMJ and MTP joints.
TMJ arthritis is difficult to detect and diagnose since pain and
physical exam findings may not be present early in disease. Once
symptoms and exam findings are present, changes to the joint
have already occurred.
TMJ involvement may affect dentofacial development, resulting in retro- or micrognathia, facial asymmetry, limitation in mouth opening, pain, and malocclusion.
Gadolinium-enhanced MRI is the gold standard imaging study
to detect active TMJ arthritis.
Standardizing terminology and
assessment for orofacial conditions in JIA: international, multidisciplinary consensus-based recommendations), and there is no
single physical exam finding that accurately predicts TMJ arthritis
identified on MRI.
The prevalence of TMJ arthritis in JIA is
reported between 40% to 96% depending on examination criteria
and imaging modality used.
Isolated TMJ arthritis can occur
as the initial joint presentation of JIA
or can occur in the face
of inactive arthritis in other affected joints.
TMJ arthritis can
occur in all JIA subtypes.
Recommendations for the orofacial
examination of JIA patients to monitor for TMJ involvement have
been published.
These include a detailed assessment of orofacial
symptoms and physical exam assessment of TMJ function, signs
of TMJ arthritis, and facial morphology and symmetry.
In 2021, the ACR published guidelines for treatment of JIA,
including TMJ arthritis.
These guidelines provide seven specific
recommendations for the treatment of TMJ arthritis, although six
are conditional, and all have very low quality of supporting evidence. In patients with active TMJ arthritis, both NSAIDs and
intra-articular corticosteroids (IACs) are conditionally recommended as part of the initial therapy. If response to NSAIDs and/
or IACs is incomplete, use of csDMARD or bDMARD is recommended.
Despite short-term improvements in TMJ arthritis with IACs, long-term consequences include heterotopic bone
formation
and impaired mandibular growth.
Therefore,
IACs should be used cautiously, especially in skeletally immature
patients. The Temporomandibular Joint Juvenile Arthritis Working Group has also published consensus-based recommendations
for the evaluation and management of TMJ arthritis and related
orofacial manifestations in patients with JIA that include specific recommendations regarding IACs and intra-articular lavage
to treat JIA patients with active arthritis or TMJ dysfunction
depending on skeletal maturity.
Physiotherapy (PT) and occupational therapy (OT) are crucial
for all children with JIA, for range of motion, strengthening, joint
protection, and endurance. Children with hand involvement need

OT assessment and input regarding writing and school accommodations. Children who are seen later in their disease may already
have flexion contractures that may require splinting and serial
casting. Some children with marked leg-length discrepancies may
require a shoe lift. Strict limitations from physical education at
school and sports are generally not encouraged because physical
activity plays an important role in rehabilitation and therapy; however, impact sports should be limited in the presence of joint effusions or when there is a concern for joint instability (i.e., C1/C2
atlantoaxial subluxation). A modified physical education program
and sports activities to patients’ own tolerance are recommended
instead. Education is considered the child’s main job, and trying
to keep the child in school is of utmost importance for social and
emotional growth. Children may be inconsistent in their report
of pain; therefore the physical examination becomes important to
understand the true extent of disease. Growth can also be affected
in children with chronic inflammation; therefore one must be
vigilant to watch for children falling off their growth curve as a
sign of uncontrolled disease activity. Growth delay, a major issue
for children with polyarticular JIA and sJIA, does improve with
biologic therapy and good disease control without glucocorticoids (TNF inhibitors and IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors for sJIA).
Adolescents begin to think independently yet rely heavily on peer
influences. This is a crucial time that can be extremely dangerous
with impulsive decisions such as stopping medication or refusing
medical care.

## Conclusion

The modern treatment of JIA has significantly decreased the longterm burden of disease but requires early recognition, diagnosis, aggressive treatment, and ongoing monitoring to extinguish
disease activity. Pediatric rheumatologists have in their favor the
remarkable capacity for childhood growth and development to
allow repair and restoration of function. The advent of new biologic DMARD and targeted synthetic DMARD therapies and
rapid translation of basic research into therapeutic strategies, as
well as collaborative multicenter research networks, should continue to improve the outlook for children with arthritis.
