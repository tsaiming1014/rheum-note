# Clinical Features and Treatment of Gout

NICOLA DALBETH

KEY POINTS

> Gout is a chronic disease of monosodium urate (MSU) crystal deposition.

> Hyperuricemia is the central risk factor for development of gout, and this

> risk is concentration dependent.

> MSU crystals trigger intermittent flares of extremely painful acute

> inflammatory arthritis, typically affecting the big toe, midfoot, and ankle.

> Tophi lead to limitation of joint movement, superimposed infection, joint

> damage, and deformity and are associated with disability and poor

> health-related quality of life.

> MSU crystal identification is the gold standard for gout diagnosis, but a

> clinical diagnostic rule has good accuracy.

> Imaging methods such as ultrasound and dual-energy CT can assist in

> situations of diagnostic uncertainty.

> Gout flares are managed with anti-inflammatory medications such

> as prednisolone, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs),

> colchicine, or anti–interleukin (IL)-1

> β

> therapy (where available).

> The central strategy for effective long-term gout management is

> dissolution of MSU crystals using urate-lowering therapy to achieve

> a serum urate target of less than 6 mg/dL for most patients and

> lower (5 mg/dL) for those with severe disease.

> Gradual dose escalation of urate-lowering therapy and anti-

> inflammatory prophylaxis with low-dose colchicine or other

> anti-inflammatory agents can reduce the risk of gout flares when

> initiating urate-lowering therapy.

> Allopurinol, the first-line urate-lowering therapy for most patients,

> should be started at low dose and dose titrated until the serum urate

> target is achieved.

> In the event that allopurinol is not tolerated or does not lead to

> achievement of the serum urate target, additional options are

> uricosuric agents or febuxostat.

> In patients with severe disease who are unable to achieve serum urate

> target with available oral therapy, intravenous pegloticase every 2

> weeks can improve outcomes. Careful monitoring of the serum urate

> response is required to reduce the risk of infusion reactions.

> The high prevalence of comorbid conditions in people with gout

> necessitates active screening and management of concomitant

> cardiometabolic and kidney disease.

> Dietary advice about the benefits of weight loss for patients with

> overweight/obesity, avoidance of sugary drinks, and reduction of

> alcohol intake for hazardous drinking is important for all people,

> including those with gout.

> Compared with usual primary care, nurse-led care according to

> rheumatology society guidelines leads to greater adherence to

> urate-lowering therapy, lower serum urate concentrations, and

> major improvements in clinical outcomes including gout flares, tophi,

> and health-related quality of care and is cost-effective.

## Clinical Features of Gout

Gout is a chronic disease of monosodium urate (MSU) crystal
deposition. MSU crystals form in the presence of high tissue urate
levels and trigger intermittent flares of acute inflammatory arthritis,
typically affecting the big toe, midfoot, and ankle. In some
patients with hyperuricemia, tophi can also form; these lesions
can lead to joint damage and deformity. Gout flares are managed with anti-inflammatory medications such as nonsteroidal
anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), colchicine, prednisone, or
anti–interleukin (IL)-1
β
therapy. The central strategy for effective
long-term gout management is dissolution of MSU crystals using
urate-lowering therapy (see
Chapter 66

Clinical Risk Factors for Flares in Patients With Gout
After the first flare, approximately one-third of people will experience at least one subsequent flare over the next 4 years.
A
number of factors are associated with gout flares in patients with
established gout; these include higher serum urate levels, acute
medical illness, surgical illness, use of diuretics, intake of purinerich foods or alcohol, dehydration, joint injury or overuse, use of
low-dose aspirin, longer disease duration and presence of comorbidities.
Gout flares are also more common at times of high

ambient temperature and extremes of humidity.
During the initiation period of urate-lowering therapy, gout flares occur more
frequently, particularly if the serum urate is rapidly reduced or if
anti-inflammatory prophylaxis is not co-administered. However,
in the long-term, urate-lowering therapy prevents recurrent gout
flares. Some other factors have been associated with reduced risk
of recurrent gout flares, including lower serum urate levels and
cherry intake.

### Associated Comorbid Conditions

People presenting with gout frequently have concomitant medical
conditions such as chronic kidney disease, obesity, cardiovascular
disease, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, hypertriglyceridemia, kidney stones, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and
heart failure (
Table 97.4
).
Cluster analysis of comorbidities
in a large cohort of patients with gout has identified five clusters (C1 to C5): cluster C1 (12%) includes patients with isolated
gout and few comorbidities; cluster C2 (17%) includes those with
obesity with high prevalence of hypertension; cluster C3 (24%)
includes those with high proportion of type 2 diabetes; cluster C4
(28%) includes those with dyslipidemia; and cluster C5 (18%)
includes those with a history of cardiovascular disease, renal failure, and diuretics.
These comorbidities contribute to higher risk
of poor outcomes for people with gout in the setting of COVID19 infection.
There is ongoing uncertainty about whether hyperuricemia
or gout contributes to the development of cardiometabolic diseases associated with gout and whether pharmacologic lowering
of serum urate levels influences the development or outcomes of
these other conditions. However, in clinical practice, it is important to recognize that people presenting with gout are at high risk
for cardiometabolic conditions and also mortality and to systematically screen and manage risk factors for kidney disease, diabetes,
and cardiovascular disease.

### Clinical Presentations of Gout

Gout typically presents as an acute monoarthritis, although oligoor polyarticular presentations can occur.
The first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra) is a common site for the first presentation
of gout (
Fig. 97.2A
). Other frequently affected sites are the midfoot, ankle, and knee joint. Gout flares have a stereotypic pattern, with rapid onset of maximal pain within 24 hours of onset
and gradual resolution over 1 to 2 weeks.
These episodes often
start during the night.
The cardinal features of inflammation are
typically present, with severe joint pain (“the worst pain ever”),
associated with joint swelling, warmth, erythema, and restricted
movement.
The joint may be exquisitely tender, and activities
such as walking are limited. The overlying skin may desquamate
during the resolution phase. In patients with severe joint inflammation, fever and other systemic symptoms may be present. Gout
flares affecting the axial skeleton, hips, and shoulders can occur
but are uncommon.
Gout flares are typically self-limiting over 1 to 2 weeks.
The
clinical features of joint inflammation completely disappear, and
the patient remains asymptomatic (or back to baseline state)
during this “intercritical period” until the next gout flare occurs.
In the absence of treatment, gout flares occur more frequently
over time, involving more joints including those in the upper
limb, including the olecranon bursa, wrist, and small joints of
the hands.
In the presence of persistent hyperuricemia, tophi and chronic
gouty arthritis may develop. A tophus appears as a “draining or
chalk-like subcutaneous nodule under transparent skin, often
with overlying vascularity, located in typical locations: joints,
ears, olecranon bursae, finger pads, tendons (e.g., Achilles)”
(
Figs. 97.2B and 97.3 to 97.6
). Tophi are usually nontender
but may become acutely inflamed with associated tenderness.
These lesions may cause joint deformity and obstruction to joint
movement.
Foot tophi may lead to difficulty finding shoes.
Participants with tophi experience high frequency of gout flares,
pain, and activity limitation. Intraosseous tophi lead to bone
erosion.
Draining tophi may be complicated by superimposed
infection. Chronic gouty arthritis manifests as nonresolving
synovitis, often in patients with tophi. In the pre–urate-lowering
therapy era, tophaceous gout typically occurred approximately a
decade after presentation of the first gout flare.
Some patients
present with tophi as the first manifestation of disease, and others never develop tophi despite persistently high serum urate levels and recurrent gout flares. Older age, female sex, diuretic use,
and chronic kidney disease are risk factors for presentation with
early tophaceous disease.
Complex clinical scenarios can occasionally present. These
include rapidly progressive “eruptive” tophaceous gout, particularly in the renal transplant setting and in older patients who have
chronic kidney disease and who are on diuretics. Although tophi
are usually found in characteristic sites such as the first metatarsophalangeal joint, Achilles tendon, olecranon bursa, and ears, many
atypical sites have been described including the head and neck;
skin; eye; ear, nose, and throat (ENT) system; cardiac structures;
breast; gut; nerves; and spine.
Compressive neuropathies, particularly carpal tunnel syndrome, have been described.
Although the clinical features of gout have been well described
in the medical literature for centuries, the SUGAR study, a large
international case-control study using microscopically monosodium urate crystal proven disease as the gold standard, has
allowed further quantification of the typical features of gout.

Blood and Urine Testing
Serum urate testing can assist with clinical diagnosis of gout in
symptomatic individuals, but hyperuricemia alone is not sufficient to make a diagnosis of gout, as most people with hyperuricemia do not have gout. The diagnosis of gout is unlikely in a patient
with serum urate levels that are persistently low (less than 6 mg/
dL).
Serum urate may drop acutely into the normal range in up
to 40% during a gout flare,
and if gout remains a possibility,
serum urate should be retested after the episode of inflammatory
arthritis has resolved.
Serum urate also plays an important role in monitoring
treatment with urate-lowering therapy. Both the ACR and the
EULAR
recommend lowering serum urate to levels less than
6 mg/dL (0.36 mmol/L) in people with gout on urate-lowering
therapy, and EULAR recommends a lower target (less than 5 mg/
dL) in those with a large MSU crystal burden (tophi). Serum urate
monitoring can ensure appropriate dose titration of urate-lowering therapy. Periodic measurement of urate also allows assessment
of adherence to urate-lowering therapy for patients who are on
long-term therapy.
The C-reactive protein is usually elevated during a flare and
may measure >100 mg/L in severe gout flares. During intercritical periods, the C-reactive protein is usually within the normal
range. The complete blood count may also show elevated platelet
and neutrophil count. These findings reflect the degree of systemic inflammation rather than the diagnosis of gout. Laboratory
biochemistry tests may also indicate associated comorbidities in
patients with gout, such as chronic kidney disease, dyslipidemia,
and type 2 diabetes.
HLA-B*5801
is a major risk factor for allopurinol hypersensitivity.
HLA-B*5801
is highest in people of Chinese, Thai,
Korean, and African American ethnicity, and screening in these
patients prior to commencing allopurinol is appropriate. In
patients who are

present with gout at a young age or when uricosuric agents are
being considered. Measurement of 24-hour urinary uric acid

excretion (UUE) following a low-purine diet is considered the
gold standard but is cumbersome and has low reproducibility
within individuals. The fractional excretion of urate (FEUA) can
be estimated from a spot urine test and is calculated as the urate
clearance/creatinine clearance ratio ([U
× P

× P
]). Renal underexcretion of uric acid is likely if the

Conventional CT allows excellent visualization of tophi and

≥

### Diagnosis

In a patient presenting with clinical features of gout (acute inflammatory arthritis, chronic synovitis, nodules), the gold standard for
gout diagnosis is microscopic confirmation of MSU crystals. In
the absence of microscopic analysis, the diagnosis of gout requires
synthesis of available clinical and other laboratory information.
The differential diagnosis of the gout flare presentation includes
other causes of acute monoarthritis, the most important of which is
septic arthritis. Other important differential diagnoses are fracture,
other acute crystal induced disease including “pseudogout” due
to calcium pyrophosphate deposition (CPPD) or basic calcium
phosphate arthritis or tendinitis, reactive arthritis, and psoriatic
arthritis (
Fig. 97.9
). Osteoarthritis also affects the first metatarsophalangeal joint but can usually be differentiated from gout by
the degree of joint inflammation and the temporal course of the
disease. In most circumstances, gout can be readily distinguished
from rheumatoid arthritis due to the temporal history and pattern
of joint involvement. The differential diagnosis for tophi includes
other causes of subcutaneous nodules, including rheumatoid nodules, tumoral calcinosis, lipid deposits, or osteophytes (
Fig. 97.10
).

## Gout Management

### Guidelines

Gout management guidelines have been published by many rheumatology societies, including the ACR and the EULAR.
All
of these guidelines include recommendations regarding the key
aspects of gout management, including treatment of gout flares,
urate-lowering therapy (ULT), gout flare prevention with antiinflammatory drugs when commencing ULT, nonpharmacologic
therapies, and management of comorbidities.

EULAR guidelines.

### Gout Flare Treatment

Principles of Gout Flare Management
All patients with a diagnosis of gout should have an action plan
for future flare management, and gout flares should be treated
with anti-inflammatory drugs as soon as possible. Rest, elevation,
hydration, and icing the acutely inflamed joint may also provide
some pain relief.
The main three drug classes used for gout flare
management are corticosteroids, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs, and colchicine. For a monoarticular presentation, intraarticular corticosteroid can be effective. Combinations of antiinflammatory agents may be needed for severe flares, particularly
for polyarticular flares. Clinical trials have indicated similar
efficacy for oral prednisolone, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs, and colchicine but fewer side effects for prednisolone
compared with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs
and for
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs compared with low-dose
colchicine.
The choice of these agents for an individual patient
often depends on medical factors including kidney disease,
diabetes, peptic ulcer disease, and concomitant medications,
including strong P-glycoprotein and/or CYP3A4 inhibitors and
anticoagulants.
In practice, a key consideration when choosing an agent for
gout flare management is patient preference and prior experience of anti-inflammatory drugs for gout flare management.
The duration of anti-inflammatory therapy should be individualized, and anti-inflammatory therapy should be continued until the flare has completely resolved. The IL-1 receptor
antagonist anakinra has similar efficacy to standard gout flare
treatments,
but may also be useful when other anti-inflammatory agents are contraindicated or not tolerated. Once
established, urate-lowering therapy should not be discontinued
at the time of a gout flare.
Corticosteroids
For many patients, particularly those with multiple comorbidities,
corticosteroids may be the most appropriate therapeutic option.
A recent large multicenter randomized controlled trial has confirmed the results of prior smaller trials that oral prednisolone is
as effective as oral NSAIDs for patients presenting to emergency
departments for management of gout flare.
In this study, oral
prednisolone (30 mg daily for 5 days) and indomethacin (50 mg
three times a day for 2 days, then 25 mg three times a day for 3
days) resulted in similar improvements in pain, and there were no
differences between groups in joint redness and tenderness, additional intake of paracetamol, and mean patient satisfaction with
analgesia. Although no serious adverse events were reported in
either group, a greater proportion of patients in the indomethacin
group had minor adverse events in the first 2 hours of treatment
in the emergency department (including dizziness, sleepiness, and
nausea) and over the 2-week period of follow-up after discharge
from the emergency department (including nausea and vomiting).
These findings provide further support for the use of oral corticosteroids for management of gout flares.
Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) acts via melanocortin
type 3 receptor to produce anti-inflammatory effects in gout. In
hospitalized patients with multiple comorbidities where NSAIDs,
colchicine and corticosteroids are contraindicated, a single dose
may be effective.
However, the availability and cost of ACTH
can be barriers to the use of this agent in current clinical practice.
For a detailed discussion of the pharmacology of corticosteroids,
see
Chapter 60
.
Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs
NSAIDs, both nonselective and cyclo-oxygenase-2 (COX-2)
selective, have proven efficacy for treatment of gout flares. There
is no clear evidence that any specific NSAID has greater efficacy
for gout flare management. The selective COX-2 inhibitors are
as effective as traditional NSAIDs but with fewer adverse effects,
particularly gastrointestinal adverse effects.
In general, NSAIDs
should be used as early as possible after development of the flare,
at full dose, and for the shortest possible period. Many patients
with gout are elderly or have concomitant kidney disease and/or
cardiovascular disease, and comorbid conditions may limit the use
of NSAIDs for gout flare treatment. For a detailed discussion of
the pharmacology of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, see
Chapter 59
of this textbook.

A placebo-controlled trial of patients early in
the disease course (serum urate
≥

led to significant reduction in the proportion of patients experiencing gout flares over 2 years.
However, less than half (41%) of
participants in the placebo group experienced a further gout flare
over the 2-year study period. These data can guide discussions and
shared decision-making about long-term urate-lowering therapy
for patients who have a recent gout diagnosis.
All rheumatology society guidelines recommend dose titration
of urate-lowering drugs to achieve serum urate targets. Both the
ACR and EULAR recommend a serum urate target less than 6
mg/dL (0.36 mmol/L) for all patients, with EULAR recommending a lower target less than 5 mg/dL (0.30 mmol/L) for patients
with high burdens of MSU crystals, such as those with tophaceous gout. The goal of long-term urate-lowering therapy is to
achieve MSU crystal dissolution and consequently prevent gout
flares, disability, and joint damage due to gout. Both laboratory
and imaging studies have demonstrated that reducing the serum
urate below these targets leads to dissolution of MSU crystals,
and several clinical trials have also demonstrated the clinical
benefits in flare prevention of long-term urate-lowering using a
treat-to-serum urate target approach.
For tophi, there is
a linear relationship between the velocity of tophus regression
and serum urate concentration, so that lower serum urate targets
are advocated in this clinical situation.
For patients who have
achieved complete dissolution of MSU crystals and have had a
prolonged asymptomatic period, dose reduction or stopping
urate-lowering therapy may be considered, with periodic clinical and laboratory monitoring. A prospective study of patients
has examined outcomes following withdrawal of urate
‐
lowering
therapy after 5 years if no tophus was present at baseline or 5

years after resolution of the last tophus. In this study, which followed patients for a mean duration of 33 months, recurrence of a
gout flare was not observed in those with average follow-up serum
urate of <7 mg/dL; however, recurrent gout flares were observed
in patients with higher serum urate concentrations after stopping
urate-lowering therapy.
Traditionally, it has been recommended that urate-lowering
therapy be initiated after the gout flares have completely resolved
(typically 2 weeks after flare resolution) because of concerns that
urate-lowering therapy initiation may worsen or prolong the gout
flare. Four clinical trials have now reported that initiation of uratelowering therapy (allopurinol or febuxostat) at the time of flare
did not prolong the flare duration or worsen pain scores,
and
the 2020 ACR guidelines conditionally recommended starting
urate-lowering therapy during a gout flare. The EULAR taskforce
did not provide guidance on this issue.
There are three main pharmacologic approaches to urate lowering. First, xanthine oxidase inhibitors such as allopurinol or febuxostat prevent urate production. Second, uricosuric agents such as
probenecid or benzbromarone) inhibit uric acid reabsorption in
the proximal renal tubule and therefore increase renal uric acid
excretion. Probenecid and benzbromarone can be used as monotherapy or coprescribed with a xanthine oxidase inhibitor. Finally,
intravenous recombinant uricases such as pegloticase convert urate
to the soluble metabolite allantoin and are used to treat patients
with severe disease that has not responded to maximum doses of
oral therapies. Pegloticase should not be coprescribed with other
urate-lowering therapies, as these may suppress detection of serum
urate elevations that are a marker of anti-drug antibodies and herald risk for infusion reaction.
For all patients commencing urate-lowering therapy, comorbidities and concomitant medications need to be considered. Prescribers should also be aware of the risk of severe bone marrow
suppression when xanthine oxidase inhibitors such as allopurinol
or febuxostat are coprescribed with azathioprine or 6-mercaptopurine, due to the risk of bone marrow suppression. Uricosuric
agents should be avoided in patients with nephrolithiasis, in those
with severe kidney disease, and in those with urate overproduction. Pegloticase should be avoided in patients with glucose6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency, due to the risk
of hemolysis and methemoglobinemia. The key properties of

Allopurinol
Xanthine oxidase inhibitors, particularly allopurinol, are the most
widely used urate-lowering therapy. Allopurinol is recommended
as the first-line therapy by the EULAR taskforce and the 2020
ACR gout management guidelines. When dose escalated to the
serum urate target, allopurinol is an effective urate-lowering therapy. On average, doses of allopurinol 400 mg daily are needed to
achieve a serum urate of <6 mg/dL.
Allopurinol is generally well tolerated and effective in reducing
serum urate to target when dosed appropriately. However, adverse
events may occur, with a mild rash occurring in 2% of patients prescribed allopurinol. Rarely (in <1/1000 patients), the potentially
life-threatening allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (AHS)
can develop, with rash (including Stevens–Johnson syndrome
or toxic epidermal necrolysis), eosinophilia, fever, hepatitis, and
acute kidney injury.
HLA-B*5801
is a major risk factor for allopurinol hypersensitivity.
Screening of
HLA-B*5801
in high-risk
populations (Chinese, Korean, Thai) is recommended, and use
of alternative urate-lowering drugs in
HLA-B*5801
carriers can
prevent this adverse drug reaction.
Other clinical risk factors
for AHS are recent commencement (within 8 weeks), kidney disease, and diuretic use. For patients without other urate-lowering
therapy options, allopurinol desensitization can be attempted for
patients with mild skin reactions but should not be attempted for
those who have had AHS.
Higher allopurinol doses, particularly in patients with kidney
disease, have been associated with AHS in a number of reports,
and these observations led to guidelines stating that allopurinol
doses should be restricted according to creatinine clearance in
patients with chronic kidney disease.
Widespread adoption of
these recommendations led to inadequate control of serum urate
in the majority of patients prescribed allopurinol.
Subsequent
work has demonstrated that the starting dose of allopurinol is
associated with AHS, and that starting dose should be reduced in
patients with kidney disease, particularly if estimated glomerular
filtration rate (eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m
).
However, once
allopurinol has been tolerated at low dose, the dose can be safely
increased until the serum urate target is achieved. This approach
is effective in achieving serum urate target and is well tolerated.
A general approach is for patients with eGFR
≥
60 mL/min/1.73
m
to start allopurinol 100 mg daily and for the dose to escalate
by 100 mg every month until the serum urate is at target; for
those with eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m
, start allopurinol 50 mg
daily and escalate the dose by 50 mg every month until the serum
urate is at target.
All patients commencing allopurinol should
be advised of the risk of hypersensitivity and to stop allopurinol if
they develop a rash.
Febuxostat
Febuxostat potently and specifically inhibits xanthine oxidase
and is an effective urate-lowering agent in people with gout.
Febuxostat also leads to prevention of gout flares and tophus
regression when prescribed long term.
The original phase 3
trials reported higher frequency of gout flares soon after starting
febuxostat at high doses,
and these initiation flares can be prevented by starting febuxostat at low dose and gradually titrating
the dose.
The most common side effects with febuxostat are
diarrhea, nausea, and deranged liver function tests. For patients
who have had allopurinol hypersensitivity, febuxostat is generally
tolerated, although occasionally patients have developed rash or
hypersensitivity with febuxostat.
The 2018 CARES trial did
report a higher risk of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality in
patients receiving febuxostat, compared with allopurinol.
However, the subsequent FAST trial and VA STOP-GOUT trial did
not confirm this risk, with a trend to lower cardiovascular events
in those treated with febuxostat, compared with allopurinol.
Uricosuric Agents
Alternatively, in the event that allopurinol dose escalation is not
tolerated or does not lead to achievement of the serum urate target, a number of uricosuric agents are available. If a lower dose
of allopurinol is tolerated, allopurinol can be continued at this
dose with the addition of a uricosuric agent, such as probenecid
or benzbromarone (if available). Probenecid and benzbromarone
can also be used as monotherapy.
All patients receiving uricosuric agents should be advised to maintain a high fluid intake
and avoid dehydration. Careful monitoring of liver function tests
is also required for patients treated with benzbromarone, due to
the risk of hepatotoxicity.

### Role of Dietary/Lifestyle Interventions

Dietary advice about benefits of weight loss for patients with
overweight/obesity, avoidance of sugary drinks, and reduction
of alcohol intake for hazardous drinking is important for all
people, including those with gout. Both the ACR and EULAR
guidelines recommend dietary modification as an adjunct to
pharmacologic treatment in patients with gout, but note that
the impact of dietary medications on serum urate is likely to
be small. The 2020 ACR guidelines recommend limiting intake
of alcohol, purine, and high fructose corn syrup and using a
weight loss program if overweight/obese.
Although dietary
strategies have an effect on serum urate and incident gout risk
in the general population, there is very little evidence for people
with established gout that avoidance of specific dietary factors
leads to sustainable or clinically meaningful reductions in serum
urate concentrations.
With the possible exception of bariatric
surgery for patients with severe obesity, dietary/lifestyle modifications alone are generally insufficient to achieve target serum
urate in people with established gout. Therefore lifestyle advice
should be considered an adjunct, rather than an alternative, to
urate-lowering medication.
There have been very few randomized controlled trials investigating the role of dietary interventions for patients with established
gout, and none have shown benefit. A Cochrane review of dietary
supplements, updated in 2021 noted “the absence of high
‐
quality
evidence to either support or refute the value of dietary supplementation in people with gout.”
Low-fat dairy or vitamin C, which
have modest effects on serum urate in the general population, have
not shown clinically meaningful serum urate-lowering or clinical
benefit in people with gout.
In addition, a placebo-randomized controlled trial of comprehensive dietary education in patients
with gout who were treated with urate-lowering therapy did not
observe differences in serum urate concentrations after 6 months.
These findings indicate that for people with gout who are treated
with urate-lowering therapy, additional benefits on serum urate are
unlikely with intensive dietary advice.

## Comorbidity Assessment and Management in Patients With Gout

The high prevalence of comorbid conditions in people with gout
necessitates active screening and management of type 2 diabetes,
cardiovascular disease, hypertension, dyslipidemia, chronic kidney
disease, obesity, and obstructive sleep apnea. Traditional cardiovascular risk factors should be intensively managed in people with
gout, due to high risk of morbidity and mortality due to cardiovascular disease.
Some strategies for comorbidity management may also affect
gout management. Observational studies have shown that very
intensive weight loss with bariatric surgery can lead to clinically
relevant reductions in serum urate in people with gout.
Losartan, fenofibrate, and SGLT2 inhibitors have modest urate-lowering effects and may be preferentially selected to manage relevant
comorbidities in patients with gout.
Although low-dose aspirin can increase serum urate, this therapy should not be withheld
in patients requiring aspirin to reduce cardiovascular risk.
Comorbidities may have a substantial impact on pharmacologic
management of gout. In the context of gout flare management, highdose corticosteroids may lead to substantial disruption in glycemic
and blood pressure control. In patients with severe chronic kidney
disease, NSAIDs are contraindicated, and colchicine requires dose
reduction. NSAIDs should be avoided in patients on anticoagulants for cardiovascular disease. Colchicine has clinical benefits in
coronary artery disease and may be the preferred anti-inflammatory
treatment in people with gout and cardiovascular disease.
In
terms of urate-lowering therapy, allopurinol starting doses should
be reduced in patients with chronic kidney disease, and patients
with chronic kidney disease typically require lower allopurinol doses
to achieve serum urate target.
Patients with chronic kidney disease are less likely to respond to uricosuric agents, and these agents
should be avoided in patients with kidney stones.
A particularly challenging clinical situation is kidney transplantation, due to high prevalence, severity of disease, and potential for drug interactions. Gout occurs in up to a quarter of kidney
transplant recipients.
The calcineurin inhibitors contribute to
hyperuricemia and gout risk and significantly interact with colchicine. Cyclosporine has a greater effect on colchicine concentrations than tacrolimus, due to its strong inhibition of CYP3A4
and P-gp.
NSAIDs are usually contraindicated. The risk of
myelosuppression with azathioprine is substantially increased by
xanthine oxidase inhibitors such as allopurinol and febuxostat.
Switching patients from azathioprine to mycophenolate may be
required in order to optimize urate-lowering therapy and allow
prescription of a xanthine oxidase inhibitor. In patients without
kidney stones, probenecid or benzbromarone can be effective if
xanthine oxidase inhibitor agents are not tolerated.

## Outcome

Patients with recurrent gout flares and tophi have high disability
scores and poor health-related quality of life.
Work productivity is also reduced in patients with severe gout, with an estimated
mean work day loss of 25.1 days per year.
The incremental

direct cost of gout has been estimated to range from US$3165
to US$5515 (2004 and 2005 values, respectively) per annum,
increasing to US$10,222 to US$21,467 (2008 values) per annum
for patients with frequent gout flares or tophi.
Data from the US National Health and Wellness Survey has
shown that people with uncontrolled gout (defined as serum
urate >6 mg/dL or at least one gout flare in the past year) have
higher presenteeism, overall work impairment, activity impairment, and more emergency department visits than those with
controlled gout (defined as serum urate
≤
6 mg/dL and no gout
flares in the past year) or people without gout.
Overall, patients
with uncontrolled gout had both higher indirect and total costs
compared with patients with controlled gout; importantly, there
was no significant difference in total costs between patients with
controlled gout and people without gout.
In patients with gout treated in primary care, physical function and health-related quality of life (HRQOL) are impaired by
gout-specific factors and comorbidities.
Gout-specific factors
include recent gout flare, frequent gout flares, tophi, oligo/polyarticular flares, and body pain. Comorbid conditions that affect
health-related quality of life in people with gout include diabetes mellitus, stroke, renal failure, myocardial infarction, and
obesity. Several clinical trials have shown that urate-lowering
therapy can significantly improve health-related quality of life,
intensive urate-lowering with pegloticase led to improvement
in SF-36 scores over 6 months,
and nurse-led care according
to British Society for Rheumatology (BSR) guidelines led to
improvements in the SF-36 physical function scale and the gout
impact scale.

## Strategies to Improve Gout Care

Despite the widespread availability of effective anti-inflammatory
medications and many urate-lowering therapy options, gout continues to have a large impact on patients, their families, and their
communities, due to painful flares, joint damage, disability, and
loss of productivity. Numerous studies from many different countries have demonstrated low rates of urate-lowering therapy prescription, infrequent serum urate monitoring, suboptimal dosing
of urate-lowering therapy when prescribed, and poor attainment
of serum urate targets in those who do have serum urate testing.
Although urate-lowering therapy may not be required
for those with infrequent flares and low MSU crystal load, many
patients with recurrent flares or other indications for urate-lowering therapy are exposed to the risks of anti-inflammatory medications such as NSAIDs and high-dose corticosteroids without
appropriate prescribing of long-term urate-lowering therapy.
Unfortunately, even when urate-lowering therapy is prescribed,
continuation rates are very low.
Perceptions of gout within the lay community and by health
care professionals contribute to stigma and ineffective management strategies. Consistent with cultural representations about
gout over many centuries, many patients describe that their
friends, family members, and health care professionals view the
disease with humor.
Within contemporary depictions of gout,
diet is widely portrayed as the cause and solution of the illness.
Interactions that promote the belief that gout is a self-inflicted
disease caused by personal indiscretion can lead to embarrassment
and prevent patients seeking medical care.
A key challenge to gout management is establishment and
maintenance of long-term urate-lowering therapy. The reasons for
low rates of urate-lowering therapy initiation and continuation
are likely multifactorial and involve both health care provider
and patient barriers, including beliefs that that gout is an intermittent flaring illness that only requires acute treatment, lack of
knowledge about the role of urate-lowering therapy, perception
of gout as self-inflicted due to personal indiscretion, and systems
barriers to long-term therapy (time constraints, inconvenience,
cost of long-term therapy).
Patients may also intentionally
stop their urate-lowering therapy because they are reluctant to
take a medicine every day or are concerned about potential side
effects.
Education strategies that focus on the biologic
basis of disease, including the physiologic role of the kidney and
gut in regulating serum urate concentrations, chronic deposition
of MSU crystals, and the role of these crystals in triggering gout
flares, provides a clear rationale for long-term urate-lowering
therapy.
Gout treatment, if it occurs at all, usually occurs in primary
care, and new approaches for improved care have been described.
Practice-based quality improvement approaches can lead to better care. A multifaceted primary care gout management improvement intervention has been described that includes engagement
of intervention-site staff, surveys of provider performance
improvement preferences, onsite live and enduring online education, electronic health record reminders, and monthly reports
to providers comparing their measures against their peers.
After 6 months, this intervention significantly improved gout
quality performance indicators, including the percentage of
patients with gout treated with urate-lowering therapy from
54.4% to 61.1%, the percentage with serum urate monitored
from 56.1% to 79.2%, and the percentage at serum urate target
increased from 26.7% to 43.3%. The numbers treated did not
significantly improve at a control site, and all quality measures
were higher with the intervention compared with the control site
after 6 months.
Primary care models of care that include nurses or pharmacists
have been described.
A large randomized controlled trial
within primary care has demonstrated that nurse-led care according to British Society for Rheumatology guidelines leads to major
improvements in outcomes for patients with gout.
In this study,
nurses provided patients with holistic assessment, discussion of
illness perceptions, and full information on gout (nature, causes,
associations, consequences, and treatment options) and encouraged them to share in decision making. Follow-up assessments and
measurement of serum urate concentrations were done as often as
required by the nurse. Telephone contact (e.g., to review serum
urate results) could be substituted for face-to-face visits, and home
visits were permitted (e.g., for older patients). If the nurses had
questions about gout management, they could seek advice from a
rheumatologist. Compared with usual general practice care, nurseled care was associated with high uptake of and adherence to uratelowering therapy in more than 95% of study participants. More
patients receiving nurse-led care had serum urate concentrations
less than 6 mg/dL at 2 years than those receiving the usual care
(95% vs 30%). At 2 years, greater improvements in gout flares,
tophi, physical function, and the patient-reported gout impact
questionnaires were observed with nurse-led care. The nurse-led
intervention was cost-effective in the short-term and potentially
cost-saving in the long-term. This model represents a major
advance in improved care for the increasing numbers of people
with gout; models such as these should be considered for adoption
around the world.
