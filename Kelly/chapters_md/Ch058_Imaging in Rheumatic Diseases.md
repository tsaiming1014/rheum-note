## Introduction

Imaging in rheumatology was for decades synonymous with
conventional radiography (radiography, x-ray). However, imaging modalities such as MRI, ultrasonography, and new nuclear
medicine techniques have dramatically increased the amount and
scope of information obtainable by imaging. Imaging is useful for
a wide variety of purposes in rheumatic diseases. These include
establishing or confirming a diagnosis, determining the extent of
disease, monitoring change in disease (e.g., activity and structural
damage), selecting patients for specific therapies (e.g., surgery or
injections), identifying complications of disease or treatment,

## Radiography

The first roentgen ray image was a radiograph of the hand. Since
then, imaging of musculoskeletal structures has always been an
important role of radiography (x-rays).
The simple radiograph
is relatively cheap, is available worldwide, and produces an image
that is almost identical regardless of technical parameters or
whether the image is analogue or digital. The reliability of the
image means that, despite the limitations of radiography, advances
in clinical practice can rely on the “good old x-ray,” knowing that
it is largely independent of technologic advance. For many years
to come, radiographs will appear much the same.

### Technical Aspects

Radiography is a two-dimensional summation and projection
image that is dependent on variable absorption of x-rays by different tissues for its inherent contrast, depending on their physical
and electron density. It has a very high spatial resolution that is
rarely surpassed by other modalities, but radiography only offers
high contrast between a limited number of structures—bone (calcium), soft tissue, and air. Fat is visible as a separate density, but
the distinction between soft tissue and fat is often subtle, and radiography cannot distinguish between the other soft tissues because
cartilage, muscle, tendon, ligament, synovium, and fluid all appear
to be the same density. These characteristics give radiography its
inherent advantages and disadvantages.
In its favor, the radiography shows skeletal structure very well,
and because it is a summation image, it allows excellent overall
assessment of skeletal trauma and alignment. The limited number
of images produced facilitates rapid review and the high bone–
soft-tissue contrast often produces radiographic manifestations of
disease in specific patterns that make the test particularly useful in
daily clinical practice. The biggest disadvantage of radiography is
its inherent lack of soft-tissue contrast, making it insensitive for
the detection of soft-tissue abnormalities. The first radiographic
sign of inflammatory arthritis may be permanent structural damage (bony erosion), and in degenerating joints, cartilage damage is not usually visualized until sufficient overall cartilage loss
allows approximation of the bone ends, resulting in “joint space
narrowing.”
Although radiographs use ionizing radiation, they should be
considered relatively safe, especially in older patients. However,
exposure differs depending on the investigated volume and susceptibility to radiation of nearby organs. Therefore a radiograph of
the pelvis or lumbar spine will result in considerably more exposure compared with radiography of the hands. In most examinations, two or more projections are required to visualize the joint
in question adequately, and radiographic quality is improved by
strict adherence to standard imaging protocols.
Digital radiography has opened the field for a variety of technical developments. It has resulted in a significant reduction of radiation exposure compared with conventional film-based systems
that is now further reduced by the application of artificial intelligence algorithms, all of which is not visible to the average user.
New biplanar imaging machines allow further reduction of radiation and three-dimensional (3D) assessments in upright imaging without the projection and distortion effects of conventional
radiographs and are especially suited for the axial skeleton and
lower limbs.
Digital tomosynthesis acquisitions can reconstruct
a stack of thin slice digital tomographic images from a single
tomosynthesis exposure with only slight increase in radiation dose
compared with a digital radiograph, thus allowing the detection
of more subtle radiographic abnormalities that may otherwise be
obscured by the complex anatomy of overlying structures. Tomosynthesis is more accurate for detecting bone erosion than radiography and has similar sensitivity to computed tomography (CT)
and MRI for detection of erosion in the hands and feet of patients
with RA.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

RA is the archetypal inflammatory joint disease and primarily targets synovium and peripheral joints. It is a systemic inflammatory
disorder in which the typical clinical manifestations are usually
symmetric, and so the radiographic signs usually follow this pattern. As in all of radiology, the observed distribution of disease
is often characteristic of the underlying cause, and in RA, the
metacarpophalangeal (MCP) and proximal interphalangeal (PIP)
joints of the hands and the wrists and the metatarsophalangeal
(MTP) joints of the feet are most often involved.
Juxta-articular osteopenia is a characteristic feature of RA best
seen in small peripheral joints. Later, generalized osteoporosis
is usually present and is exacerbated by disuse. Erosion of bone
(

) occurs later and usually appears first at the margins of
the joint where the proliferating synovium lies directly on the surface of the bone between the edge of the cartilage-covered articular surface and the capsular attachment—the “bare areas.” These
marginal erosions may be subtle and first appear as disruption of
the thin white cortical line, especially at the radial aspect of the
metacarpal heads. Dedicated projections, such as the ball catcher’s
view, can increase the sensitivity for erosions at hands and feet.
However, given its overall low sensitivity and the low prevalence
of erosion in early disease, usually only one (anterior–posterior)
projection is favored. In large joints, synovial proliferation may
be very severe before bony erosion is detectable on radiography.
This is especially notable in the knee where pain and swelling due
to arthritis and bursitis occur long before erosion. In addition to
bare-area erosions, two other types of erosions have been described
in RA. Compressive erosion refers to remodeling of inflamed and
osteoporotic bone with gradual invagination of one bone into
another typified by protrusio acetabuli of the hip (Fig. 58.1E).
Surface erosion of bone may also be seen, usually resulting from
inflammation of an adjacent tendon sheath, and a typical location
for this is at the outer margin of the ulnar styloid process secondary to extensor carpi ulnaris tenosynovitis.
Joint space involvement is characteristic in RA.
The inflammatory processes can result in progressive destruction of articular
cartilage, which in turn causes the radiographic finding of concentric joint space narrowing. This is usually diffuse in RA as all the
cartilage is involved at the same time, and this feature may allow
differentiation from the focal or asymmetric type of joint space
loss that occurs with degenerative disorders. Occasionally cartilage destruction precedes synovial erosion of bone. Continued
cartilage damage may result in partial or complete fibrous ankylosis, but progression to bony ankylosis is uncommon, although it
may occur in end-stage disease in the wrist or midfoot. Subchondral radiolucent areas are common in RA and are often referred
to as cysts, geodes, or pseudocysts. They may develop as a result

### Axial Spondyloarthritis

Axial spondyloarthritis
is an umbrella term that includes AS. It is an
inflammatory joint disease that primarily targets fibrocartilage and
the axial skeleton. Cartilaginous joints and sites of enthesis (tendon and ligament insertion) are involved early, and the characteristic involvement of the axial skeletal includes a predilection for the
sacroiliac joints and all the articulations and entheses of the spine.
Sacroiliac Joints
Erosion and ankylosis of the sacroiliac joints are the hallmarks of
spondyloarthritis.
Sacroiliitis is usually the first manifestation and
is characteristically bilateral and symmetric in AS.
Early radiographic findings predominate on the iliac side of the cartilage compartment with erosion of subchondral bone, causing loss of definition
of the articular surfaces, usually accompanied by variable degrees of
adjacent osteoporosis and surrounding reactive sclerosis. Bone erosion may result in the radiographic observation of focal joint space
widening (
Fig. 58.3A
), and as the disease progresses, definition of
the joint is completely lost with radiographic superimposition of erosion, sclerosis, and new bone formation, which fills in the erosions
and the original cartilaginous “joint space.” The joint may disappear
completely in late disease with ankylosis and remodeling of the bone
(Fig. 58.3B). The ligamentous compartment of the sacroiliac joint
is frequently affected by bony erosion and entheseal proliferation,
although these may be hard to see radiographically.
Spine
Traditional descriptions of the initial sites of spinal involvement
are enormously influenced by the inability of radiography to adequately visualize many parts of the spine. Radiographic reports
indicate that the lumbosacral and thoracolumbar junctions are
first affected, whereas the MRI literature clearly indicates a predilection for early involvement of the midthoracic spine, an area
that is extremely difficult to evaluate radiographically. The cervical spine is rarely affected first, but this can occur occasionally in
women, and spinal disease rarely occurs in the absence of significant sacroiliac joint involvement.
The early radiographic manifestations of AS in the spine are
most often due to enthesitis at the corners of the discovertebral
joints.
Focal sclerosis (a “shiny corner”) and erosion (a “Romanus lesion”) develop at the attachment of the annulus fibrosis to
the anterior corner of the vertebral endplate and are characteristic features of early AS (
Fig. 58.4A
).
The anterior borders of
vertebrae may appear straight or “squared” due to periosteal proliferation of new bone filling in the normal concavity or erosion
at the anterosuperior and anteroinferior vertebral margins. This
observation is much easier to make in the lumbar spine, where
normal vertebrae are always concave anteriorly in comparison to
the thoracic and cervical spine, where the normal contour is much
more variable and may be square or occasionally convex.
The hallmark of spinal disease in AS is the development of characteristic bony spurs, syndesmophytes (Fig. 58.3C). These start as
thin, vertically oriented projections of bone that develop due to
ossification within the outer fibers of the annulus fibrosus of the
intervertebral disk. Syndesmophytes are radiographically visible
on the anterior and lateral aspects of the spine starting from the
corner of the vertebra. Progressive growth will bridge the intervertebral disk causing ankylosis, and extensive syndesmophyte formation produces a smooth, undulating spinal contour, known as
the
bamboo spine.

both sides of the joint, erosions, and loss of joint space) or grade

These definitions of radiographic changes have been subsequently included in
the Assessment of Spondyloarthritis International Society (ASAS)
classification criteria for axial spondyloarthritis

but the pattern of joint involvement varies between patients and
over time. Radiographic appearances are usually asymmetric and
may have a ray distribution with the affected joints involving

Use in Diagnosis, Monitoring, and Prognosis
While RA is characterized by mainly osteo-destructive lesions, in
PsA there are both osteo-destructive and osteo-proliferative manifestations, which may even coexist not only in the same patient but
also in the same joint.
In particular juxta-articular new bone formation in radiography of hands and feet appearing as ill-defined
ossification (but excluding osteophytes) is characteristic and is
included in the new classification criteria (ClASsification criteria for
Psoriatic ARthritis [CASPAR]) for PsA.
Structural joint damage on conventional radiography is an
important outcome measure in PsA. Different radiographic scoring methods of peripheral joints have been developed, for example, the Sharp–van der Heijde modified scoring method for PsA,
which is a detailed scoring system for evaluating erosions and joint
space narrowing, while osteolysis and pencil-in-cup phenomena
are assessed separately.
Scoring systems are primarily used in
clinical trials. No specific scoring systems for spine and sacroiliac
joints in PsA exist, but axial involvement in PsA can be monitored
as in AS (as discussed previously).

### Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis

JIA is the most common rheumatic disease of childhood,
with
seven subtypes defined by the International League of Associations
for Rheumatology (ILAR)
representing a heterogeneous set of
chronic arthropathies with overlapping genetic predispositions.
JIA was previously misleadingly known as juvenile rheumatoid
arthritis (JRA). The pattern of involvement of the polyarticular
rheumatoid factor–positive subtype does bear some resemblance
to RA, but other subtypes do not. JIA can involve just one joint
(oligoarticular), multiple joints (polyarticular), primarily the axial
skeleton such as spine and sacroiliac joints (enthesitis-related or
undifferentiated subtype), and other organs including the skin
(psoriatic arthritis), serosa, spleen, and lymphatic tissue (systemic
subtype).
The exact imaging features of arthropathy seen at a
particular joint in JIA are less important than a broader overview
of the distribution of joint involvement in the body over time and
patterns of extra-articular involvement. Particularly because many
articular structures are not yet ossified in children, radiographs
may appear normal until late in the disease course.
Rather than
the erosions seen in adults, in children many radiographic changes
relate to chronic hyperemia from articular inflammation, such as
periarticular bony overgrowth seen as widening or squaring of
structures such as tibial spines or carpal bones (
Fig. 58.6
).
Use in Diagnosis, Monitoring, and Prognosis
Radiographic abnormalities indicate that structural damage has

CT has been available for 50 years, and each decade has brought
further development in computing and engineering that has
resulted in remarkable changes in the technology. Although still
limited in soft-tissue contrast, CT offers fast and reliable acquisition, high resolution, and multiplanar capabilities that have
enhanced its use in recent years. Modern techniques like dualenergy CT or photon-counting CT allow for improved tissue
characterization. The modern CT scanner is a remarkable tool
that will likely have an increasingly important role in arthritis
imaging in the future.

### Technical Aspects

CT is a radiography-based topographic technique and therefore shares many of the properties of radiography, including the
low soft-tissue contrast. However, its cross-sectional multiplanar reconstructions improve the sensitivity for most findings,
whereas signs that rely on summation and projection, such as
juxta-articular osteopenia, are harder to appreciate. Despite the
many advantages, including the high spatial resolution (usually
higher than MRI) and unsurpassed tissue-to-bone contrast, CT is
only randomly used in the assessment of most rheumatic diseases,
a fact partly attributable to the fear of high radiation exposure.
However, recent developments in detector technology and new
reconstruction techniques make ultralow-dose scanning with
similar or less radiation compared with radiography and sufficient image quality in high-contrast areas (e.g., bone to soft tissues) feasible.
Another development is spectral CT imaging, which can characterize and quantify tissues beyond the classical CT density.
This is useful for various applications, including dose reduction,
contrast media, metal-artefact reduction, crystal depositions,
and bone marrow imaging, whereas the latter was previously
reserved for MRI. There are many different approaches to performing these kinds of examinations with different advantages

and disadvantages, the most modern being photon-counting CT.
Although not entirely correct, this chapter will summarize these
applications under the term
dual-energy CT
(DECT), which is
technically only one method of gathering spectral CT information, but the term
DECT
is most often used in recent literature. Of note, most DECT applications are incompatible with
ultralow-dose scanning. Therefore the additional information
gathered with this technique will usually come at the expense of
increased radiation exposure.
CT, in contrast to MRI, has no absolute contraindications and
is often performed when MRI is not feasible. It produces thinsliced images with an isotropic spatial resolution of 0.5 mm and
below that can be reconstructed in every orientation or threedimensionally. CT scans usually consume only a few minutes
of scan time—one of the reasons why patients often prefer CT
over MRI. CT machines are more readily available than MR, and
many hospitals perform examinations also during the night and at
weekends in an emergency setting.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

The exquisite inherent contrast between bone and soft tissue that is
afforded by CT makes it a gold standard reference for the detection of
bone erosion, and as such CT is ideally suited to the investigation of
erosion in inflammatory arthritides. Modern CT with three-dimensional visualization allows accurate detection and quantification of
bone erosion with good intraobserver agreement, whereas radiography is limited by its two-dimensional projection and superimposition of structures.
Although recent publications show that CT,
when using contrast media and DECT or subtraction techniques,
can demonstrate soft-tissue inflammation, evidence so far has not
established CT as an alternative imaging method to ultrasound or
MRI for the detection of synovitis.
DECT also allows the virtual
subtraction of calcium from the image and, thereby, differentiation
of normal fatty bone marrow and pathology. This specific postprocessing can make bone marrow edema visible in CT scans and has
been demonstrated in RA.
Use in Diagnosis, Monitoring, and Prognosis
CT is not currently used in routine clinical practice for the diagnosis of RA. However, it could be potentially useful for diagnosis
as CT appears to be the most sensitive technique for detection of
erosion,
and CT of the extremities is simple with a very low
radiation dose. CT is used for problem solving in specific cases,
when MRI is not available or for ruling out certain differential
diagnoses such as crystal arthropathy.
The use of CT for longitudinal assessment of damage progression has potential merit.
No CT data are available for prognosis in RA. The current use of CT in RA is very limited, but
it has detected erosion reliably,
scoring systems are in development,
and CT can detect and quantify repair of erosion.

### Axial Spondyloarthritis

CT allows visualization of the same pathologic processes as conventional radiography—erosion, osteoporosis/sclerosis, and new
bone formation/ankylosis—with the added benefit of multiplanar imaging free from superimposition of overlying structures

best depicted by CT. Considering a recent shift in focus from
osteitis as the main finding of the disease toward a combination
of active and structural lesions, CT might become a valuable
tool when MRI is not available or when findings are equivocal.
Given its high spatial resolution, it can detect structural lesions of
the small joints in the spine (i.e., facet and costo-vertebral joints),
usually not visible with thick MR slices.

and more recently MRI but not CT specifically. In the spine, CT

Although

### Septic Arthritis

CT is rarely used for the investigation of septic arthritis. Acute
septic arthritis of large joints is a surgical emergency, and while
radiography, fluoroscopy, and ultrasonography may be helpful for
either diagnosis or image-guided aspiration, there is no use for CT
in the peripheral skeleton. In chronic septic arthritis and infectious discitis, CT may be used to investigate complications, particularly osteomyelitis. However, MRI or a variety of scintigraphic
studies are generally preferred.
In complex cases in which MRI
scanning is problematic, contrast-enhanced CT can be beneficial
to detect abscess formation in deep soft tissues that are not easily
assessed by ultrasonography, and CT guidance may be preferred
for spine biopsy.

### Osteoarthritis

Even though CT can detect bone changes such as osteophytes
more accurately than radiography, overall it offers little additional

diagnostic utility in peripheral joints. Hyaline cartilage is not
accessible with standard CT techniques, because low soft-tissue
contrast and weight-bearing positioning is not possible with
standard scanners.
It may be used in specific problem-solving
situations where anatomy is complex and/or poorly visualized on
radiography, such as in the lumbar spine facet joints.

MRI provides multiplanar tomographical imaging with unprecedented soft-tissue contrast, without the use of ionizing radiation,
and allows assessment of all the structures involved in musculoskeletal diseases. MRI is more sensitive than clinical examination
and radiography for detection of inflammation and damage in
inflammatory and degenerative rheumatologic disorders in adults
and children. Disadvantages of MRI include higher costs and
lower availability than radiography, longer examination times,

restriction to a limited anatomic area per session. It should be
remembered, however, that the costs of MRI are only a small fraction of the cost of biologic treatment or of the indirect costs of
sick leaves and early retirements. After a section on key technical

### Technical Aspects

T1-weighted (T1w) imaging sequences are favored by offering
relatively short imaging times and good anatomic detail. Further)
more, after intravenous contrast (paramagnetic gadolinium [Gd]
compounds) injection, these sequences visualize tissues with high
perfusion and permeability, including the inflamed synovium.
Fat- and Gd-enhanced tissues have a high signal intensity on T1w
images (
Fig. 58.15
). T2-weighted (T2w) images depict both fat
and fluid/edematous tissues with a high signal intensity. T2w
images are used together with T1w images in degenerative spine
disease. In inflammatory conditions, T2w images with fat saturation (FS) (
Fig. 58.16
) are particularly useful, because the signal
from fat is suppressed in such sequences, allowing detection of
edematous tissue/fluid located in areas with fatty tissue, for example, bone marrow edema. The short tau inversion recovery (STIR)
technique, another fat-suppressed sequence based on relaxation
time differences, is robust to artefacts and can provide information
on bone marrow edema,
although with less detail.
Whereas MRI examination of axial joints requires wholebody MRI units, MRI of the peripheral joints can be performed
with whole-body MRI units or dedicated extremity MRI units
(E-MRIs). E-MRIs increase the potential for widespread rheumatologic use and markedly lowers costs, allow more comfortable
patient positioning, and eliminate claustrophobia
but has been

Diagnosis.

### Psoriatic Arthritis

The clinical appearance of PsA is very diverse, involving the spine,
sacroiliac joints, peripheral joints, and/or entheses, and accordingly MRI findings vary. PsA shares clinical manifestations with
RA and SpA, and this also applies to its MRI features.
Peripheral PsA synovitis and erosions do not have disease-specific
MRI features, and MRI bone edema can involve any bone. A general agreement on which joints to image to assess PsA activity and
damage is not established and possibly needs to be individualized, based on the disease pattern. Peripheral enthesitis is common in PsA and can be visualized and assessed by MRI.
Whole-body MRI, allowing imaging of both peripheral and axial
joints and entheses of the entire body in one examination, may be
a future solution to this, at least in clinical trials, but still requires
more validation before its use in clinical practice.
MRI can visualize both peripheral and axial musculoskeletal
anatomy and PsA disease manifestations. Findings include synovitis, tenosynovitis, periarticular inflammation, enthesitis, bone
edema, bone erosion, and bone proliferation (
Fig. 58.24
).
As in other types of SpA, dactylitis and spondylitis can be
seen.
Dactylitis has shown on MRI to be a result of
tenosynovitis with effusion, sometimes associated with diffuse
soft-tissue edema and/or synovitis in nearby finger or toe joints
(Fig. 58.24).
There are only a few MRI studies in axial PsA,
discovering no findings specific for PsA while mostly reporting
findings similar to axial SpA findings, although more frequently
asymmetric and more frequently solely in the spine, compared
with what is seen in axial SpA.
More studies are needed,
aiming for an MRI definition of axial PsA.
The entheses have attracted attention as a possible primary
location of disease.
Nail disease is common in PsA, and DIP
joint inflammation on MRI has been described to extend to the
nail bed.
PsA can be clinically silent. In patients with psoriasis without
arthritic signs or symptoms, pathologic findings on MRI (including periarticular edema, tendon sheath effusion, intra-articular
effusion, synovial pannus, bone erosion, bone cysts, subchondral
changes, and joint subluxation) have been reported in more than
two-thirds (68% to 92%) versus none to one-twelfth or less of
healthy controls.
The clinical importance of these findings
is not yet clarified.

### Septic Arthritis

The MRI appearance of septic arthritis is nonspecific, and similar
findings can be observed in other inflammatory arthritides. Thus
in case of clinical suspicion of septic arthritis, a joint aspiration
must be performed without delay to avoid irreversible articular
damage. Characteristic findings are synovitis, joint effusion, and
soft-tissue edema (see Fig. 58.9) followed by bone erosion and
cartilage destruction.
MRI may be helpful in diagnosing
complications of septic arthritis, such as abscesses and osteomyelitis.
It should be remembered that most of the individual
findings on MRI are not specific, and BME alone does not necessarily denote osteomyelitis.
Contrast-enhanced MRI is particularly useful to identify abscess/necrosis in either soft tissue or bone
that may confirm the diagnosis and/or requirement for surgical
debridement. Investigation of the painful diabetic foot is a specific
clinical presentation for which MRI may now be routinely performed to help distinguish between infection and trauma and to
identify nonviable tissues, although scintigraphic studies also play
an important role in these complex patients.

### Single-Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT) and SPECT/CT

SPECT uses the principle of tomography to generate cross-sectional
images from multiple planar images. With the introduction of the
SPECT technique, 3D imaging with improved contrast and spatial
resolution became possible. Tomographic bone scintigraphy imaging
provides improved sensitivity for the detection pathologies such as
active facet arthritis or spondylolysis.
However, precise anatomic
location of the active pathology seen in conventional SPECT images
(i.e., without concurrent CT) can be very difficult. This reduces its
specificity. For example, facet arthritis versus active spondylolysis or
other pathology in the posterior elements of a vertebral segment may
be difficult to distinguish on SPECT imaging alone.
SPECT/CT is an example of modern “fusion imaging.”
SPECT/CT is the acquisition of a CT study concurrent with the
acquisition of a nuclear medicine SPECT study, while the patient
remains in the same body position. This is performed by a single

## Conclusion

Imaging is an integral part of management of patients with rheumatic diseases. This chapter has outlined the status of imaging in
rheumatic diseases, with a particular focus on inflammatory joint
diseases. Please see the key point boxes for the main messages. The
chapter has explained the important role of conventional radiography and also the exciting opportunities gained with newer
imaging techniques. A vast amount of new knowledge has been
acquired over the last decade that has dramatically changed the
way our patients with rheumatic diseases are managed. It is exciting that with continued dedicated research and rapid technical
development, it is likely that continuous large improvements may
also occur in the decade to come, for the benefit of our patients.
