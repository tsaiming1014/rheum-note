## Introduction

Mass-forming lesions are often involved in synovial-lined joints
and periarticular structures. Arthrosis, arthritis, trauma, crystal
deposits, and structural derangements produce the most common lesions, which are nonneoplastic and include ganglion cysts,
synovial cysts, tophaceous gout, and loose bodies. In contrast, it
is uncommon for neoplasms, whether benign or malignant, to
develop in joints and periarticular structures.
Joint neoplasms can be divided into tumors that are primary or
arise de novo within the synovium and tumors that are secondary
and access the joint by invading from neighboring bones and soft
tissues or by spreading from distant sites via the vascular system.
Primary joint neoplasms are more common and tend to recapitulate the phenotype of tissues that normally compose the joint and
synovial sheath—synovium, fat, blood vessels, fibrous tissue, and
cartilage. Regardless of the histologic type, benign variants greatly
outnumber their malignant counterparts and, as a group, these
tumors more frequently develop in the synovium in comparison
with the other periarticular structures.
Tumors and tumor-like lesions of the joints and periarticular
structures are biologically and morphologically diverse. They often
pose significant challenges in diagnosis and treatment, and their
clinicopathologic features are the focus of this chapter.

## Non-neoplastic Lesions

### Synovial and Ganglion Cysts

Cysts are defined as closed compartments or sacs that are lined by
epithelium and are frequently filled with fluid. Neither the synovial cyst nor the ganglion cyst is considered a true cyst because
each lacks an epithelial lining.
Synovial cysts are common and develop from the synovial lining of a joint, tendon, or bursa. They are nonneoplastic lesions
and are caused by herniation of the synovium through the joint
capsule or tendon sheath into neighboring tissues or by expansion
of a pre-existing bursa. In adults, synovial cysts frequently develop
in association with a variety of joint disorders, including trauma,
osteoarthrosis (OA), crystal arthropathies, infection, and rheumatoid arthritis (RA) or one of its variants. Most synovial cysts have
an anatomic attachment to a joint, and most originate in the posterior medial aspect of the knees of adults, where they are known
as popliteal or Baker’s cysts. Anatomically, this region of the knee
is prone to the development of synovial cysts because the joint
capsule can be structurally insufficient and in this age group there
is co-existing joint disease.
Synovial cysts of the posterior knee
joint are purported to affect 2.4% of children, who, in contrast

to adults, are usually asymptomatic and have an otherwise normal knee joint.
Other joints affected by synovial cysts in decreasing order of frequency are the shoulder, hip, and spine. In the
spine they originate from the facet joints and are most commonly
located in the lower lumbar region.
Synovial cysts can enlarge over time as they become further
distended with increasing amounts of synovial fluid.
Consequently, they may manifest as a periarticular mass, produce
progressive joint pain and swelling, limit joint mobility, and
compress adjacent neurovascular structures. An example of
compression occurs in the spine, where synovial cysts that arise
from facet joints may impinge or extend along spinal nerves,
causing radicular pain.
Other complications of synovial
cysts, which sometimes can produce dramatic clinical findings,
are rupture with extravasation of synovial fluid and secondary
infection.
A variety of radiographic techniques have been used to image
synovial cysts. Modalities that provide the greatest diagnostic
information include arthrography, ultrasonography, computed
tomography (CT), and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).
All of these techniques reveal synovial cysts to be simple or septated, thin-walled structures that are filled with fluid that has a
density similar to that of water (

They are

Occasionally, they

been shown to develop from dissection of joint fluid along an

### Loose Bodies

Loose bodies
and
joint mice
are generic terms for free-floating structures within a joint cavity. They are the most common tumor-like
lesions of joints and may be exogenous, such as fragments of a
bullet, or endogenous, such as pieces of articular cartilage, osteophytes, menisci, ligaments, or bone.
When not otherwise
specified, the term
loose bodies
refers to detached pieces of articular cartilage or subchondral bone (osteoarticular loose bodies) or
both that lie free within the joint or that have become secondarily
embedded in the synovium. Loose bodies can cause pain, crepitance, and locking, and they can limit the range of joint motion.
Osteoarticular loose bodies are a secondary complication of
a variety of conditions, including trauma, osteochondritis dissecans, and arthritides of various causes. When dislodged, the
sloughed articular cartilage remains viable because it receives its
nourishment from the synovial fluid, but the subchondral bone
dies because it derives its nutrition solely from blood vessels.
Over time, as the loose body tumbles within the joint, its edges
become rounded and smooth; however, it eventually becomes
embedded within the synovium. Subsequently, the synovium
may digest and resorb it, or adjacent subsynovial connective tissue
cells may undergo a proliferative and metaplastic response. These
cells produce layers of newly formed fibrocartilage and hyaline
cartilage, which may undergo endochondral ossification and are

### Intra-articular Ossicles

Small bony nodules normally occur in the knees of some rodents
and other mammals, and they rarely occur in humans.
In
rodents, small bony nodules are consistently found in the anterior portions of the joint and frequently in the posterior portions
as well. In humans, nodules develop within the substance of the
meniscus of the knee joint adjacent to its attachments to the tibia.
The exact origin of such structures is unknown, although they
probably are true sesamoid bones, as seen in rodents. Alternatively, they may represent ossification secondary to local injury.
This latter possibility is supported by the fact that previous knee
trauma has been noted in numerous reported cases. The main
symptom of meniscal ossicles is pain after exertion, such as walking or prolonged standing, with relief when the knee is at rest.

### Pseudotumors and Tumors in the Setting of Total Joint Arthroplasty

Joint arthroplasty is among the most common, cost-effective, and
beneficial elective orthopedic procedures. While complications are
infrequent, some degree of prosthetic loosening invariably occurs
over time and with increased use. Loosening may be due to infection, wear debris reaction, delayed hypersensitivity reaction, or,
rarely, the development of an associated sarcoma.
A
pseudotumor
is one such category of complications, defined as a solid or
cystic mass found in continuity with a prosthetic joint and not
attributable to infection, malignancy, bursa, or scar tissue. The
true incidence is unknown, but reported incidences range from
approximately 1% to 10% or higher in populations undergoing
systematic screening protocols.
Pseudotumors occur in the setting of a range of articulations, including metal-on-metal, metalon-polyethylene, and metal-on-ceramic.

Examination of tissue removed at the time of pseudotumor
removal or revision arthroplasty serves to characterize the disease
process and (1) document the presence or absence of infection,
(2) identify the spectrum of wear debris reactions, and (3) assess
for histologic patterns associated with immunologic hypersensitivity reactions. As prostheses undergo wear and corrosion over
time, fragmentation of their components leads to the microscopic
and submicroscopic components being released into the periarticular tissues and phagocytized by synoviocytes and macrophages.
This progressive accumulation of macrophages and particulate
debris transforms the connective tissue sheath into an expanded
synovial-like structure, also described under the terminology
detritic synovitis
. The different types of prosthesis-derived foreign material can be recognized by light microscopy and include
barium-impregnated poly methyl methacrylate (bone cement),
polyethylene fibers, and metal particles (
Fig. 129.16
).
A very
rare but nonetheless well-recognized potential late complication
of joint implant is the development of an associated sarcoma. The
patients are typically older adults with a mean latency of 6 to 11
years after arthroplasty. Histologically the sarcomas are high in

## Neoplasms

### Fatty Lesions of the Synovium

Although the subsynovial connective tissue of diarthrodial joints
is rich in fat, a true lipoma of the synovium is rare. When lipomas develop, they most frequently affect the knee joint and the
synovial tendon sheaths of the hands, ankles, and feet, where
they are more common in the extensor than in the flexor synovial
sheaths.
Synovial lipomas can be sessile or pedunculated and,
when pedunculated, they may produce pain if they twist on their
stalks and become secondarily ischemic. Synovial lipoma, similar
to its subcutaneous counterpart, is composed of lobules of mature
white adipocytes that are delineated by a thin fibrous capsule (
Fig.
129.17
).
A more common but still unusual fatty lesion of the joint is
lipoma arborescens, also known as
villous lipomatous proliferation
and
lipomatosis of the synovium.
This disorder is characterized by
a diffuse increase in the quantity of subsynovial fat, which bulges
into the overlying synovial lining, producing a villous architecture.
It is uncertain whether the proliferating fat is neoplastic (lipomatosis) or is a manifestation of a hyperplastic or reactive process.
Most cases occur in the context of degenerative or inflammatory
arthritis, supporting the idea that the fatty proliferation may represent a secondary reactive process in most instances.
Affected
patients are usually adults, but the lesion sometimes develops in
adolescents and rarely in children.
In younger patients, lipoma
arborescens may occur in the absence of an identifiable etiologic
agent and affect joints other than the knee, raising the possibility
of a distinct primary form of the disease.
Lipoma arborescens
causes chronic effusions, pain, and swelling and restricts joint
motion.
The duration of symptoms is often long, and symptoms
can be present for as long as 30 years; however, acute onset has
been documented.
Lipoma arborescens most commonly arises in the knee (
Fig.
129.18
), especially the suprapatellar portion, although it has also
been observed in the hip, ankle, and wrist joints. Lipoma arborescens is typically localized to one joint, but several cases of bilateral
knee involvement have been described.
One study found that
nearly a third of cases demonstrate an atypical presentation, with
multifocality or disease outside of the knee.

veins, and capillaries; have abnormal interconnections; and are
arranged in a disorganized tangle.

excision or debulking may be the only surgical option. Radiation
therapy is not indicated.

### Fibroma of Tendon Sheath

Fibroma of tendon sheath is an uncommon benign neoplasm that
clinically mimics tenosynovial giant cell tumor but is morphologically distinct. Fibroma of tendon sheath was first identified
as a clinicopathologic entity in 1936 and, since that time, more
than several hundred cases have been reported.

variants have been described with areas reminiscent of nodular fasciitis, and these variants appear to have different molecular aberrations.
A translocation t(2;11) (q31-32;q12) has been identified
in classical fibroma of tendon sheath and is likely the driver of the
neoplastic process.

of tendon sheath; therefore different molecular aberrations are
associated with this neoplasm.

### Malignant Diffuse Tenosynovial Giant Cell Tumor

Malignant tenosynovial giant cell tumor is rare and clinically
aggressive. The joints of the lower extremity are most commonly
affected, in particular the knee. Malignant tenosynovial giant
cell tumor is considered primary when it arises in conjunction
with co-existing, benign-appearing tenosynovial giant cell tumor
and secondary when it presents as a sarcomatous recurrence at

a site of a previously documented benign tenosynovial giant cell
tumor.
The neoplastic cells appear cytologically malignant and may be spindle or polyhedral shaped. Rarely osteosarcomatous or chondrosarcomatous differentiation has been noted.
Malignant tenosynovial giant cell tumors have the capacity to
behave aggressively; approximately 33% of patients die of disease,
30% have pulmonary metastases, 20% have lymph node involvement, and 15% have distant metastases.
A recent report of 10
cases showed that the malignant cells have an immunophenotype
similar to that of normal synoviocytes and the mononuclear cells
of benign tenosynovial giant cell tumor.
The presence of
CSF1
rearrangements and CSF1 overexpression in the absence of an
identified rearrangement has also been documented in malignant
diffuse tenosynovial giant cell tumor.
Deletion of portions
of 15q and trisomies involving chromosomes 5 and 7 have also
occurred.
Conventional cytotoxic chemotherapy provides
clinical benefit while targeted TKI therapy appears to be of limited
utility.

### Localized Tenosynovial Giant Cell Tumor of the Joint

Localized tenosynovial giant cell tumor of the joint manifests as
a solitary, well-circumscribed mass. It usually consists of a single
sessile or pedunculated, sometimes lobulated, tumor that ranges
from 1 to 8 cm in diameter (
Fig. 129.41
). Most commonly, it is
unilateral, arises in the knee, and is equally distributed between
the sexes.
Symptoms are similar to those of diffuse tenosynovial giant
cell tumor, except that in the localized variant, a higher frequency
of joint locking is reported because the mass interferes with
motion.
A few patients may present with acute severe joint
pain caused by torsion and infarction of the tumor. Effusions are
common, but the synovial fluid tends to be less bloody than in the
diffuse variant, and it may be clear.
Imaging studies show a heterogeneous nodular mass that contains lipid and hemosiderin deposits (
Fig. 129.42
). In the knee
joint, the tumor frequently arises in the suprapatellar notch, in the
femoral notch, and between the meniscus and the joint capsule.
Usually, no bone invasion occurs. Marginal excision is generally
curative. Small lesions can be extirpated arthroscopically.
Histologically, localized tenosynovial giant cell tumor of the
joint is identical to the nodules of the diffuse variant. The main
difference is that the prominent synovial villi present in the diffuse
variant are absent or sparse.

## Malignant Tumors of the Joint

Malignant tumors of joints are uncommon and are classified into
primary and secondary types. Primary malignancies are very rare
and are virtually always sarcomas arising within the synovium of
large diarthrodial joints, especially the knee. Patients are adults or
adolescents who present with chronic symptoms of pain, swelling,
and effusion, and most tumors are chondrosarcomas or synovial
sarcomas. The largest reported series of primary de novo intraarticular sarcomas comprised 15 cases, all involving the knee
joint; malignancies arising from pre-existing benign tumors were
excluded. Patients showed a slight male predominance (3:2) with
a mean age of 44 years (range 16–84 years). Histologies included
synovial sarcoma (five), extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma
(three), myxofibrosarcoma (two), undifferentiated pleomorphic
sarcoma (two), myxoinflammatory fibroblastic sarcoma (one),
conventional hyaline chondrosarcoma (one), and high-grade myofibroblastic sarcoma (one).
Our experience has also included
cases of intra-articular pleomorphic fibrosarcoma, malignant
tenosynovial giant cell tumor, angiosarcoma (
Fig. 129.48
), and
epithelioid sarcoma (
Fig. 129.49
). Secondary malignant tumors
of joints, by definition, originate beyond the confines of the joint;
most are sarcomas that extend from neighboring bone or surrounding soft tissue. Although synovial tissue is highly vascular,
metastases or involvement of the synovium by carcinoma, melanoma, lymphoma, or leukemia is uncommon.

## Primary Sarcomas of Joints

### Conventional Chondrosarcoma

Conventional chondrosarcoma arising in the synovium is
unusual; fewer than 100 cases have been reported in the English
language.
In about two-thirds of cases, the chondrosarcoma arises in association with pre-existing synovial chondromatosis, and a large series of synovial chondromatosis reported
malignant transformation into chondrosarcoma in 6.4% of
cases.
Patients are usually in the fifth to seventh
decade of life, and the disease is slightly more prevalent in males
than in females (approximately 3:2).
Typically, the tumor
manifests as a progressively enlarging joint mass that may cause
mechanical dysfunction, pain, and stiffness. In patients who have
pre-existing synovial chondromatosis, the duration of symptoms
is usually long (average 11.2 years), with repeated prior excisions
of locally recurrent disease, and in some instances, symptoms may
be present for 25 years.
Approximately half of chondrosarcomas arise in the knee joint and one-third arise in the hip. The
ankle, shoulder, elbow, temporomandibular joints, and spine are
rarely involved.
Clinically, rapid deterioration of longstanding synovial chondromatosis or rapid recurrence after synovectomy should raise concern for malignant transformation.
Radiographic studies usually show a periarticular soft tissue mass that may have dense irregular or ringlike calcifications.
Occasionally, invasion into the medullary cavity of adjacent
bone is evident. The radiographic differential diagnosis varies
according to the presence of calcification and includes synovial

### Synovial Sarcoma

Synovial sarcoma is a common sarcoma that accounts for approximately 6% to 10% of soft tissue sarcomas. It usually develops in
the deep soft tissues and rarely arises in joints (
Fig. 129.50
), but
it may secondarily invade articular synovium from neighboring
soft tissues. Earlier descriptions of this tumor attest to its wide
spectrum of morphology because such names as
adenosarcoma
and
synovial fibrosarcoma
were used until the term
synovial sarcoma
first introduced in 1936, became commonplace. The morphology of synovial sarcoma mimics a joint in its early stage of development in that it contains cleftlike spaces and glands delineated
by large polygonal (epithelioid) cells surrounded by fascicles of
spindle cells. The clefts and glands simulate a microscopic “joint
space” that is bounded by synovial lining cells and is supported by
subsynovial mesenchymal cells. Because epithelial or spindle cells
may predominate, synovial sarcoma has been subtyped into biphasic and monophasic spindle cell and epithelial types, although the
last type is exceptionally rare.
Synovial sarcoma commonly affects adolescents and young
adults. In a series of 121 cases, the age range was 9 to 74 years,
with a median age of 34 years; however, it occurs in children with
significant frequency.
The term
synovial sarcoma
implies that the tumor originates
from the synovium; however, fewer than 10% of cases are intraarticular or show continuity with a synovial lining.
The cell of
origin is thought to be an immature mesenchymal progenitor cell
or myoblast that is still thus far incompletely characterized.
Approximately 60% to 70% of synovial sarcomas arise in the
extremities, especially the lower limb, in the vicinity of large joints,
particularly the popliteal areas of the knee and foot.
Regions
of the thigh, hand, leg, and digits may be affected and, in the distal
extremities, tumors are often adjacent to joint capsules or tendon
sheaths or both. Tumors also have occurred in the neck, torso,
craniofacial region, retropharyngeal space, retroperitoneum, spermatic cord, orbit, tongue, mediastinum, soft palate, heart, liver,
kidney, lung, pleura, and prostate.

from other sarcomas. The most common complaint is the development of a slowly enlarging, deep-seated, palpable mass that is

is usually recommended for patients who are at high risk—that is,
patients with tumors larger than 5 cm.

VEGF-alpha monoclonal antibody, targeting HDAC-mediated
epigenetic modulation with HDAC inhibitors, and utilizing an
immunotherapeutic approach through vaccines or genetically
engineered lymphocytes.

## Secondary Malignant Tumors of the Joint

### Sarcomas

Primary sarcomas of bone, such as osteosarcoma and chondrosar,
coma, infrequently involve a joint because intact articular cartilage
usually acts as a barrier to direct tumor extension. However, when
it is observed, joint invasion usually occurs via a pathway created
by a transarticular fracture, via growth along tendoligamentous
structures, or through capsule insertion sites. Because of this circumstance, on histologic grounds alone it may be difficult to distinguish some forms of synovial chondromatosis from a low-grade
intraosseous chondrosarcoma that has secondarily spread into the
joint. Rarely, sarcomas may involve the joint space secondary to
local metastasis, such as in a reported case of sacrococcygeal chordoma metastatic to subcutaneous tissue of the ankle with extension into the ankle joint.
Similarly, primary soft tissue sarcomas gain access into the interior of a joint by growing through the joint capsule in conduits
occupied by pre-existing vascular structures or along tendons and
ligaments. This complication can make providing adequate therapy challenging because treatment may require en bloc resection
of the joint.

### Metastatic Carcinoma and Melanoma

The synovium, in contrast to other richly vascular tissues, is rarely
the site of metastatic carcinoma or melanoma; this may reflect the
fact that only clinical cases in which joint symptoms prevail are
reported because, at autopsy, joints are not routinely examined.
Approximately 50 cases have been reported in the literature.
Most carcinomas that metastasize to the synovium originate in
the lung, followed by the gastrointestinal tract and breast and,
less frequently, the kidney, bladder, cervix, and tongue.
Rare
cases of metastatic melanoma have also ocurred.
Affected
patients are usually elderly, and the knee is the most frequently
involved joint. In many reported cases, the underlying bone also
contains metastatic deposits and joint involvement may be secondary to direct extension of a bone metastasis into the joint.

Prognosis is poor, with an average survival of 5 months after
diagnosis.

### Malignant Lymphoproliferative Disease

The various types of malignant lymphoproliferative diseases,
including leukemia, lymphoma, and myeloma, can involve the
synovium and may produce osteoarticular symptoms.
This
complication occurs most frequently in individuals with leukemia
and is seen in both acute and chronic forms.
Joint symptoms
have been observed in 12% to 65% of children and in 4% to 13%
of adults with leukemia.
Arthritis can develop at any time during the disease course and can be the presenting symptom. Large
joints are affected more commonly than small joints, and the
arthritis is often pauciarticular, asymmetric, migratory, and severe.
Symptoms may result from leukemic infiltration of the synovium
or irritation of the neighboring periosteum. When arthritis is the
major presenting symptom, it may cause confusion with septic
arthritis, rheumatic fever, subacute bacterial endocarditis, or RA.
Primary synovial lymphoma is exceptionally rare and is sometimes
associated with immunocompromised states or prior prosthetic
joint arthroplasty.
