## Introduction

A common challenge in caring for patients with connective tissue diseases (CTDs) is how to approach the care of those whose
signs and symptoms suggest an underlying autoimmune disease
but whose clinical features do not fit neatly into a single, classically
defined disease or syndrome. One reason for this diagnostic ambiguity is that rheumatic diseases are often defined descriptively
using classification criteria that rely primarily on clinical and serologic pattern recognition rather than any single gold-standard test,
and this leaves many patients without a precise name to call their
disease. For example, a man who develops late-onset Raynaud’s
phenomenon and interstitial lung disease (ILD) in the setting of
high-titer nucleolar-pattern anti-nuclear antibodies (ANAs) likely
has an underlying CTD, but what to call it and how to predict
whether it will ever evolve into a definable or clinically significant
rheumatic disease is less clear. Developing a better understanding
of overlap CTDs is critical to improving diagnosis, prognosis, and
treatment of these patients.

## Historical Background

The concept of overlap CTDs is best understood in the historical
context of how systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and mixed
connective tissue disease (MCTD), a prototypic overlap syndrome, came to be recognized and defined. In the 19th century,
lupus erythematosus was originally described as a skin disease
with distinctive clinical, anatomic, and histologic findings.
By
the beginning of the 20th century, a growing number of lupus
patients had internal organ involvement, and from these observations emerged the concept of SLE as a single systemic disease with
diverse clinical manifestations that for some included the skin.
Further advances in serologic testing including fluorescent ANA
testing and the identification of autoantibodies specific for human
double-stranded DNA led to the more modern unifying concept
of SLE as an immunologic rather than anatomic disease.
By the 1960s and early 1970s, investigators recognized a distinct subset of atypical SLE patients who exhibited overlapping
features of SLE, dermatomyositis, and systemic sclerosis (SSc).
Like many of those with SLE, these patients demonstrated autoantibodies to a macromolecular ribonuclear antigen derived from
a saline nuclear extract, the extractable nuclear antigen.
Further
characterization of the antigen through enzymatic treatment
with trypsin and ribonuclease revealed that patients with “atypical SLE” reacted to a different component of the macromolecule, the ribonuclear protein, than did the patients with classical
SLE.
Armed both with a clinical description and a matching
serologic test, investigators named the newly described overlap
syndrome “mixed connective tissue disease” and suggested classification criteria to help identify such patients for further study.
Although this generally accepted classification has now been in
use more than 40 years, there still remains some debate over
whether MCTD is, in fact, a single disease or whether it may in
fact be an intermediate stage of an eventually classifiable disease
in evolution.
Furthermore, the classification of MCTD fails
to adequately describe many patients who have overlapping or
incomplete features of one or more autoimmune diseases. Given
our limited understanding of the molecular mechanisms that
underlie such diseases, it remains unclear whether such patients
have a unique, definable “undifferentiated connective tissue disease” (UCTD), an incomplete variant of a classically defined
rheumatic disease, or two or more separate but coexistent autoimmune diseases.

recent studies have identified single-nucleotide polymorphisms in

with the risk for developing MCTD, suggesting that IFN pathways may play an important role in MCTD pathogenesis as with
other CTDs.

some of its more specific clinical manifestations.
Indeed, different polymorphisms confer increased SLE risk in different populations of patients, which may explain variations in the general
clinical spectrum of disease between different racial-ethnic populations.
Some of the same SNPs that confer increased risk for
SLE have also been linked independently to other autoimmune
diseases that include SSc, inflammatory myositis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, RA, and Sjögren’s syndrome.

## Autoantibodies in Overlap Syndromes

Autoantibody production is a prominent feature of patients who
have overlap CTDs including MCTD.
In many cases, the
autoantibody response in patients with overlap CTD targets the
same autoantigens as those described in patients with more classically defined rheumatic diseases such as SLE or SSc. ANAs are
observed in practically all patients with MCTD by definition
and are also reported in between 58% to 100% of patients with
UCTD.
Examples of specific ANAs found in UCTD include
antibodies to the Ro (SSA) antigen, reported in 8% to 30% of
patients, and topoisomerase 1 (Scl-70), particularly in those
overlap patients with clinical features of limited scleroderma and
SSc.
Often, this autoantibody response targets unique autoantigens or distinct epitopes on more ubiquitous autoantigens and
may correlate with specific clinical features and/or prognosis.
These target antigens are usually complex macromolecules consisting of polypeptides and nucleic acids that are involved with
splicing, transcription, or other molecular machinery essential
for cellular function.

human spliceosome molecule, the U1 small nuclear ribonuclear
protein (U1snRP), is a target of autoimmunity in patients with
SLE, MCTD, and other CTDs

## Mixed Connective Tissue Disease

### The Controversy

Originally described in the early 1970s, the concept of MCTD as
a unique disease remains a subject of controversy up to the present
time. Experts on both sides of this debate have squared off against
one another in special editions of scientific journals focusing on
this very topic.
The original description of MCTD was culled
from a single cohort of SLE patients who appeared clinically to
behave differently than the rest of their peers. This subgroup
exhibited overlapping features of multiple CTDs, including SLE,
inflammatory myositis, SSc, and RA.
Anti-RNP antibodies were
prevalent in but not exclusive to this subgroup and helped to differentiate MCTD from other diseases like SLE.
As a general rule, these patients were described as having
a “milder” course of disease than those with more typical SLE,
namely a lower incidence of glomerulonephritis (GN), less severe
internal organ damage, and a more benign prognosis with favorable survival rates compared with other CTDs.
Some studies
have cast doubt on these initial impressions and reported more
severe outcomes and a worse prognosis than previously thought.
Many of these studies also question the stability of the clinical phenotype of MCTD, reporting high rates of evolution from MCTD
to more well-defined rheumatic diseases such as SLE or SSc with
worse outcomes.
As a result, some experts have suggested using
the term
undifferentiated autoimmune rheumatic disease
to describe
these patients rather than MCTD.

Joint Involvement
The spectrum of joint involvement in MCTD varies from a mild
nonerosive and symmetric polyarthritis akin to what is observed
in SLE to a more severe, erosive, and potentially destructive polyarthritis practically indistinguishable from RA.
Polyarthralgias
and arthritis are common and often present early in the disease,
affecting between 60% and 95% of patients with MCTD.
Many of these patients are rheumatoid factor positive and a few
even demonstrate RA-specific autoantibodies to citrullinated proteins.
When these seropositive patients present with erosive
polyarthritis, it may be difficult to distinguish them from RA
patients unless or until other overlapping features arise.

Kidney
MCTD can involve the kidneys in 25% to 40% of patients.
In many cases this involvement is mild, patients are often
asymptomatic, and the prevalence of severe kidney disease is
rare.
Membranous GN is the most common pathologic lesion
observed and is often associated with immune complex deposition localized to the mesangium and/or subepithelial space.
However, the spectrum of pathology can range from mild
mesangial GN to more severe diffuse proliferative GN with
subendothelial immune deposits, cellular crescents, and hypocomplementemia.
Children with MCTD have a relatively higher prevalence of proliferative GN than do adults, and
this has significance for both diagnosis and treatment of their
kidney disease.
Rarely, MCTD patients can experience malignant hypertension, acute kidney injury, and microangiopathic
hemolytic anemia akin to what is seen in SSc-associated renal
crisis.
Interstitial nephritis, thrombotic microangiopathy, and collapsing glomerulopathy have also been reported
as well.
The overall prognosis for adult MCTD patients with
kidney disease is excellent, although several studies have shown
that up to 14% of patients with kidney involvement, particularly those with proliferative GN, can progress to end-stage renal
disease.

Cardiovascular Manifestations
Cardiac involvement is common and can be detected in between
24% and 63% of MCTD patients, according to one systematic
review of the literature.
This wide variation between studies is
at least partly attributable to differences in how cardiac disease
is defined and detected.
The most common cardiac
manifestation is pericarditis, which can affect between 30% and
43% of patients.
Although often silent, as with SSc, the
pericardial effusions associated with MCTD can rarely be significant enough to cause tamponade physiology and hemodynamic compromise.
In addition to pericarditis, MCTD
can affect all of the other layers of the heart. Valvular heart
disease is well reported, especially mitral valve proplapse.
Other studies report evidence of conduction system abnormalities ranging from asymptomatic interventricular conduction
delay on electrocardiogram to complete heart block, myocardial
dysfunction and myocarditis, and left-ventricular diastolic filling anomalies.
Cardiomyopathy, left- and right-ventricular systolic dysfunction, and congestive heart failure can occur
through several mechanisms, including as a result of PAH, intimal hyperplasia and arteriopathy of the coronary arteries, and
frank myocarditis.
Gastrointestinal Tract

and dysphagia are by far the most common complications,
reported in as many as 77% and 61%, respectively, of patients
with SSc, MCTD, and UCTD.
Those figures were somewhat lower, but still quite significant, in a study limited to
MCTD patients, with 48% experiencing heartburn and 38%
of patients, dysphagia.

Inflammatory myositis is a hallmark overlap feature and part of
the original description of MCTD.
Approximately 80% to 90%

or another.
Myalgias are common, often without demonstrable

electromyography (EMG) testing. When present, inflammatory
myositis can range from asymptomatic elevations in serum muscle
enzymes to clinical features reminiscent of polymyositis, featuring
symmetric proximal muscle weakness and CK elevation. Diagnostic evaluation can reveal nonspecific myopathic features on EMG
and evidence of edema in proximal musculature on T1-weighted

While some MCTD patients present with a subacute onset of
their muscle disease, others more often present more acutely in
the setting of systemic disease activity that can include fever.
Neurologic Disease
Involvement of the central and peripheral nervous system is relatively uncommon in MCTD compared with other CTDs such as
SLE. Nonetheless, there are characteristic clinical manifestations
that include trigeminal neuralgia, which can be an initial presenting symptom in MCTD patients and involves both sides of the
head.
Some of these patients may be corticosteroid responsive.
Polyradiculopathy, mononeuritis multiplex, and peripheral neuropathy have also been reported.
Headaches, often
migraine-like, are a relatively common finding, but psychosis and
seizure disorders are not. Central nervous system manifestations of
MCTD include reports of aseptic meningitis, transverse myelitis,
cerebral infarcts and hemorrhage, cauda equina syndrome, adhesive arachnoiditis, myasthenia gravis, retinal vasculitis, optic neuritis, and anti-aquaporin 4–positive neuromyelitis optica.

## Patients Presenting With Undifferentiated Rheumatic Disease

At disease onset, many patients present with signs and symptoms
suggestive of a CTD but without a clear diagnosis. These patients
are usually ANA positive and have at least one clinical feature
of CTD but do not fulfill classification criteria for any defined
CTD.
For example, a patient with Raynaud’s phenomenon,
arthralgias, and puffy fingers may have a positive ANA but no
other clinical features suggestive of SSc, SLE, or any other CTD.

### Early Undifferentiated Systemic Sclerosis

Limited and diffuse SSc often presents in patients who do not
display all classical features of the disease at its onset. Indeed,
isolated Raynaud’s phenomenon is usually the presenting symptom in an ANA-positive patient, and a shorter time of progression from Raynaud’s to other features of SSc portends a more
aggressive disease course.
However, the vast majority of patients
with Raynaud’s phenomenon will never progress to SSc or any
other CTD. Those patients with higher-titer ANAs, those with
antibodies for SSc-specific autoantigens such as topoisomerase-I,
and those with nailfold capillary abnormalities are more likely to
progress from an undifferentiated state to SSc.

### Undifferentiated Connective Disease: A Stable Phenotype

For those patients who present with clinical features suggestive but
not diagnostic of a CTD, there remains considerable controversy
over whether these patients each have their own distinct CTD as
opposed to a clinical variant of a “named” rheumatic disease. One
study of 91 patients examined the clinical and serologic profiles of
UCTD patients over a mean duration of 5 years. Only 13% evolved
over time into SLE while none evolved into either Ssc or idiopathic inflammatory myositis.
Clinically, these UCTD patients
experienced one or more of the following: arthralgias, Raynaud’s
phenomenon, arthritis, leukopenia, sicca symptoms, alopecia, and
photosensitivity. These symptoms remained remarkably consistent
over time.
Those few patients who did evolve into SLE did so
distinctively, with progression to nephritis, serositis, and hemolytic
anemia as examples. Serologic profiles remained stable as well, with
most patients exhibiting a single, definable ANA specificity, often
against either the Ro(SSA) or RNP antigens.
Another cross-sectional, multicenter study of 184 patients found
a similar frequency and constellation of signs and symptoms and
overall milder clinical profile.
A third study utilizing a large
Hungarian cohort of more than 600 UCTD patients reported that
two-thirds of the cohort remained undifferentiated after 5 years’
follow-up, with Raynaud’s, arthralgias, and arthritis representing
the majority of reported complaints.
Finally, a recent systematic
literature review of 1118 UCTD patients published in six studies,
followed for a duration ranging from 1 to 15 years, found that only
28% evolved to a clinically classifiable rheumatic disease.
Thus,
UCTD may represent a distinct and phenotypically stable group
of CTDs that is often associated with a milder clinical course, particularly in those patients who, in the first few years, do not show
evidence of transformation or evolution into other CTDs.

## Overlap Syndromes With Dominant Features of Scleroderma

Many patients with limited and diffuse SSc share overlapping
clinical features with other autoimmune diseases. In some

instances, patients may actually have two or more separate, classifiable autoimmune diseases occurring simultaneously, such
as those SSc patients who also display signs and symptoms of
inflammatory myositis. Two separate studies found evidence of
inflammatory myopathies in 14% to 17% of SSc patients, but
this number has varied widely in other studies depending on how
stringently myopathy is defined.
A third study reported a
5.9% prevalence of a specific “scleroderma-polymyositis overlap
syndrome” in an observational cohort of SSc patients from a
single center.
SSc-myositis overlap is more common in diffuse rather than
limited SSc and often lacks the characteristic autoantibody profile of either SSc or myositis alone.
It is rarely seen in patients
who have anti-centromere antibodies.
Instead, patients
with this and other SSc overlap syndromes frequently exhibit
characteristic autoantibody profiles that correlate with their
overlap phenotype. In the case of SSC-myositis overlap, some
patients have nucleolar-pattern ANAs specific for the PM/SCL
antigen. Patients with SSc-myositis overlap may have a higher
prevalence of interstitial pulmonary fibrosis; more myocardial
involvement, including congestive heart failure and cardiacrelated death; and an increased mortality compared with those
with SSc alone.
Autoimmune hepatobiliary disease is another clinical entity
long recognized to be associated with both SSc and primary
Sjögren’s syndrome. In one large registry consisting of 1572 SSc
patients, 7.5% had evidence of hepatobiliary disease, more than
60% of whom had PBC.

## Sjögren’s Syndrome and Autoimmune Thyroid Disease

Symptoms compatible with Sjögren’s syndrome are prevalent in
patients with autoimmune diseases that include SLE, RA, and others. These patients are more commonly seronegative than are patients
with primary Sjögren’s syndrome and have a lower risk of extraglandular involvement. Clinically, both primary and secondary Sjögren’s
patients experience similar symptoms of dryness of the eyes, mouth,
or skin. On histopathologic examination, those with secondary disease will often have milder exocrine gland involvement with or without characteristic lymphoid aggregates. Autoimmune thyroid disease
is also commonly seen in patients with RA, SLE, and other autoimmune conditions. In some cases, the thyroid disease may antedate the
onset of other rheumatic diseases. Most patients with Hashimoto’s
thyroiditis make autoantibodies targeting thyroid peroxidase and/or
thyroglobulin and those with Graves’ disease make antibodies to the
thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor. These patients may present
across the spectrum of disease, from subclinical autoantibody positivity to overt signs and symptoms of thyroid hormone deficiency or
excess, including thyrotoxicosis or myxedema.
