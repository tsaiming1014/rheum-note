# Epigenetics of Rheumatic Diseases

AMR H. SAWALHA AND MATLOCK A. JEFFRIES

KEY POINTS

> Epigenetic mechanisms include DNA methylation, histone modifications,

> and noncoding RNA regulation. Collectively, epigenetic mechanisms

> determine chromatin architecture, accessibility of genetic loci to

> transcriptional machinery, and gene expression levels.

> Epigenetic changes are cell type–specific, and epigenetic mechanisms

> play an important role in regulating the normal immune response,

> such as T cell differentiation.

> Epigenetic alterations are increasingly recognized to play an important

> role in the pathogenesis of a growing number of rheumatic diseases.

> Epigenome-wide studies in rheumatology have focused primarily on

> DNA methylation changes and resulted in the identification of novel

> target genes and pathogenic mechanisms in immune-mediated

> diseases.

> The epigenome is dynamic, allowing for the development of novel

> biomarkers for disease activity, specific disease manifestations, and

> response to treatment.

> Some epigenetic changes reflect the effect of environmental triggers

> that cause disease in a genetically susceptible host. An integrated

> ‘omics approach would help better understand disease pathogenesis

> in rheumatology.

> The developing field of epigenetic editing has the potential both to

> prove “causality” of epigenetic changes associated with rheumatic

> conditions and to potentially reverse epigenetic modifications

> associated with these diseases. Cell type–specific epigenetic editing

> approaches promise potential therapeutic value in the future.

## Introduction

Epigenetics refers to changes in gene regulation without changes
in the DNA sequence. These changes are often described as
heritable,
indicating a relative stability of epigenetic changes during
cell division. Three main epigenetic mechanisms are thought to
operate in concert to regulate chromatin architecture, thereby the
accessibility of regulatory regions within the genome to transcriptional machinery. Therefore epigenetic changes are often described
as
silencing
or
activating,
indicating the net effect of a given epigenetic change on gene expression (transcription). DNA methylation,
histone modifications, and regulatory RNAs (such as microRNAs)
have evolved in mammalian systems to provide a complex array
of epigenetic changes that determine specific chromatin architecture in any given genetic locus to regulate gene expression. These
epigenetic mechanisms are essential during development and tissue differentiation, as well as in dictating the repertoire of genes
expressed by the various cell types and tissues. Every nucleated
cell contains the same repertoire of the human genome, yet each
cell type maintains a specific assortment of expressed genes that is
necessary for its function, while the rest of the genome is silenced.
This silencing is achieved by epigenetic changes and the relative
presence or absence of transcription factors. Indeed, two factors
have to coexist for any gene to be expressed in any cell type: chromatin accessibility and appropriate transcription factors. If either
of the two is absent in a particular gene locus, that gene cannot
be expressed.

## Epigenetic Regulation

Genetic material (DNA) is tightly packed within the nucleus with
histone proteins to form the chromatin. The chromatin basic units
are known as nucleosomes, which consist of
∼
147 base pairs of
DNA wrapped twice around a core protein octamer consisting
of two of each H2A, H2B, H3, and H4 histone proteins.
This
architectural arrangement allows for the genomic material to be
compacted in such a way that it fits within the tight space of the
nucleus and provides an avenue for regulating gene expression
through epigenetic modifications.
DNA methylation refers to the addition of a methyl group
(–CH
) to the fifth carbon of cytosine rings.
This reaction is
mediated by DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs). DNMT3A and
DNMT3B are considered de novo DNMTs as they determine and
establish DNA methylation patterns in utero.
DNMT3 ligand
(DNMT3L) is a recently discovered catalytically inactive member
of the DNMT family that works in concert with DNMT3a and
DNMT3b to establish genomic methylation patterns.
DNMT1
is the “maintenance” DNMT as it maintains DNA methylation
patterns throughout cell division ex utero.
The methyl group
in DNA methylation reactions is derived from
S
-adenosyl-methionine, which gets converted to
S
-adenosyl-homocysteine after
donating a methyl group (
Fig. 25.1
). Therefore dietary changes
that affect micronutrient levels in
S
-adenosyl-methionine metabolism can potentially alter DNA methylation and provide a link
between metabolism and gene expression patterns.
DNA methylation most often occurs in cytosine residues within cytosine–
guanosine dinucleotides (CG). However, non-CG methylation,
which is very frequent in plants, has also been described in mammalian cells. This typically occurs in cytosines within CA or CT
dinucleotides and is generally restricted to embryonic stem cells
and developing brain tissue.

DNA methylation in promoter or regulatory sequences generally silences gene expression. Demethylation or hypomethylation,
on the other hand, is associated with active transcription. DNA
methylation inhibits gene expression by several mechanisms. One
important mechanism is through interacting with and inducing
histone deacetylation (
Fig. 25.2
).
Conversely, the removal of
DNA methylation marks results in gene activation and occurs
via both active and passive mechanisms.
Passive demethylation
occurs with a failure of the normal copying of the DNA methylation pattern (via DNMT1) to newly synthesized DNA strands
during cellular division. Active DNA demethylation also occurs
and involves a conversion of 5
′
-methylcytosine to 5
′
-hydroxymethylcytosine mediated by
ten-eleven translocation (TET)
gene
family members. Following this first critical and rate-limiting step,
hydroxymethylcytosine is then converted to subsequent chemical
intermediates, finally undergoing base-excision repair (BER) to
produce unmethylated cytosine.
There are a number of histone tail modifications that play a
role in epigenetic regulation. As mentioned above, histone acetylation (primarily of histones H3 and H4) is associated with transcriptional accessibility and active gene expression. Deacetylation
of H3 and H4 silences gene expression. Other histone tail modifications have variable effects on gene expression, depending on
the specific modification and the specific location of these modifications within histone proteins.
Mapping chromatin modifications across the genome and in multiple cell types and tissues
has resulted in a better understanding of chromatin regulation and
architecture, as has been demonstrated by the ENCODE project.
The importance of gene regulation by noncoding (nc) RNA
(such as microRNAs) was recognized by a Nobel Prize in Medicine and Physiology awarded in 2006. MicroRNAs are small RNA
molecules generally between 19 to 25 nucleotides that regulate the
expression of target genes by binding to regulatory elements within
their specific target genes such as the 3
′
-untranslated regions (3
′
-
UTR).
For example, X chromosome inactivation is partially
directed by coating of the chromosome in a short noncoding
RNA known as the X-inactive specific transcript (X-ist).
Long
noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) are intricately linked with paternal
imprinting (the process by which the redundant copies of each
gene, either maternal or paternal in origin, are inactivated).
Several novel functions of miRNAs have been proposed, and several new classes of larger noncoding RNA with important transcriptional significance have been recently described. A summary
of epigenetic mechanisms and major methods to analyze the epigenome are outlined in

## Epigenetics and the Immune Response

Epigenetic mechanisms are critically involved in the regulation of
multiple aspects of the normal immune response.
Only a few
examples will be discussed in this review in order to demonstrate
two important points: the importance of epigenetic changes in the
normal functions of immune cells that are thought to be key in
the development of autoimmune and inflammatory diseases and
the utmost importance of examining specific cell types (not a mixture of cells) in epigenetic studies in immune-mediated diseases.
T cell activation is associated with a rapid production of interleukin (IL)-2, resulting in a feedback loop of further expression
of the high-affinity IL-2 receptor
α
(IL-2R
α
or CD25) and more
IL-2 production (autocrine mechanism). This rapid and efficient
IL-2 production is associated with a robust and rapid demethylation of the
IL-2
promoter sequence. Indeed, this demethylation
starts within minutes after T cell stimulation and therefore is
clearly independent of cell cycle or cell division.
This was a
strong argument for the presence of an active DNA demethylation
process, discussed in the previous section.
The differentiation of naïve CD4
T cells to T helper (Th)1 and
Th2 effector cells is accompanied by essential and locus specific epigenetic changes.
In naïve CD4
T cells, the Th1 locus
(IFNG)
and
the Th2 locus (
IL4, IL5,
and
IL13
common locus control region)
are inaccessible for transcription as the DNA is methylated and
histone tails in these two loci are deacetylated, resulting in compact
chromatin that is transcriptionally repressive. Upon Th1 differentiation, the Th1 locus
(IFNG)
gets demethylated, and histone tails
become acetylated, leading to a locus-specific chromatin accessibility and the production of the key Th1 cytokine IFNγ
. At the same
time, the Th2 locus control region becomes even more methylated
compared with naïve CD4
T cells, to ensure no production of
Th2 cytokines.
The exact opposite happens in the Th2 locus
control region and the Th1
IFNG
locus upon naïve CD4
T cell
differentiation into Th2 effector cells.
Importantly, these locusspecific methylation changes are maintained upon memory Th1
and Th2 cell generation, to ensure a rapid clonal T cell response
upon reexposure to the same pathogen. Indeed, the 2 to 3 days it
generally takes for an initial Th1 or Th2 immune response is largely
the time needed for the aforementioned locus-specific methylation changes to happen, and this explains why a second-exposure
response from memory T cells is much faster.

mucocutaneous or arthritic complaints are likely to represent a distinct phenotype compared with lupus patients who present with
hematologic disease and nephritis and will likely have substantial
variations in epigenetic patterns of diseased tissues. Similarly, important endophenotypes (that is, patient groups defined by molecular

scope; suffice it to say that studies wherein more attention was paid
to carefully phenotyping both patients and the tissue of interest
should instill a higher level of confidence than more general, lesswell-defined study populations.

## Epigenetics in Rheumatic Diseases

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

The etiology of systemic lupus erythematosus (hereafter, lupus)
is incompletely understood. There is a growing body of literature
that supports a role for epigenetic changes in the pathogenesis of
lupus.
Most of these studies are focused on T cells, as demethylated T cells are sufficient to induce lupus in animal models. Initial studies in the epigenetics of lupus and in rheumatology in
general show that epigenetics is a critical component the current
paradigms of lupus pathogenesis. Indeed, epigenetics provides a
link between environmental triggers and genetic susceptibility in
autoimmune diseases.
DNA Methylation Regulation and Candidate Gene Studies
in Lupus T Cells
Initial studies demonstrated that procainamide and hydralazine
cause drug-induced lupus by demethylating T cells, thereby
resulting in T cell autoreactivity. Both inhibit the activity of the
maintenance DNA methyltransferase DNMT1. Procainamide
directly inhibits DNMT1 in T cells, while hydralazine inhibits
mitogen-activated protein kinase (MEK)/extracellular signalregulated kinase (ERK) signaling, which regulates and mediates
DNMT1 expression.
CD4
T cells treated with procainamide
or hydralazine, the DNA methylation inhibitor 5-azacytidine,
or MEK/ERK pathway inhibitors overexpress mRNA and proteins encoded by methylation-sensitive genes (such as CD11a,
CD70, perforin, CD40L, and killer cell immunoglobulin-like
receptor [KIR] family) and become autoreactive capable of killing
autologous macrophages and stimulating B cell immunoglobulin
production in vitro.
In vivo experiments using adoptive transfer of similarly treated and demethylated T cells into syngeneic
mice cause autoantibody production and a lupus-like disease.
T
cells isolated from lupus patients demonstrate reduced MEK/ERK
signaling, reduced DNMT1 expression and activity, and demethylation of the promoter sequences resulting in overexpression of
CD11a, CD70, perforin, CD40L, and KIRs, similar to 5-azaCtreated T cells.
Therefore T cells from lupus patients resemble
normal T cells treated with MEK/ERK pathway inhibitors or
DNA methylation inhibitors. The extent of MEK/ERK signaling
defect and reduction in DNMT1 expression in lupus T cells is
proportional to disease activity, with T cells isolated from active
lupus patients showing more extensive demethylation compared
with patients with inactive disease.
To provide further a proof of principle that reduced MEK/ERK
signaling in T cells can result in T cell demethylation and lupus
rather than the alternative possibility that the observed MEK/
ERK signaling defect in lupus is the result of and not causal to
the disease, a transgenic mouse model with induced T cell MEK/
ERK signaling defect was generated.
These mice demonstrated
that inducing a defect in the MEK/ERK signaling pathway results
in reduced DNMT1 expression, demethylation of methylationsensitive genes, and overexpression of these same genes similar to
T cell isolated from lupus patients.
In addition, this mouse
model developed anti–double-stranded DNA antibodies and a T
cell interferon expression signature, reminiscent of the interferon
signature in lupus patients.
On a genetic background permissive to autoimmunity, these mice also develop clinical lupus-like
disease such as glomerulonephritis.
Taken together, inducing a DNA methylation defect in T cells
is sufficient to cause autoimmunity in an autoimmune-resistant
genetic background and to cause clinical lupus-like phenotype
in the presence of a genetic background that is permissive to
autoimmunity. This resembles the observation in humans taking
hydralazine (which is a MEK/ERK inhibitor), with the majority
of patients developing autoantibodies and only a small fraction,
presumably those with genetic background for lupus, developing a
clinical lupus-like disease. Furthermore, investigations have demonstrated that the lupus-prone mouse strain MRL/
lpr
possesses
similar CD70 demethylation in CD4
T cells as do human lupus
patients.
These observations provide a basis for genetic–epigenetic interaction in the pathogenesis of lupus, other evidence for
which is discussed in the sections below.
Subsequent studies mapped the origin of the MEK/ERK signaling defect in lupus T cells, which causes the observed DNA
methylation defect, to an upstream signaling defect in PKC
δ
.
Interestingly, oxidative stress, such as oxygen free-radical species,
can induce a defect in PKC
δ
signaling similar to that observed in
lupus T cells, resulting in T cell DNA demethylation.
These
data provide a link between environmental triggers of oxidative
stress, such as infections, and autoimmunity in the susceptible
host. Infections are known to induce flares in lupus patients, and
this might be mediated at least partly by increased oxidative stress
resulting in T cell demethylation. In fact, mitochondrial dysfunction resulting in increased production of reactive oxygen species and reduced levels of the antioxidant glutathione has been
reported in lupus T cells.
Increased oxidative stress in lupus T
cells also results in activation of the mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) signaling pathway, which also inhibits DNMT1.
Treatment of lupus patients with the anti-oxidant and glutathione precursor
N
-acetylcysteine blocked mTOR in T cells and
improved disease activity.
Additional evidence shows that other mechanisms play a role
in lupus T cell DNA methylation defects. For example, protein
phosphatase 2A (PP2A), which is involved in multiple cellular
processes including proliferation and activation, is overexpressed
in lupus T cells. Increased PP2A expression and activity has
been linked to reduced IL-2 production, which is characteristic
of lupus T cells.

a finding that correlates with the clinical

Genetic–Epigenetic Interaction in Lupus
Methyl-CpG-binding protein 2 (MeCP-2 encoded by
MECP2
)
is a key transcriptional regulator that is intimately involved in
DNA methylation–mediated transcriptional repression. Numerous studies have identified and replicated the genetic association between
MECP2
and lupus, suggesting that genetic variants
within
MECP2
are associated with increased risk for lupus.
This was the first link suggesting a potential role for genetic–epigenetic interaction in the pathogenesis of lupus. Subsequent studies indicated that the lupus-risk variant in
MECP2
is associated
with increased mRNA expression of one
MECP2

gene expression profile characterized by an interferon signature in
activated CD4
T cells.
Importantly, the DNA methylome of
stimulated T cells from healthy individuals carrying the lupus-risk
variant in
MECP2
reveal hypomethylation of several interferonregulated genes.
Genetic–epigenetic interaction plays a role in flare severity in
lupus patients. There is a positive correlation between genetic risk
to T cell DNA methylation ratio and lupus disease activity in lupus
patients.
Moreover, male lupus patients require a higher genetic
risk and/or lower T cell DNA methylation to achieve a lupus flare
of equal severity to women.
Genetic risk alone or T cell DNA
methylation alone could not explain the difference between men
and women in lupus flare severity. These data provide further evidence for genetic–epigenetic interaction in lupus and suggest that
while genetic risk is relatively stable over time, dynamic changes
in T cell DNA methylation might play an important role in lupus
flares, in interaction with the genetic susceptibility for lupus.
Recent advances in genome-wide DNA methylation methodologies have made possible more detailed analyses of the interactions between epigenetic modifications and underlying genetic
risk for autoimmune and rheumatic conditions. These associations, specifically between DNA methylation changes and genetic
susceptibility loci, are known as methylation quantitative trait loci
(meQTLs). A large meQTL analysis performed on SLE patients
and healthy controls using a mixed whole blood tissue sample was
performed in 2017. Like previous genome-wide DNA methylation studies, they again saw a pattern of DNA hypomethylation of
interferon-regulated genes.
They also identified seven meQTLs,
located within the genes encoding CD45,
MHC-class III,
HRF1BP1, IRF5, IRF7, IKZF3
and
UBE2L3.
This study strongly
suggests that lupus risk alleles may exert their pathogenic influence at least in part by colluding with local epigenetic patterns.
Conversely, certain epigenetic changes, particularly those most
strongly associated with interferon-regulated genes, also arise

outside of any genetic influence.
In a recently published study,
genome-wide DNA methylation data were generated from naïve
CD4
T cells from patients with SLE and matched healthy controls. These patterns were then compared via a meQTL analysis
and genotyping data. Although genetic–epigenetic interaction
were identified among three previously reported lupus susceptibility loci (
TMEM86B-PTPRH, CFB,
and
IRF7
), the overwhelming majority (>99%) of differentially methylated loci in lupus
were not associated with a meQTL, suggesting only minimal
genetic contribution to epigenotypes in lupus. Another study
recently demonstrated that only a relatively small fraction (16%)
of ethnicity-specific DNA methylation changes are explained by
genetic–epigenetic interactions.
In agreement with previous
studies, these data also suggest that certain SLE genetic susceptibility alleles may exert pathogenic affects via altering or interacting
with local DNA methylation patterns.
Histone Modifications in Lupus
Histone acetylation status is a product of the balance between
histone acetyltransferases and histone deacetylases. As the name
implies, the former increases histone acetylation, while the latter leads to its reduction. As discussed above, histone acetylation/
deacetylation determines chromatin accessibility and is influenced by the DNA methylation status (
Fig. 25.2
). One of the
earliest reports related to histone changes in lupus suggested that
histone deacetylase inhibitors can abrogate lupus-like disease in
murine models. Histone deacetylase inhibitors downregulated the
production of several cytokines including IFN
γ
, IL-12, IL-10,
and IL-6 in MRL/
lpr
mice. Further, mice treated with histone
deacetylase inhibitors showed global increase in histone H3 and
H4 acetylation as expected and, more importantly, demonstrated
significant improvement in renal diseases.
Similarly, NZB/W
lupus-prone mice treated with a histone deacetylase inhibitor also
showed improvement of renal disease, reduction in Th17 cells, and
increased Tregs and acetylation of the Treg key transcription factor
FoxP3.
Therefore histone deacetylase inhibitors might provide
therapeutic effect in lupus by altering T cell subsets and reducing
pathogenic Th17 cells while increasing Tregs.
Gene-specific studies in human disease demonstrated the
importance of histone changes in suppressing IL-2 and inducing
IL-17 production in lupus T cells.
In lupus monocytes,
TNF
(encoding TNF) locus is more acetylated compared with normal control monocytes, and this hyperacetylation is associated
with increased
TNF
mRNA expression.
While histone changes
in lupus have not been as extensively studied as DNA methylation, at a genome-wide level, CD4
T cells from lupus patients
demonstrate reduced global H3 and H4 acetylation.
This is
consistent with a beneficial effect of HDAC inhibitors in lupus
mice discussed above. Mapping histone H4 acetylation in lupus
monocytes suggested that the majority of hyperacetylated loci are
potentially regulated by type I interferons.
A potential area of SLE epigenetic research and therapy
involves the transcription regulator enhancer of zeste homolog
2 (EZH2). EZH2 is a histone lysine methyltransferase and part
of the polycomb repressor complex PRC2, which functions by
trimethylating lysine 27 of histone H3, resulting in repression of
gene expression. A study in 2016 demonstrated an enrichment
of EZH2 binding sites among differentially methylated genomic
locations correlated with lupus disease activity.
Interestingly
enough, EZH2 expression is at least partially regulated by another
level of epigenetic control, microRNAs miR-26a and miR-101 in
response to glucose restriction.
miR-26a expression was indeed
positively correlated with lupus disease activity scores.
In a follow-up study in 2017, researchers both confirmed overexpression
of EZH2 and reduced expression of miR-26a and miR-101 in
lupus.
Furthermore, they examined the epigenetic consequences
of EZH2 overexpression in CD4
T cells and found substantial
differential methylation, particularly in cell adhesion and leukocyte migration-related genes. Linking these epigenetic findings to
cellular phenotype, they then demonstrated increased capacity of
EZH2-overexpressing T cells to adhere to endothelial cells, suggesting a potential future therapeutic avenue for SLE treatment.
Further research has shown that this increase in EZH2 is mediated
by activation of the mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) and increased
glycolysis in lupus CD4
T cells, due to increased oxidative
stress.
Indeed, inhibiting mTORC1 suppresses EZH2 expression in these cells, whereas challenging with additional oxidative
stress (by H
O
treatment) increases EZH2 expression.
MicroRNAs and the Pathogenesis of Lupus
Differential expression of microRNAs has been reported in
multiple cell types in lupus patients.
As microRNA expression is involved in the regulation of multiple aspects of the normal immune response, it is not surprising that changes in the
microRNA milieu can be associated with autoimmunity. Some of
the dysregulated microRNAs in lupus tie in specific and credible
pathogenic aspects of the disease and therefore can be targeted
as novel therapeutic targets in the future. Overexpression of several microRNAs in lupus CD4
T cells contribute to the DNA
methylation defect described above. The expression of microRNAs miR-126 and miR-148a is increased in lupus CD4
T cells,
and the microRNAs directly target and inhibit
DNMT1
.
miR-21, which is also upregulated in lupus CD4
T cells, inhibits
DNA methylation by targeting
RASGRP1,
an upstream component in the MEK/ERK signaling pathway that regulates
DNMT1
expression.
These data demonstrate the interaction between two
distinct epigenetic mechanisms in lupus: DNA methylation and
microRNA regulation.
miR-41 is an example of downregulated microRNAs in lupus.
Low miR-31, which directly targets and represses RhoA, contributes to impaired IL-2 production in lupus T cells.
Another
downregulated microRNA in lupus is miR-146a, which targets
several interferon-related genes, such as
IRAK1, TRAF6, IRF5,
and
STAT1
.
Downregulation of miR146a results in increased
activation of type I interferon, a characteristic pathogenic feature
of lupus.
Of interest, a genetic association between
miR146a
locus and lupus has been described, and the risk variant in this
locus is associated with downregulation of miR146a.
This
is another example of genetic–epigenetic interaction in lupus,
whereby a genetic variant might contribute to lupus pathogenesis by affecting the expression of an epigenetic affecter molecule.
Another such example is the genetic polymorphism in the 3
′
-UTR
region in
TLR7
in lupus patients, which is located in a binding
site for miR-3148, suggesting that this genetic variant in
TLR7
might induce pathogenicity by altering the epigenetic regulation
of
TLR7.
Indeed, the lupus-associated variant in this locus is associated with increased TLR7 mRNA and protein expression, and
the mRNA expression levels of
TLR7
in lupus and normal peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) was inversely correlated
with miR-3148 expression.
lncRNAs have also been explored in the context of SLE. In one
experiment, investigators demonstrated differential expression of

nearly 2000 lncRNAs in CD3
T cells of lupus patients.
Two
in particular, uc001ykl.1 and ENST00000448942, were strongly
correlated with various clinical parameters in these patients,
including the presence of anti-Smith antibodies, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), and C-reactive protein (CRP). Differential
expression of lncRNA-mRNA matched pairs has also been noted
in PBMCs of lupus patients.
The expression of lncRNAs in
monocyte-derived dendritic cells have also been correlated with
disease activity scores (SLEDAI) in lupus patients.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis

Fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS) are thought to play an important
role in rheumatoid arthritis (RA). They are activated and produce a
number of pro-inflammatory cytokines into the joints of patients
with RA. Further, this pro-inflammatory phenotype is associated
with increased production of matrix metalloproteinases and exported
to synovial fluid, leading to joint destruction. Earlier studies showed
that FLS in patients with RA are characterized by global DNA hypomethylation and as a result express L1 retrotransposable elements,
and this global hypomethylation is presumably due to decreased
expression of DNMT1.
Treating normal FLS with DNA methylation inhibitors results in phenotypic changes resembling activated
FLS from patients with RA and in overexpression of several genes,
including miR-203, which is increased in RA FLS and correlates
with levels of IL-6 production.
FLS from patients with RA demonstrate an aggressive phenotype with aberrant signaling when cultured in vitro, produced by epigenetic imprinting leading to aberrant
gene transcription, transcription factor changes, and downstream
signaling.
This epigenetic reprogramming is directed in part by
chronic exposure of FLS to inflammatory cytokines, including TNF,
establishing a long-term “inflammatory memory.”
Aberrations in
epigenetic control of nutrient and metabolic pathways in FLS have
also been recently demonstrated in RA.
A recent study integrated
transcriptomic and epigenomic data in RA FLS and demonstrated
clusters of RA FLS with distinctive transcription factor responsiveness, providing new insight into RA heterogeneity.
Global reductions in PBMC DNA methylation have been
documented in patients with RA. Similar to active lupus, this
hypomethylated phenotype correlates with reduced levels of
DNA methyltransferases
DNMT1
and
DNMT3B.
Furthermore,
the degree of hypomethylation within total PBMCs, CD4
and
CD8
lymphocytes, CD14
monocytes, and CD19
B cells correlates with clinical disease activity indices of patients with RA.
A demethylated “senescent” CD4
T cell subset (CD4
CD28
T
cells) is expanded in RA and overexpresses a number of proteins
encoded by methylation-sensitive genes, such as CD70, perforin,
and KIRs.
How much and what causal role this T cell subset
plays in RA is currently not clear but is unlikely to be specific for
RA as this T cell subset is expanded in multiple chronic inflammatory diseases. It is interesting, however, that this CD4
CD28
T
cell subset is found in atherosclerotic lesions and is implicated in
plaque development and rupture,
given that RA is an independent risk factor for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease.
Genome-wide DNA methylation studies in RA have focused
on elucidating DNA methylation differences between FLS from
patients compared to OA and, in some cases, normal controls. In
one study examining genomic DNA isolated from FLS of patients
with RA and OA controls, a number of differentially methylated
genes that could play a role in RA were revealed. These include
hypomethylation of
CASP1, STAT3, MMP20, TRAF2,
and
MEFV,
among others.
Hypomethylation was enriched in pathways related to cell migration, adhesion, and extracellular matrix
interactions. Interestingly, several CG sites in the TNF-encoding
gene
(TNF)
were hypermethylated in RA FLS.
A subsequent
study added to these data, demonstrating differences in DNA
methylation and gene transcription signatures in RA versus OA
control FLS but also showing substantial differences in epigenetic and transcriptomic patterns in knee joints compared with
hip joints.
These differences between knee and hip joints were
concentrated in inflammatory gene pathways, including IL-6 signaling and the JAK-STAT pathway, IL-17A signaling, and transforming growth factor (TGF)-
β
signaling. These data suggest that
joint-specific differences in natural history and drug response seen
in patients with RA may, at least in part, be due to epigenetic
variation in joint tissues. This finding is further supported by data
demonstrating differences in epigenetically directed chromatin
accessibility within
IL6, IL6R,
and
JAK1,
key inflammatory mediators of RA (and targets of RA biologic drugs) according to joint
location.
Alterations in histone acetylation, another epigenetic
control mechanism, were also noted. Furthermore, these alterations lead to functional consequences; specifically, knee FLS are
less sensitive to the JAK inhibitor and RA drug tofacitinib than
hip FLS. Another study examined and integrated changes in the
DNA methylome with microRNA expression and gene expression
analysis in FLS from RA and OA patients.
This study identified
differential methylation in novel genes in RA, including hypomethylation in
IL6R, CD74, TNFAIP8,
and
CAPN8
and hypermethylation in
DPP4, CCR6,
and
HOXC4,
among others. These
data also demonstrated inverse correlation between DNA methylation levels and gene expression in over 200 genes in RA. Importantly, several novel dysregulated microRNAs were revealed in this
study, such as miR-503, miR-551b, miR-550, and miR-625*.
A number of genome-wide DNA methylation studies of the
peripheral blood cells of patients with RA have also been performed. In 2014 researchers determined DNA methylation patterns in CD3
T cells and CD19
B cells from patients with
RA using Illumina 450k array technology.
Unlike findings in
primary Sjogren’s syndrome (discussed below), they found evidence for more differential methylation within patient with RA
T cells compared with B cells and identified a set of 32 genes
with concordant differential methylation in both cell types.
The same group published a follow-up study in 2016, in which
they examined T and B cells from early (treatment-naive) patients
with RA.
They demonstrated a distinct methylation signature
in each cell type, again showed more differential methylation in T
cells than B cells, and identified a methylation “signature” consisting of 150 CpG sites in T lymphocytes and 113 CpG sites in B
lymphocytes that accurately clustered all patients with RA separately from controls.
In 2017 CD4
T cells from a Han Chinese RA patient cohort were examined and significant differential
DNA methylation of the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) region
was identified.
Interestingly, the global reductions in DNA
methylation in RA peripheral blood cell subpopulations appear
to be reversible by disease-modifying treatment. In a 2015 study,
global DNA methylation levels of five major blood cell subpopulations (T, B, NK, monocyte, and polymorphonuclear leukocytes)
from early patients with RA before and 1 month after treatment
with the first-line RA disease-modifying drug methotrexate was
determined. Following drug therapy, global DNA methylation
significantly increased in T cells and monocytes to levels indistinguishable from controls; this change was accompanied by

increases in the de novo and maintenance DNA methyltransferases DNMT1 and DNMT3A, respectively.
Genetic–epigenetic interactions have also been demonstrated
in RA. In one large study including genetic and epigenetic data
from several hundred RA cases and matched controls, DNA methylation levels in two clusters within the major histocompatibility
complex (MHC) region were found to be strongly correlated both
RA and with the underlying genetic sequence in peripheral blood
lymphocytes.
In 2015 a large-scale integrative ‘omics study was
performed to identify genetic–epigenetic interactions in RA.
They considered genomic locations that met three criteria for
pathogenic significance: genetic risk (based on genome-wide association studies [GWASs]), differential gene expression in RA FLS,
and epigenetic risk via differential DNA methylation in RA FLS.
They also identified several candidate genes not previously linked
to RA pathogenesis, including
AIRE, CASP8, CSF2, ELMO1,
ETS1, HLA-DQA1,
and
LBH
.
In 2016 this same group
expanded their analysis to include differentially methylated CpG
sites located outside of “traditional” promoter regulatory regions;
specifically, they included enhancer regions in a new analysis.
In their new analysis, they identified a novel enhancer associated
with the
LBH
gene, which demonstrated hypomethylation in RA
FLS and was enriched in activating histone H3K4me1 marks.
Furthermore, they demonstrated a meQTL at this location,
wherein the underlying RA-associated genetic risk allele conspired
with RA-associated DNA demethylation to substantially increase
gene expression beyond what was seen in nonrisk-allele control
patients. These data strongly suggest that DNA methylation may
act as an intermediary of genetic risk in RA, similar to the riskenhancing effects of meQTLs in SLE and OA, discussed elsewhere
in this chapter.
Multiple microRNAs are also overexpressed in RA FLS, such
as miR-155, which is similarly upregulated in RA PBMCs and
synovial macrophages.
miR-155 is a pro-inflammatory
microRNA as miR-155–deficient mice are resistant to collageninduced arthritis.
miR-223 is overexpressed in the synovium, in
peripheral blood naïve CD4
T cells, and in the serum in patients
with RA, and its expression levels in the serum correlate with disease activity in treatment naïve early RA.
The overall role
of miR-223 as a therapeutic target in RA remains controversial.
Overexpression of miR-223 suppresses osteoclastogenesis in vitro,
suggesting a potential beneficial therapeutic effect of miR-223
overexpression
; however, suppressing miR-223 in vivo ameliorates arthritis, bone erosions, and osteoclastogenesis in mice with
collagen-induced arthritis,
suggesting a pathogenic effect of this
same microRNA molecule. Of interest, miR-146a, which is downregulated in lupus peripheral blood leukocytes, is overexpressed in
PBMCs and FLS from patients with RA,
although the exact
role of miR-146a in RA has not been clearly elucidated.

### Primary Sjögren’s Syndrome

Primary Sjögren’s syndrome (pSS) is a systemic autoimmune disease characterized by lymphocytic infiltration of exocrine glands,
primarily salivary and lacrimal glands, resulting in dryness of the
eyes and mouth. Global DNA methylation levels in labial salivary gland epithelial cells from patients with pSS is decreased,
and this reduction correlates with lower DNMT1 levels and
increased expression of the demethylating cofactor GADD45
α
(85). The presence and extent of the DNA methylation defect in
salivary gland epithelial cells in pSS correlates with the presence
of B cell infiltrates, which was confirmed in vitro using salivary
gland epithelial cell/B cell coculture.
However, no global reduction in peripheral blood B cell or T cell methylation levels were
detected.
Gene-specific DNA methylation studies in peripheral
blood CD4
T cells in pSS revealed demethylation and overexpression of the costimulatory molecule CD70 similar to what had
been previously observed in lupus patients.
In contrast,
FOXP3
is hypermethylated and transcriptionally repressed in CD4
T
cells from pSS, which is consistent with Treg functional defect
reported in pSS.
The first genome-wide DNA methylation study published in
pSS, performed in 2014, examined DNA methylation differences
in over 485,000 methylation sites in naïve CD4
T cells compared
with age-, sex-, and ethnicity-matched controls.
This study identified 753 differentially methylated sites, with the majority being
hypomethylated in pSS compared with controls. Several hypomethylated genes of interest in pSS include
CD247, TNFRSF25,
PTPRC, GSTM1,
and
PDCD1.
Several interferon-regulated genes
were also hypomethylated, consistent with an interferon signature
in pSS (88).
LTA,
which encodes lymphotoxin
α
(LT
α
) and promotes interferon production, was also hypomethylated in naïve
CD4
T cells in pSS. LT
α
is overexpressed in the serum and salivary gland from patients with pSS and a clinical trial to block the
lymphotoxin pathway as a therapeutic option in pSS is underway.
An interesting group of solute carrier protein genes were also differentially methylated in naïve CD4
T cells from pSS.
Subsequent genome-wide DNA methylation studies have been
conduct in pSS in other cell subtypes. In 2014 investigators determined changes in DNA methylation patterns using the same 450k
technology in CD4
T cells and CD19
B cells from pSS patients
and demonstrated an increased frequency of epigenetic alterations
in B cells; genes found to be differentially methylated clustered in
signaling pathways related to cytokines (IL-4 and IL-8), as well as
chemokine signaling (CXCR4), and B cell receptor signaling.
In 2016 CD19
B cells were examined; led to identification, similar to that of SLE patients, of a hypomethylation signature in
interferon-regulated genes, including
MX1, IFI44L,
and
PARP9;
and demonstrated increased gene expression of several interferonregulated genes associated with gene DNA hypomethylation.
Interestingly, they went on to show similar DNA methylation
differences in cells obtained from minor salivary gland biopsies,
and identified a meQTL within the HLA region, as well as two
pSS-associated susceptibility alleles located within interferon regulatory factor 5
(IRF5)
.
Reductions DNA methylation of the
Sjogren’s syndrome B (SSB) gene promoter P1 in minor salivary
gland tissue was associated with both lymphocyte infiltration into
salivary glands and with increasing titers of anti-SSB (also known
as anti-La) autoantibodies in pSS patients.
Further adding to
evidence for altered DNA methylation in salivary gland tissues,
a 2016 study showed persistent alterations in DNA methylation
patterns in long-term cultures of salivary gland epithelial cells
from pSS patients, again mostly in interferon-regulated genes.
Taken together, these studies strongly suggest, similar to SLE, a
role for epigenetic dysregulation of interferon-regulated genes in
the pathogenesis of pSS. Importantly, these alterations extend to
the target tissues of pSS pathophysiology; namely, salivary glands.

### Systemic Sclerosis

Systemic sclerosis (scleroderma) is an autoimmune disease
characterized by immune activation, vasculopathy including
microvascular endothelial cell dysfunction, and excessive collagen production and fibrosis of the skin and internal organs.

Epigenetic studies in scleroderma have therefore focused on fibroblasts, microvascular endothelial cells, and CD4
T cells.
Fibroblasts from scleroderma patients underexpress FLi-1, a
negative regulator of collagen synthesis
which is hypermethylated in fibroblasts from scleroderma patients compared with
controls (92). Further, H3 and H4 acetylation levels are reduced
in the
FLI1
promoter region, probably contributing to its transcriptional repression. Treating scleroderma fibroblast with
DNA methylation inhibitors combined with histone deacetylase
inhibitors increases
FLI1
expression and restores collagen production to levels comparable with normal fibroblasts.
These
data suggest that hypermethylation and hypoacetylation of
FLI1
in scleroderma fibroblasts might be playing a role in the pathogenesis of this profibrotic disease. Indeed, DNMT1 protein levels and the protein levels of the histone deacetylases HDAC1
and HDAC6 were elevated in scleroderma fibroblasts,
which
could explain the hypermethylated and hypoacetylated repressive status of
FLI1.
Recent data suggest that the Wnt pathway
signaling antagonists genes
DKK1
and
SFRP1
are underexpressed and silenced by hypermethylation in scleroderma fibroblasts.
Importantly, inhibiting DNMT1 reduced canonical
Wnt pathway signaling in scleroderma fibroblasts and reduced
bleomycin-induced fibrosis in mice.
Multiple microRNAs are
dysregulated in scleroderma fibroblasts compared with controls,
with several targeting components in TGF
β
signaling. For example, miR-21 and miR-146 are overexpressed, putatively targeting
SMAD4 and SMAD7, respectively.
A genome-wide DNA methylation study in skin fibroblasts
from diffuse and limited scleroderma patients identified a large
number of differentially methylated genes between patients
and age-, sex-, and ethnicity-matched healthy controls.
It
is interesting that despite previous reports showing increased
DNMT1 expression in scleroderma fibroblasts, most DNA
methylation changes observed using this genome-wide unbiased approach were hypomethylation changes in patients compared with healthy controls. This report revealed methylation
changes that are common between diffuse and limited disease
and also subset-specific DNA methylation changes. Hypomethylated genes in both diffuse and limited scleroderma included
collagen genes such as
COL4A2
and
COL23A1, PAX9, TNXB,
ITGA9, ADAM12,
and the RUNX transcription family members
RUNX1, RUNX2,
and
RUNX3.
Increased mRNA expression levels in multiple of these hypomethylated genes were also
demonstrated in the same study.
A more recent analysis of
scleroderma skin endothelial cells and fibroblasts used the assay
for transposase-accessible chromatin with high-throughput
sequencing (ATAC-Seq) to identify genomic regions with epigenetic changes associated with open/accessible or closed/inaccessible.
They found that overall chromatin accessibility was
significantly reduced in diffuse scleroderma patients compared
with healthy controls. Regions of the genome with significant
differences in chromatin accessibility in these patients included
nitric oxide–, nervous system–, and cell membrane projection–
related pathways. The group went on to confirm that epigenetic dysregulation of ETV2 and NRXN1 affected angiogenesis
in endothelial cells, whereas
ENTPD1
dysregulation enhanced
profibrotic responses in fibroblasts.
Studies in microvascular endothelial cells in scleroderma demonstrated increased methylation in
BMPRII
encoding bone morphogenic protein receptor II, which plays a role in endothelial
cell resistance to apoptosis. BMPRII is underexpressed in microvascular endothelial cells from scleroderma patients, and in vitro
treatment with DNA methylation/histone deacetylase inhibitors
restores expression levels of BMPRII to levels similar to healthy
controls.
CD4
T cells in scleroderma patients express less
DNMT1 compared with normal controls
and overexpress
proteins encoded by methylation-sensitive genes such as CD70
and CD40L, and the promoter sequences of these genes are hypomethylated.
This is very similar to what has been previously
observed in lupus CD4
T cells. At least one genome-wide DNA
methylation analysis of circulating inflammatory cells has also
been conducted in the context of systemic sclerosis. In 2018, a
study was published describing an analysis of CD4
and CD8
T
cells from scleroderma patients.
Similar to what has been seen
in other systemic autoimmune diseases, this study identified substantial epigenetic dysregulation of the type I interferon signaling
pathway in both CD4
and CD8
T cells. Additionally, this work
determined significant elevations of both type I interferonα
and
-
β
proteins in the sera of scleroderma patients.
A few studies have also recently demonstrated associations
between histone post-translational modification changes and
scleroderma pathology. HDAC5 is an enzymatic effector of histone post-translational modifications, and it is linked to angiogenesis; specifically, HDAC5 expression is inversely correlated
with angiogenic activity in endothelial cells.
In a 2016
study, the role of HDAC5 in impaired angiogenesis in endothelial cells from scleroderma patients was examined.
The authors
first demonstrated increases in
HDAC5
expression in dermal
endothelial cells isolated from scleroderma patients and then
went on to show that knockdown of
HDAC5
by siRNA restores
normal angiogenesis in scleroderma endothelial cells in  vitro.
Finally, they demonstrated that knockdown of
HDAC5
resulted
in widespread epigenetic changes, as measured by increases in
open chromatin regions identified through ATAC
‐
Seq. Remarkably, gene ontology analysis of locations “opened up” following
HDAC5
knockdown revealed 16 genes known to be involved in
angiogenesis and 3 involved in fibrosis, 8 of which were differentially expressed following
HDAC5
knockdown. This study offers
a good example of the types of epigenetic–cellular phenotype
analyses that are critical to link epigenetic associations with disease pathophysiology.
Another recent study demonstrated that histone methyltransferase enhancer of zeste homolog 2 (EZH2) was overexpressed in
scleroderma dermal fibroblasts and endothelial cells compared
with healthy controls.
Treatment with an EZH2 inhibitor
both in vitro and in vivo halted fibrosis. Intriguingly, the effects
of EZH2 in dermal fibroblasts was traced to epigenetic dysregulation of
LRRC16A,
a gene involved in actin filament network
formation, whereas, in endothelial cells, the effects were mediated
by activation of the NOTCH pathway. These findings suggest that
targeting upstream epigenetic regulators may hold promise in the
treatment of autoimmune diseases, like scleroderma, that have a
dysregulated epigenome as a key pathogenic mechanism.

### Behçet’s Disease

Behçet’s disease is an immune-mediated inflammatory disease
characterized by recurrent oral–genital ulcers, inflammatory eye
disease, skin involvement, conserved noncoding sequence involvement, and recurrent thrombosis. The etiology of Behçet’s disease
is incompletely understood, but unsurprisingly genetic and environmental factors play a role in the pathogenesis of the disease.
A recent genome-wide DNA methylation study in Behçet’s disease shed light on the epigenetic architecture of CD4
T cells

and monocytes in Behçet’s disease patients with active untreated
disease compared with healthy age-, sex-, and ethnicity-matched
controls.
The study identified key differentially methylated loci
and pathways across the genome in Behçet’s disease that can be
targeted for the development of novel therapeutic interventions in
the future. Several regulatory and structural cytoskeletal components showed consistent DNA methylation change in both CD4
T cells and in monocytes in this disease. Differentially methylated genes in Behçet’s disease include
RAC1, RSG14,
and
FSCN2,
among others. Moreover, these epigenetic changes were dynamic,
as the study showed clear evidence for reversibility of DNA methylation changes when the same patients were examined at a subsequent time point after disease remission.
Therefore this study
in Behçet’s disease provides evidence that DNA methylation does
change throughout the course of the disease and that some of
these epigenetically altered loci can be explored as novel disease
biomarkers and therapeutic targets.
Differential DNA methylation of other cell types has also been
associated with Behçet’s disease. For example, interferon regulatory factor 8
(IRF8)
is hypermethylated in dendritic cells of ocular
Behçet’s patients and is associated with decreased gene expression;
correction of this hypermethylation with 5-aza-2
′
-deoxycytidine
increased
IRF8
expression and downregulated the expression of a
variety of costimulatory molecules and interleukins, suggesting a
potential epigenetic therapy for Behçet’s.

### Osteoarthritis

Several recent genome-wide DNA methylation studies in articular cartilage tissue have been reported and suggest that epigenetic
profiling can identify novel candidate genes, disease aspects, and
disease subsets in OA.
In a study examining DNA methylation in
∼
27,000 CG methylation sites in cartilage tissue from
knee OA patients compared with cadaveric controls, 91 differentially methylated CG sites were identified between the two groups.
Importantly, genome-wide methylation data and gene expression
profiling identified a cluster of OA patients with substantial differential methylation within inflammatory genes.
These data
suggested that epigenetic and transcriptional profiling can identify disease endophenotypes in OA, which could help target specific therapeutic options in a more individualized manner in the
future. Another study examined DNA methylation changes in hip
joint OA across over 485,000 methylation sites using a unique
approach comparing eroded with noneroded cartilage tissue from
the same joint.
This eliminates any confounding effect from
genetic variation across the groups. Remarkably,
∼
40% of genetic
risk variants previously reported in OA showed evidence of differential methylation in this study, suggesting that genetic–epigenetic interaction might play a role in the pathogenesis of OA
and that genetic or epigenetic alteration in these same genes are
associated with OA risk. The study identified a total of 550 differentially methylated genes in OA, with over two-thirds of them
hypomethylated. Bioinformatic analysis to identify common regulating factors among these differentially methylated genes identified TGFβ
1 and several microRNAs such as miR-128, miR-27a,
and miR-9. Importantly, this study showed correlation between
DNA methylation in 20 CG sites and histologic severity scores in
OA.
Subsequent studies have confirmed these previous findings
across several cohorts. A consistent pattern has emerged including the aforementioned inflammatory subset, as well as substantial differences in DNA methylation between knee and hip OA
samples.
As with SLE and RA, researchers have also examined the
interaction of epigenetic variation and genetic susceptibility in
OA. One study identified 31 genes for which changes in DNA
methylation was affected by local genetic variation and 26 genes
for which changes in gene expression was affected by both local
epigenetic patterns and genetic variation.
A second study published in 2015 performed a meQTL analysis in knee and hip OA
cartilage samples, focusing on 16 previously identified European
OA genetic susceptibility loci.
Four meQTLs were identified,
consisting of nine CpG sites in which genetic variation was associated with changes in local DNA methylation patterns. Interestingly, the effects of these variations on gene transcription patterns
were seen both in diseased and disease-free tissue. A later study
confirmed a meQTL within the
SUPT3H/RUNX2
region and
demonstrated that alterations in gene expression produced by
changes in DNA methylation within this region are amplified by
the presence of an underlying OA genetic risk allele.
Similar
effects have also been noted in the iodothyronine deiodinase 2
gene
(DIO2)
, where DNA methylation levels mediate the OA susceptibility effect of single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) locus
rs225014
.
There has also been interest in epigenetic changes within
noncartilage joint tissue; specifically, subchondral bone. The
first study in this regard characterized the methylome of subchondral bone underlying eroded and intact cartilage sections
of end-stage OA hip patient joints.
It identified an order of
magnitude more differentially methylated CpG sites in subchondral bone than the matched overlying cartilage, with 44%
of differentially methylated genes in cartilage also being differentially methylated in subchondral bone. Gene ontology suggested differential methylation of TGFβ
–related genes and
various cytokines. A second study in 2016 divided subchondral
bone into three distinct regions of the tibial plateau; to early,
intermediate, and late disease and the corresponding overlying
cartilage.
Interestingly, they were able to determine that DNA
methylation changes occurring in both subchondral bone and
cartilage appear first in the subchondral bone compartment.
Gene ontology analysis demonstrated differential methylation
of genes involved in both stem cell development and differentiation and a cluster of homeobox family (HOX) genes, as well as
a TGFβ
signature.
Recent work has also begun to evaluate the epigenetic consequences of individual OA risk factors on the epigenome. For
example, a 2022 publication in a mouse OA model demonstrated
widespread DNA methylation changes within knee cartilage associated with aging.
Among 716 differentially methylated CpG
sites induced early on in the development of OA in young mice,
roughly 40% (279) were also seen in OA-free old mice. Intriguingly, the magnitude and direction of methylation change were
concordant in all of these overlapping sites in both conditions,
suggesting that aging “primes” cartilage for the development of
OA.

## Integration of Epigenetic Data Streams and Clinical Applications

Epigenetic changes do not exist in a biologic vacuum; indeed, as
outlined in the introduction section of this chapter, epigenetic
modifications are key to setting the chromatin landscape for gene
transcription or silencing. Thus a key area of interest among epigenetic researchers is the integration of epigenetic data sets with

additional ‘omics domains, including gene transcription, proteomics, etc. Particularly in recent years, when large-scale multiomics analyses have become more feasible owing to advances in
technology and reductions in sequencing costs, integrative analyses are shedding new light onto the ways in which the epigenome
can have significant downstream effects in rheumatic disease
pathogenesis.
As an example of this computationally driven multiomics
integration, a recent study collected GWAS data from 39 previous publications involving various complex systemic autoimmune diseases.
The authors then performed clustering
of these diseases based on shared genetic susceptibility to produce a map identifying previously unknown relationships. For
example, roughly 70% of genetic polymorphisms (SNPs) were
shared in at least two autoimmune diseases. They went on to
perform advanced analytical analysis to identify the immune cell
subtype most likely to be involved in each of these diseases and
overlaid epigenomics data from the National Institutes of Health
Epigenomics Project and the Encyclopedia of DNA Elements
(ENCODE) and then reclustered individual cell types based on
these epigenomics patterns. They were then able to generate a
list of overlaps between immune cell subtype, genetic mutation,
and epigenetic modification characteristic of autoimmune disease. In the end, they estimated that genetic changes in enhancer
regions producing mixed genetic–epigenetic effects account for
∼
60% of all disease-associated genetic variation within autoimmune diseases.
Integrated multiomic analyses can also be quite informative
when tasked with closely examining a single pathogenic tissue in
a given disease. A recent integrative and multiomics analysis of
cartilage from OA patients offers a good example of how such
state-of-the-art studies can be used to distill enormous amounts
of data to actionable targets.
In this study, investigators collected osteochondral samples from eroded and intact regions of
discarded joint tissues in patients undergoing total knee replacement compared with cadaveric control samples. Then, investigators evaluated cellular changes at the protein level (via liquid
chromatography–mass spectrophotometry [LC-MS]), transcriptomics (via RNA-Seq), and epigenomics (via genome-wide
DNA methylation array). From these data, the investigators then
performed multiomics integration and found 49 genes that differed in at least 2 domains and 3 genes with significant evidence
for OA involvement across all 3 domains: aquaporin 1
(AQP1),
the collagen 1 gene
(COL1A1)
, and
CLEC3B
gene. All three of
these genes were upregulated at both a protein and mRNA level
and also exhibited reduced DNA methylation at all CpG probes
associated with them included in the Illumina arrays. Interestingly, of the 49 genes with differential regulation across at least 2
domains, fully one-third had not been previously implicated in
OA pathogenesis.
Apart from disease pathogenesis, this sort of complex multiomics analysis can also assist researchers in identifying novel
candidate drug targets for rheumatic diseases. For example,
investigators recently generated a comprehensive epigenetic and
transcriptomic landscape of RA and OA FLS.
In this study,
a total of 191 genome-wide data sets were generated across 11
RA and 11 OA samples, which included measures of chromatin
configuration (via ATAC-seq), localized histone modifications
specific to promoter and/or enhancer regions (via chromatin
immunoprecipitation sequencing [ChIP-Seq]), DNA methylation (via whole-genome bisulfite sequencing), and transcriptomic analysis (via RNA-Seq). This high-dimensional analysis
allowed the investigators to identify comodified regions (by
generating multidimensional clusters), which resulted in the
identification of 13 clusters with significant enrichment in RA
for further functional pathway analysis. Intriguingly, the key differences distinguishing RA from OA epigenetically were related
to transcriptional regulatory elements including enhancers and
promoters. RA-associated epigenetically dysregulated pathways
included some unsurprising (e.g., “role of macrophages, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells in rheumatoid arthritis”) but also
some novel associations (e.g., “p53 signaling,” “integrin signaling,” and “Huntington’s disease signaling”). The investigators
then went on to further investigate this novel finding of Huntington’s disease pathway involvement, which was driven mainly
by the
HIP1
gene. Surprisingly,
HIP1
deficiency decreased RA
FLS invasion by close to 50% in an in vitro RA-mimicking cellular invasion model. In the aforementioned OA multiomics
study,
researchers applied their list of 49 candidate epigenetically dysregulated genes to Drugbank,
a comprehensive
databank that lists information on drug targets, and identified
10 compounds that targeted on nine of the 49 dysregulated
proteins, all of which already had Food and Drug Administration marketing authorization for use in human patients. Some
were expected, including nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs,
but a few were novel, including vitamin K
, the antipsychotic/
antiemetic trifluoperazine, and the lipid-regulating ezetimibe,
among others.
Another area of biomarker research where epigenetics can
play a strong role is in predicting the response to drug therapy.
In RA, although dozens of disease-modifying antirheumatic
drugs (DMARDs) are available (with multiple new drugs
approved yearly), therapies are not universally effective, with
patients having to try numerous drugs before finding the “right
fit.” This experience is worsened by the fact that most biologic
drugs require several months to reach peak efficacy, making it
quite difficult to determine whether a lack of clinical response
early in treatment represents a true treatment failure or simply a
slow response. Epigenetic profiling may help address these problems. Indeed, some traditional DMARDs may work partially by
inducing epigenetic changes. Methotrexate, for example, induces
widespread epigenetic changes in peripheral blood inflammatory cells by increasing expression of the DNA demethylationinducing
TET1, TET2,
and
TET3
gene family members.
Whole blood DNA methylation differences have also been seen
in patients with RA who responded to methotrexate compared
with nonresponders.
These epigenetic techniques have also
been applied to biologic drugs. For example, a previous study
evaluated baseline peripheral blood DNA methylation changes
that were predictive of clinical response to the anti-tumor necrosis factor
α
drug etanercept.
Specifically, researchers identified
five CpG sites with significant differential methylation between
etanercept responders and nonresponders; intriguingly, all five
demonstrated reduced methylation among good responders
compared with poor responders. Interestingly, 2 of the top 5 and
4 of the top 15 most differentially methylated CpG sites between
groups were located within exon 7 of the
LRAP1
gene; they went
on to demonstrate that three
genetic
single-nucleotide polymorphism mutations were highly correlated with the DNA methylation levels of two of these CpGs, indicating that epigenetic and
genetic mechanisms in
LRAP1
interact to produce a good or bad
TNF inhibitor response in patients with RA. Additional studies
have confirmed these findings and suggest that changes in DNA
methylation profiles of CD4
T cells, natural killer (NK) cells,

neutrophils, and monocytes are most significantly associated
with a positive TNF inhibitor response.
Beyond contributions to disease pathogenesis and prediction
of therapy response, epigenetic changes may also offer clinically
relevant biomarkers for diagnosis or severity stratification in
many rheumatic diseases. In SLE, a study in 2015 examined
peripheral blood mononuclear cell DNA methylation patterns
from lupus patients, healthy controls, and nonlupus autoimmune rheumatoid arthritis and Sjogren’s syndrome patients
for potential disease-associated biomarkers.
They identified
differentially methylated locations within the
IFI44L
gene as
highly associated with SLE, confirming two specific CpG sites
within this region in a much larger group of patients as part of a
discovery cohort using a high-throughput bisulfite pyrosequencing method. They confirmed the utility of DNA methylation of
these two CpG sites in multiple validation cohorts, including
among the same ethnic group (Chinese) and among a different
ethnic group (Europeans). Among Chinese patients, this assay
performed with a sensitivity in the 90% range, whereas in Europeans, it performed in the 70% to 80% range. A subsequent
study in 2016
identified a differential methylation in naive
T cells of a single CpG within the
CHST12
gene from lupus
patients as highly associated with the presence of lupus nephritis
with a sensitivity of 86% and specificity of 71%. Other studies
have similarly noted strong associations between DNA methylation alterations and disease activity in SLE. These include
IL10
and
IL12
correlations with lupus disease activity
and
IL6
methylation correlations with lupus disease activity, flare,
and serum complement levels.
FOXP3
DNA methylation
is also associated with lupus disease activity,
and retroviral
element
HERV-E
and
HERV-K
methylation is associated with
both disease activity and several autoantibody specificities.
In OA, machine learning models based on peripheral blood
inflammatory cell DNA methylation patterns have been developed to predict future radiographic progression in early-stage
patients.
These models demonstrated an accuracy of 73%, with
a receiver operator characteristic area under the curve (ROCAUC) of 0.81 for prediction of future radiographic progression
24 months in advance, although the cohort studied was small (n
= 116) and no external validation was performed. A 2022 study
expanded on these findings to 554 patients within the larger
OsteoArthritis Biomarkers Consortium (OABC) cohort, including additional progressor subtypes (pain, radiographic, and dual
pain + radiographic), and leveraged the newest DNA methylation array technology.
These updated models demonstrated
increased accuracy (87% for radiographic, 90% for pain, 72%
for dual, and 78% for any progression) 48 months in advance.
Importantly, this study also included external validation in independent cohorts and showed accuracy with ROC-AUC of 80%
and 0.88, respectively.

## Conclusion

Epigenetic dysregulation has been increasingly recognized as a
major factor involved in the pathogenesis of rheumatic diseases.
New technologies have recently emerged allowing ever-moredetailed phenotyping of cellular subsets, including the ultimate
in subphenotyping: single-cell analyses,
which may become the
standard form of epigenetic analysis in the future. The dynamic
nature of epigenetic changes may allow for the development of
novel disease biomarkers. Future studies would focus and expand
on disease subset–specific epigenetic changes. Some of the epigenetic changes that we need to discover and validate in future
studies can also help assess disease activity, predict disease flares,
or determine what treatment option to use in an increasingly personalized medicine approach. A longitudinal approach would also
help address the question of “cause” versus “effect” in epigenomic
changes we associate with the various diseases. Ideally, a longitudinal approach following and collecting biologic samples from a
group of individuals before they develop disease would be most
informative in dissecting the issue of causality.
Another, and a potentially quite powerful, technique to examine the causality of epigenetic changes is the emerging field of
epigenetic editing. Spurred by the development of novel DNA
localization and binding technologies, most notably nucleasedefective Cas9 proteins (dCas9, part of the clustered regularly
interspersed short palindromic repeats, or CRISPR, system), epigenetic editing approaches have now been demonstrated in several
cell types. A sequence-specific epigenetic editing system generally
consists of at least three parts: a DNA-binding domain, a flexible
linker, and a catalytically active epigenetic modification module
(
Fig. 25.5
). To date, epigenetic editing has been demonstrated
using a variety of epigenetic enzymatic effectors.
Several studies directly demonstrating epigenetic editing in rheumatic diseases
have been published.
Cell-specific targeting of epigenetic
modification is also a promising approach that has been demonstrated using lupus-mouse models.
Although still in its infancy,
the field of epigenetic editing, through both specific genomic targeting and delivery to particular cell subtypes, holds great promise
for correcting many of the epigenetic aberrancies that have been
associated with autoimmune diseases.
