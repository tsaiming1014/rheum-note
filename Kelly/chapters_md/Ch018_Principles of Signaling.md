KEY POINTS

> Cells of the immune system sense environmental stimuli via receptors,

> which reside either on the cell surface or intra-cellularly.

> Engagement of receptors by their ligands initiates signaling cascades,

> which lead to changes of cell function.

> Signaling pathways typically involve phosphorylation (by kinases) and

> dephosphorylation (by phosphatases) of molecules.

> Signaling eventually leads to cell responses such as changes in growth,

> activation, proliferation, production of cytokines, and differentiation.

## Introduction

Immune cells respond to a vast variety of stimuli to carry out their
role in maintaining immune homeostasis. Physiologic and innocuous or foreign and dangerous signals must be recognized and
communicated within the cell, where they culminate into cellular
responses such as changes in shape, motility, growth, activation,
differentiation, or production of effector molecules. Distinct cascades of interacting molecules and second messengers connect the
perceived stimuli and relay information into the cytosol and/or
nucleus to initiate effector functions either directly or through the
initiation of gene transcription. Signaling pathways can be categorized based on the mechanisms by which environmental stimuli
are sensed, such as cell surface receptor-mediated interactions or
intra-cellular detection of lipid-soluble molecules or other receptor ligands that are brought into the cytosol. Receptor-mediated
signaling can further be classified by the presence or absence of
enzymatic activity. This chapter focuses on fundamental concepts
of signaling based on these groups of receptors and their intracellular signaling pathways.

## Receptors With Enzymatic Activity

Most ligands are water soluble and therefore impermeable through
the lipid bilayer of the plasma membrane and they interact with
cognate receptors on the cell surface. These ligands include antigens, antibodies/immune complexes, chemokines, cytokines,
microbial components, and metabolites. Interactions of ligands
with their receptors initiate downstream catalytic activity and
recruitment of similar signaling molecules or adaptor molecules.
Receptors with enzymatic activity comprise extra-cellular ligandbinding domains, membrane-spanning domains, and intra-cellular
signaling domains. The intrinsic enzymatic activity of the receptor
may include kinase, phosphatase, or guanylate cyclase activity.
The receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) family is a common class
of these receptors. The human RTK family consists of 20 subfamilies that detect ligands such as stem cell factor, insulin, epidermal
growth factor, vascular endothelial growth factor, platelet-derived

## Receptors That Recruit Molecules With Enzymatic Activity

### Immunoreceptors: T Cell Receptor, B Cell Receptor, and FcRs

Immune cells express antigen recognition receptors that are generated and selected in a complex fashion.
T cells and natural
killer (NK) T cells express the T cell receptor (TCR) and recognize peptides or lipids associated with the major histocompatibility complex (MHC), or CD1d, respectively. B cells, through the
B cell receptor (BCR), recognize soluble antigens. Lymphoid and
myeloid cells, such as NK cells and macrophages, express receptors of the Fc receptor (FcR) family, which cause activation upon
detection of antibodies bound to antigen (immune complex).
These receptors are heteromultimeric complexes, with a subunit
of one or two chains that recognizes the ligand, coreceptors (CD4,
CD8, or CD19 for T and B cells, respectively), and associated or
adaptor molecules that contain from one to six immunoreceptor
tyrosine-based activation motif (ITAM) domains (
Fig. 18.2
).
Antigen recognition by the immune receptors and coreceptors
induces the phosphorylation of the ITAM motifs by a Src family
kinase. The Src family phosphotyrosine kinase family comprises
eight members (Fgr, Fyn, Src, Yes, Blk, Hck, Lck, and Lyn), which

### Co-stimulatory Receptors

T cell activation requires two signals, one mediated through the
TCR and a second signal through a co-stimulatory molecule, a
process known as
co-stimulation
. This signal is provided by a family
of receptor-ligand molecules that mediate interactions between T
cells and antigen-presenting cells (APCs). The B7/CD28 is one of
the most studied pathways of co-stimulation. B7-1 and B7-2 are
expressed by APCs and bind the co-stimulatory molecule CD28
on T cells. This interaction triggers downstream signaling to
amplify the primary signal and initiate effector responses. CD28
is phosphorylated by Lck and activates the PLC
γ
and PI3K/Akt
pathways, which are shared with the CD3/TCR signaling process,
and this explains the amplification of the signal.
An important
consequence of B7/CD28 signaling is to enhance mRNA stability
of IL-2, which is responsible for the increase in interleukin (IL)-2
secretion. Cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4) is closely
related to CD28 in structure but binds to B7 molecules with
much higher avidity and curbs the activation and proliferation of
T cells.
Another co-stimulatory pair involves the inducible costimulator (ICOS), which is expressed by activated T cells, which
interacts with B7-H2/ICOS ligand present on activated dendritic
cells, monocytes, and B cells. The signaling lymphocyte activation molecule (SLAM) family, a subtype of the immunoglobulin superfamily consisting of nine transmembrane proteins, also
functions in a co-stimulatory fashion. SLAMs are important for
the function of multiple cell types, including T, B, and NK cells.
Seven of the members of the SLAM family engage themselves in
a homophilic fashion, and two engage in a heterophilic way. The
SLAM proteins bear a tyrosine-based switch motif through which
they bind the SH2-bearing proteins, SLAM-associated protein
and EAT2, with high affinity.
Once activated, T cells express other co-stimulatory molecules,
such as CD40L and the programmed cell death 1 (PD1) receptor.
CD40L interacts with CD40 on APCs and cross-activates APCs
to further increase their co-stimulation properties. These amplification mechanisms need molecular breaks to prevent uncontrolled immune cell activation. Such breaks are executed by PD1,
expressed on T cells in an inducible manner, which binds B7-H1/
PDL1 and B7-DC/PDL2 expressed on APCs.
PD1 belongs to
the immunoglobulin superfamily, and its intra-cellular domain
bears an immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibitory motif (ITIM)
and an immunoreceptor tyrosine-based switch motif (ITSM). The
tyrosine in the ITSM binds to the phosphatases SHP1 and SHP2,
which inhibit the PI3K/Akt pathway. PD1 signaling inhibits
induction of the anti-apoptotic molecule BcL-xL and transcription factors such as T-bet, GATA-3, and Eomes, which are important for T cell differentiation.
Thus co-stimulatory signals may activate or inhibit TCR/
BCR signaling and participate in the fine-tuning of the immune
response. Defects of these co-stimulatory pathways are of high
importance in the pathogenesis of autoimmune diseases and

### Adhesion Molecules

A variety of cellular functions, including lymphocyte activation,
migration, and cell–cell interaction, involve adhesion molecules.
These include selectins, integrins, and immunoglobulin superfamily molecules. L-selectin (CD62L) is expressed by lymphocytes and
is important for their homing to lymphoid tissues. Platelet- (P-)
selectin is expressed by activated platelets and endothelial cells and
interacts with its ligand P-selectin glycoprotein ligand-1 (PSGL-1)

cells) to promote immune cell rolling and migration.

axis induces outside–in signaling through PSGL-1’s ITAM/SYK
phosphorylation and subsequently affects immune cell function
(

For example, P-selectin engagement on circulating T
regulatory cells leads to downregulation of FoxP3 and limitation
of their suppressive function. Adhesion molecules are responsible
for priming immune cells so they can be fully responsive once they
have migrated to inflammatory sites.

## Sphingosine 1-Phosphate

Sphingosine 1-phosphate (S1P) is a bioactive lipid that derives
from membrane sphingolipids.
S1P may interact with intra-cellular targets (inhibition of histone deacetylases 1/2 or TRAFF) or
exit the cell and engage its receptors in an autocrine or paracrine
manner.
On the cell surface, S1P signals through five GPCRs
(S1PR1 to 5) to regulate cell migration, differentiation, and
survival. Importantly, the extra-cellular concentration of S1P is
highly controlled, especially by its degradation by the S1P lyase,
which is expressed in tissues but not in the blood and lymph.
Hence, S1P concentration is high in the blood and lymph and
scarce in tissues and lymphoid structures. This gradient of S1P
is responsible for the migration of immune cells, which express
S1PRs from the blood marrow or the secondary lymphoid organs
to the blood/lymph. Indeed, the expression of S1PRs is dynamic,
and, after activation in the lymphoid organs, the “trained” T cell
upregulates S1PR1 to promote remigration in the blood and ultimately to a site of inflammation. Drugs that target the S1P/S1PR
pathway have been developed to treat autoimmune and inflammatory diseases. Fingolimod, which is prescribed in multiple sclerosis (a neuroinflammatory disease), promotes the internalization
of S1PR1 and thereby inhibits the sensing of the S1P gradient.
Fingolimod therefore blocks the recirculation of activated T cells
and ultimately their migration in tissues (i.e., the brain). Finally,

## Innate Receptor Signaling

TLRs and nucleotide-binding oligomerization-like receptors
(NLRs) constitute the major pathogen sensors of innate immunity
and work in a cell-autonomous fashion to initiate anti-microbial
responses, including inflammation and apoptosis (
Fig. 18.5
). The
major downstream effect of the cascades initiated by pathogenassociated molecular pattern (PAMP) recognition is IRF transcriptional activation of IFN genes that mediate inflammation
and tissue repair. PAMPs are detected in endosomes and on the
surface of cells by a variety of TLRs. These receptors have leucinerich repeat domains to detect ligands, a single-pass membrane
domain, and an intra-cellular TIR motif for downstream signaling. Their activities are most relevant in macrophages, monocytes,
and dendritic cells.
TLR1, 2, 4, and 6 recognize varieties of endotoxin lipopolysaccharides from gram-negative bacteria, whereas TLR3, 7, 8,
and 9 respond to nucleic acids that may also derive from viruses.
TLR11 detects profilin protein, and the ligand for TLR10 remains
uncharacterized. Response to ligand binding includes dimerization of receptors, which promotes docking of a variety of adaptor
proteins, including myeloid differentiation factor (MyD)88, TIRdomain-containing adaptor protein-inducing IFNβ
(TRIF),
TIR-associated protein (TIRAP), and TRIF-related adaptor molecule (TRAM). A common downstream target of these cascades
is NFκ

cytokines and IFN.
NLRs detect cytosolic PAMPs and endogenous inflammatory
signals. Common motifs in these receptors include the nucleotidebinding oligomerization domain (NOD) and leucine-rich repeat.
There are more than 20 versions of these receptors in mammals.
The best characterized is NOD1/caspase-recruitment domain
protein (CARD) 4 and NOD2/CARD15. NOD1 senses mesodiaminopimelic acid, found in gram-negative strains, whereas the
NOD2 ligand is muramyl dipeptide, present in all bacteria.

## Intra-cellular Receptor Signaling

Steroid hormones are synthesized from cholesterol in the adrenals
and gonads and vitamin D present in dietary sources or produced
in the skin by photochemistry, and they exert direct effects on
gene expression via interaction with nuclear receptors. Because
they are lipid soluble, these hormones can diffuse through plasma
membranes to the nucleus to interact with receptors that directly
modulate transcription, making them potent and common drug
targets. In addition to endogenous molecules, many xenobiotics,
such as endocrine disruptors, modulate these receptors. Almost
half of the roughly 50 mammalian nuclear receptors have poorly
characterized or multiple low-affinity ligands and are therefore
known as
orphan receptors
.
Nuclear hormone receptors are broadly divided into two
classes. Type I are retained in the cytoplasm until ligand binding drives a conformational change that releases binding partners,
such as heat shock proteins, after which they translocate to the
nucleus. Examples include glucocorticoid, androgen, and estrogen receptors. Glucocorticoid agonists, such as dexamethasone
and prednisolone, are important immunosuppressants. Activation not only drives gene expression changes following nuclear
translocation but also transrepression of pro-inflammatory transcriptional complexes, such as AP-1 and NFκ
B.
Another type I nuclear receptor of importance in immune signaling is the retinoic acid receptor (RAR)–related orphan receptor
γ
(ROR
γ
). mRNA for this protein is detectable in a variety of
tissues, but its physiologic role is largely restricted to the ROR
γ
t
isoform, which is critical for the development and maintenance
of Th17 cells. This T helper cell is the primary source of proinflammatory IL-17, which makes ROR
γ
t a target for novel drug
antagonists.
Type II nuclear receptors generally reside on DNA and are
inhibited by corepressor proteins until detected by a ligand.
Examples include thyroid hormone receptor, RAR, and vitamin
D receptor. They act as heterodimers in conjunction with coreceptors, the most common of which is the retinoid X receptor.
Although its major action is to regulate calcium absorption in the
intestine and bone remodeling, vitamin D has increasingly been
recognized as an immune modulator. Macrophages within granulomas convert vitamin D into its active form as part of an innate
immune regulatory cascade.
Activated monocytes also generate
active vitamin D, and its effects are potentiated by IFNγ
. In contrast, IL-4 generated by Th2 attenuates vitamin D effects, perhaps
by stimulating its degradation.

## Adaptor Molecules

Adaptor proteins are noncatalytic and act as docking sites for
other proteins via different motifs. These proteins help receptors
recruit signaling proteins and activate different pathways.

### Transmembrane Adaptor Proteins

CD3 complex and CD79 are molecules that associate with TCR
and BCR, respectively, and consist of one to six chains that contain ITAM domains, which undergo phosphorylation and recruit
other proteins through their SH2 domain (see
Fig. 18.3
).
DAP10 and DAP12 signaling subunits are highly conserved
in evolution and are expressed in a wide variety of cells, but
especially in those of the innate immune system. DAP12 bears a
single ITAM, which, after tyrosine phosphorylation, recruits and
activates SYK in myeloid cells and SYK and ZAP70 in NK cells.
DAP10 has a YINM sequence in its cytoplasmatic tail, which,
after tyrosine phosphorylation, allows the binding of PI3K and a
Grb2–Vav1–SOS1 complex.
Linker for activation of T cells is a transmembrane protein,
whereas SH2-domain-containing leukocyte protein-76 and B cell
linker protein are cytoplasmic. In their absence, activation of Src
and SYK kinases fail to stimulate the pathway and calcium flux
induced by PLC
γ
.

### Grb2 Family

Grb2, a member of a family of proteins that also includes GADS
and GRAP, is an adaptor protein required for signaling by nearly
all RTKs. Grb2 associates with the GEF SOS or Vav using its
SH3 domains and is recruited to phosphorylated RTKs through
its SH2 domain. This event translocates SOS and Vav close to
Ras and Rac, allowing them to catalyze the exchange of GDP
to GTP.
Other molecules with similar function and structure are Crk
and CrkL, which play an important role in B cells, among other
cells.

### Tumor Necrosis Factor Receptor Signaling Adaptor Molecules

Upon TNF binding to the TNFR1, the DD recruits the TRADD
protein, an adaptor protein that subsequently recruits the Fasassociated protein with DD. This triggers activation of caspase 8
and caspase 3, leading to apoptosis. TNF may also activate apoptosis through the mitochondrial pathway, cytochrome C release,
and activation of caspase 9 and caspase 3 (
Fig. 18.4
).

### Myeloid Differentiation Primary Response Gene 88 and IL-1R Associated Kinase

Interaction of TLRs with components of bacterial and viral pathogens, or the activation of the receptors for IL-1, IL-18 or IL-33,
leads to the recruitment of the adaptor molecule MyD88, followed by the recruitment and interaction of different subunits
of the IL-1R-associated kinase (IRAK) family to form a complex
known as the Myddosome, which switches on pro-inflammatory
pathways.

### TIR-Domain-Containing Adaptor-Inducing Interferon- β

Production of type I IFNs depends on TRIF signaling. TRIF
recruits TRAF3, RIP1, and RIP3 and is also implicated in the
activation of IRAK1 and IRAK2.

### TRAM and TIRAP

These adaptor molecules act as bridging adaptors for MyD88 and
TRIF in the context of specific TLRs. In this way, TIRAP binds
MyD88 to TLR2 and TLR4, whereas TRAM couples TRIF to
TLR4.

### Nuclear Factor- κ B

NFκ
B transcription factors can act both as activators and
repressors for gene expression. There are five members in mammalians: RelA (p65), RelB, c-Rel, p50/p105 (NFκ
B1), and
p52/p100 (NFκ
B2), which form different complexes by combinations that can be homo- and heterodimers. NFκ
B complexes
are retained in the cytoplasm by a family of inhibitory proteins
known as
inhibitors of NFκ
B
(I
κ
Bs). IKK, which is the activator
of NFκ
B, is composed of two catalytic subunits and one regulatory subunit known as the
NFκ
B essential modulator
. Activation
of NFκ
B typically involves the phosphorylation of I
κ
B by the
IKK complex, which results in I
κ
B degradation and translocation of NFκ
B to the nucleus, where it drives the transcription of
pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-6, IL-18, chemokines,
cyclooxygenase 2, and 5-lipoxygenase, controlling programmed
cell death (apoptosis), cell adhesion, and proliferation, among
other pathways.

### Activator Protein-1

The AP-1 family in humans is composed of homo- and heterodimer members of the ATF (ATF2, ATF3/LRF1, B-ATF,
JDP1, and JDP2), Fos (c-Fos, FosB, Fra-1, and Fra-2), Jun
(c-Jun, JunB, and JunD), and MAF (c-Maf, MafB, MafA,
MafG/F/K, and Nrl) protein families. The AP-1 complex
binds to the promoters of specific genes and transactivates or
represses them.

### NFAT

There are four NFAT proteins in mammalian cells, NFAT1–4,
which are expressed in different cell types from the immune system. NFAT-family proteins are activated by calcium signaling
downstream of various receptors, including TCR, BCR, and FcR,
and receptors coupled to certain heterotrimeric G proteins. NFAT
is involved in regulation of cytokines like IL-2 and different surface receptors.

### CREB/CREM

CREB and CREM are two transcription factors involved, among
others, in the regulation of IL-2. They compete for binding to
the promoter of
IL-2
and several other genes where CREB
exerts a positive effect on transcription and CREM is a negative
regulator.

## Conclusion

Elucidation of immune cell signaling pathways has provided
valuable insights into pathogenesis of autoimmune and immunodeficiency diseases. Moreover, these molecular pathways have
proven to represent important therapeutic targets. In recent years,
a significant number of new agents (both biologics and small molecules) have been developed to target signaling pathway to treat
autoimmune diseases. A nonexhaustive and ever-growing list
includes JAK/STAT inhibitors, Bruton tyrosine kinase inhibitors,
endosomal TLRs, and inflammasome inhibitors. Among these,
JAK inhibitors are the most widely adopted treatment in a wide
range of organ-specific and systemic autoimmune diseases. JAK
are expressed in virtually all cells, including nonimmune, making JAK inhibition a potent strategy to inhibit inflammation systemically. However, these characteristics are not only responsible
for the adverse effects linked to JAK inhibition, such as cytopenia
(since JAK mediates hematopoietic growth factor signaling), but
also for more a recently and largely unexplained impact on the
risk of thrombosis and cancer development.
Therefore, continued research to develop more targeted compounds with limited
off-target effect may lead to better treatment safety and a global
improvement in the care of patients with autoimmune diseases.
An alternative approach, to take full advantage of the beneficial
effects of cell signaling modulators, involves precision nanomedicine where drugs are encapsulated in nanoparticles that can be
tagged with antibodies or other molecules and directed to deliver
the cargo only to the cell whose function needs to be corrected.
Such approaches are bound to have fewer or no side effects because
the total required dose is only a small fraction of would be needed
if the drug was administered systemically.
