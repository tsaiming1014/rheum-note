## Introduction

Scleroderma or systemic sclerosis (SSc) is an orphan disease of
unknown cause, diverse clinical manifestations, and variable
natural history. The hallmarks of SSc are (1) autoimmunity and
inflammation, (2) functional and structural alterations in small
blood vessels, and (3) widespread interstitial and vascular fibrosis
affecting the skin and most internal organs. This unique constellation of distinct but related pathophysiologic processes, illustrated
in
Fig. 84.1
, defines SSc and accounts for its characteristic clinical
manifestations. In its early stage, SSc is often dominated by clinical manifestations reflecting inflammation and vascular injury; in
advanced stages of disease, intractable fibrosis and vascular insufficiency underlie the prominent manifestations. However, there is
enormous patient-to-patient variability in the presence, severity,
and tempo of progression of these features and ultimate outcomes.
Recent advances in cell and molecular biology, mouse genetic engineering, and cell fate mapping, combined with human metabolic,
microbiome, genetic, and functional genomics studies, have uncovered a large number of molecules, signaling pathways, and cell
types and fate changes associated with SSc. The following sequence
represents a contemporary integrated view of SSc pathogenesis: in
response to environmental triggers, epigenetic modifications in an
individual with permissive genetic background set off a cascade of
events with early systemic vascular injury, platelet and immune cell
activation and metabolic reprogramming, autoimmunity, oxidative
stress, and mesenchymal cell differentiation, resulting in persistent
myofibroblast activation, excessive matrix accumulation, and progressive tissue damage. Over time, chronic vascular insufficiency
and widespread fibrosis lead to architectural disruption and failure
of vital organs, accounting for substantial morbidity and mortality.

## Etiology

The causes of SSc are unknown, and the precise contribution
of genetic predisposition is not well understood. Evidence suggests that environmental exposures, infectious agents, dietary and
lifestyle factors, toxins, and drugs might be potential triggers for
disease pathogenesis directly via inducing immunity and tissue
damage, as well as indirectly through modulating gene expression
via epigenetic mechanisms.

susceptibility, but such clustering might also be explained by shared
environmental exposures, shared genetic background, or the interaction between genes and environment.

compared with the general population (0.026%). In one study for

factor for SSc.

### Genetic Association Studies

Genetic association studies using both candidate gene and hypothesisfree genome-wide association (GWA) approaches have been performed
in large multinational and multiethnic patient cohorts. Similar to other
autoimmune diseases, the strongest genetic association for SSc is with
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) region loci on chromosome
6, including human leukocyte antigen (HLA). The interpretation of
HLA associations is complicated by the extensive linkage disequilibrium of risk haplotypes. Specific HLA alleles are associated with SSc
and more strongly with specific autoantibody profiles. For instance, a
case-control study revealed strong associations of Topo-1–positive SSc
with HLA DRB1*1104, DQA1*0501, and DQB1*0301 haplotypes.
Non-HLA SSc susceptibility loci include two genes within the MHC
region: PSORSIC1 and NOTCH4, with the latter also implicated by
whole-exome sequencing in a rare familiar form of SSc. SSc-associated
genes located outside the MHC region include the protein-tyrosine
phosphatase nonreceptor 22 (PTPN22), which has been associated
with SLE, myasthenia gravis, vitiligo, and Addison’s disease; Sox5, a
cell fate regulator; DNAse 1L3, a homolog of DNase 1 involved in
DNA processing and apoptosis; the c-Src kinase CSK involved in myofibroblast differentiation; and interferon regulatory factor 5 (IRF5), a
transcription factor also associated with SLE, as well as interstitial lung
disease (
Table 84.1
). Several SSc-associated genetic variants are shared
with other autoimmune diseases but have not been linked to other
fibrotic conditions such as idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. In addition
to classic single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), SSc-associated
genetic polymorphisms also include copy number variations and rare
allelic variants, along with epigenetic changes.

In aggregate, the results from
studies of SSc genetics performed to date can be broadly summarized as follows: (1) The majority of SSc susceptibility loci are
located in intronic gene regions that might influence transcription
of genes at a distance from the locus or transcription of noncoding
RNA or represent linkage disequilibrium with coding or regulatory
region genetic variants, (2) many SSc-associated genetic variants
appear to be involved in innate and adaptive immune responses,
and (3) they are frequently shared with SLE and other autoimmune and inflammatory diseases. It needs noting that important
genetic associations can be missed by GWA studies using current
technologies, which can only capture common genetic variants.
Ongoing efforts using whole-exome sequencing to identify rare
coding variants linked to SSc is expected to enhance our understanding of pathogenesis. Despite the wealth of emerging information, the genetic associations discovered to date are of relatively
modest magnitude, with odds ratios that are generally less than
1.5. This finding points to the potential role of gene–gene interactions (epistasis), particularly for genes within the same molecular
pathways. Likely to be even more important are the contributions of the exposome and gene–environment interactions, which
remain largely obscure to date. How genetic risk loci shared
among diverse autoimmune conditions with very distinct clinical
features contribute to disease-specific phenotypes in SSc remains
to be elucidated. Furthermore, the mechanistic links between SScassociated genetic variants and disease pathomechanisms driving
clinical manifestations are still largely unknown.

## Infectious Agents and Viruses

Along with exposure to certain environmental, occupational, and
dietary factors, and drugs, Epstein–Barr virus (EBV), human
cytomegalovirus (hCMV), and parvovirus B19 have been implicated in SSc. Patients with SSc have antibodies directed against
the UL83 and UL94 protein epitopes on hCMV, which can
induce endothelial cell apoptosis and fibroblast activation. Antibodies to topoisomerase-I can cross-react with hCMV-derived
proteins, implicating molecular mimicry as a mechanism linking
hCMV infection and SSc.
In dermal fibroblasts, hCMV stimulates synthesis of profibrotic growth factors.
In a recent study,
genetic material from EBV was identified in SSc dermal fibroblasts and microvessels. Moreover, EBV induces Toll-like receptor
(TLR)–mediated cellular responses in SSc fibroblasts, suggesting
a potential pathogenic role. Although these findings await further confirmation, they raise the intriguing possibility that infections in a genetically predisposed host may trigger the cascade of
endothelial cell injury, inflammation, and fibrosis in SSc. Alterations in the gut microbiome (dysbiosis) have been described in
SSc patients and may contribute to modulation of immune or
fibrotic processes directly or via production of metabolites that are
absorbed into the systemic circulation (discussed below).

## Environmental Exposures, Dietary Factors, Drugs, and Radiation

Although geographic clustering of disease suggests a potential
causal role for shared environmental exposures, careful investigations have generally failed to substantiate apparent clusters of
SSc. On the other hand, well-documented epidemic outbreaks of
SSc-like illnesses with acute onset and chronic course have been
reported. One such illness, called
toxic oil syndrome,
was linked
to the ingestion of contaminated rapeseed cooking oils.
More
recently, consumption of dietary supplements containing l-tryptophan was implicated in an explosive outbreak of the eosinophilia–myalgia syndrome (EMS) in 1989.
The EMS epidemic
subsided after the ban on l-tryptophan, but rare sporadic cases
of EMS associated with ingestion of l-tryptophan and other food
supplements continue to be reported. Although scleroderma-like
skin and organ fibrosis are prominent in these
de novo
toxicoepidemic syndromes, the associated clinical, immunologic, and laboratory features clearly differentiate them from SSc.
The frequency of SSc appears increased in men with occupational exposure to silica dust. This observation was recently confirmed by a meta-analysis of 16 observational studies, with risk
estimates as high as 15.
Other environmental exposures linked
with SSc include polyvinyl chloride, toluene, xylene, trichloroethylene, and organic solvents. Additional reports alleged an association between SSc and pesticides, hair dyes, and industrial fumes.
Although cigarette smoking is known to increase the risk of multiple autoimmune diseases, no evidence to date implicates it as a
risk factor for SSc.
Certain drugs have been implicated in SSc-like illnesses. The
best studied is the anti-cancer drug bleomycin, which induces skin
and lung fibrosis in the mouse (discussed in a subsequent section).
Other drugs potentially implicated include pentazocine, docetaxel
and paclitaxel, and cocaine. Appetite suppressants have been linked
to the development of pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH).
While the occurrence of SSc following cosmetic breast augmentation with silicone implants raised significant concern regarding a
possible causal association,
subsequent large-scale epidemiologic
surveys and a meta-analysis failed to confirm an increased risk of
SSc or of other connective tissue diseases.
Radiation therapy for
malignant neoplasms has been linked with the onset of
de novo

SSc and localized scleroderma, as well as exacerbation of fibrosis
in patients with pre-existing SSc.

## Microbiome Alterations in Systemic Sclerosis

The gut microbiota (the composition of bacteria, fungi, and viruses
in the gut ecosystem) greatly influence the development of immunity and immune tolerance and play a role in several diseases. In
healthy adults, the gut microbiota is dominated by Bacteroides
and Firmicutes phyla. Multiple observational and cross-sectional
studies have documented altered gut microbial composition in SSc
patients compared with geographically matched healthy controls.
In particular, a decrease in bacteria that are generally beneficial,
such as
Faecalibacterium
, and concomitant increase in potentially
noxious genera including
Fusobacterium
, have been described.
These changes could reflect dietary differences, genetic factors, and
geographic location, as well as disease duration. Despite intriguing
experimental evidence that early-life dysbiosis could result in latelife change in fibrosis propensity, for example, it remains unclear
whether and how the observed changes in gut microbiome composition in SSc play a pathogenic role or whether they are a result
of the disease and its complications, such as bowel dysmotility, or
whether the microbiome changes occur iatrogenically in response
to treatment with immunomodulatory drugs.

## Microchimerism

Some studies found elevated numbers of circulating fetal cells in
women with SSc compared with healthy women.
It has been
suggested that persistence of fetal cells in some SSc patients, a
phenomenon known as microchimerism, might be pathogenic
through a graft-versus-host–like response triggered by the fetal
cells or through a maternal (auto)immune response against the
fetal cells. However, the number of fetal cells detected in SSc
patients seems very low, and microchimerism is not restricted to
SSc but is also observed in other autoimmune diseases.

## Pathology

### General Features

The pathologic hallmarks of SSc are noninflammatory proliferative/obliterative vasculopathy affecting small arteries and arterioles in multiple vascular beds, loss of capillaries (rarefication), and
fibrosis that is most prominent in the skin, lungs, and heart.
Tissue inflammatory cells are largely absent in long-standing SSc but
are prominent in some patients with early-stage disease. Infiltrates
are located predominantly around blood vessels and are composed
primarily of CD4
T lymphocytes, B cells, dendritic cells (DCs),
and monocytes/macrophages.

### Vascular Pathology

Vascular injury and activation are the earliest and possibly primary
events in the pathogenesis of SSc. Histopathologic evidence of
vascular damage can be detected in both involved and uninvolved
skin and is present before fibrosis.
Clinical signs of systemic vasculopathy include Raynaud’s phenomenon, commonly an early
manifestation of SSc, along with mucocutaneous telangiectasia;
nailfold capillary alterations (giant capillaries, hemorrhages, and
avascular areas or dropout); PAH; digital tip pitting and ischemic
ulcers; gastric antral vascular ectasia (also called
watermelon stomach
); and scleroderma renal crisis.
The most characteristic histopathologic finding in the small
and medium-sized arteries is bland intimal proliferation (
Fig.
84.2
). Intimal hypertrophy, a finding that SSc shares with chronic
allograft arteriopathy, is thought to result from proliferation and
migration of myointimal cells and local accumulation of collagen and other ECM components.
The vascular basement membranes are thickened and reduplicated. These changes are most
prominent in the heart, lungs, kidneys, and gastrointestinal tract.
A systematic survey of SSc skin biopsies revealed a marked reduction in the number of capillaries (rarefaction) and loss of vascular
endothelial cadherin, a molecule required for vascular tube formation.
Remarkably, this study showed that clinical improvement
after high-dose immunosuppressive therapy was accompanied by
capillary regeneration in the skin.
Impaired fibrinolysis, increased levels of von Willebrand factor,
and ongoing platelet aggregation are prominent and contribute
to a procoagulant milieu. Endothelial cell injury itself causes further platelet aggregation, release of platelet-derived growth factor
(PDGF) and endothelin-1 (ET-1), and endothelial cell apoptosis.
Vasculitic lesions and immune complex deposition in the
vessel walls are uncommon in SSc. In late-stage disease, extensive
fibrin deposition and perivascular/adventitial fibrosis cause progressive luminal occlusion, and eventually there is a striking paucity of small blood vessels and capillaries in lesional tissue.
Loss
of vascular supply leads to chronic tissue hypoxia, which directly
contributes to fibrosis. Widespread proliferative/obliterative vasculopathy of small and medium-sized arteries and capillary rarefaction are the pathologic hallmarks of all forms of SSc. In SSc
patients with PAH, pulmonary arteriolar intimal proliferation and
evidence of veno-occlusive disease are prominent. In contrast to
idiopathic PAH, however, plexogenic arteriopathy is uncommon
in SSc.

### Tissue Fibrosis

Fibrosis due to excessive ECM deposition is a distinguishing
pathologic hallmark of SSc and can synchronously affect virtually every organ. The ECM is a complex and dynamic scaffold
composed of hundreds of proteins that provides architectural support for stromal cells and presents signals that instruct cell fate
and behavior. Fibrosis is characterized by excessive accumulation
of fibrillar collagens, primarily type I and type III, glycoproteins (fibronectin, cartilage oligomeric matrix protein [COMP],
laminins and tenascin) proteoglycans (heparin sulfate, versican),
matricellular proteins (CCN2), elastin, and other structural ECM
components.
Unchecked matrix deposition causes disruption
and, ultimately, complete obliteration of tissue architecture,
impairing the function of affected organs. Fibrosis in SSc is most
prominent in the skin, lungs, gastrointestinal tract, heart, tendon
sheath, and perifascicular tissue surrounding skeletal muscle. Histopathologic examination of these organs shows relatively acellular
connective tissue with thick, densely packed, and hyalinized collagen bundles.

## Organ-Specific Pathologic Findings

as a diverse group of scleroderma-like syndromes. The severity of
skin fibrosis, measured clinically by palpation, tends to correlate
with fibrosis in internal organs, as well as disease outcomes. Fibrosis is accompanied by marked expansion of the dermis with obliteration of the hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and
other skin appendages and replacement of the intradermal white
adipose layer. In the fibrotic dermis, there is an increase in collagen accumulation; moreover, collagen bundles show a loss of the
random alignment characteristic of normal skin. Recent studies

### Gastrointestinal Tract

The gastrointestinal tract is virtually always affected in SSc, and
pathologic changes are seen at any level from the mouth to the

## Pathogenesis

### Integrated Overview

The pathogenesis of SSc is complex and poorly understood. As
previously discussed, SSc shows a high degree of patient-to-patient
variability, and existing animal models only imperfectly capture its
diverse pathologic, immune, and clinical attributes and dynamic
natural history. A comprehensive systems-level paradigm for SSc
pathogenesis needs to integrate the following cardinal “intermediate pathomechanisms”: vascular damage, activated innate and
adaptive immunity, and persistent myofibroblast activation resulting in synchronous and irreversible fibrosis in multiple organs.
Each of the three “intermediate pathomechanisms” is operative in
patients with SSc; however, the relative contribution of individual processes to the disease manifestations may vary considerably
from one patient to another and at different stages over the course
of the disease. As illustrated in

, complex and dynamic
interplay between these distinct processes is responsible for initiating, amplifying, and sustaining tissue damage underlying the
symptoms and signs of SSc.

### Vascular Damage and Failure of Compensatory Vascular Repair

In medium-sized and larger vessels, a combination of intimal
and medial hypertrophy and adventitial fibrosis occurs. Together
with endothelial cell apoptosis and thrombosis, the process culminates in obliterative vasculopathy with the characteristic paucity of blood vessels seen by angiography in late-stage SSc. Loss of
microvasculature results in reduced blood flow and chronic tissue
hypoxia, which induces hypoxia-inducible factor-1 (HIF-1)–
α
dependent genes, such as VEGF and its receptors, and promotes
myofibroblast transformation.
The balance between angiogenic and anti-angiogenic factors
is fundamentally dysregulated in SSc. The plasma levels of the
angiogenesis inhibitor endostatin, a degradation product of type
XVIII collagen, are elevated.
Other investigators found elevated
levels of the angiogenic factors such as VEGF and its receptors, basic fibroblast growth factor, IL-8, CXCL-12, and PDGF
in circulation and in tissue.
It remains unclear why, in the
face of tissue hypoxia and elevated angiogenic factors, there is a
failure of compensatory angiogenesis or vasculogenesis in SSc.
An intriguing possibility implicates alternate mRNA splicing of
VEGF to generate an anti-angiogenic isoform called VEGF
The VEGF
isoform is released by SSc platelets and is overexpressed in SSc skin, suggesting that an enzyme-mediated alternate mRNA splicing event causing a switch from angiogenic to
anti-angiogenic VEGF isoform plays a crucial role in the failure to
mount an appropriate compensatory response to hypoxia in SSc.
Vasculogenesis, or
de novo
blood vessel formation, is critically
dependent on bone marrow–derived circulating endothelial progenitor cells (EPCs) and their differentiation into mature endothelial cells. A reduction in the number of CD34
EPCs and their
impaired differentiation into mature endothelial cells has been
documented in patients with SSc.
Because EPCs are required
for physiologic vasculogenesis in ischemic tissues, defective progenitor cell mobilization or function might compromise vascular
repair. Whether the reduction in circulating EPCs in SSc is due
to diminished release from the bone marrow as a result of fibrosis
or “exhaustion” or their destruction in the peripheral circulation
remains unresolved.

### Hypoxia

The widespread microangiopathy and resultant capillary loss in SSc
leads to diminished blood flow and consequent hypoxia of affected
tissues. With the onset of fibrosis, excessive ECM accumulation
increases diffusion distance from blood vessels to cells, further
aggravating tissue hypoxia.
Hypoxia is itself a potent inducer of
the basic helix–loop–helix transcription factors HIF-1
α
and HIF1
β

rapid proteasomal degradation mediated by the protein von Hippel–Lindau (pVHL) tumor suppressor. In hypoxic cells, pVHL is
unable to bind to its target, and therefore HIF-1
α
is protected from
degradation and translocates into the nucleus, where it binds to
hypoxia-responsive DNA elements and induces the transcription of
VEGF and other hypoxia-regulated genes involved in erythropoiesis, angiogenesis, and glucose metabolism. Hypoxia is also a potent
stimulus for upregulation of ECM genes, including collagens, prolyl
hydroxylases, and lysyl oxidase, and directly stimulates epithelial–
myofibroblast differentiation.

profibrogenic responses are partially mediated by autocrine TGFβ

is provided by a recent study that shows increased fibrosis in mice
with myeloid cell-specific deletion of VEGF.

### Oxidative Stress and Reactive Oxygen Species

Oxidative stress results when there is an imbalance between the
generation of chemically reactive oxygen-derived molecules called
ROS and anti-oxidant defense mechanisms. Substantial evidence
indicates a role for elevated ROS generation with SSc.
The
endothelium generates ROS in response to episodes of ischemiaperfusion, while fibroblasts generate ROS in response to TGFβ
, PDGF, angiotensin II, and ET-1, or stimulatory anti-PDGFR
antibodies. Explanted SSc dermal fibroblasts and monocytes
spontaneously produce ROS.
Several cellular enzymes, as well as
mitochondria, can generate ROS. In SSc, increased activity of the
membrane-associated enzyme nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide
.
phosphate oxidase (Nox 4) plays a predominant role. Hydrogen
peroxide and other oxygen free radicals can stimulate collagen synthesis, myofibroblast differentiation, and secretion and activation
of TGFβ
and Wntβ
–catenin pathways, as well as induce cellular senescence. Alterations in cellular metabolism are increasingly
recognized in SSc. These include an increase in glycolysis, generation of lactic acid and acidic microenvironment, and reduction in
fatty acid oxidation. Similar to the process of biologic aging, SSc
cells show evidence of mitochondrial stress and altered mitophagy.
These mitochondrial changes contribute to metabolic reprogramming, cellular stress, genomic instability, and genotoxic damage.

## Inflammation and Immune Dysregulation in the Pathogenesis of Systemic Sclerosis

In early-stage disease, biopsies from lesional skin and lungs may
show perivascular accumulation of mononuclear cells, macrophages, pDCs, mast cells, and innate lymphoid cells (ILCs). In
the blood, the number of circulating monocytes is elevated, and T
cells, B cells, monocytes, and DCs show evidence of activation and
polarization. The “type I IFN signature” characteristic of SLE and
other autoimmune diseases can also be seen in patients with SSc.
Humoral autoimmunity manifested by highly specific and mutually exclusive autoantibodies can be detected even prior to clinical
disease manifestations and is prominent throughout the course of
the disease (
Fig. 84.9
). The striking genetic associations of SSc with
the class II HLA locus, as well as numerous genes related to B and
T cell function and innate immunity (see above), provide compelling support for an immunologic basis of disease risk. Results from
recent therapeutic trials of global (myeloablative) and targeted
immunomodulatory interventions in SSc provide further evidence
that the immune system contributes to progressive disease.

Patients with SSc display an altered Th1/Th2 cytokine balance
with a predominant Th2 profile. For example, IFNγ
serum levels
and in vitro IFNγ
production by peripheral blood monocytes are
reduced in SSc. Microarray analysis of SSc peripheral blood leukocytes showed elevated expression of GATA3, which controls Th2
polarization. Cloned CD4
T cells from SSc skin biopsies secrete
IL-4 but not IFNγ
.
T cell lines generated from SSc skin biopsies
or alveolar fluid produce high levels of Th2 cytokines, and the Th2
predominance predicts accelerated decline in lung function.
Proteomic and DNA microarray transcriptome analysis also demonstrated a Th2 cytokine predominance in SSc bronchoalveolar
lavage fluids and CD8
lymphocytes. Furthermore, a longitudinal
study of patients with SSc showed that skin improvement over
time was associated with a decline in serum levels of Th2 cytokines
and a concomitant increase in IL-12, a Th1 cytokine.
Other T Cell Subsets in Systemic Sclerosis
Th17 lymphocytes are implicated in the pathogenesis of rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory diseases. Studies described

increased numbers of IL-17–producing CD4
T cells in both
peripheral blood and bronchoalveolar lavage fluid in SSc, but their
direct roles in pathogenesis remain to be elucidated.
Suppressor Treg cells (Foxp3/CD25) are important in controlling autoimmunity and are implicated in SSc. Notably, the organs
prominently affected in SSc, including the skin, gut, and lungs,
harbor a majority of the body’s pool of Tregs. One study found that
although the frequency of peripheral blood Tregs was increased in
patients with SSc, these Treg cells showed impaired suppressive
capacity and defective secretion of TGFβ
and IL-10.
Other
studies demonstrated reduced numbers of Foxp3
CD4
T cells in
the lesional skin in SSc compared with other inflammatory skin
diseases. Mice with transient genetic ablation of Tregs developed
skin fibrosis. Enhanced Treg engraftment and an increase in the
number of circulating Tregs correlates with clinical improvement
in SSc patients undergoing autologous hematopoietic transplantation, further supporting an anti-fibrotic beneficial role of Tregs
in SSc.

### Monocytes and Macrophages

Monocytes and macrophages originating from circulating or tissue-resident progenitors are emerging as key actors in tissue repair
and fibrosis. Macrophages regulate both innate immunity and tissue repair by virtue of their ability to secrete paracrine regulators
of cell function.
Bone marrow–derived monocytes are a major source of cytokines and chemokines, including IL-1, monocyte chemotactic
protein-1 (MCP-1), PDGF, and TGFβ
, each of which is important in inflammatory and fibroproliferative processes. Additionally, monocytes produce collagenases and other matrix-degrading
enzymes and their inhibitors, as well as collagen–cross-linking
enzymes such as transglutaminase 2, a potential therapeutic target in SSc. In early-stage SSc, macrophages are prominent in the
lesional skin and express activation markers Siglec-1 and AIF-1.
SSc skin and lung biopsies show a prominent gene signature of
alternately activated macrophages.
In SSc patients, circulating monocytes express elevated levels of CCL18 and CD163, an alternatively activated monocyte/
macrophage marker, and their numbers are correlated with the
severity of lung disease. The number of macrophages expressing
CD163, CD204, MARCO, and other activation markers is also
elevated in the lesional skin and lungs. Inflammatory monocytes
characterized by surface expression of the scavenger macrophage
receptor with collagenous structure (MARCO), CD206, and the
NAD hydrolase ectoenzyme CD38 accumulate in affected organs
and play a role in fibrosis. Selectively targeting monocytes or macrophages, or particular subsets of these cells, might represent a
potential approach to therapy.

### Dendritic Cells

DCs bearing CD11c surface markers are potent antigen-presenting cells that reside in skin and other tissues and bridge
innate and adaptive immunity by modulating T cell, B cell, and
monocyte/macrophage function (see
Fig. 84.9
). DCs are broadly
classified as conventional (myeloid) and plasmacytoid (pDCs).
Primarily known as the major source of type I IFN, pDCs play
a fundamental role in innate anti-viral immunity. pDCs are
generally absent from normal nonlymphoid tissue but under
the influence of chemokines such as CCL19 and chemerin are
recruited to sites of inflammation. They secrete inflammatory
and profibrotic cytokines and chemokines including CXCL4
(also called PF4) and TGFβ
, suggesting that they may participate in fibrogenesis independent of their T cell–stimulatory
properties. pDC activation is mediated via TLR7 and TLR9,
which recognize endogenous DNA and RNA in the form of
immune complexes. Recent studies show that activated CD11c
DCs accumulate in lesional tissue in animal models of fibrosis
and in patients with SSc.
Activated pDCs might directly or
indirectly modulate activation of resident fibroblasts. However,
their precise roles and mechanisms of action in SSc pathogenesis
require further investigation.

### Mast Cells and Innate Lymphoid Cells

Mast cells accumulate in fibrotic tissue in SSc, as well as in animal
models. Activated mast cells release fibrogenic cytokines including
TGFβ
, IL-4, and IL-13, as well as serine proteases such as tryp
tase that activate profibrotic receptors on fibroblasts. In a recently
described transgenic mouse model, activated mast cells adhere to
fibroblasts, and this heterotypic interaction resulted in fibroblast
activation and fibrosis.
ILCs are a recently identified cell type
that represents the innate immune counterpart of polarized T
helper cells. ILCs are elevated in lesional tissue, as well as in the
circulation in SSc; appear to be skewed to an ILC2-polarized phenotype
; and are potential sources of fibrogenic cytokines such
as IL-13. However, the precise role of ILCs in SSc pathogenesis
remains to be defined.

## Humoral Autoimmunity and B Cells

### Autoantibodies in Systemic Sclerosis: Pathogenetic Considerations

Humoral autoimmunity, manifested by serum autoantibodies
with multiple antigenic specificities, can be detected in virtually
all SSc patients and can precede clinical disease manifestations by
years. The presence of SSc autoantibodies aids in diagnosis and
classification, as well as predicting organ-specific complications
and clinical course. While a direct role of SSc autoantibodies in
tissue damage has been proposed, to date this has not been conclusively established. There is some evidence that the levels of SSc
autoantibodies, particularly anti-DNA topoisomerase I, correlate
with the extent of skin and lung fibrosis and fluctuate with disease
activity.
Various hypotheses exist to explain the generation of autoantibodies in SSc. Self-antigens such as DNA topoisomerase I might,
in the presence of ROS, undergo proteolytic cleavage, exposing
cryptic epitopes that break immune tolerance.
Other potential
mechanisms include molecular mimicry as a consequence of viral
infection, chronic B cell hyperreactivity, and altered expression
or subcellular localization of potential autoantigenic peptides.
Additionally, SSc autoantibodies might be generated by B cells
in response to endogenous TLR ligands such as nucleic acid–containing cellular debris.
Recent reports in SSc described a range of functional antibodies directed against a variety of ECM components, cell surface
receptors, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells. These stimulatory
autoantibodies can induce target cell activation or apoptosis
in vitro.
In one study, autoantibodies against PDGFRs were
detected in patients with SSc and shown to induce ROS generation and myofibroblast differentiation in cultured fibroblasts. It
is not known whether these autoantibodies precede, or are consequent to, fibrosis, and their direct pathogenic role in SSc remains
to be established.

### B Cells in Systemic Sclerosis

Recent reports and clues from genetic studies provide increasing
evidence for a potential direct role for B cells in the pathogenesis of SSc. B cells have multiple immune regulatory functions in
addition to the generation of antibodies, including antigen presentation, IL-6 and cytokine production, lymphoid organogenesis, and T cell differentiation. Candidate gene and GWA studies
in SSc have implicated several B cell genes, including
BANK1,
BLK, CSK,
and
PTPN22,
all of which are involved in regulating
B cell signaling. Although increased B cells were found in the
lungs in SSc-associated interstitial lung disease,
they are not
generally strictly in lesional skin and are found in surrounding
blood vessels. Nevertheless, microarrays demonstrate molecular
signatures indicative of B cell and plasma cell activation in SSc
skin biopsy specimens, particularly in early-stage disease. Patients
with SSc display intrinsic B cell abnormalities, with elevated
numbers of naïve B cells, reduced numbers of plasma cells, and
increased expression of activation markers CD95 and CD86, as
well as CD19, a cell surface receptor that regulates intrinsic and
antigen receptor–induced B cell responses.
Transgenic mice
that overexpress CD19 develop spontaneous autoimmunity and
high titers of anti-topoisomerase I antibodies. Chronic B cell
activation in SSc also may contribute directly to fibrosis, because
activated B cells secrete profibrotic TGFβ
and IL-6. Levels of
BAFF (B cell activating factor belonging to the TNF family) are
elevated in the serum and in lesional skin in SSc patients, as is
the expression of the BAFF receptor on B cells. BAFF specifically
stimulates effector B cells and promotes fibrogenic cytokines.
Depletion of B cells, or blocking BAFF, in the mouse attenuates scleroderma. Clinical trials of B cell depletion by targeting
CD20 or CD19 have reported improvement in SSc, particularly
in patients who show an elevated plasma cell gene signature in
baseline skin biopsies.

### Type I Interferon Signature and Innate Immune Signaling: Parallels With Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

Stimulation of pDCs by ligands of TLR3, TLR7, and TLR9
results in robust secretion of type I IFN. Accordingly, the presence of IFN or IFN-dependent cellular responses suggests ongoing TLR-mediated innate immune signaling. Type I IFNs (
α
and
β
) are themselves potent modulators of innate immunity. Elevated expression of IFN-regulated genes (the “IFN signature”) in
peripheral blood cells, indicative of their exposure to IFN in the
circulation or in the periphery, was first described in patients with
SLE.
Circulating immune complexes containing antibodies to
nucleic acids serve as endogenous TLR ligands that stimulate type
I IFN secretion by pDCs and macrophages. Similar to SLE, a
prominent IFN signature is detected in leukocytes from patients
with SSc. Indeed, a recent study reported that the most highly
expressed genes in SSc peripheral blood leukocytes were IFNregulated.
Moreover, incubation of leukocytes with SSc sera
induced secretion of IFN, indicative of TLR activation by nucleic
acid–containing immune complexes in the serum.
While these
observations, along with immunogenetic studies, highlight parallels between SLE and SSc, the reasons why these two related
autoimmune diseases display largely nonoverlapping clinical phenotypes remain elusive. Moreover, the pathogenetic role of type I
IFN in SSc vasculopathy and fibrosis and the mechanisms responsible remain to be established.

## Fibrosis

Fibrosis is characterized by replacement of normal tissue architecture with rigid pauci-cellular connective tissue. Fibrosis in multiple organs is the distinguishing pathologic hallmark of SSc that
results from a series of vascular and immune-mediated responses
to injury and is perpetuated by self-amplifying cross-talk between
stromal cells and the scar
Fig. 84.10
.

### Extra-cellular Matrix

The connective tissue consists of a cellular compartment of resident fibroblasts, myofibroblasts and other stromal cell types, and
infiltrating cells, all embedded in an ECM composed of collagens,
proteoglycans, elastin, microfibrils, adhesive proteins, and other
structural proteins. The ECM additionally serves as a reservoir for
sequestering growth factors and matricellular proteins that control
the differentiation, function, and survival of mesenchymal cells.
To date, a comprehensive characterization of the fibrotic ECM,
called the “matrisome” has not been achieved. Excessive ECM
accumulation results from overproduction by stromal cells in
response to autocrine and paracrine signals such as cytokines and
chemokines; hypoxia and ROS; a variety of biologic, structural,
and mechanical signals emanating from the surrounding ECM; or
via cell–cell interactions (
Table 84.3
). Impaired ECM degradation
and turnover, as well as expansion of the tissue pool of ECMproducing mesenchymal cells, also play roles.

### Regulation of Collagen Synthesis

The collagens comprise a family of more than two dozen structural
proteins with critical roles in organ development, growth, and differentiation. The skin, bones, and tendons are composed largely
of type I collagen, the most abundant protein in mammals, with
smaller amounts of associated type III collagen. Type II collagen
is found mainly in articular cartilage, whereas type IV collagen is
found in the basement membranes. The fibrillar collagens consist of three
α
chains wound into a characteristic triple helix. This
structure is made possible by the presence of a glycine at every
third residue of repeating Gly-X-Y sequence, where X is frequently
proline and Y is frequently hydroxyproline. The cellular biogenesis
of proline is regulated by PYCR1, which is upregulated in fibrosis. Fibrillar collagens undergo extensive enzymatic modifications
inside the cell followed by additional extra-cellular maturation
and assembly. Covalent cross-linking stabilizes the collagen fiber
network in the extra-cellular space.

tissue requirements during development and wound healing
(

### Effector Cells of Fibrosis: Fibroblasts

Fibroblasts are embryonic mesenchyme-derived stromal resident
versatile cells capable of both ECM production and resorption.
Although at homeostasis adult fibroblasts are biosynthetically
quiescent, upon injury they are triggered to secrete ECM macromolecules and proteolytic enzymes, growth factors, cytokines,
and chemokines; adhere to and contract connective tissue; transdifferentiate into apoptosis-resistant myofibroblasts; or undergo
cellular senescence. Under normal conditions these biosynthetic,
proinflammatory, adhesive, and prosurvival functions combine to
enable fibroblasts to execute effective wound healing. In contrast
to homeostatic ECM maintenance in which the fibroblast repair
program is tightly regulated and self-limited, pathologic fibrosis is
characterized by uncontrolled fibroblast activation that results in
exaggerated, disorganized, and persistent ECM accumulation and
remodeling. Fibroblasts derived from diverse anatomic locations
retain their distinct gene expression profiles.
The so-called “positional memory” of fibroblasts is governed by genetic imprinting
by the homeobox (HOX) family transcription factors. Moreover,

recent studies using single-cell RNA sequencing of samples from
different organs, in steady state as well as in perturbed states, identified fibroblast populations that are common across organs and
fibroblast populations that are tissue- or disease-specific.
Some
of the universal fibroblasts can serve as a reservoir for differentiation into specialized, tissue- or context-specific fibroblast populations. Healthy human lungs appear to contain two major and
one minor fibroblast population characterized by high expression
of SPINT2 (serine peptidase inhibitor, Kunitz type 2), MFAP5
(microfibril-associated protein 5), and WIF1, respectively. Similarly, in the skin two major fibroblast subpopulations defined by
the expression of SFRP2 (secreted frizzled-related protein 2)/DPP4
and FMO1 (flavin-containing dimethylaniline monooxygenase
1)/LSP1 (lymphocyte-specific protein 1), respectively, as well as
five minor populations, each of them expressing distinctive genes
(CRABP1 [cellular retinoic acid-binding protein 1), COL11A1,
FMO2, PRG4 [proteoglycan 4], or C2ORF40 [augurin]), have
been identified.
Further work provided evidence that myofibroblasts in SSc arise from a fraction of SFRP2
/DPP4-expressing fibroblasts that additionally expresses SFRP4 and FNDC1.
Another study demonstrated numerical and functional perturbation of an LGR5-positive homeostatic fibroblast subpopulation in
SSc skin.
These LGR5-positive fibroblasts express several genes
associated with ECM degradation (including MMP2, TIMP2,
and the dipeptidyl peptidase IV [DPP4/CD26]), genes associated
with vascular formation and remodeling, platelet aggregation and
coagulation, and atypical receptors for the chemokines CXCL11
and CXCL12.
Together, these emerging studies unravel a profound heterogeneity of fibroblasts and identify disease-specific
populations that may open venues for specific targeting of these
pathogenic populations as novel therapeutic approaches in SSc.

### Effector Cells of Fibrosis: Myofibroblasts, Pericytes, Endothelial Cells, and Cellular Plasticity

In fibrosis, the pool of activated resident mesenchymal cells
is expanded not only by proliferation of fibroblasts but also by
transdifferentiation of other stromal progenitor cell types, as well
as by the influx of bone marrow–derived progenitor cells. Myofibroblasts are characterized by the incorporation of
α
–smooth
muscle actin into cytoskeletal stress fibers. They are specialized
contractile nonmuscle mesenchymal cells in the skin and other
parenchymal organs that arise from resident fibroblasts, as well
as epithelial, endothelial, and perivascular mural cells (pericytes)
and preadipocytes. While no unique distinguishing surface markers have been identified to date, myofibroblasts display elevated
levels of cadherin-11, integrins and receptors for PDGF (PDGFRA), secrete fibronectin-EDA, matricellular proteins such as
CCN2, collagens, tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases (TIMPs),
and other structural components of the ECM and are a major
site of TGFβ
activation during the fibrotic response. In normal
wound healing, myofibroblasts transiently populate early granulation tissue, followed by their disappearance via apoptosis. Indeed,
the ordered removal of activated myofibroblasts is a crucial step
in normal wound repair. In contrast, failure of effective removal
of myofibroblasts by apoptosis in pathologic fibrogenesis leads to
their persistence in lesional tissue, contributing to the formation
of a rigid and contracted ECM characteristic of a chronic scar.
This may offer potential for therapeutic intervention. A recent
study provided evidence that mitochondria in activated myofibroblasts are primed by death signals, which creates a requirement for
tonic expression of the anti-apoptotic proteins. In consequence,
myofibroblasts were found to be particularly susceptible to apoptosis induced by “BH3 mimetic” drugs.
Residing within the walls of small blood vessels, pericytes
(mural cells) are in intimate contact with the underlying endothelium that regulates vascular homeostasis. In patients with SSc, a
marked increase in the microvascular pericyte compartment and
increased expression of PDGFRs have been reported. Activated
pericytes expressing CD248, ADAM12, or the hedgehog (HH)
pathway transcriptional activator Gli1 can transdifferentiate into
collagen-producing myofibroblasts in fibrosis. The pericyte-myofibroblast transition is thought to represent a link between microvascular injury and fibrosis.
Under certain conditions, epithelial cells can also undergo
phenotypic transformation to fibroblasts. EMT plays a vital role
during vertebrate embryonic development. Pathologic EMT is
prominent in cancer and has also been implicated in renal fibrosis
and idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, although its significance in SSc
pathogenesis has not been established. Similar to epithelial cells,
vascular endothelial cells, mesothelial cells, and intradermal adipocytes can also transition to myofibroblasts upon fibrotic injury.
These processes have been demonstrated in various forms of experimental fibrosis and might also be implicated in SSc. Whether
myofibroblasts originating from different progenitors show identical or distinct phenotypes and functions remains to be determined.

### Fibrocytes and Monocyte-Derived Mesenchymal Progenitor Cells

Fibrocytes are rare bone marrow–derived CD34
circulating mesenchymal progenitor cells that can present antigen and synthesize
collagen.
They express CD14
(a monocyte marker), as well as
chemokine receptors (CCR3, CCR5, and CXCR4), which allows
them to accumulate in specific tissues. The role for fibrocytes in
fibrosis was established in animal models using neutralizing antibodies and in CXCR4-deficient mice. Mice with accelerated senescence have an increased number of circulating fibrocytes and show
increased sensitivity to bleomycin-induced fibrosis. Other studies
have identified monocyte-derived mesenchymal progenitor cells
in peripheral blood. Despite these experimental observations, the
pathogenic significance of bone marrow–derived progenitor cells
in SSc remains unknown.

## Cellular Senescence and Fibrosis in Systemic Sclerosis

Cellular senescence, a hallmark of biologic aging, is a state of stable
cell cycle arrest. Cells exposed to genomic, proteomic, oxidative, or
metabolic stress stop dividing, which normally serves as a protective mechanism to limit transmission of deleterious mutations.
However, as senescent cells progressively accumulate in tissues,
due in part to inefficient immune clearance, their deleterious
effects, including promotion of aberrant tissue remodeling, may
come to predominate. Accumulation of senescent cells in many
organs is a hallmark of aging. Detrimental effects of senescent
myofibroblast accumulation in the skin and lung include depletion of the mitotically active progenitor cell pool and secretion
of a senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) secretome,

composed of PAI-1, matrix-degrading metalloproteases, and prototypical inflammatory and profibrotic chemokines and cytokines
including TGFβ
, IL-6, MCP-1, and PDGF. While elevated
expression of p16 and p21 and expression of
β
-galactosidase are
commonly used as markers of senescent cells, precise identification of senescent cells remains problematic, as no unique markers
have been determined to date. Recent studies indicate that organ
fibrosis is accompanied by premature accumulation of senescent
myofibroblasts that are characterized by expression of prosurvival
and anti-apoptotic pathways (ephrins and Bcl-2), mitochondrial
alterations, elevated expression of NAD-depleting enzymes CD38
and NNMT, and reduced activity of anti-aging HDAC enzymes
(sirtuins). These senescent myofibroblasts also display evidence of
genome and telomere damage, and the SASP secretome. While
these findings support the view of fibrosis as an “accelerated aging
phenotype,” the importance of chronic cellular senescence in SSc
pathogenesis and as a therapeutic target using “senolytic” strategies still remains to be determined.

## Molecular Determinants of Fibrosis: Transforming Growth Factor- β

The expression of ECM genes is normally tightly controlled by
paracrine/autocrine mediators, cell–cell contact, hypoxia, and
contact with the surrounding ECM. Of the multitude of cytokines implicated in SSc (see
Table 84.3
), TGFβ
is considered to
be the master regulator of both physiologic fibrogenesis (wound
healing and tissue repair) and pathologic fibrosis.
A member of a large cytokine superfamily that also includes activin
and bone morphogenetic proteins, TGFβ
is secreted by platelets,
monocytes/macrophages, DCs, and fibroblasts, and most cell types
express surface receptors for TGFβ
. TGFβ
plays essential roles in
tissue repair, angiogenesis, and immune regulation, and its dysregulation is implicated in cancer, fibrosis, and autoimmunity. TGFβ
is
generally secreted as a latent complex that is sequestered in an inactive form within the ECM. Upon injury, latent TGFβ
is converted
in situ to its biologically active form. Activation of latent TGFβ
is
controlled in part by fibrillin-1 and is mediated by cell membrane
integrins, thrombospondins,
α
β
integrin, and proteolytic enzymes.
Because of its fundamental role in orchestrating fibrotic responses,
TGFβ
is considered a potential therapeutic target in SSc.

TGFβ

fibroblast proliferation, migration, adhesion, and senescence, and
it suppresses the production of matrix-degrading metalloproteinases (

adipocytes, TGFβ
drives transdifferentiation into myofibroblasts.

TGFβ

In addition to TGFβ
, soluble mediators such as CTGF, PDGF,
IL-4, IL-6, IL-11, IL-13, FGF9, adenosine, prostaglandin F2
α
,
adipokines, and lysophosphatidic acid (LPA) are aberrantly
expressed, contribute to SSc pathogenesis, and represent potential
therapeutic targets.

### Connective Tissue Growth Factor/CCN2

CTGF (also called CCN2) is a cysteine-rich 40 kDa matricellular
growth factor that is implicated in angiogenesis, wound healing,
and development. Although it is undetectable in healthy adults,
CTGF is markedly elevated in fibrotic tissues. In SSc patients,
serum CTGF levels correlate with the extent of skin and pulmonary fibrosis. The expression of CTGF can be induced by TGFβ
,
IL-4, and VEGF, whereas TNF and prostacyclins block stimulation. Transgenic mice overexpressing CTGF develop diffuse skin
fibrosis and microvascular disease.
While cellular receptors for
CTGF and the mechanism of action underlying CTGF profibrotic responses remain incompletely characterized, targeting
CTGF with monoclonal antibodies represents a potential therapeutic strategy for controlling fibrosis.

### Platelet-Derived Growth Factor

PDGFs are disulfide-bonded heterodimeric proteins that act
mainly on stromal cells and play key roles in wound healing. Originally isolated from platelets, PDGF isoforms are also secreted from
macrophages, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and senescent cells. By
signaling via the
α
and
β
transmembrane PDGFRs, PDGF acts
as a potent fibroblast mitogen and chemoattractant and induces
ROS generation and synthesis of collagen, fibronectin, and proteoglycans, as well as the secretion of TGFβ
1, MCP-1, and IL-6.
Transgenic mice expressing a constitutively active PDGFα
receptor spontaneously develops skin and multiple organ fibrosis.
Lesional skin fibroblasts from patients with SSc show elevated
PDGF and PDGFβ
receptor expression,
and PDGF levels
are increased in SSc bronchoalveolar lavage fluid. Some patients
with SSc appear to have functional circulating antibodies to the
PDGFR that can induce fibroblast activation and ROS generation; however, such autoantibodies have also been detected in
patients with graft-versus-host disease, and their significance in
pathogenesis remains to be established.

### Developmental (Morphogen) Pathways: Wnt, Hedgehog, and NOTCH

The morphogens Wnt, HH, and NOTCH are involved in embryonic developmental pathways that appear to be deregulated in
fibrosis and SSc. The Wnts comprise a family of poorly soluble
glycoproteins with dual roles in cell–cell adhesion and transcriptional regulation. While they play essential roles in morphogenesis, stem cell homeostasis, and cell fate determination, abnormal
Wnt signaling is implicated in cancer, rheumatoid arthritis and
osteoarthritis, osteoporosis, PAH, fibrosis, and aging. Intra-cellular Wnt signaling is mediated via both canonical (
β
-catenin) and
noncanonical pathways, with extensive cross-talk with TGFβ
signaling. Transgenic mice overexpressing Wnt10b or a constitutively active mutant
β
-catenin develop noninflammatory tissue
fibrosis.
Lungs from patients with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis show increased nuclear
β
-catenin accumulation at fibrotic foci.
Genomewide transcriptome analysis of SSc skin biopsies shows
elevated expression of Wnt ligands, receptors, and targets. Constitutive Wnt–
β
-catenin signaling in SSc is due, in part, to epigenetic silencing of endogenous Wnt inhibitors such as DKK1.
NOTCH, the transmembrane receptor for Jagged, has a fundamental role in embryonic development, wound healing, and tissue repair. NOTCH signaling via Snail, Twist, and Slug regulates
endothelial cell and fibroblast responses, including myofibroblast
differentiation. NOTCH signaling was markedly enhanced in the
skin of SSc patients. Activity of ADAM17, a proteinase that initiates NOTCH signal transduction, is elevated in SSc skin biopsies.
Inhibition of NOTCH signaling ameliorated fibroblast
activation of experimental fibrosis in mice.
HH signaling
represents another essential developmental pathway involved in

tissue repair. Sonic hedgehog (SHH), the best characterized mammalian HH ligand, binds to its Patched receptor on a variety of
cell types, and triggers an intra-cellular signaling pathway via Gli
family transcription factors. Components of the HH pathways
are elevated in SSc biopsies, and SHH elicits profibrotic responses
in vitro. Besides this canonical activation, Gli transcription factors
can also be induced by TGFβ
to drive the expression of HH regulated genes.
Remarkably, the activity of multiple morphogen
developmental pathways is regulated by their localization in the
primary cilium, potentially implicating altered cilia formation in
aberrant HH and NOTCH signaling in fibrosis.

### Interleukins

Members of the IL-1 family are secreted from multiple cell types.
A recent study showed that SSc epidermal keratinocytes secreted
IL-1
α
, and their coculture with normal skin fibroblasts resulted
in fibroblast activation and myofibroblast differentiation that was
mediated by IL-1
α
. The Th2 cytokine IL-4 stimulates fibroblast
proliferation, chemotaxis, collagen synthesis, and production of
TGFβ
, CTGF, and TIMP. Serum levels of IL-4 are elevated in
SSc, and the number of IL-4–producing T cells is increased in
both peripheral blood and skin. IL-6, which is produced by monocytes, B and T cells, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells, directly
stimulates collagen and TIMP-1 synthesis in fibroblasts and promotes a Th2-polarized immune response. The biologic activities
of IL-6 are mediated via the JAK–signal transducer and activator
of transcription (STAT) intra-cellular signaling pathway that is
shared with multiple other cytokines. Recent observations reveal
evidence of JAK–STAT pathway activation associated with fibrosis
in SSc and provide the rationale for therapeutically targeting JAK
to attenuate fibrotic cellular responses.
Serum levels of IL-6
are elevated in SSc and correlate with the severity of skin involvement. Another Th2 cytokine, IL-13, is implicated in asthma and
a variety of fibrotic conditions. IL-13 has profibrotic effects that
are both indirect via stimulation of TGFβ
production by macrophages and direct via stimulation of fibroblast proliferation and
collagen synthesis. Serum levels of IL-13 are elevated in SSc.

### Chemokines

Chemokines represent a superfamily of more than 40 low-molecular-weight soluble mediators. Originally identified based on their
chemotactic effects on leukocytes, chemokines have a broad range
of cellular targets and biologic activities, including regulation of
angiogenesis, wound healing, and fibrosis. The CC chemokine
MCP-1 stimulates collagen production directly, as well as through
induction of endogenous TGFβ
. Serum and bronchoalveolar
lavage fluid levels of MCP-1, along with those of macrophage
inflammatory protein (MIP)–1
α
, IL-8 (CXCL8), and CCL18,
are elevated in SSc and correlate with the severity of skin fibrosis.
Mononuclear cells and dermal fibroblasts from patients with SSc
spontaneously produce these chemokines, and lesional SSc fibroblasts show constitutively high expression of the MCP-1 receptor
CCR2. Because MCP-1 drives a Th2 response, the MCP-1–
CCR2 axis is thought to play a major role in the pathogenesis of
SSc by both amplifying collagen stimulation and promoting Th2
cytokine production. Significantly, MCP-1 null mice are resistant
to the development of fibrosis induced by bleomycin.
A global
survey of chemokine expression in SSc skin revealed enhanced
levels of MCP-1, CCL19, CXCL13, and MCP-3, particularly in
early-stage disease.
Additional chemokines overexpressed in lesional tissue or
serum in patients with SSc or in animal models of scleroderma
include the CC chemokines regulated upon activation, normal T
cell expressed and secreted (RANTES), pulmonary and activationregulated chemokine (PARC), CXCL4 (also called PF4), as well
as MIP-2 and fractalkine (CXC chemokines). Insulin-like growth
factor-binding protein-1 (IGFBP-1) stimulates collagen synthesis
and fibroblast proliferation and induces TGFβ
. Patients with SSc
have elevated levels of IGF-1 in the bronchoalveolar lavage fluid.
Expression of IGFBP-3 is markedly elevated in SSc fibroblasts.
Adenovirus-mediated overexpression of IGFBP-5 caused chronic
scleroderma-like fibrosis in mice.

### Angiotensin II

Angiotensin II is a vasoconstricting peptide hormone generated
via proteolytic cleavage of angiotensin I by the angiotensin-converting enzyme. The renin–angiotensin system is implicated in
pathologic fibrosis in the heart, liver, lung, and kidneys. Serum
levels of angiotensin II are elevated in patients with diffuse SSc
(dcSSc), functional antibodies targeting the angiotensin II type 1
receptor have been detected. Moreover, angiotensin II is elevated
in SSc dermal fibroblasts. In cultured fibroblasts, angiotensin II
promotes collagen synthesis and TGFβ
production. Chronic subcutaneous administration of angiotensin II induces sclerodermalike skin fibrosis and inflammation in mice, whereas treatment
with an inhibitor of the angiotensin II type I receptor ameliorates
fibrosis.

### Bioactive Lipids

Bioactive lipids are potent modulators of fibroblast function and
tissue repair. Although some prostanoids inhibit fibrotic responses,
prostaglandin F (PGF
) stimulates collagen production and
fibroblast proliferation and is elevated in patients with pulmonary
fibrosis.
Mice with targeted deletion of the PGF receptor are
protected from bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis. Another
lipid mediator capable of activating fibroblasts and inducing collagen release is LTA4.
LPA, generated from membrane phospholipids via hydrolysis, exerts multiple biologic activities through
G protein–coupled transmembrane receptors. LPA has profibrotic
effects including stimulation of fibroblast chemotaxis and CTGF
production. A recent study indicated that LPA induces
α
β
integrin-mediated TGFβ
activation in epithelial cells, contributing
to sustained autocrine TGFβ
signaling.
LPA levels are elevated
in the lungs of patients with pulmonary fibrosis. Moreover, mice
lacking the LPA receptor are protected from bleomycin-induced
skin and lung fibrosis.

### Regulation of Fibroblast Function via Innate Immune Signaling: Toll-like Receptors, Endogenous Ligands, and Inflammasome

The prominent type I IFN signature in SSc leukocytes and genetic
studies showing an association of innate immunity genes with SSc
support the role of TLR-mediated innate immune responses in
pathogenesis. All members of the TLR family are expressed by
normal fibroblasts. Activation of TLR4 by its microbial ligand
LPS plays a critical role in liver fibrosis, with sensitization to
TGFβ
as the underlying mechanism. In SSc, fibroblast TLRs are
activated by endogenous TLR ligands. These so-called
damageassociated molecular patterns
(DAMPs) generated by tissue injury,

autoimmunity, and oxidative stress can be broadly grouped into
three categories: ECM-derived molecules such as hyaluronan
and its small-molecular-weight degradation products, tenascin C, alternatively spliced extra domain A (EDA) fibronectin
(Fn-EDA), and biglycan; cellular stress proteins (alarmins) such
as HMGB1 released by activated platelets and other injured or
necrotic cell types; and nucleic acids and immune complexes. The
levels of TLR3 and TLR4 are elevated in SSc skin and lung biopsies and correlate with disease progression. The damage-associated
molecules Fn-EDA and tenascin-C are alternatively spliced isoforms of ECM proteins that are highly expressed during normal
development but are largely absent in adult tissue at homeostasis. They are transiently generated during tissue injury but during
fibrosis accumulate in lesional SSc skin and lung, where they are
sensed by resident stromal cells as endogenous TLR4 ligands to
elicit persistent profibrotic responses.
The cytosolic innate immune sensors NOD-like receptors
(NLRs), retinoic acid inducible gene I (RIG-I), and NALP3
respond to cytosolic nucleic acids, damage-associated endogenous
molecules, oxidative stress, and environmental signals such as
silica, bleomycin, and gadolinium. Once activated, these sensors
facilitate inflammasome assembly with activation of caspase-1 and
secretion of proIL-1
β
and IL-18.
NLRP1
has been identified as a
susceptibility gene for SSc-associated pulmonary fibrosis. Inflammasome activation and IL-1
β
generation play an important role
in experimental mouse fibrosis and might be involved in SSc as
well.

### Matrix Regulation of Fibrosis via Mechanosensing and Mechanotransduction

The fibrotic scar itself plays a key role in maintaining persistent
myofibroblast activity and impeding the resolution of fibrosis. Resident stromal mesenchymal cells sense structural and
mechanical cues from their surrounding matrix via mechanosensing involving integrin receptors, as well as by recognizing matrixderived biochemical cues, such as tenascin-C, through TLRs and
other pattern recognition receptors. A rigid fibrotic microenvironment triggers cellular mechanotransduction pathways involving
focal adhesion kinases and Yes-associated protein (YAP), as well
as STAT3, which translocate into the nucleus. Mechanotransduction pathways drive myofibroblast activation, while also blocking
myofibroblast apoptosis by inducing expression of the prosurvival
factor BCL-X. In addition to the stiffness of the matrix, fibroblasts also sense structural changes in collagen. It has been recently
shown that increased alignment of collagen bundles in the fibrotic
dermis, in contrast to random collagen orientation characteristic of a normal dermis, is sufficient to induce and maintain an
activated fibroblast phenotype. Thus a fibrotic microenvironment
is both a result and a driver of myofibroblast activation and is
responsible for persistence of fibrosis. Pharmacologic targeting of
mechanosensing and mechanotransduction to prevent persistent
myofibroblast activity and survival are therefore emerging as therapeutic strategies for intractable fibrosis.

### Negative Regulation of Myofibroblast Activity and Matrix Accumulation

In addition to inhibitory cytokines such as IFNγ
and adiponectin, fibroblasts are equipped with a variety of endogenous molecules that repress ECM gene expression and TGFβ
stimulation
in order to prevent excessive scarring after injury. For example,
Smad7 blocks TGFβ
signal transduction by accelerating ubiquitin-mediated TGFβ
receptor degradation. Functional impairment
of Smad7 was demonstrated in SSc fibroblasts. Other cell-intrinsic
endogenous repressors of collagen synthesis potentially implicated
in SSc pathogenesis include the transcription factors Sp3, Fli-1,
p53, and Nrf2; the nuclear hormone receptor peroxisome proliferator-activated receptorγ
(PPARγ
); the deacetylases SIRT1
and SIRT3; and miRNAs such as miR29a. Impaired expression or
function of these endogenous inhibitors might contribute to the
failure to limit fibroblast activation that underlies fibrosis in SSc.

### Interferon- γ

IFNγ
, the prototypical Th1 cytokine, is a negative regulator of
fibroblast activation that abrogates stimulation of collagen gene
expression, fibroblast-mediated matrix contraction, and myofibroblast transdifferentiation elicited by TGFβ
.
Some studies
showed that SSc fibroblasts were relatively resistant to the inhibitory effects of IFNγ
. However, clinical trials of IFNγ
in SSc have
demonstrated a modest and inconsistent improvement in skin
fibrosis.

### Peroxisome Proliferator-Activated Receptor- γ

The nuclear receptor PPARγ
modulates TGFβ
signaling and
mesenchymal cell plasticity and is functionally associated with
fibrosis. Originally identified as a key regulator of adipogenesis,
glucose homeostasis, and metabolism, PPARγ
has a dual function
as both a nuclear receptor and ligand-inducible transcription factor. Electrophilic lipid moieties and prostanoids such as 15d-prostaglandin J
(15d-PGJ
) serve as endogenous ligands for PPARγ
,
while the thiazolidinedione class of insulin-sensitizing anti-diabetic drugs, including rosiglitazone and pioglitazone, are potent
pharmacologic PPARγ
agonists. PPARγ
has profound effects
on vascular and inflammatory processes, and abnormal PPARγ
function is implicated in lipodystrophy, atherosclerosis, PAH, and
inflammatory diseases. In fibroblasts, 15d-PGJ
or pharmacologic
PPARγ
ligands abrogated TGFβ
–induced collagen production,
myofibroblast transdifferentiation, and other fibrotic responses,
while treatment with PPAR agonists mitigated fibrosis in mice.
Both the expression and the activity of PPARγ
are impaired in
patients with dcSSc.
Of note, multiple SSc-associated pathogenic factors, including TGFβ
, Wnt ligands, IL-13, hypoxia,
LPA, and CTGF, inhibit PPARγ
function. However, a recent
clinical trial using a pan-PPAR agonist to treat SSc failed to reach
its primary endpoint.

## Scleroderma Fibroblast

Fibroblasts explanted from SSc skin or lungs display an abnormal
activated phenotype that persists during their serial passage in vitro,
indicating intrinsic and durable cellular reprogramming. However,
ex vivo cultured SSc fibroblasts do not fully recapitulate the altered
profibrotic gene expression profile of SSc skin biopsies, suggesting
that in SSc, fibrosis driven by activated fibroblasts represents a combination of cell-autonomous gene changes together with in vivo
activation by extra-cellular cues, including biophysical and matrix
ultrastructural guidance cues emanating from the microenvironment. The
SSc fibroblast phenotype
is characterized by enhanced
ECM synthesis, constitutive secretion of profibrotic cytokines and
chemokines, activation of FAK and consequent myofibroblast differentiation, insensitivity to inhibitory signals, mitochondrial and

energetic perturbations, generation of ROS, and resistance to apoptosis. Many of these features of SSc fibroblasts are reminiscent of
the senescent cell phenotype, suggesting that targeting anti-apoptotic or senescence pathways could promote selective killing of SSc
fibroblasts and enhance resolution of fibrosis
Numerous molecules involved in intra-cellular signal transduction are abnormally expressed, mislocalized, or constitutively activated in SSc fibroblasts. The list includes Smad3, STAT3, protein
kinase C, FAK, Akt, Egr-1, p300, EN1, GLI proteins, AP1 family
members, and sirtuins SIRT1 and SIRT3. Elevated expression of
the prosurvival factors Bcl-2 and Akt in SSc fibroblasts potentially
reflecting mechanotransduction from a rigid microenvironment
may play a role in their resistance to apoptosis. Mitochondrial
alterations and spontaneous ROS generation contribute to the
sustained activation of SSc fibroblasts in a self-amplifying loop

## Conclusion

Insights from genetic association and whole-exome sequencing
studies, genome-scale analysis of gene expression, and epigenetic
modifications in bulk tissues and individual cell lineages have
identified a very large number of alterations in immune, inflammatory, developmental, aging, and metabolic processes that arise
from gene–environment interactions and underlie the clinical and
pathologic manifestations of SSc. At the same time, these unbiased high-throughput studies also highlight the surprising degree
of molecular heterogeneity and redundancy that are hallmarks
of SSc and represent a considerable challenge in classification,
prognostication, and treatment decisions. Of interest, the genes
associated with increased risk in SSc are predominantly those that
are involved in inflammation, innate immunity, and immune cell
signaling and that show pervasive overlap with the genetic risk
associated with SLE, psoriasis, rheumatoid arthritis, and other
autoimmune conditions. These observations suggest that immune
activation might have a primary role in determining SSc disease
susceptibility.
Nonetheless, it remains unknown why distinct autoimmune
diseases with shared genetic risk factors, such as SSc and SLE,
show such diverse and nonoverlapping clinical and pathologic
manifestations, natural histories, responses to immunomodulatory therapies, and disease outcomes. The roles of cellular aging,
and environmental exposures in SSc and how they elicit cell type–
specific durable epigenetic modifications that contribute to the
persistence of inflammatory, autoimmune, and fibrotic processes,
are beginning to be uncovered. These insights are likely to contribute identification of novel biomarkers of diseases activity and subtype and discovery and validation of novel therapeutic targets. An
improved granular understanding of pathogenesis is opening the
door for selective immunomodulatory, anti-fibrotic agents, and
epigenetic modifiers that can be applied in a precision-targeted
