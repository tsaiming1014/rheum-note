## Introduction

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is one of the most common inflammatory arthritides, affecting from 0.5% to 1% of the general population worldwide. Although the prevalence is surprisingly constant
across the globe, regardless of geographic location and race, there
are some exceptions. For instance, the occurrence of RA is somewhat lower in China (
∼
0.3%), whereas it is substantially higher in
other groups, such as the Pima Indians in North America (
∼
5%).
Because of its prevalence and the ready accessibility of joint samples for laboratory investigation, RA has served as a useful model
for the study of inflammatory and immune-mediated diseases. As
such, the information gleaned from these studies provides new
unique insights into the mechanisms of normal immunity.
RA is primarily considered a disease of the joints, but abnormal systemic immune responses are evident throughout its course
and can cause a variety of extra-articular manifestations, such as
vasculitis, nodules, and accelerated atherosclerosis. These manifestations clearly show that RA has features of a systemic disease that
can involve many organs. In some cases, autoantibody production
with the formation of immune complexes that fix complement
contribute to these extra-articular findings. One of the mysteries of RA is why the synovium is the primary target, although
the unique structure of its vascular bed and the display of certain
modified proteins (especially on solid surfaces like cartilage) provide an environment that is ideal for innate and adaptive immune
responses.
Although the causes of RA remain uncertain, combinations
of environmental and genetic influences clearly contribute. Clues
have been provided by detailed immunogenetic research and
observational studies demonstrating that asymptomatic autoimmunity (pre-RA) antedates the onset of arthritis by many years.
Studies after the onset of clinical symptoms have implicated smallmolecule mediators of inflammation, autoantibodies, cytokines,
growth factors, chemokines, adhesion molecules, epigenetics,
aberrant signal transduction, and matrix metalloproteinases
(MMPs) in the pathogenesis of disease. Synovial mesenchymal
cells display a variety of transcriptionally defined phenotypes,
exhibit aggressive behavior, and can invade joint tissues. Irreversible loss of articular cartilage and bone can begin soon after the
onset of RA; in some cases, bony abnormalities can be detected
in the microarchitecture associated with progression to disease.
Early treatment and very tight disease control through “treat-totarget” strategies improve long-term outcomes. Increased appreciation of how comorbidities, especially cardiovascular disease and
accelerated atherosclerosis, affect mortality has also led to aggressive therapeutic paradigms to suppress synovial and systemic
inflammation and improved long-term survival. Clinical trials in
RA have also provided insights into the diversity of disease mechanisms, which can, in turn, help investigators translate clinical
observations into a deeper understanding of disease etiology and
pathogenesis (Video 70.1).

## How Rheumatoid Arthritis Begins

The cause and pathogenesis of RA are complex and multifaceted.
A variety of predetermined factors (genes) and stochastic factors
(random events and environmental triggers) contribute to susceptibility and pathogenesis. A summary of how these mechanisms
interact to create and perpetuate RA is shown in
Fig. 70.1
. Individual mechanisms are discussed in greater detail throughout the
chapter.
The initiation of RA begins years before the onset of clinical
symptoms. The onset involves certain polymorphisms in specific genes and inflammation at mucosal surfaces that can lead
to modification of proteins, ultimately leading to production of
antibodies against these neoepitopes. This process evolves through
antigen spreading and ultimately helps break immunologic tolerance with concomitant autoreactivity. The earliest phases are
marked by environmental stress, either through toxic exposures
or activation of innate immunity (see

products, viral components, local infections on mucosal surfaces,
and other environmental stimuli. These stresses induce enzymes
that lead to the modification of normal peptides, most commonly
through conversion of arginine to citrulline but possibly through
other pathways like carbamylation or production of malonyldialdehyde–acetaldyde adducts.
Protein modification occurs often in healthy people, is selflimited, and has no obvious deleterious effects. Nevertheless, in
people with certain genetic backgrounds, most notably the class
II major histocompatibility complex (MHC) genes implicated
in RA, citrullinated peptides bind very tightly into the antigenbinding groove and are presented to T cells more efficiently than
the native unmodified protein. Because T cells are not exposed to
the altered peptide during development, developing T cells with
such specificity are not deleted in the thymus and, once present in
the periphery, can respond, driving an adaptive immune response
directed against these new epitopes.

These exposures often occur at mucosal surfaces, such as the
gut and oral cavity, although the data are particularly compelling for the lung. Altered peptides are loaded onto dendritic cell
(DC) MHC proteins. Antigen presentation can then occur in the
synovial germinal centers or in central lymphoid organs after the
loaded DCs migrate via the lymphatics. T cells are activated and
can produce pathogenic cytokines at these sites and help B cells
produce antibodies. These antibodies are most commonly directed
against citrullinated peptides, but some recognize other altered
peptides, such as through carbamylation. The anti-citrullinated
protein antibodies (ACPAs), including IgA isotype at mucosal surfaces, are produced against a variety of proteins, including fibronectin, vimentin,
α
-enolase, histones, and many others.
Over a period of years, the titers of ACPAs increase many-fold,
with expansion of the antigens recognized. Oligoclonal expansion
of antigen-specific B cells is followed by affinity maturation that
increases the avidity of the antibodies for their targets.
There
is no unique “RA-associated ACPA” but instead a general rise of
many different antibodies that bind to a variety of modified proteins. These systemic changes do not lead to significant synovial
pathology during pre-clinical stages of RA as shown in synovial
biopsy studies, even if individuals have arthralgias.
The ACPAs themselves are not sufficient to initiate synovitis,
and a “second hit” is necessary. The nature of the second signal
is not well defined, but immune complexes can serve this purpose in animal models. These complexes do not have to be specific
for altered peptides but can be directed at a myriad of antigens,
such as those including viruses or other xenoantigens. They can
engage Fc receptors in the synovium, especially on mast cells,
which release vasoactive mediators and increase access of ACPAs
to the joint. Once in the joint, if they encounter their antigen,
they can fix complement and initiate a cascade of events, leading
to recruitment of innate and adaptive immune cells, production
of additional cytokines and chemokines, and activation of local
fibroblast-like synoviocytes (FLS) and macrophages. As the cells
are recruited and activated, clinical synovitis ensues.
Additional genetic associations beyond the MHC can amplify
the adaptive response to modified proteins and autoreactivity
against the native proteins. For example, polymorphisms in genes
encoding key T cell signaling proteins (e.g., protein tyrosine phosphatase-22 [PTPN22]) can favor these events. Cytokine gene promoter polymorphisms, signal transduction gene polymorphisms,
and other genetic markers also could provide positive feedback to
enhance the response. Epigenetic changes that imprint synovial cells
through DNA methylation, microRNA (miRNA) production, or
histone modification can enhance local immune dysregulation.
Ultimately, a destructive phase ensues that has antigen-dependent and antigen-independent mechanisms and is mediated by
mesenchymal elements such as fibroblasts that degrade cartilage and
macrophage-derived cells like osteoclasts that erode into bone. Progressive epigenetic modifications occur during the evolution of disease that can alter the function of some synovial cells and enhance
matrix damage. Although anti-inflammatory molecules such as
soluble cytokine receptors (e.g., TNF receptors), suppressive cytokines (e.g., interleukin [IL]-10), cytokine-binding proteins (e.g.,
IL-18bp), protease inhibitors (e.g., tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases [TIMPs]), lipoxins, antioxidants, anti-angiogenic factors,
and natural cytokine antagonists may be present, they are not sufficient to arrest the inflammatory and destructive process. Suppressing the inflammatory response requires therapeutic interventions
that either modulate pathogenic cells, neutralize the effector molecules produced by the rheumatoid process, or restore tolerance.
Highly targeted therapeutics to individual cytokines, mediators, or cell lineages demonstrate remarkably diverse clinical
responses. Although overlap exists, the spectrum of responses
demonstrates that some patients have cytokine-dependent disease,
some have B cell–dependent disease, while others have T cell–
dependent disease (and some have a more complex combination).
These clinical observations suggest that RA is not a single pathogenic entity but is instead a spectrum of disorders that depends
on the genetic makeup and environmental triggers for individual
patients. By understanding the basic mechanisms of disease, we
can begin to untangle which RA a particular person might have,
determine how to individualize therapy, and eventually develop
new disease taxonomies that focus on pathogenesis rather than
clinical features.

The interaction of environmental and genetic factors conspires to
launch the complex process that leads to the initiation of rheumatoid synovitis. The most compelling evidence for a genetic
component is in monozygotic twins, in whom the concordance
rate is about 12% to 15% when one twin is affected, compared
with 1% for the general population. Nevertheless, the fact that
concordance is not higher suggests that other influences such as
the environment or epigenetics are as important as the genetic
component. The risk for a fraternal twin of a patient with RA is
also high (
∼
2% to 5%) but similar to the rate for other first-degree
relatives.
Although the immunogenetics is not fully understood, one of
the best-studied and perhaps most influential genetic risk factors
is the class II MHC haplotype of an individual. Single-nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs) in
PTPN22
and
PADI4
increase risk in
some (but not all) racial and ethnic groups. Genome-wide screens
have implicated more than 100 genes, many of which are involved
with immune function.
Most genes, however, have a relatively
modest contribution, and the susceptibility polymorphism confers only a 1.05- to 1.2-fold increase. Combinations of genes can
clearly interact with one another. For example, a 45-fold increase
in risk is conferred by expression of a combination of the risk
alleles of
HLA-DR, PTPN22,
and
TRAF1-C5.
Nevertheless,
this combination is found in less than 1% of people with RA,
and algorithms of other SNPs have not yet identified a critical

combination that can account for the disease. Additional progress
in understanding the role of genes in RA, including rare variants
that might be more important than some common polymorphisms, might clarify how individual alleles contribute to susceptibility, severity, and responses to targeted therapies.

### Role of Human Leukocyte Antigen-DR in Disease Susceptibility and Severity

The structure of class II MHC molecules on antigen-presenting
cells is associated with increased susceptibility and severity of RA
and accounts for about 40% of the genetic influence. A genetic
link between HLA-DR and RA was initially described in the
1970s with the observation that HLA-DR4 occurred in 70% of
patients with RA, compared with about 30% of control subjects,
giving a relative risk of having RA to those with HLA-DR4 of
approximately four- to fivefold.
The susceptibility to RA was originally ascribed to an association with the third hypervariable region of DR
β
chains, from
amino acids 70 through 74. The epitope is glutamine–leucine–
arginine–alanine–alanine (QKRAA), a sequence found in DR4
and DR14, in addition to some DR1
β
chains. The “susceptibility
epitope” (SE) on DR4
β
chains with the greatest association with
RA are DRB*0401, DRB*0404, DRB*0101, and DRB*1402
(
Table 70.1
). Up to 96% of patients with RA exhibit the appropriate HLA-DR locus in some populations.
In certain ethnic and
racial groups, the association with QKRAA is not as prominent.
The QKRAA epitope also predicts increased severity of established
RA, with a greater prevalence of extra-articular disease and erosions
in patients with two copies. Other HLA genes such as DRB*1301
contain the DERAA sequence and are associated with decreased
susceptibility to RA.
Microchimerism with transfer of maternal
cells expressing the SE could potentially contribute to disease susceptibility in individuals lacking the classic MHC genes.
Many explanations have been offered as to how the region associated with RA exerts its effect. The QKRAA sequence primarily
faces away from the antigen-binding cleft of the DR molecule,
which makes a direct effect on antigen binding unlikely. QKRAA
could serve as an autoantigen as a result of molecular mimicry in
some situations because some xenoproteins such as gp110 from
the Epstein–Barr virus (EBV) also include this sequence.
However, the mechanism is probably related to linkage disequilibrium between the SE and the residues at sites 11 and 13,
which lie in the antigen-binding groove of HLA-DR. A leucine or
valine at amino acid 11 is associated with increased risk of developing disease, whereas serine at amino acid 13 confers decreased
risk. Studies of antigen-binding affinities show that citrullinated
vimentin peptides bind much more avidly to the RA-associated
alleles than the unmodified protein. HLA-DR genes that are not
associated with RA do not display this disparity. Functional studies show that these same RA-associated alleles present antigen
more efficiently to T cells with higher amounts of cytokines such
as IL-17 and interferon (IFN)-
γ
.
A recent unbiased study isolated a panel of native and citrullinated peptides from HLA-DR–
peptide complexes. Several of the endogenous peptides induce
expression of CD40L on RA peripheral blood T cells and induce
IFNγ
production. These data support the notion that the RAassociated alleles play a key role selecting and displaying pathogenic peptides.
Based on these observations, the MHC association likely represents an independent risk factor for RA as a marker for immunoreactivity to altered peptides that lead to ACPAs.

### Epigenetics

Epigenetics describes phenotypic or gene expression properties
caused by mechanisms other than changes in the underlying DNA
sequence. Modification of cytosine–phosphatidyl-guanine (CpG)
DNA sequences by methylation, for example, can suppress gene
expression and plays a role in cell differentiation. Histone acetylation also alters accessibility of DNA for transcription factors
and RNA polymerases. miRNAs can bind to DNA and suppress
expression of key genes involved with the inflammatory process.
Epigenetic information, such as DNA methylation, can be transferred from one generation to the next, thereby providing an alternative mechanism for rapidly altering disease susceptibility in a
population as a result of the environment.
Most information on epigenetics in RA comes from studies of
RA synovium or cultured synoviocytes, the fibroblast-like cells that
form the lining of the joint. For example, candidate gene approaches
have identified several genes, such as
IL6, CXCL12,
and ephrin-B1
(EFNB1)
, that are hypomethylated, and others, like
HLA-DRB3,
EBF2,
and
IRX1,
that have increased methylation.
Unbiased
approaches suggest that the overall level of methylation in RA FLS
is not different from osteoarthritis (OA) or normal cells. However,
there are distinct patterns of differential methylation in RA, with
some loci being hypermethylated, whereas others are hypomethylated.
RA-specific methylation patterns in FLS are apparent in early
RA but are somewhat different from longstanding RA. Epigenetic
changes that distinguish early and late disease are involved in cell
differentiation and proliferation pathways, perhaps indicating that
the disease becomes more aggressive after it is established.
Detailed analysis of synoviocyte methylation patterns indicates
that the differentially methylated genes are enriched in pathways
such as cell adhesion, focal adhesion, matrix regulation, cytokine–
cytokine receptor interactions, and other functions important to
immune function. Some of these pathways could be activated due
to the local cytokine milieu because IL-1 can suppress expression
of DNA methyl transferases. However, the effect of cytokines is
transient in culture, and the cells return to baseline after the cytokine is removed. Permanent FLS imprinting in RA could be due
to a variety of additional influences, such as reactive oxygen or
nitrogen, or they could be inherited.
The patterns of DNA methylation also demonstrate joint specificity in RA.
Some of these changes are disease-independent (i.e.,
they occur in other diseases or in normal individuals) and are probably defined by the different functional needs of each joint. In addition, certain epigenetic marks are specific to RA and suggest that the

pathogenesis of disease in individual joints might vary. For example, RA hip and knee FLS have distinct transcriptomes and DNA
methylation that show differences in pathways related to targeted
therapies like IL-6 or Janus kinases (JAKs) (see
Fig. 70.2
). Distinct
gene expression profiles could account for why some joints might
respond to a particular agent, whereas others have less benefit.
DNA methylation abnormalities also occur in adaptive
immune cells in RA (
Fig. 70.2
). For example, peripheral blood
naïve T cells are differentially marked in RA and share some of
the same marks as observed in RA FLS.
Numerous differentially
methylated loci have been observed in RA peripheral blood B cells
and involve genes related to immune responses.
Some of the

This observation is particularly

Notably, the clinical response

expression of the p53 tumor suppressor gene and TNF-induced
expression of MMP-1.
Cytokines such as TNF can increase
HDAC1 expression in cultured FLS. SIRT1 also exhibits HDAC
activity and is overexpressed in RA FLS and synovium. Blocking
SIRT1 in FLS reduces IL-6 and IL-8 production. HDAC inhibitors
are also effective in animal models of arthritis, including adjuvant
arthritis in rats and collagen-induced arthritis in mice.
These data
indicate that histone marks can play a role in remodeling the chromatin to increase production of inflammatory mediators in RA.
More recently, the epigenomic landscape of RA FLS was
mapped, including whole genome DNA methylation, histone
marks, chromatin accessibility, and transcriptomics.
Using novel
computational tools, several patterns of epigenetic imprinting were
identified, with most of the characteristic marks in RA involving
active enhancers and promoters. Pathway analysis revealed several
anticipated changes related to immune responses. Unexpected
findings were also discovered, such as an overlap between the
Huntington’s disease pathway and RA pathways. Biologic validation showed that at least one member of the Huntington’s disease
gene, Huntington-interacting protein-1, regulates FLS cytoskeletal rearrangement, migration, and invasion. Mining these comprehensive data sets identified changes in the epigenetic landscape
of genes related to nutrient transporters and metabolic pathways
and will hopefully provide more new insights into pathogenesis, as
well as identify interesting therapeutic targets.
Several miRNAs, such as miRNA-124a, are decreased in RA
FLS compared with OA cells. This particular miRNA can suppress cell cycling and chemokine genes. Increasing miRNA-124a
levels in RA synoviocytes decreased the production of the chemokine macrophage chemoattractant (MCP)-1.
Forced expression
of miRNA-203 increases metalloproteinase and IL-6 expression
by synoviocytes as well.
miRNA-155 is especially interesting
because it is overexpressed in RA synovial cells and peripheral
blood cells. Knocking down this miRNA reduces MMP and cytokine expression in these cells. In vivo miRNA-155 deficiency has
mixed effects in animal models, with decreased severity in collagen-induced arthritis and no effect in the passive K/BxN serum
transfer model.
miRNA-147 deficiency improves synovitis in
the pristane-induced model of arthritis, and the mechanism is
related to alterations of the zinc finger protein ZNF148.
Several other miRNAs are differentially expressed in RA, including
miRNA-16, -124a, -202, -134a, -23, -346, and -15a.
miRNA34a in RA patients can also contribute to abnormal DC function
by regulating the tyrosine kinase receptor AXL.
The enormous volume of accumulating data on the genetic
and epigenetic influences that contribute to the initiation and
perpetuation of RA is daunting. No single gene polymorphism or
epigenetic mark can account for the disease. It is more likely that
complex combinations incrementally increase risk and that the

full expression of RA requires one or many environmental triggers. The nature of these stochastic events is increasingly understood (see below), which creates opportunities to intervene and
potentially prevent RA.

### Environment Influences on Rheumatoid Arthritis Risk

As noted above, environmental stress is a likely cofactor that can
lead to progression from “at-risk” individuals with a genetic/epigenetic burden to the onset of clinically apparent synovitis. Smoking
is the best-defined environmental risk factor through activation of

The idea that aberrant immune responses are directed toward selfantigens in RA was recognized with the discovery of RF in the
blood of patients with the disease. Initially described by Waaler
and later by Rose, it was not until the mid-1950s that investigators
firmly established that RF is an autoantibody. ACPAs have largely
supplanted RFs as the most sensitive and specific antibody associated with RA. They reflect the underlying immune dysregulation in
RA and, in addition to being a biomarker of disease, can also play
an important role in the pathogenesis of inflammation in the joint
and perhaps even in mucosal sites. Although ACPAs have largely
supplanted RF as the most sensitive and specific test for diagnosing
RA, the presence of combinations of antibody classes, like ACPA
and other specificities, could increase specificity for RA and help
identify very-high-risk populations for development of synovitis.

### Anti-citrullinated Protein Antibodies

Antibodies that bind to CPs are produced by patients with RA
and have significant prognostic implications. The discovery originated with reports in the 1970s that antibodies directed against
keratin were detected in rheumatoid serum and that the primary
target antigen was filament-aggregating protein, filaggrin. These
antibodies bind to epitopes on filaggrin that contain citrulline,
which is derived from post-translational modification of arginine by PADI. Humans have four isoforms of PADI. PADI2 and
PADI4 are especially abundant in synovium,
and certain SNPs
are associated with RA. The function of PADIs in normal immune
responses is not certain, and citrullination of some chemokines
can decrease their chemoattractant properties. Histones are also
citrullinated by PADs, which can alter chromatin accessibility and
gene expression and adds a significant gene regulatory component
to citrullination as a stress response.
PADI
induction and citrullination of peptides are not specific
to RA and can occur in many inflammatory settings.
Not only
are other inflammatory arthropathies marked by CPs, but other
organs such as the lungs in smokers also have significant
PADI
activity. The presence of CPs in the lungs of smokers or in other
mucosal sites could provide the antigen exposure that can contribute to anti-CP antibody production and begin the long road
to the development of RA. CPs are also present in most animal

extra-cellular deposits that often colocalize with various isoforms
of

especially
PADI2

the microenvironment during release of DNA.

### Antibodies to Other Modified Proteins

ACPAs are the best characterized antibodies directed against modified antigens in RA. Other antibody systems are also present and
define additional subsets of disease. For example, some patients
have antibodies that recognize carbamylated proteins. In this case
lysine is converted to homocitrulline through a nonenzymatic reaction with isocyanic acid and creates neo-epitopes in much the same
manner as conversion of arginine to citrulline. Both ACPAs and
anti-carbamylated protein antibodies (ACarPs) are present in a subset of patients with RA. More intriguingly, up to 30% of ACPA
patients have ACarPs, including IgG and IgA antibodies.
Like ACPAs, antibodies to modified proteins like carbamylated
fibrinogen can occur before the onset of clinical disease in nearly
40% of patients with RA, and carbamylation of vimentin can
be induced by exposure to cigarette smoke in pre-clinical models.
The antibodies are also associated with more severe disease and increased joint damage.
They can participate in the
inflammatory response, and mice immunized with carbamylated
peptides can develop erosive arthritis. Like ACPAs, the antibodies alone do not cause synovitis. Nevertheless, lymphocytes from
immunized animals can transfer disease to naïve mice.
An additional antibody system directed against malondialdehyde–acetaldehyde (MAA) adducts was recently described.
The
modified peptides are present in RA synovial tissue and colocalize
with B cells and CPs. Elevated levels of anti-MAA antibodies are
also present in serum. Anti-MAA titers are higher in the joint than
in the blood, suggesting local production.
These antibody systems, like ACPAs, can be detected in some
healthy individuals that are at risk for developing RA. For example,
ACarPs are found in the pre-RA phase, often with ACPAs, and are
associated with subsequent destructive disease.
IgG and IgA antibodies that detect MAA also predict future RA.
Another antibody
system associated with pre-RA detect EBV early antigen, although
the mechanisms and pathogenic significance are still uncertain.

### Rheumatoid Factor

The identification and characterization of RF as an autoantibody
that binds to the Fc portion of IgG was the first direct evidence that
autoimmunity might play a role in RA. Their utility as a diagnostic test for RA has largely been replaced by ACPAs, including the
diagnostic test detecting antibodies to cyclic citrullinated peptides
(CCPs). RF
patients have more severe clinical disease and complications than do seronegative patients, including increased cardiovascular complications, although this outcome might be due to the
association of RFs with ACPAs.
RF can activate synovial fluid
complement by the classic pathway. Large quantities of IgG RF
and RF-containing complexes are produced by rheumatoid synovial tissue and detected in cartilage. B cell–targeted therapies such
as rituximab can deplete peripheral B lymphocytes and modestly
decrease titers of RF. This effect does not correlate precisely with
clinical responses, and synovial RF production is not significantly
changed by B cell depletion. Nevertheless, RF levels often decrease
in responders and increase again coincident with clinical relapses.
About three-quarters of RA patients have detectable IgM RF
levels in their serum. Like ACPAs, first-degree relatives of RFpositive patients with RA are frequently seropositive, suggesting
a genetic contribution. Although IgG and IgM RFs are the most
abundant in RA, IgA and IgE RF have also been detected. IgE RF
can potentially complex with aggregated IgG in synovial tissue,
and the subsequent complexes could then degranulate synovial
mast cells through activation of Fc receptors in the synovium.
RF is commonly found in individuals without RA, especially
with increasing age. Most of these people will never manifest this
disorder. This observation casts doubt on a direct role of RF in the
pathogenesis of RA. However, it is become increasingly clear that
the RFs produced in people with RA differ from those produced
by healthy people or from patients with paraproteins. The avidity of RF for the Fc portion of IgG is much greater in RA than
in Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia or in cryoglobulins. Many
RFs expressed by abnormal B cells (such as Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia) and by normal B cells in human tonsils are derived
from the germline. In contrast, RFs in RA are derived through
rearrangements and somatic mutations of the germline genes. RF
production can be quite high in rheumatoid synovium; IgM RF
represents about 7% of the total IgM and 3% of the total IgG
produced by synovial cell cultures.
RFs in RA primarily use the IgG variable heavy 3
(IGHV3)
gene and a variety of variable light (VL) genes, whereas natural
antibodies with RF activity use
IGHV1
or
IGHV4
and the
IGKV3
genes.
The
κ
light chain repertoire expressed in RF-producing
cells isolated from one RA patient was enriched for two specific V
κ
genes but contained many somatic mutations and non–germlineencoded nucleotides.
Therefore the selection and production
of these RFs, like ACPAs, were likely due to antigenic drive with
somatic mutation rather than derived directly from the germline.

### Autoimmunity to Cartilage-Specific Antigens

The emerging picture of autoimmunity in RA implicates patterns of self-directed responses against articular and nonarticular

antigens that vary between individuals and different stages of
disease.
Type II Collagen
Immunization with type II collagen can cause arthritis in rodents,
and the disease can be passively transferred by serum containing anti-collagen antibodies or by transfer of lymphocytes from
affected animals. T cells are required for initiation of collageninduced arthritis, and a major immunogenic and arthritogenic
epitope on type II collagen resides in a restricted area of the type
II collagen chains. The disease is caused by anti-collagen antibodies that localize to the joint and activate complement.
Type II collagen antibody generation probably does not initiate
RA but can amplify the inflammatory response (
Table 70.3
). Collagen immunoreactivity, like other native proteins, likely begins
with antibodies that bind to citrullinated type II collagen, with
epitope spread to include the unmodified or denatured protein. Of
interest, T cell responses to type II collagen, especially a dominant
epitope at amino acids 263 to 270, are much greater if the protein is glycosylated or citrullinated.
Sera from patients with RA
contain antibody titers to denatured bovine type II collagen that
are significantly higher than those found in control sera.
Anticollagen antibodies purified from the sera of patients with RA can
activate complement, generating C5a when they bind to cartilage.
In addition, isolated synovial tissue B lymphocytes actively secrete
anti–type II collagen antibodies in almost all patients with seropositive RA, whereas articular cells from patients who do not have
RA do not secrete these antibodies.
The presence of anti–type II collagen antibodies might have
prognostic significance in RA. A Swedish cohort of patients with
early RA was stratified based on the presence of these antibodies.
High levels were associated with elevated acute phase reactants and greater disease severity, especially in patients with acute
onset of disease. When patients were followed for up to 5 years,
the presence of anti–type II collagen antibodies was associated
with less disease activity and a favorable outcome in that study.

also elicit autoantibody responses in RA. Proteomic analysis of RA

identifies anti-gp39 antibodies in patients with early RA, which
may be associated with less aggressive disease.

### Autoimmunity to Nonarticular Antigens

Autoimmune responses in RA can also involve antigens that are
broadly expressed beyond the joint.
These antigen-antibody
systems comprise a pattern of autoimmune responses that can
potentially lead to synovial inflammation.
Glucose-6-Phosphoisomerase
Spontaneous inflammatory arthritis develops in K/BxN mice as a
result of antigen-specific immunity against a seemingly irrelevant
nonarticular antigen.
Autoantibodies to the ubiquitous enzyme
glucose-6-phosphate isomerase (GPI) cause the disease, and transient synovitis also develops in normal mice injected with the
serum of the affected K/BxN mice or anti-GPI antibodies. Mice
immunized with GPI also develop antibodies and then inflammatory arthritis. The passive arthritis model requires the alternate
complement pathway, Fc receptors (especially FcR
γ
III), and mast
cells, but not T or B cells. IL-1 is more important than TNF in
this model, and the IL-1 knockout mice are almost completely
protected from disease. Notably, this effect can be overcome by
administration of a Toll-like receptor (TLR) ligand like lipopolysaccharide (LPS), which shares a downstream signaling pathway
with IL-1. Other cytokines, such as IL-6, and signaling pathways,
such as p38 MAPK, and upstream kinases, such as MKK3 and
MKK6, are also required for full expression of the disease.
Immunohistochemical studies show that GPI adheres to the surface of cartilage, which permits local antibody binding and complement fixation. Initiation of synovial inflammation increases vascular
permeability and provides access to the synovium and cartilage.
This phase is followed by antibody-mediated complement fixation
when serum proteins have access to GPI-decorated cartilage.
The display of GPI on the surface of cartilage suggests that the
pathogenesis might be similar to arthritis models caused by “jointspecific” antigens. Anti-GPI antibodies are detected in a relatively
small percentage of patients with RA and are not specific for the
disease. Nevertheless, it might, along with several other antibody
systems, contribute to local complement fixation by binding to
GPI arrayed on cartilage.
Heterogeneous Nuclear Ribonucleoprotein-A2 and
Heavy-Chain Binding Protein
Several other autoantigens that are expressed in synovium have
been characterized in RA, although they are also produced in many
other locations. For instance, antibodies directed against the heterogeneous nuclear ribonucleoprotein-A2 (hnRNP-A2), sometimes
called RA33, occur in about one-third of patients with RA, as well as

patients with other systemic autoimmune diseases. Anti-RA33 antibodies are also produced in the TNF transgenic mouse model of RA,
suggesting that proinflammatory cytokines can independently lead
to a breakdown of tolerance for this particular protein.
Of interest, RA33
patients with early RA (symptoms for less than 3 months)
tend to have less destructive disease.
Although not especially sensitive or specific for RA when used in isolation, an algorithm involving
anti–RNP-A2, RF, and ACPA can be used to predict which patients
with early synovitis will progress to erosive RA.
Autoantibodies that bind to stress-protein Ig heavy-chain binding protein (BiP) have also been observed. About 60% of patients
with RA have anti-BiP antibodies, and the specificity is reportedly
more than 90%. In addition to humoral responses, RA T cells can
proliferate in response to this protein.
Heat Shock Proteins (HSPs)
Immunity against HSPs contributes directly to synovitis and
joint destruction in some pre-clinical models, including adjuvant
arthritis in rats, in which T lymphocytes recognize mycobacterial
HSP65. Some of these cells cross react with cartilage proteoglycan
epitopes, perhaps explaining the targeting of joints.
Some patients with RA have elevated levels of antibodies to
mycobacterial HSPs, especially in synovial fluid. The majority of
T cell clones isolated from RA synovial fluid with specificity to
mycobacterial components express the
γδ
-TCR and do not display CD4 or CD8 surface antigens. Freshly isolated synovial fluid
T cells from patients with RA briskly proliferate in response to
recombinant 65 kDa HSP, whereas the response to other recall
antigens such as tetanus toxoid is not increased. Synovial fluid
mononuclear cells activated by the 60 kDa mycobacterial HSP
inhibit proteoglycan production by human cartilage explants.
Overall, the panoply of antibodies to native and modified antigens
reflects immune dysregulation and the propensity for auto-reactivity.
The precise role of these antibodies in disease etiology is uncertain,
although it is more likely that most of them exacerbate rather than initiate synovitis. ACPAs, however, might play a more pivotal role than
the other autoantibodies in seropositive RA as a marker that indicates
high risk and can create proinflammatory immune complexes.

### T Cell Metabolism in Early RA

Compared with cells derived from individuals without disease,
naive CD4
CD45RA
T cells from RA patients have a decreased
expression of the glycolysis-activating enzyme, 6-phosphofructo2-kinase/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase 3 (PFKFB3), which results
in a decrease of ATP levels and lactate and an activation of the
pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) to generate NADPH and biosynthetic precursors to products to fuel T cell expansion and function.
T cells from patients with RA also proliferate in part due
to activation of the cell cycle kinase ATM, component of a redoxdependent signaling pathway, and a shortening of the G
/M phase
of the cell cycle and commit these cells to the Th1 and Th17 cell
lineage rather than Treg.
RA T cells also have defective activation of the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) pathway and
aberrant activation of mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1
(mTORC1), which are also critical for T cell differentiation.

### Innate Immunity: Toll-like Receptors and Inflammasome in the Joint

Infectious agents could contribute to the initiation or perpetuation of RA through a variety of mechanisms. Some arthrotropic
microorganisms could potentially infect the synovium and cause
a local inflammatory response. The innate immune system could
also indirectly lead to the onset of synovitis. Pathogen-associated
molecular pattern receptors, especially the TLRs, are expressed by
sentinel cells in the host that provide a first line of defense (see
Chapter 19
). These receptors recognize preserved structures in bacteria and other infectious agents and permit responses to pathogens.
Repeated engagement of TLRs in the synovium could potentially initiate or exacerbate inflammation. Even if pathogens are not
detected or are not unique for RA, nonspecific TLR activation could
increase vascular permeability and ingress of antibodies (e.g., ACPAs).
Supporting this notion, several animal models of disease require TLR
ligands for initiation, such as TLR9 in adjuvant arthritis. TLR2 is
required for streptococcal cell wall arthritis, and the chronic T cell–
dependent phase of that model requires TLR4. Mice lacking TLR4
have significantly less joint damage induced by IL-1 overexpression,
even though synovial inflammation is still robust.
Thus, endogenous TLR ligands play a key role in matrix regulation independent
of inflammatory responses. TLR2 stimulation also impairs activity of
M2 macrophages, which normally produce a variety of anti-inflammatory factors that could suppress synovial inflammation.
As discussed in more detail in
Chapter 95
, the inflammasome also regulates innate immunity and involves the recognition
of “danger signals” and products of pathogens such as muramyl
dipeptides and uric acid. One central component is NALP3, which
is linked to caspase 1 (IL-1 convertase) by adapter proteins. When
the inflammasome is engaged, caspase 1 is activated, and IL-1 is
processed and released. Mutations in this pathway, especially in
cryopyrin, have been associated with autoinflammatory disorders
such as Muckle–Wells syndrome and familial cold autoinflammatory disease. NALP3 is abundant in RA synovium and is constitutively expressed by FLS and macrophages. Other inflammasome
components and related genes are highly expressed in RA peripheral blood cells, including apoptotic speck protein (ASC), Mediterranean fever (MEFV), and cryopyrin-NALP3.
Expression in
cultured FLS is markedly increased by TNF. In addition, a NALP3
SNP has been associated with increased risk of RA. Because the
TNF-induced inflammasome activation can induce cytokine production after exposure to bacterial products and other danger signals, it could participate in synovial IL-1 and IL-18 regulation.
TNF also leads to mitochondrial (mt) DNA release and triggers
a type I IFN response that is dependent on the cyclic guanosine
monophosphate-AMP synthase (cGAS) stimulator of IFN genes
(STING) pathway.
Notably, an IFN gene signature is present
in about half of early RA patients and is associated with decreased
initial response to treatment.

### Bacteria, Mycobacteria, Mycoplasma, and Their Components

Active infection of synovial tissue by pyogenic bacteria is an
unlikely cause of RA, and extensive searches for a unique or specific organism in synovial tissue or joint effusions have been negative. Antibodies to certain organisms such as
Proteus
are reportedly
elevated in the blood of patients with RA, but this finding could
represent nonspecific B cell activation. Most patients with RA
and reactive arthritis contain bacterial DNA sequences in their
synovium. The bacteria identified are not unique and generally
represent a cross-section of skin and mucosal bacteria, including
Acinetobacter
and
Bacillus
spp. The accumulation of bacterial
products is probably due to phagocytosis by local macrophages.
Bacterial peptidoglycans are also abundant in RA synovial tissue. Antigen-presenting cells containing these products express
TLRs that can be ligated and produce proinflammatory cytokines.
Either the peptidoglycans could activate cells in situ, or phagocytic cells from other sites or the blood could engage the molecules
and then migrate to the joint. In either case, it is not difficult to
imagine how they can contribute to synovial inflammation.
Several animal models of arthritis are dependent on TLR2,
TLR3, TLR4, or TLR9. For instance, rodents injected with streptococcal cell walls (TLR2 ligand) develop severe polyarticular
arthritis. The initial phase of disease resolves and is then followed
by a chronic T cell–dependent phase that resembles RA. The arthritogenicity of complete Freund’s adjuvant in the rat adjuvant
arthritis model is dependent on mycobacterial DNA that binds
to TLR9 and activates an adaptive immunity. Endogenous TLR4
ligands such as HSPs and fibrinogen also play a role in immune
complex models such as passive K/BxN arthritis.
Mycoplasma-derived superantigens such as from
Mycoplasma
arthritidis
can directly induce T cell–independent cytokine production by macrophages and exacerbate or trigger arthritis in mice
immunized with type II collagen. Despite circumstantial evidence,
most efforts to identify
Mycoplasma
and
Chlamydia
organisms or
DNA in joint samples have been negative, and no direct evidence
exists to support these organisms as etiologic agents.

### Epstein–Barr Virus, DnaJ Proteins, and Molecular Mimicry

EBV is a polyclonal B lymphocyte activator that increases the production of RF. Rheumatoid macrophages and T cells have defective suppression of EBV-induced proliferation of human B cells.
Patients with RA have higher levels of EBV shedding in throat washings, an increased number of virus-infected B cells in the circulating
blood, higher levels of antibodies to normal and citrullinated EBV
antigens, and abnormal EBV-specific cytotoxic T cell responsiveness compared with control subjects. CD25
B cells from patients
with RA respond more briskly to EBV than do those from healthy
people, with increased polyclonal IgG and IgM production.
Defective elimination of EBV-transformed lymphocytes in
RA has fueled speculation that immune dysfunction contributes
to initiation of disease, although anti-EBV (and anti-parvovirus
B19) antibody titers might be lower in ACPA
RA patients.
Persistent EBV infection is also associated with lymphoproliferative disease in RA.
Of interest, EBV DNA can also exacerbate
collagen-induced arthritis in mice with increased IFNγ
and
IL-17 production as an alternative mechanism to enhance synovial inflammation.
Sequence homology between the susceptibility cassette in
HLA-DR proteins and the EBV glycoprotein gp110 is a tantalizing clue that molecular mimicry might be important. Like
DRB*0401, gp110 contains the QKRAA motif, and patients with
serologic evidence of a previous EBV infection have antibodies
against this epitope. Hence T cell recognition of EBV epitopes
in some patients with the SE might elicit an immune response
directed at innocent bystander cells. RA T cells, especially synovial
fluid T cells, have increased proliferative responses to gp110, perhaps supporting the molecular-mimicry link between a variety of
QKRAA-containing proteins and arthritis.
gp110 is only one of many xenoproteins such as the
Escherichia
coli
HSP DnaJ containing QKRAA, so this might be a more general mechanism. Of particular interest, responses directed against
DnaJ protein also recognize human Hsp40 proteins DnaJA1 and
DnaJ2. Therefore molecular mimicry is not limited to HLA-DR
and can involve other cellular proteins.

### Parvovirus

Parvovirus B19 infection has been implicated in some patients
with RA based on serologic evidence, including the nonstructural
protein NS1. Only about 5% of patients have evidence of recently
acquired parvovirus B19 infection at the time of disease onset.
Of interest, 75% of RA synovium samples contain B19 DNA
compared with about 20% of non-RA control subjects. Immunohistochemical evidence of the B19 protein VP-1 was detected in
patients with RA but not in patients with other forms of arthritis.

Nevertheless, evidence of the B19 genome in RA joint samples
was not found in other studies.
The mechanisms of B19-induced synovitis, when it does occur,
could also involve alterations in FLS function.
In a cell-culture
model of synoviocyte invasion into cartilage, infection with the
parvovirus significantly increased the migration of cells into the
matrix. Mice that are transgenic for the B19 protein NS1 were
more susceptible to collagen-induced arthritis and developed high
titers of anti–type II collagen antibodies. These data suggest that
the B19 genome might not cause arthritis but can enhance an
arthritogenic response to other environmental stimuli. The observation that B19 VP-1–induced IFNγ
production by peripheral
blood cells is low in B19
RA patients supports the possibility that
a possible RA-B19 link relates to immune dysregulation rather
than direct infection.

### Other Viruses

COVID-19 could potentially activate or exacerbate autoimmune
diseases, including RA. Several cases of ACPA-positive RA have
been described after severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS–CoV-2) infection. One study carefully monitored
the patient’s autoantibodies from the beginning of their SARS–
CoV-2 infection to the onset of clinical RA. Although ACPAs
were already detectable when the patient tested positive for
SARS–CoV-2; by the time arthritis developed, ACPA titers had
substantially increased. Increasing ACPA titers could indicate epitope spreading before clinical RA.
Because rubella virus and the rubella vaccine can cause arthritis
in humans, the virus has attracted some attention as a possible triggering agent. Live rubella virus can be isolated from the synovial
fluid of some patients with chronic inflammatory oligoarthritis
or polyarthritis without clinical evidence of rubella. Nevertheless,
most patients with rubella do not have the classic polyarticular
involvement and display an oligoarthritis involving large joints.
As with B19 infection, it is possible that a small subset of patients
with chronic polyarthritis that is called RA actually have direct
infection with wild-type or attenuated rubella virus.
Studies of synovial tissue in a variety of inflammatory and
noninflammatory arthropathies have also demonstrated DNA of
other viruses such as cytomegalovirus and herpes simplex, but not
adenovirus or varicella-zoster. As with bacterial DNA, parvovirus,
and EBV, the localization of viral DNA to the inflamed joint is
probably related to the migration of inflammatory cells containing
the viral genome or other nonspecific mechanisms rather than an
active infection.
Retroviral infections could also contribute to the etiology of
RA. However, endogenous retroviruses are abundant in both
inflamed and normal synovium. In one study, higher levels of
HERV-K10 gag protein from a common endogenous retrovirus
were detected more often in RA compared with OA and normal
peripheral blood mononuclear cells. In addition, the pX domain of
one human retrovirus, human T lymphotropic virus-1 (HTLV-1),
causes synovitis in transgenic mice, and synoviocytes from patients
infected with HTLV-1 have increased cytokine production.

## Microbiome

The observation that airway inflammation might contribute to
disease risk has led to various studies to evaluate contributions at
other mucosal sites. Similar associations are observed in animal
models of arthritis, in which arthritis susceptibility and severity
can be lower in rodents raised in a germ-free or defined bacteria
environment. The most prominent association is with periodontitis, commonly caused by
Porphyromonas gingivalis,
which was
confirmed in a Swedish cohort.
This bacterium is one of the
few that produces its own PADI, which could lead to local citrullination of proteins such as fibronectin.
P. gingivalis
can also act
by stimulating TLR2, which increases IL-1 production and leads
to production of pathogenic Th17 cells.
It is not clear whether
P. gingivalis
is specific or causative in new onset RA because it is
equally abundant in many control populations (78% and 83%
for early RA and control, respectively).
Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans
was implicated in oral dysbiosis in RA.
The
organism produces a pore-forming toxin (leukotoxin A), which
releases hypercitrullinated proteins from neutrophils and provides
substrate for subsequent ACPA formation.
In the gut, expansion of
Prevotella copri
species occurs in
patients with early-stage RA, with a concomitant decrease in
Bacteroides
(
Fig. 70.7
).
These differences are not seen in longstanding RA, although increased immune reactivity to a 27 kDa
P.
copri
protein and anti–
P. copri
antibodies were detected and associated with ACPA positivity.
In other studies, distinct patterns
of gut bacteria were observed in spondyloarthropathies, RA, and
healthy controls, including confirmation of the
P. copri
observations and noting a new correlation between the degree of synovitis
and
Euryarchaeota
organisms.
The abnormal gut microbiome is not fixed, as treatment of patients with a TNF inhibitor
modified the dysbiotic microbiome toward the healthy controls.
Blood markers of the microbiome, much of which is obtained
from mucosal surfaces, have similar findings and suggest that
blood tests might be able to distinguish disease-specific patterns
that can be altered with novel approaches like fecal transplantation.
Certain gut bacterial taxa and their genes are associated
with clinical response to drugs such as methotrexate.
The importance of the microbiome is also evident in murine
models, in which an increasing segmented filamentous bacteria
population is associated with increased follicular T helper (Th)
activity and increased arthritis severity in the K/BxN model of
arthritis.
Antibiotic treatment to decrease the gut flora
decreased arthritis severity. Of interest, oral administration of
P. gingivalis
exacerbates synovial inflammation in the mouse collagen arthritis model.
Not only the dysbiosis but the intestinal
barrier integrity plays a role in translating autoimmunity to inflammation. Increased levels of zonulin, a potent regulator for intestinal
tight junctions, in serum were accompanied by a leaky intestinal
barrier, dysbiosis, and inflammation. Treatment with the zonulin
antagonist larazotide acetate, which specifically increases intestinal
barrier integrity, effectively reduced arthritis onset in an animal
model of arthritis.
These emerging data on the critical role of
the bacteria and intestinal barrier integrity creates opportunities
to modulate the microbiome as a form of treatment or prevention.
